# The Llama 3 Herd of Models

## TL;DR
Meta's technical report on the Llama 3 herd — 8B, 70B, and a flagship 405B dense Transformer — pre-trained on roughly **15.6T multilingual text tokens** with a context window extended to **128K tokens** through a continued-pre-training stage on top of an 8K base. The 405B is roughly **3.8 × 10²⁵ FLOPs**, ~50× the largest Llama 2, and the paper claims parity with GPT-4 and Claude 3.5 Sonnet on a broad benchmark sweep. The interesting datapoint for Linus is not the training pipeline — irrelevant at 32 GB — but that the scaling-laws-driven recipe pushes context length, not on-board state, as the lever for "memory."

## The problem (in plain language)
Foundation models keep improving largely as a function of scale: more parameters, more high-quality tokens, longer contexts, more careful curation. The Llama line is Meta's open-weights bet on that frontier — a counterweight to closed GPT-4-class systems — and Llama 3 is the generation that actually closes the gap on most public benchmarks while remaining downloadable. The paper's question is essentially engineering: how do you actually pull off a 405B-parameter, 15T-token, 128K-context training run without it falling over, and what data and architectural choices matter at that scale?

The framing the paper offers ("data, scale, managing complexity") is conservative on architecture — a vanilla dense Transformer with grouped-query attention, RoPE at base frequency 500,000, SwiGLU, RMSNorm, no MoE — and aggressive on data and infrastructure. The bet is that quality and quantity of tokens, plus a long-context retrofit, dominate clever architecture changes at this scale.

## What the paper proposes
A three-model herd of dense Transformers, all sharing a 128K-token vocabulary (100K from tiktoken plus 28K for non-English) and grouped-query attention with 8 KV heads. The 405B has 126 layers, model dimension 16,384, FFN dimension 53,248, and 128 attention heads; the 70B and 8B are scaled-down variants. Pre-training proceeds in two stages: a standard pre-training stage at 8K context on the bulk of the 15.6T tokens, then a **continued pre-training stage** that extends the context window in steps up to 128K, using an attention mask that prevents cross-document attention (irrelevant at 8K, important at long context). Post-training is deliberately simple — SFT, rejection sampling, and DPO rather than RLHF — to keep training stable and reproducible.

The data pipeline is where most of the work lives: aggressive de-duplication at URL, document (MinHash), and line levels; heuristic and model-based quality filters (fasttext + DistilRoberta classifiers trained against Llama 2 judgments); separate code and math extraction pipelines; multilingual filtering across 176 languages identified by fasttext. The final mix is roughly **50% general knowledge, 25% math/reasoning, 17% code, 8% multilingual**, with a final "annealing" stage on small high-quality code/math sets that helps the 8B substantially (+24% on GSM8K validation) but is negligible for the 405B — a striking signal that in-context learning saturates the small-data benefit at scale.

Scaling laws are extended beyond Chinchilla-style next-token loss to *predict downstream benchmark accuracy* via a two-step fit (compute → log-likelihood on the task → accuracy). This is what justified picking 405B parameters at 3.8 × 10²⁵ FLOPs as the compute-optimal-ish point for the budget.

## Key results
- **Model sizes**: 8B, 70B, 405B dense parameters; the 405B has 126 layers, 16,384-dim, 128 attention heads, 8 KV heads.
- **Training tokens**: ~15.6T text tokens for the flagship (vs. 1.8T for Llama 2). Tokenizer compresses English from 3.17 to 3.94 chars/token vs. Llama 2.
- **Compute**: 3.8 × 10²⁵ FLOPs for the 405B, ~50× Llama 2's largest.
- **Context window**: 8K base, extended to **128K via continued pre-training**. RoPE base frequency raised to 500,000 to support the extension.
- **Benchmarks (405B)**: MMLU 87.3, MMLU-Pro 73.3, HumanEval 89.0, GSM8K 96.8, MATH 73.8, ARC-Challenge 96.9, IFEval 88.6, BFCL 88.5 (tool use), MGSM 91.6 (multilingual). Competitive with GPT-4o (88.7 / 74.0 / 90.2 / 96.1) and Claude 3.5 Sonnet (89.9 / 77.0 / 92.0 / 96.4).
- **Long context**: 95.2 on ZeroSCROLLS/QuALITY, 83.4 on InfiniteBench/En.MC, **98.1 on NIH/Multi-needle** at 128K — strong needle-in-a-haystack performance.
- The 8B itself scores 69.4 MMLU, 84.5 GSM8K, 72.6 HumanEval — best-in-class for its size and the actually-Linus-relevant tier.

## What's reusable for Linus
Linus will not train anything at this scale, and the 405B will not fit on an M1 Max even quantized aggressively. The paper is **background reference**, not a build target. Two threads do connect:

The first is **long-context as Meta's chosen memory substitute**. Llama 3's approach to "remembering more" is to grow the context window from 8K to 128K via a continued-pre-training stage, and lean on RoPE base scaling plus document-boundary attention masking to make it stable. Read against the [Garrison memory framework](../../context/notes/garrison_memory_makes_computation_universal.md), this is exactly the move Garrison pushes back on — paying quadratic cost to *simulate* memory inside attention rather than treating recursive state and reliable history access as first-class architectural primitives. The 98.1 NIH/Multi-needle score shows the simulation works, but the 128K wall is real and the cost curve is what it is. Useful for Linus as a calibration point: a frontier lab burning a 405B-parameter, 15.6T-token, multi-thousand-GPU run still tops out near the regime Garrison flags as the architectural ceiling.

The second is **the 8B as a credible Worker candidate**. Llama 3.1 8B Instruct fits on the M1 Max in quantized form and posts numbers (MMLU 69.4, GSM8K 84.5, HumanEval 72.6, 128K context, tool use, multilingual) competitive with anything in its class. It is already pulled by many Ollama users. For Phase 1 baselines and Phase 2 Worker dispatch ([2502.16721v1.md](2502.16721v1.md)), Llama 3.1 8B Instruct should be in the bake-off with Qwen2.5-Coder-7B and Mistral-7B variants — not because the paper's training was special, but because the *artifact* is now a strong open-weights baseline with native long context and tool use.

The 128K-context capability is itself worth keeping in the back pocket for episodic-memory experiments: holding a long session transcript in context is a (wasteful, quadratic) but operational form of "reliable history access" for a single session, even before Linus has a real episodic store.

## What's NOT applicable / hype filter
The bulk of this paper is irrelevant to Linus. The pre-training data pipeline (15T-token web curation, fasttext language ID across 176 languages, MinHash de-dup at scale, model-based quality classifiers trained on Llama 2 outputs) is infrastructure for a lab with a research SuperCluster and production GPU clusters; Linus is downstream of pre-trained checkpoints, full stop. The scaling-laws methodology (IsoFLOPs across 6 × 10¹⁸ to 10²² FLOPs to fit a power law and pick 405B) is interesting but not actionable on a MacBook. The training-infrastructure section, the post-training SFT/DPO recipes, the safety pipeline (Llama Guard 3), the multimodal compositional adapters for vision/video/speech — all of this is reference material, not a build target.

The benchmark-parity claim against GPT-4o and Claude 3.5 Sonnet is also worth a hype filter: parity is on *public benchmarks*, not on the Maestro tasks that hosted Claude actually serves Dan. Llama 3.1 8B is not a Maestro substitute; it is a Worker candidate. The paper's framing ("accelerate a responsible path towards AGI") is marketing, not architecture.

Most importantly: the implicit architectural claim — that a vanilla dense Transformer plus more data plus more context wins — is exactly the brute-force scaling line that the [Garrison note](../../context/notes/garrison_memory_makes_computation_universal.md) and the [BitNet line](2402.17764v1.md) push against on different axes. Llama 3 is the strongest current evidence that the brute-force line still works *if you have the budget*. Linus does not have that budget, and that is a feature, not a bug.

## Connections
The Llama 3 result anchors the "scale + emergent capability" pole that the [Garrison memory note](../../context/notes/garrison_memory_makes_computation_universal.md) is in dialogue with — Garrison's quadratic-wall critique reads more sharply with Llama 3 in mind, since Meta's chosen path for "more memory" is exactly to push the wall outward via continued pre-training rather than to add a separate state mechanism. The opposite pole is the [BitNet 1.58 paper](2402.17764v1.md) and its successors ([2310.11453v1.md](2310.11453v1.md), [2504.18415v2.md](2504.18415v2.md), [2510.13998v1.md](2510.13998v1.md)) plus the [bitnet.cpp / Bonsai-demo](../../repos/Bonsai-demo/) inference path: "design hardware for the model" rather than "train ever larger dense Transformers." Linus sits squarely on the BitNet side of that argument by hardware constraint.

Llama 3's data pipeline — aggressive de-duplication, model-based quality filtering, knowledge classification — is the "scale" sibling of the curation argument in [FineWeb (2406.17557v2.md)](2406.17557v2.md). Both make the same methodological point that data quality is the dominant lever. The [conversational-speed paper (2502.16721v1.md)](2502.16721v1.md) actually benchmarks Llama 3 8B and finds it among the *slowest* in tok/s but among the *fastest* in task-completion time on short-answer tasks because it generates fewer output tokens — a directly Linus-relevant operational note for picking it as a Worker. The 128K NIH/Multi-needle result also touches the [sentence-embedding line (2408.08073v2.md)](2408.08073v2.md) indirectly: long-context retrieval inside the model is a competitor to external embedding-based retrieval, and Linus needs both.

Phase mapping: relevant to Phase 1 (Recon & Baselines) as a Worker candidate to bench, and to Phase 2 (MVP) if Llama 3.1 8B Instruct ends up as a default Worker. The training pipeline never enters the Linus roadmap.

## Open questions for Dan
1. **Worker bake-off scope.** Should Llama 3.1 8B Instruct (Q4_K_M or Q5_K_M via Ollama) be added to the Phase 1 bake-off alongside Qwen2.5-Coder-7B and Mistral-7B, with the [2502.16721v1.md](2502.16721v1.md) three-task protocol (minimal / fixed-length / open-ended) as the measurement schema? The paper plus the speed paper together suggest it would rank well on task-completion time even where its tok/s lags.
2. **Long context vs. episodic store.** Llama 3's bet is "buy memory by extending context to 128K." The Garrison framework argues that's the *wrong* axis — memory should be a separate component with its own integrity guarantees, not attention pretending to be a tape. For Linus, is the right Phase 2 design to deliberately *cap* in-context window usage low (say 8–16K) and route everything beyond that through a real episodic store, even if the underlying Worker model technically supports 128K? Setting that policy up front prevents the lazy "just stuff it all in context" pattern.
3. **Multi-needle as an episodic-store benchmark.** The 98.1 NIH/Multi-needle score is the closest public proxy to "reliable history access" Garrison's framework demands. Should `benchmarks/dan_tasks/` include a synthetic multi-needle task — but evaluated against Linus's *episodic store* rather than against a model context — to measure whether the orchestration layer's memory subsystem actually meets the four sub-requirements (addressable, distinguishable, ordered, integrity-preserving)?
4. **Distillation rather than fine-tuning.** Llama 3.1 405B is too large to host, but its outputs are not. Is there a Phase 6 path where Linus uses a hosted Llama 3.1 405B (or Claude) as a teacher to distill domain-specific behaviors into a local 8B or BitNet student, rather than fine-tuning on raw corpus? This sidesteps the 405B-on-M1 impossibility while still capturing some of what scale buys.
5. **Open-weights longevity.** Llama 3 is released under the Llama 3 Community License (not OSI-open). Does the Linus principle of "fully under Dan's control" require treating Llama 3 as a *transitional* Worker — usable now, replaceable when a comparably strong fully-open or BitNet-class model appears — and if so, where in [DECISIONS.md](../../DECISIONS.md) should that policy live?
