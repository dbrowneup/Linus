# Generative design of synthetic gene circuits for functional and evolutionary properties

## TL;DR

Gallup & Steel's 2026 paper from Oxford's Department of Engineering Science demonstrates that a **conditional variational autoencoder (CVAE)** — a relatively simple generative model — can design synthetic three-node RNA gene circuits with prescribed **adaptation behavior** (sensitivity ≥10 plus precision ≥1, the integral-feedback signature) plus an **evolutionary property** (low ruggedness around the circuit in the binding-energy landscape). The construction is precise: simulate ~2 × 10⁵ random three-node RNA circuits via IntaRNA 2.0 minimum-free-binding-energy predictions, label each circuit with its (sensitivity, precision) signature from ODE-simulated dynamics, train a CVAE conditioned on a normalized adaptation metric, and sample new circuit topologies with prompted adaptation values in $[-0.2, 1.2]$. Two empirical findings are load-bearing. First, **prompt adherence is high** ($R^2 = 0.97$ for reconstruction; KDE distribution overlap with prompted target close to 1.0 across the operational range) even at very small dataset sizes — as few as ~1500 training samples deliver high prompt adherence, contradicting the synthetic-biology field's assumption that generative models require massive curated datasets. Second, the **CVAE's latent space recapitulates known biology**: hierarchical clustering on the binding-interaction parameters of adaptable circuits identifies five distinct adaptation motifs, matching the buffer/balancer/proportioner classes from Ma et al. 2009 (the canonical Cell paper that enumerated adaptation-capable network topologies). The model can also be **jointly prompted for function and evolvability** ("design me an adaptable circuit with low ruggedness"), producing circuits that cluster in low-ruggedness subregions of the latent space — a proof of concept for combined phenotype + evotype design. For Linus, this paper is a **canonical example of typed structured prediction wrapping free-text rationale at the level the BioReason-Pro convention prescribes (S25)** — the structured output is `{topology, adaptation_score, ruggedness_score, motif_class, evidence: [...]}` and the verification is grounded in physical simulation. The CVAE itself is small enough to run on M1 Max (~5 min training on RTX 4090, comparable wall time on Apple Silicon); the IntaRNA simulation step is the expensive part (~2-3 hours of CPU time for the sequence-sampled dataset, much faster for the parameter-sampled variant).

## The problem (in plain language)

Synthetic biology engineers genetic circuits to do specific things — sense a chemical, oscillate, integrate signals, return to a baseline after perturbation (adaptation). The dominant design loop has been: have a biological intuition for what topology produces the desired dynamic, build it, characterize it, iterate. This is slow, expensive, and relies on accumulated domain knowledge that doesn't generalize across product classes (the design rules for an oscillator don't transfer to an adaptation circuit).

The promise of generative ML for synthetic biology is to **flip the loop**: instead of building first and characterizing second, prompt a generative model with the desired function (an adaptation curve, a steady-state response, etc.) and have the model output candidate topologies and binding parameters. Several lines of work have demonstrated this for proteins (RFdiffusion for protein structures, ESM-style protein language models, etc.) and for DNA/RNA sequences for individual parts (riboswitches, promoters, repressors). But **whole-circuit generative design** — generating an entire genetic circuit with prescribed dynamic behavior — has remained underdeveloped, in part because the data is sparse (circuits are expensive to characterize) and in part because behavior depends on a complex interaction between topology and parameters that's hard to capture in a generative model.

The specific challenge the paper addresses: **adaptation**. An adaptable circuit responds strongly to a step change in its input (high sensitivity) and then returns close to its pre-stimulus level (high precision). Ma et al. 2009 enumerated the network topologies capable of this — only two classes, both involving integral feedback (either a buffer node or a proportioner-balancer pair) — and showed that only ~0.01% of random network topologies sit in the adaptable region. So the design problem is genuinely sparse: most candidate circuits don't work, and the few that do work follow specific topological rules.

The further complication is **evolutionary stability**: a perfectly tuned circuit at the parameter level might sit on a "rugged" evolutionary landscape, where small mutations destroy its function entirely. For applied synthetic biology in living cells, where mutations happen continuously, the circuit needs to be both functional and robust to perturbation. Designing for function alone is insufficient if the resulting circuit can't survive its own evolution.

The paper attacks both problems with one model. The CVAE learns the joint distribution of `(circuit topology, adaptation level, ruggedness level)`; at inference time, the user prompts for desired function and/or robustness, and the decoder samples circuits matching the prompt.

## What they propose

**Substrate: three-node RNA circuits via IntaRNA 2.0.** Each circuit is a fully connected three-node graph where nodes are RNA molecules and edges are pairwise RNA-RNA binding interactions (six interactions total: $k_{11}, k_{12}, k_{13}, k_{22}, k_{23}, k_{33}$, where $k_{ij}$ is the minimum-free-binding-energy between RNA $i$ and RNA $j$, with self-interactions included). The choice of RNA over protein is deliberate: RNA-RNA binding is well-characterized by physics-based simulators (IntaRNA 2.0 from Mann et al. 2017), the energy-to-rate conversion is well-grounded via the Gibbs free-energy equation, and the same topological rules from Ma et al. 2009 apply to mutual-repression networks (which is what RNA-RNA binding implements).

**Two training datasets (Fig. 1b).** The sequence-sampled dataset starts from random nucleotide sequences (~2 × 10⁵ samples), runs IntaRNA on every pair to get binding energies, then maps energies to ODE reaction rates. Computationally expensive (~2-3 hours single-threaded for 2 × 10⁵ circuits). The parameter-sampled dataset bypasses sequence generation by uniformly sampling binding energies directly from $[-30, 0]$ kcal/mol, then doing the energy-to-rate mapping (~5 min for 2 × 10⁶ circuits). The parameter-sampled dataset is larger and more uniformly distributed across the design space; the sequence-sampled dataset is biased toward the structures RNA sequences naturally produce. The paper compares both.

**Conditioning: adaptation metric (Eq. 5).** The custom adaptation metric is $a = -(\alpha(\log_{10}(s) - s_{\text{centre}})^2 + (\log_{10}(p) - p_{\text{centre}})^2) + C$ with $s_{\text{centre}} = 7$, $p_{\text{centre}} = 7.5$, $\alpha = 3$, $C = 1000$. This is a smooth concentric gradient toward the adaptive region (sensitivity ≥10, precision ≥1) — the metric peaks at the operational target and decreases smoothly elsewhere. Normalizing to $[0, 1]$ gives the conditional variable $c$ for the CVAE.

**CVAE architecture (Fig. 1d).** Encoder and decoder are stacks of 3 linear-32-leakyReLU-He-normal layers each. The encoder takes $[x, c]$ (circuit parameters concatenated with the adaptation label), produces mean and log-variance for a 32-dimensional latent $z$; the decoder takes $[z, c]$ (or at sampling time, $[\text{noise}, c]$) and outputs the reconstructed circuit. Trained with MSE reconstruction loss plus KL divergence (weight $\sim 10^{-3}$ to $10^{-4}$ — the paper sweeps to identify this narrow workable range). Trained on a single RTX 4090 GPU; ~5 min wall time per full training run.

**Joint prompting (Fig. 4).** To prompt for both adaptation and ruggedness, the conditional variable becomes a two-element vector $c = [\text{adaptation}, \text{ruggedness}]$, and the decoder takes $[\text{noise}, c]$ for sampling. The paper shows that joint-prompted circuits cluster in the appropriate latent-space region (e.g., high-adaptation + low-ruggedness samples cluster differently from high-adaptation + high-ruggedness samples), demonstrating that the latent space genuinely encodes both axes.

**Ruggedness metric (Eq. 6, Eq. 7).** Perturb each of the six binding-interaction strengths $k_{ij}$ by a small Gaussian perturbation, re-simulate dynamics, measure the change in adaptation value. Aggregate the squared changes across all six perturbations. High ruggedness = small perturbations cause large dynamics changes = evolutionarily unstable. Low ruggedness = circuit dynamics survives mutations = evolutionarily stable.

**Comparison baselines.** The paper compares: (1) **categorical labels** vs. **continuous labels** — continuous wins; (2) **single objective** (adaptation only) vs. **two objectives** ($\log s$, $\log p$ separately) vs. **three objectives** (add overshoot/initial); single-objective on the custom adaptation metric wins; (3) **scalar conditioning** vs. **one-hot conditioning** — modest difference. The paper concludes that **simpler objectives with the right encoding outperform complex objectives** — a general lesson for synthetic-biology generative models.

## Key results

**Reconstruction and prompt adherence (Fig. 2).** $R^2 = 0.97$ on held-out test data for reconstruction of the binding-interaction parameters. KDE distribution of CVAE-generated circuits centered near the prompted adaptation target across the operational range $[-0.2, 1.0]$. Prompt adherence is high even for prompts outside the training range ($[1.0, 1.2]$), showing limited but real extrapolation.

**Latent space recapitulates known motifs (Fig. 2d-f).** UMAP of CVAE latent space, colored by adaptation, shows clear separation by adaptation level. Hierarchical clustering on the binding-interaction parameters of high-adaptation circuits identifies **five distinct motif clusters** (A-E in the paper). Visual inspection of representative circuits from each cluster shows they correspond to the buffer/balancer/proportioner classes from Ma et al. 2009 — the canonical Cell paper enumerating adaptation-capable topologies. **The CVAE learns the same topology classes as the analytical theory**, without being told them.

**Small data is sufficient (Fig. 3b).** Reconstruction accuracy $R^2 = 0.97$ holds even when the training set is as small as ~1500 samples. Prompt adherence remains high (~0.8 KDE overlap) at ~500 samples. The paper's key methodological claim: **generative models for synthetic biology don't require massive datasets**; the right architecture and the right conditioning can work with experiments-scale data (~1000s of characterized circuits), which is achievable in a wet lab.

**Joint prompting works (Fig. 4).** Circuits prompted with $(c_{\text{adapt}}, c_{\text{rugg}}) = (1.0, 0.6)$ vs. $(1.0, 0.9)$ produce qualitatively different topologies — both adaptable, but the low-ruggedness samples have more interaction symmetries (specifically more uniform $k_{ij}$ values that buffer mutations better). The motif clusters split into evolvable vs. stable subclasses.

**Objective design strongly shapes learning (Fig. 3c).** Multi-parameter objectives that treat metrics separately (e.g., $[\log_{10} S, \log_{10} P]$) reduce prompt adherence compared to the unified scalar adaptation metric. The unified scalar collapses sensitivity and precision into a single value but, paradoxically, leads to **better** learning because the model doesn't have to reason about the trade-off between two correlated metrics.

**Released artifacts.** Code at `github.com/olive004/EvoScaper` and `github.com/olive004/genetic_circuit_generator`. Trained models and the generated 1M-circuit parameter-sampled dataset on Zenodo (DOI: 10.5281/zenodo.17194085). Both repositories use JAX + Haiku for the CVAE, IntaRNA 3.4.1 for binding-energy predictions, and `diffrax` for ODE simulation. Docker container provided. **Apple-Silicon-friendly stack** (JAX has Metal support; IntaRNA is C++ and builds natively on M1; diffrax is JAX-native).

## What's reusable in Linus

**Phase 7 — canonical typed-structured-prediction skill for biology (S25, BioReason-Pro convention).** The strongest fold-in. The Linus engineering convention "typed structured prediction wrapping free-text rationale" (S25) is canonical in the BioReason-Pro shape: `{predicted_function, confidence, evidence: [...], rationale: "..."}`. Gallup & Steel's CVAE provides the same shape for circuit design: `{topology: [k_11, k_12, ..., k_33], adaptation_score, ruggedness_score, motif_class, evidence: ["simulated_dynamics_match_Fig_2c"], rationale: "..."}`. The model output is machine-queryable; the rationale field can be populated by a downstream Worker that explains which motif class the circuit belongs to and why. For Linus's Phase 7 biology Workers (per [`biology-phase7-roadmap.md`](../specs/biology-phase7-roadmap.md)), this is a directly portable skill: wrap the CVAE as a Linus tool with the typed-structured-prediction output shape, and the rest of the Linus orchestration layer can compose it with other tools (sequence validators, dual-use safety filters per DEC-0047).

**Phase 7 — RNA circuit-design tool (`linus.tools.bio.generate_circuit`).** The most concrete tool addition. Inputs: target adaptation level (continuous, $[-0.2, 1.2]$), optional target ruggedness level. Outputs: a list of N candidate circuits with their predicted dynamics, ranked by typed-structured-prediction confidence. Substrate: the released EvoScaper repository + the trained models + IntaRNA. The tool fits within the Linus tool-registry deployment-field model (DEC-0046): "local-only" (no external API call), "compute_required: GPU-or-CPU-medium", "validation: physics-based simulation". The Phase 7 biology roadmap should list this as a Tier-1 skill.

**Phase 7 — biosecurity tiering for circuit-design tools (DEC-0047).** Circuit design for adaptation is a **dual-use** capability — adaptive integral-feedback circuits are foundational engineering primitives for synthetic biology, but the same generative-design pattern extends to circuits with pathogenic dynamics (e.g., circuits engineered to respond to specific environmental signals in ways that release toxic products). DEC-0047's biosecurity tier framework applies: an adaptation-circuit-design tool is **Tier 2** (no direct toxin generation, but enables design of dynamic genetic systems). The biosecurity gating layer (per DEC-0047) should require an auditable rationale field on every generation; the BioReason-Pro typed-structured-prediction output shape naturally provides this audit surface. The paper itself doesn't discuss biosecurity, but Linus's adoption should layer DEC-0047 controls on top.

**Phase 7 — wet-lab integration model.** The paper's wet-lab workflow (Section 9) is informative even though it doesn't run a wet-lab validation in this work. The pipeline they describe — generate sequences from a CVAE, score via IntaRNA, validate via ODE simulation, then take top-K to the wet lab — is the **generate→score→filter→wet-lab pattern** the [`generative-biology-synthesis.md`](../syntheses/generative-biology-synthesis.md) already documents as the canonical workflow. The Gallup & Steel paper is a clean exemplar of the in-silico portion; the Phase 7+ Linus tool registry should expose each step as a separately invocable tool, so that Linus Workers can compose the full pipeline (generate → simulate → rank → output) and human-in-the-loop wet-lab handoff happens at a well-defined boundary.

**Phase 1c — small-data lesson as a benchmark calibration.** The paper's key methodological finding — generative models for synthetic biology don't require massive datasets, with ~1500 training samples sufficient for high prompt adherence — is a useful calibration for Linus's domain skill benchmarks. The default assumption (especially for users coming from LLM foundation models) is that more data is always better; this paper is a counter-example where the right architecture and the right conditioning extract clean signal from small data. For Phase 1c+ benchmarks on Dan's domain corpus, this argues for **starting with small curated datasets** rather than maximally-comprehensive ones. The Phase 1c spike ([`phase1c-spike.md`](../specs/phase1c-spike.md)) should include a small-vs-large data-scaling sweep on a synthetic-biology task to confirm the finding generalizes.

**Methodology — IntaRNA 2.0 as a validation oracle.** The paper's use of IntaRNA 2.0 (Mann et al. 2017) as the ground-truth oracle for RNA-RNA binding energies is a clean methodological pattern: a fast physics-based simulator validates the generative model's outputs. The Phase 7+ biology tool registry should include physics-based validators alongside generative tools, as a paired pattern. For protein circuits, the analog is AlphaFold or ESMFold; for metabolic pathways, the analog is COBRApy or ECMtool. The point is that **every generative biology tool should ship with a paired validator** — this is the engineering discipline that prevents Phase 7+ tools from outputting plausible-but-wrong predictions.

**Phase 1+ — `evolvability` as a first-class objective for biology skills.** The paper's joint prompting for function + ruggedness is a useful framing beyond synthetic biology. Many Linus biology skills will face the same trade-off: a prediction can be confident-but-fragile (sensitive to small input perturbations, e.g., single-amino-acid substitutions) or robust-but-conservative (stable across input perturbations, but missing edge cases). The "ruggedness" metric in Eq. 7 generalizes: $r = \sum_i (\Delta_i / \epsilon)^2$ where $\Delta_i$ is the change in prediction under perturbation $i$. The Phase 7 biology tool registry should expose a `ruggedness` field alongside `confidence` for any predictive skill, computed by the tool's wrapper at inference time.

## What's NOT applicable / hype filter

**Three-node circuits are toy-scale; real synthetic-biology projects need 5+ nodes.** Section "Beyond existing models" acknowledges this: "Beyond existing models, we argue that there is an opportunity to further develop machine learning tools and architectures such as hypergraph models, biologically inspired neural networks, self-supervised learning, or hybrids for synthetic biological tasks. Given the difficulty of predicting binding between biological molecules, using separate models for the tasks of designing circuit topology vs. sequence is perhaps a more feasible option. Novel architectures better geared to operate at multiple levels of abstraction than VAEs could reduce more complex biological networks, such as large adaptable biochemical pathways [13] or >3-node genetic circuits [106], into overarching symbolic motifs." For Linus's Phase 7+ biology Workers, the three-node CVAE is a starting point; production-scale circuit design needs different architectures (graph neural networks, transformers over circuit topologies). The Phase 7 biology roadmap should list this as a known scaling limitation.

**RNA-RNA binding is the simplest substrate; protein-protein and protein-DNA interactions are harder.** The paper deliberately chose RNA-RNA binding because IntaRNA gives a clean energy-to-rate mapping. Protein interactions don't have this clean substrate — predicting protein-protein interaction strength is itself a research problem (ESM2-style protein language models, AlphaFold-Multimer). For Linus to extend this pattern to protein-circuit design, the validator stack is more complex. The Phase 7 tool registry should not pretend "the same CVAE generalizes to protein circuits" — that's a research project, not an integration.

**The KL-weight sweep (Fig. 3a) shows a narrow workable range.** The CVAE's KL-divergence weight has to be in $[10^{-4}, 10^{-3}]$ for prompt adherence to hold; outside this range, the model degenerates (high KL = ignores conditioning, low KL = posterior collapse). This is a known instability of VAE-based generative models, and the paper acknowledges it ("posterior collapse"). For production deployment in Linus, the CVAE-based tool should ship with the KL weight pinned at the paper's value, and users should not be exposed to a hyperparameter that has a 1-order-of-magnitude usable window. This is an operational caveat, not a fundamental flaw.

**The "evolutionary stability" metric is one specific definition of robustness.** The paper's ruggedness metric (Eq. 7) measures sensitivity to **binding-energy perturbations**, treating each $k_{ij}$ independently. In real biology, mutations don't perturb binding energies uniformly — single-amino-acid substitutions in an RNA-binding protein can dramatically change one interaction while leaving others unchanged. A more biologically realistic ruggedness metric would account for the **mutation spectrum** at the sequence level, not the energy level. The paper acknowledges this ("we account for the change in adaptation between original and perturbed circuits... High ruggedness arises more readily when many topological links are vulnerable"); Linus's biology tool wrapper should not over-claim that the ruggedness number is the full evolutionary-stability picture.

**No wet-lab validation in this paper.** The paper is fully in-silico. The generative model produces circuits, the IntaRNA-based simulator validates them, and the comparison is between simulated and prompted behavior. **No actual RNA was synthesized; no actual circuit was built.** Therefore the strong claim — that CVAE-designed circuits work in cells — is **not yet experimentally established**. The paper's framing ("This work serves as an important proof of concept for utilising generative models in genetic circuit design") is appropriately hedged. Linus's adoption should preserve this hedge in the tool documentation: "in-silico design candidates, requires wet-lab validation."

**The "evolvability vs. stability" trade-off is shallow at this scale.** The paper's joint-prompting demonstration (Fig. 4) shows that the model can be prompted for both adaptation + ruggedness, with the latent space distinguishing the two regimes. But the difference between high-ruggedness and low-ruggedness adaptable circuits at the three-node level is modest — both classes are functional; the low-ruggedness class is just slightly more robust. At larger scales (>5 nodes), the trade-off should be sharper. For Linus's purposes, the result is encouraging but not yet conclusive evidence that "evotype design works."

## Open questions for Dan

1. **CVAE-as-Linus-tool vs. CVAE-as-reference.** Should the Phase 7 biology roadmap include direct integration of EvoScaper as a Linus tool (`linus.tools.bio.generate_circuit`), or treat it as a reference implementation that Linus's own circuit-design tool will be re-implemented from? The first is faster (the released code works) but introduces a JAX dependency that conflicts with the Linus Python core stack. The second is cleaner architecturally but is real implementation work (~1 week).

2. **Biosecurity tier for circuit-design tools (DEC-0047).** Adaptation circuit design is a foundational synthetic-biology primitive — not directly dangerous, but enables design of dynamic genetic systems including pathogenic ones. Where does it sit in the DEC-0047 tier framework? Tier 1 (read-only knowledge) is too restrictive; Tier 3+ (actively dangerous content generation) is too restrictive. Tier 2 seems right but warrants explicit policy: what auditing controls apply? What rate-limits? Who in the audit log?

3. **Adopt "ruggedness/evolvability" as a generic skill output field?** The paper's ruggedness metric is specific to perturbing binding energies, but the framing — "how much does the prediction change under small input perturbations?" — generalizes. Should every Linus biology skill expose a `ruggedness` field alongside `confidence` and `rationale`? The cost is one extra forward pass per prediction (with perturbed input); the benefit is a measurable robustness indicator that the user can act on.

4. **Wet-lab validation gating policy.** The paper is fully in-silico. For Linus to expose this as a Phase 7 skill, what is the policy on user-facing predictions? Options: (a) ship as "in-silico design candidates, requires wet-lab validation"; (b) ship only when paired with a Linus tool that scores candidates against known wet-lab-validated circuit databases; (c) defer skill release until wet-lab validation of Gallup & Steel's specific designs is published. (a) is the obvious choice but worth being explicit.

5. **Generative tool + physics-based validator as a paired-pattern convention.** The paper's IntaRNA-as-validator pattern is portable: every generative biology tool should ship with a paired validator. Should this be a Phase 7 convention in the tool registry — that any tool with `category: generative_biology` must also reference a `validator: <tool_name>` in its registry metadata? The validator would be invoked automatically by the orchestration layer when generative output is requested.

6. **The MAUDE / ARC-AGI-style "biology benchmark" for the Phase 1c+ suite.** Gallup & Steel's CVAE benchmark — recapitulate the Ma et al. 2009 motifs from the data — is a clean diagnostic: can the model rediscover known biological structure? This generalizes to a benchmark format: "show the model perturbed/incomplete representations of known biological systems and measure whether it converges to the known structure." For Phase 1c+, this would complement ARC-AGI (memory diagnostic, DEC-0035) and the MK-NIAH suite (long-context retention) with a domain-specific Linus-relevant benchmark.

7. **EvoScaper as a Phase 7 reference repo for inclusion in `repos/`.** The EvoScaper repository is small (~5K LOC), MIT/Apache-licensed, JAX-based, and includes both the training pipeline and a working pre-trained model. Should it be cloned into `repos/` as a reference for Phase 7 biology Workers? It would be a good companion to the existing BioReason and Bacformer repos (per [`g9-bio.md`](../syntheses/repo-clusters/g9-bio.md)) — three reference implementations across different biology levels.
