# The Era of 1-bit LLMs: All Large Language Models are in 1.58 Bits

## TL;DR

The Microsoft Research follow-up to the original BitNet. It shows that an LLM whose every weight is restricted to one of
three values — `{-1, 0, +1}` — can match an FP16 LLaMA model of the same size and training-token budget on perplexity
and downstream tasks, _starting at 3B parameters_, while using ~3.5× less memory, ~2.7× less latency, and ~71× less
energy on the matrix multiplications. The "1.58" in the name is `log₂(3)` — the information-theoretic cost of storing
one of three values.

## The problem (in plain language)

Modern LLMs spend most of their compute on matrix multiplications between 16-bit floating-point weights and 16-bit
floating-point activations. Floating-point multiply is expensive in both energy and silicon area; you also have to
shuttle these big weights from DRAM to on-chip SRAM constantly, and that movement dominates inference cost more than the
math itself. Post-training quantization (squashing trained FP16 weights down to 4-bit or 8-bit _after_ the fact)
recovers some of this cost, but it's a lossy retrofit — the model wasn't trained to live in low precision.

The alternative pursued here is to _train from scratch_ in low precision. If every weight is constrained to
`{-1, 0, +1}` from the start, then "matrix multiply" reduces to "add, subtract, or skip" — pure integer addition, no
multiplications. The math gets cheap, the weights get tiny, and (the paper claims) the model can still learn.

## What they propose

**BitNet b1.58** = the original BitNet architecture (a Transformer where `nn.Linear` is replaced with `BitLinear`),
trained from scratch with 1.58-bit ternary weights and 8-bit activations, using LLaMA-style components (RMSNorm, SwiGLU,
rotary embeddings, no biases) so it slots into existing infra (HuggingFace, vLLM, llama.cpp).

The technical core is the **absmean quantization function**: scale the weight matrix by its mean absolute value, then
round each entry to the nearest of `{-1, 0, +1}`. Adding the zero state is the headline change vs. the original 1-bit
BitNet (which only used `{-1, +1}`) — it gives the model an explicit "feature filter" (a way to drop a connection
entirely), which the authors argue is what unlocks parity with FP16 baselines.

In practice: every linear layer in the transformer becomes a sparse ternary matrix that you can implement with integer
adders.

## Key results

- **Perplexity parity at 3B**: BitNet b1.58 3B matches LLaMA 3B perplexity (9.91 vs 10.04 on the FP16 baseline, on
  RedPajama 100B tokens).
- **3.55× memory, 2.71× latency** at 3B; gap widens with scale — at 70B, **4.1× faster and 7.16× less memory**.
- **71.4× less energy** for matrix-multiply arithmetic on 7nm silicon (estimated, not measured).
- **8.9× higher throughput at 70B** on dual A100 80GB (max batch 176 vs 16 for FP16 LLaMA).
- New scaling-law equivalences claimed: 13B BitNet ≈ 3B FP16 cost; 30B BitNet ≈ 7B FP16; 70B BitNet ≈ 13B FP16.
- Trained to 2T tokens, BitNet b1.58 3B beats StableLM-3B on Winogrande, PIQA, SciQ, LAMBADA, and ARC-easy.

Scale: production-relevant. Up to 70B params on real GPU clusters. Not toy-scale.

## What's reusable in Linus

**Direct (Phase 6 fine-tuning, possibly sooner):** This is the founding paper for the entire BitNet family that Dan
already has [BitNet/](../../repos/BitNet/) and [Bonsai-demo/](../../repos/Bonsai-demo/) cloned for. It's the _why_
behind those repos. Bonsai-demo is the practical lead — a pre-built 1-bit 8B MLX model that should run within the 32 GB
unified memory budget and inference cheaply on the M1 Max, freeing memory and ANE bandwidth for parallel agents.

**Indirect (architecture):** The "design hardware for the model, not models for the hardware" framing here is the same
logic behind [pmetal/](../../repos/pmetal/) and the ANE work — Apple Silicon's ANE is closer to "specialized adder
array" than "GPU shader" and may map onto ternary inference better than any CUDA path. This paper is the strongest
published argument that the approach works at production scale.

**Aspirational (training):** Training a _new_ BitNet from scratch needs the same infrastructure as training an FP16
model — distributed pipeline parallelism, large clusters, large datasets. That's not on the table for Linus. We are
downstream consumers of pre-trained 1-bit checkpoints, not trainers.

## What's NOT applicable / hype filter

- **Energy claims are 7nm-silicon estimates**, not Apple Silicon measurements. M1 is 5nm and has fundamentally different
  power/area tradeoffs; the 71× number is illustrative, not predictive of M1 performance.
- **2-bit kernel** they benchmark with is a CUDA kernel via Ladder. Apple Metal/MLX equivalents need to be checked or
  built — that's exactly the kind of gap [pmetal/](../../repos/pmetal/) and similar projects are trying to close.
- **Throughput numbers assume A100 cluster + pipeline parallelism**. Single-machine M1 Max latency/memory benefits are
  real, throughput claims aren't directly transferable.
