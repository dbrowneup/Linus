# Trading-R1: Financial Trading with LLM Reasoning via Reinforcement Learning

## TL;DR

Trading-R1 is the same-lab successor to **TradingAgents** ([`2412.20138v7.md`](2412.20138v7.md)) — Tauric Research /
UCLA collapse the seven-role multi-agent trading firm into a **single Qwen3-4B model** fine-tuned with LoRA via two
coupled techniques: (a) **supervised reverse chain-of-thought distillation**, where API-only proprietary reasoning
models (`o3-mini`, `o4-mini`, GPT-4.1, GPT-4.1-nano) supply final trading recommendations and a planner LLM reconstructs
the hidden CoT trace post-hoc to use as SFT supervision; and (b) a **three-stage easy-to-hard RL curriculum** (Structure
→ Claims → Decision) using GRPO, where each stage is warm- started by SFT, refined by RFT, and augmented by
self-distillation with reject-sampling. Training corpus is **Tauric- TR1-DB**: ~100k samples spanning 18 months (Jan
2024 – May 2025), 14 large-cap blue-chip equities + ETFs, and **five heterogeneous data sources** (technical,
fundamentals, news, insider sentiment, macro). Output is a typed structured prediction: a five-tier rating
(`Strong Sell` / `Sell` / `Hold` / `Buy` / `Strong Buy`) mapped to portfolio weights, plus a free-text investment thesis
with XML-tagged claim/evidence/source structure, evaluated against volatility- adjusted reward labels. Backtested June –
August 2024 on AAPL, GOOGL, AMZN, NVDA, META, MSFT, SPY: outperforms small-LM baselines, instruction-following large LMs
(LLaMA-3.3, LLaMA-Scout, GPT-4.1, Qwen3-32B), and reasoning models (DeepSeek, `o3-mini`, `o4-mini`) on Sharpe Ratio +
Maximum Drawdown trade-off — Sharpe 1.88 with 8.08% return on NVDA, Sharpe 1.80 vs GPT-4.1's 1.24 on AAPL, drawdowns
~3.7% vs the reasoning models' ~7.9%. **For Linus, the contribution that matters is the output shape — not the trading
numbers.** Trading-R1 is the canonical non-biology instance of the typed-structured-prediction-wrapping-rationale
convention (CLAUDE.md §Typed structured prediction for biology skills), and the canonical demonstration that a mature
multi-agent skill can be **distilled into a single RL-trained model** once its rubric stabilizes — a Phase 6+ pattern
Linus should plan for, not a Phase 7 default.

## The problem (in plain language)

Two interlocking gaps motivate the paper. First, **reasoning LLMs trained for math/coding/science transfer poorly to
trading** because financial decisions are inherently uncertain, path-dependent, multi-factor, and noisy in a way that
verifiable-reward training pipelines (RLHF on math; RL with unit tests on code) cannot easily replicate. The empirical
manifestation is striking: in the paper's own Tables 3 – 4, off-the-shelf reasoning models (DeepSeek, `o3-mini`,
`o4-mini`) systematically **underperform** plain instruction-following large LMs (GPT-4.1, LLaMA-3.3, Qwen3-32B) on
trading metrics. The authors' read: reasoning RLMs were optimized for narrow verifiable domains and have learned
reasoning patterns that drift away from market-relevant evidence when applied zero-shot to noisy multi-source financial
inputs. The "longer reasoning is better" prior fails here.

Second, **financial QA benchmarks are open-ended and don't match the structure trading actually demands.** Existing
financial-LLM benchmarks (FinMA, FinGPT-style sentiment classification) optimize models for retrieval-and-summarization,
not for the disciplined "form a thesis → cite evidence → make a typed decision under uncertainty" workflow that human
analysts execute. So even when finance-specific fine-tunes exist, they don't structure outputs the way a trading desk
needs them. The authors frame the requirement as two coupled priors: an **external prior** (the input data must be
broad, deep, and reliably curated — what enters the context window) and an **internal prior** (the reasoning scaffolding
inside the model's generation must impose investment-thesis structure: thesis → claims → evidence → decision, in that
order, with format pressure throughout).

The synthesis the paper attempts: build a typed-output LLM that emits investment theses with rating-action tags, trained
from a high-quality dataset, where SFT supplies the structural priors and RL supplies the market-grounding — the
**structured rubric is what makes RL tractable for an otherwise unverifiable domain**.

## What they propose

**The Tauric-TR1-DB corpus (Section 3.2).** A ~100k-sample financial reasoning corpus collected with three explicit
objectives: breadth across companies (14 mega-cap and large-cap tickers — NVDA, AAPL, MSFT, AMZN, META, TSLA, BRK, JPM,
LLY, JNJ, XOM, CVX, plus SPY and QQQ ETFs — collectively > $11T market cap), depth of information per asset-day
(technical price/EMA/return data; fundamentals from Finnhub + SimFin; Google News headlines; `stockstats`-derived
indicators; insider sentiment; macro features), and robustness to variation (random-subset sampling across modalities
during label generation, so models train to reason under partial-observation conditions). Five heterogeneous data
sources, 18 months coverage (Jan 2024 – May 2025), structured per asset per day. The corpus design is itself a
contribution — finance LLM training has historically been data-starved relative to math/code, and Tauric-TR1-DB is
released alongside the paper (URL in the abstract).

**Reverse reasoning distillation (Section 3.4).** The crux of the SFT data pipeline. The problem they're solving:
proprietary reasoning models (OpenAI's `o3`, `o3-mini`, `o4-mini`) produce excellent final answers but **do not expose
their chain-of-thought** through API. You can't naively distill what you can't read. Their solution is a two-stage
synthesis pipeline (Figure 2 of the paper):

1. **Investment thesis distillation.** For each ticker-date, sample a random subset of categorical features from
   Tauric-DB and pass them to `o3-mini` or `o4-mini`, which returns a final trading recommendation (the "front-end
   response" — typically a `Strong Buy`/`Buy`/`Hold`/`Sell`/`Strong Sell` plus a brief justification). Reject-sample on
   whether the recommendation matches the volatility-driven label generated in Section 3.5.
2. **Reverse reasoning distillation.** Feed the (input data, recommendation) pair into a planner LLM (GPT-4.1) to
   **decompose** the recommendation into reasoning perspectives — Factor 1: competitor analysis; Factor 2: technical
   analysis; Factor 3: insider transactions; etc. Then a lightweight LLM (GPT-4.1-nano) generates per-perspective
   reasoning text and the segments are programmatically stitched into a coherent CoT. The final artifact is a
   high-quality (input → step-by-step thesis → recommendation) tuple suitable for SFT.

The pattern matters beyond trading: it is a method for **synthetic-CoT-bootstrapping when the teacher model is API-
opaque**. This is the dual of the Kimi K2 agentic-data pipeline (which uses simulated tool environments + LLM judges to
manufacture trajectories the model can imitate); reverse-CoT distillation is the technique to manufacture the
_reasoning_ portion when the teacher's reasoning is hidden behind an API. Useful regardless of whether you ever train a
trading model.

**Volatility-aware label generation (Section 3.5).** Rather than predicting raw returns (which language models handle
poorly because the targets are noisy and unstable), the authors discretize the action space into five tiers
(`Strong Sell`/`Sell`/`Hold`/`Buy`/`Strong Buy`) and generate labels from a **multi-horizon, volatility-normalized
composite signal**: compute exponential-moving-average prices, calculate forward returns over 3-, 7-, and 15-day
horizons, normalize each horizon by its rolling 20-period volatility (Sharpe-like), combine the three with empirically
determined weights (0.3 / 0.5 / 0.2), and assign tier labels by **asymmetric percentile thresholds** (85% / 53% / 15% /
3%) so the per-class distribution is `Strong Buy` 15% / `Buy` 32% / `Hold` 38% / `Sell` 12% / `Strong Sell` 3%. The
asymmetric distribution is justified by the bullish bias of the 2024 – 2025 large-cap-tech-heavy training universe and
by the fact that real markets have long-term upward drift on blue-chip baskets. Labels are asset-specific.

**Three-stage SFT × RFT curriculum (Section 3.3, Table 1, Figures 1, 3, 4).** The pedagogical core. Each stage is run as
**SFT warm-start → RFT with stage-specific reward → augmentation by self-distill-and-reject-sampling**, then the model
is passed to the next stage. Table 1 summarizes the stages:

- **Stage I — STRUCTURE.** SFT on Qwen + OpenAI-derived data (no reject sampling) teaches "what an investment thesis
  looks like": XML-tagged sections (`<MarketAnalysis>`, `<Competition>`, `<Strategy>`, etc.), introduction/claims/
  table/conclusion organization. RFT layers a structured-format reward; augmentation reinforces clean structure
  examples.
- **Stage II — CLAIMS.** SFT focuses on evidence-based reasoning. RFT rewards opinion + quote + source triples
  (`<Insight>` / `<Evidence>` / `<Source>` shape) — the mechanism that grounds claims in input context and addresses
  hallucination. Augmentation reject-samples on professional-and-faithful claim formation.
- **Stage III — DECISION.** SFT teaches investment recommendation patterns. RFT applies the **equity- and volatility-
  adjusted reward** with smoothing — penalizing wrong directional predictions in proportion to the volatility-adjusted
  return magnitude. Augmentation reinforces correct directional predictions.

Across stages, the asymmetric reward matrix is `R = α(Structure) + β(Claims) + γ(Decision)` per Figure 4; structural
correctness, evidence faithfulness, and directional accuracy each contribute additively. The "easy-to-hard" progression
— structure first, then evidence, then decision — is the curriculum lesson: don't try to land a market- correct decision
on a model that hasn't learned to organize a thesis. After all three stages, a **mixture SFT** pass re-balances the
model and produces final Trading-R1.

**Policy optimization with GRPO (Section 3.7).** Group Relative Policy Optimization replaces PPO. For each input `q`,
sample G candidate outputs from the old policy, compute a per-candidate reward, normalize within the group to derive the
advantage `Â_i = (r_i − mean(r)) / std(r)`, and apply a clipped policy ratio with a KL penalty against the SFT
reference. The authors choose GRPO because it (a) eliminates the value model (no critic), saving memory; (b)
group-normalizes rewards which stabilizes training when absolute reward magnitudes are noisy; (c) integrates naturally
with the structure + evidence + decision compositional reward.

**Backbone and footprint (Section 3.6, "Backbone Model and Stability").** Trading-R1 is **Qwen3-4B fine-tuned with
LoRA** — chosen because Qwen3-4B is already optimized for reasoning, accelerating convergence. SFT runs on a single
8×H100 (96 GB), RFT on a single 8×H200 (141 GB). The 4B-parameter form factor is presented as a deliberate strength:
"runs on standard commercial GPUs without massive servers; deployment more affordable, inference faster, locally and
privately runnable." The locally-deployable framing is exactly what Linus needs from Phase 6 onwards — a 4B parameter
model with LoRA fine-tuning fits inside a 32 GB M1 Max comfortably even at FP16.

## Key results

The backtest covers June 1 – August 31, 2024 (held out from training data) on six equities + ETFs: AAPL, GOOGL, AMZN,
NVDA, META, MSFT, SPY. Metrics: Cumulative Return (CR%), Sharpe Ratio (SR), Hit Rate (HR%), Maximum Drawdown (MDD%).
Tables 3 – 4 of the paper present the full grid; the Sharpe-ratio heatmap (Figure 5) gives the visual summary.

- **Trading-R1 vs. baselines, headline numbers.** NVDA: CR 8.08%, SR 1.88, HR 63.6%, MDD 3.80% — best Sharpe of any
  model evaluated. AAPL: CR 5.82%, SR 1.80, HR 63.6%, MDD 3.68% — beats GPT-4.1 (SR 1.24) and far ahead of reasoning
  models. AMZN: CR 5.39%, SR 1.72, HR 63.0% — second-best Sharpe and best drawdown. SPY: CR 3.34%, SR 1.60, HR 64.0% —
  outperforms every baseline despite the broad-market index being the hardest signal-extraction target.
- **Reasoning-LM underperformance is the surprising finding.** DeepSeek, `o3-mini`, and `o4-mini` all post **negative
  Sharpes** on most assets in the backtest (DeepSeek SR -0.66 on NVDA; `o3-mini` SR -1.13 on AAPL). Plain-LLM baselines
  (GPT-4.1, LLaMA-3.3, Qwen3-32B) are mixed but generally positive. The authors interpret: reasoning RLMs optimized for
  math/code have learned a reasoning pattern that drifts away from market-relevant evidence when applied zero-shot to
  noisy financial inputs.
- **The performance hierarchy.** SLM < RLM < LLM < Trading-SFT ≈ Trading-RFT < Trading-R1. Both the SFT-only and
  RFT-only ablations underperform full Trading-R1, demonstrating that the curriculum — SFT establishing structural
  priors followed by RFT aligning to market signals — is more than the sum of its parts.
- **Drawdowns.** Trading-R1's MDD ~3.7% on AAPL; reasoning models' MDD ~7.9% on the same asset. The volatility-aware
  reward shaping during Stage III appears to translate into measurable downside protection, not just better Sharpe.
- **Behaviour observations (Section 5, "Observations").** Trading-R1 outputs are stable, structured, and professional;
  baseline outputs frequently collapse into poor profitability _or_ erratic, unreadable theses. The authors frame this
  as a deliberate trade-off — "we deliberately prioritize enhanced readability, interpretability, and structured
  argumentation over marginal performance gains" — citing DeepSeek-R1's language-mixing issues during pure RL as
  cautionary precedent.
- **Limitations as honestly disclosed by the authors.** Hallucinations persist in 32K-token noisy contexts;
  hybrid-reward training is unstable; thesis-to-decision gap (good theses can still be wrong); blue-chip / bullish-2024
  universe is structurally biased; not for high-stakes deployment.

## What's reusable in Linus

**Phase 6 — single-model RL distillation of mature multi-agent skills.** _Seed: future ADR_. Trading-R1 is the canonical
demonstration that a mature multi-agent system can be **collapsed into a single RL-trained model** once its rubric
stabilizes. The path is reproducible: (a) operate the multi-agent shape long enough to identify which roles' outputs
reliably correlate with task success; (b) freeze the rubric — what counts as a structured thesis, what counts as
evidence-grounded, what counts as a market-aligned decision; (c) generate SFT data via reverse-CoT distillation from a
strong proprietary teacher; (d) run a three-stage RL curriculum where each stage rewards a different rubric component.
For Linus this is **explicitly Phase 6+, not Phase 7**. Phase 7 skills should default to the multi-agent shape
(TradingAgents, Sketch2Simulation, BioGuider — see [`agentic-systems-synthesis.md`](../syntheses/
agentic-systems-synthesis.md)) because the rubric isn't yet mature; once a Phase 7 skill has been operating long enough
that the rubric stops drifting, it becomes a Phase 6 distillation candidate. The single-model variant is operationally
cheaper (one model loaded, one inference call instead of seven), interpretable in the same way the multi-agent variant
is (the typed output exposes the intermediate reasoning for audit), and substantially faster at inference.

**Phase 7 — typed structured prediction as the canonical output shape.** _Seeds: CLAUDE.md §Typed structured prediction
for biology skills (S25)_. Trading-R1's output is the **non-biology canonical instance** of the BioReason-Pro
typed-structured-prediction shape: a structured machine-queryable head
(`{rating: "Strong Buy", ticker: "NVDA", confidence: 0.87, evidence_sources: ["technical", "fundamentals", "news"]}`)
wrapping a free-text rationale
(`"<MarketAnalysis>...</MarketAnalysis> <Competition>...</Competition> <Insight>...<Evidence>...<Source>...</Source> </Evidence></Insight>"`).
The XML-tagged section structure plus rating-action head plus evidence-source audit trail should be the **template** for
any Phase 7 Linus skill that produces a predictive output across a noisy multi-source input. The generalization from
biology (BioReason-Pro: protein function prediction) to trading (Trading-R1: investment recommendation) to any domain
skill (the Linus eventual interface) is the convention's central claim; Trading-R1 is the second exemplar that locks the
convention in place. Future skill-spec authoring should cite both papers as the convention's foundation rather than just
BioReason-Pro alone.

**Phase 6 — reverse chain-of-thought distillation as a synthetic-data technique.** _Seed: future ADR_. Independently of
the trading-skill story, the reverse-CoT distillation pipeline is a reusable methodology import. The technique addresses
a problem Linus will hit in Phase 6: when the teacher model has the best final answers but its reasoning is hidden
behind an API (e.g., distilling from Claude or `o3` for any Worker-training pipeline), the straightforward distillation
path is blocked. Reverse-CoT distillation — let the teacher produce the answer, then use a planner LLM to _reconstruct_
a plausible CoT from (input, answer), then use a lightweight LLM to elaborate each reasoning step, then stitch —
synthesizes the missing reasoning trace. Two parts of this matter beyond Trading-R1: (a) the planner-LLM decomposition
step is itself a typed multi-step structured prediction (decompose into N reasoning perspectives), so the technique is
recursive — typed prediction methods produce the data for typed prediction training; (b) the rejection sampling against
an external label (the volatility-adjusted target in Trading-R1's case) filters bad CoT traces before they pollute SFT
data. Linus's Phase 6 fine-tuning lane should treat reverse-CoT distillation as a default option for any skill where the
best teacher is API-opaque.

**Phase 6 — three-stage easy-to-hard RL curriculum as a fine-tuning pattern.** Trading-R1's STRUCTURE → CLAIMS →
DECISION curriculum is a lesson about how to compose a compositional reward. Each stage's SFT establishes priors that
the next stage's RFT can refine without overwriting; jumping straight from a base model to outcome-based RL was
explicitly tried and reported as unstable. This generalizes: any Phase 6 skill where the desired output has multiple
quality dimensions (structure + faithfulness + correctness, in order of how easy they are to verify) should consider the
same staged-warm-start architecture. The rule of thumb: order stages from cheapest-to-evaluate to hardest-to- evaluate,
SFT-warm-start each, then RFT with the stage-specific reward, then augment by reject-sampled self-distill, then proceed
to the next stage.

**Phase 6d — small-model viability anchor for local LoRA fine-tuning.** Trading-R1 is **Qwen3-4B with LoRA** on a single
8×H100 — a configuration that maps closely to what Linus's Phase 6d weight-streaming target spec
([`docs/specs/phase6d-streaming-target.md`](../specs/phase6d-streaming-target.md)) and Phase 6 LoRA lane (DEC-0014) have
planned for. The result anchors a useful claim: **a 4B-parameter base + LoRA + careful curriculum + good data can match
or beat 32B+ instruction-tuned LMs and proprietary reasoning models** on a structured domain task. For Linus this is
direct evidence that the Phase 6 LoRA path on Qwen3 (currently the named worker base in CLAUDE.md) is not asymptotically
capped at "good enough for bulk implementation"; with the right pipeline it can be the centerpiece of a domain skill.
The corollary: Linus's Phase 6 plan should keep Qwen3-4B / Qwen3-7B alongside larger candidates as serious
skill-tier-base options, not just bulk-Worker bases.

**Phase 2 — locally-deployable Anthropic-or-OpenAI-compatible serving as a confirming signal for DEC-0005.** The authors
close the paper emphasizing that Trading-R1's value proposition includes "ensures the system can run locally and
privately" with "data privacy and analytical consistency across all deployment scenarios" — exactly the Linus north
star. Trading-R1's release strategy (open weights, GitHub-published Trading-R1 Terminal) reinforces the emerging norm
that domain LLMs ship as locally-runnable artifacts. Linus's DEC-0005 OpenAI-compatible-protocol commitment positions
the orchestration layer to consume any such release directly.

## What's NOT applicable / hype filter

The trading numbers themselves are **not the contribution that should drive Linus design**. The backtest universe is six
tickers + SPY, the period is three months (June – August 2024) inside a structurally bullish 2024 large-cap-tech cycle,
and the holding period is one week. The training universe (14 mega-cap blue chips + ETFs) collectively > $11T market cap
is, by the authors' own acknowledgement, "biased toward blue-chip and large-cap companies, especially in AI-related
sectors during the bullish 2024 – 2025 cycle." A bullish universe with a positive Sharpe is a much weaker claim than a
bull-and-bear-cycle backtest with a positive Sharpe. The authors are explicit that Trading-R1 "is best used as a
research and thesis-generation tool, not as a substitute for independent due diligence." For Linus, the trading numbers
are vehicle, not signal — useful as concrete demonstration that the typed-output + curriculum + reverse-CoT recipe
produces a system whose outputs are _good enough that experts find them useful_, not as evidence that LLM-based trading
is solved.

The 32K-token long-context hallucination problem the authors flag in Limitations is a known SOTA boundary — it is not
specific to Trading-R1. Larger context windows (128K+ in Qwen3-Max, Claude, Gemini) help; the 4B-parameter form factor
exacerbates the problem. For Linus's Phase 6 LoRA plan on small-to-mid bases, this is a useful empirical caution: don't
expect a 4B parameter model to reason coherently across a 32K-token noisy multi-source input even after disciplined
fine-tuning. Either constrain the input (RAG-style summarization first), use a larger base, or accept that long-context
faithfulness is a bound that fine-tuning alone won't lift.

The reward design is "hybrid" (structure + evidence + decision rewards combined additively) and the authors flag
training instability — "our hybrid reward function can introduce training instability, while excessive reinforcement
learning may erode the structured reasoning format established during supervised fine-tuning." This is an honest
admission that compositional rewards across SFT-vs-RFT stages are an ongoing research problem, not a settled recipe. For
Linus, the lesson is to expect Phase 6 fine-tuning runs to require multiple instability-recovery iterations, not to
expect the published curriculum to "just work" on a different domain; budget accordingly.

The **decision-to-thesis gap** (a thesis can be well-structured and well-grounded yet still produce a wrong trade,
because the underlying market dynamics genuinely defeat the analysis) is a domain-specific limitation that has no direct
Linus analogue in scientific reasoning — scientific results are also probabilistic but the tooling-feedback loop is
shorter and the reality-grounding is constrained by physics. This is the same point the TradingAgents paper-note
([`2412.20138v7.md`](2412.20138v7.md)) makes in its connections section. Don't over-generalize trading- specific failure
modes into a universal claim about LLM reasoning.

The training-infrastructure half of the paper (single-server LoRA on 8×H100/H200) is a positive signal for Linus because
it scales _down_, not up — but it doesn't transfer: the H100/H200 SXM with NVLink is not the M1 Max with unified memory.
The recipe ports because LoRA-on-Qwen3-4B is well-supported by MLX and HuggingFace's PEFT; the runtime cost will need
re-measurement on the local hardware.

## Open questions for Dan

1. **Reverse-CoT distillation viability with Claude as teacher.** Claude (current and future) is among the strongest
   API-only reasoning models Linus would distill from in Phase 6. Claude does not expose its CoT through API. Does
   Trading-R1's reverse-CoT pipeline (planner-LLM decomposition + lightweight-LLM elaboration + rejection sampling on
   external labels) preserve quality when the teacher is Claude rather than `o3-mini`/`o4-mini`/GPT-4.1, and when the
   target task is a Linus skill (kernel-codegen, biology-prediction, scientific-literature-synthesis) rather than
   trading? A small-scope spike — distill 1k samples on a controlled task using Claude-as-teacher and a public
   ground-truth label — would test the assumption before any larger Phase 6 commitment.

2. **Three-stage curriculum generalization beyond trading.** Trading-R1's stages (Structure → Claims → Decision)
   correspond cleanly to investment-thesis structure. For other Linus skill domains, what's the right stage
   decomposition? In kernel codegen the analogous stages might be `(format → correctness → performance)`; in biology
   `(taxonomy → claims-with-citations → predictions)`. Is the staged-easy-to-hard pattern truly general, or is it
   domain-shaped enough that each Phase 6 skill needs its own curriculum design exercise? A meta-spec for staged-
   curriculum design would help future Worker spec authors.

3. **GRPO vs DPO vs PPO for Linus Phase 6 RL.** Trading-R1 uses GRPO. The paper's argument for GRPO is value-model
   elimination + group-normalized rewards. Other Phase 6 candidates (BioReason-Pro uses GSPO; Kimi K2 uses joint
   verifiable + critic rewards) make different choices. Is there a Phase 6 ADR worth opening on RL-algorithm choice for
   Linus's first fine-tuning runs, or is this empirical-per-skill and the convention is "try GRPO first, fall back to
   DPO if unstable"?

4. **Typed structured prediction convention — explicit XML vs JSON vs other tagging.** Trading-R1 uses XML-tagged
   sections (`<MarketAnalysis>...</MarketAnalysis>`, `<Insight><Evidence><Source>...</Source></Evidence></Insight>`).
   BioReason-Pro uses JSON-shaped predictions with separate rationale text. Both work; the choice is presumably
   downstream-tooling-driven. For Linus's typed-output convention, should the canonical form be standardized (e.g.,
   "always JSON-with-rationale, never XML-tagged sections") or should the choice remain skill-specific? The decision
   shapes how downstream Linus code consumes typed-prediction outputs.

5. **Volatility-aware label generation as a methodology import for non-trading skills.** Trading-R1's labels are
   discretized via percentile-thresholded multi-horizon volatility-normalized signals — i.e., the **labels themselves
   are derived from a structured multi-feature pipeline**, not provided externally. For non-trading skills, are there
   analogous label-generation pipelines worth carrying as a convention? Examples: kernel-codegen labels derived from
   compile-and-benchmark composite scores; biology-prediction labels derived from multi-method confidence-weighted
   consensus. This is the same flash-moe pattern (DEC-0027) — measurement over intuition — applied to label generation
   rather than to caching.

6. **When does a Linus Phase 7 multi-agent skill become a Phase 6 distillation candidate?** The TradingAgents →
   Trading-R1 progression took the same lab on the same data ~6 months and was triggered when the seven-role
   architecture's rubric (what counts as a good thesis, what counts as a market-aligned decision) had stabilized. For
   Linus skills, what's the operational signal that a Phase 7 multi-agent skill is rubric-stable enough to distill?
   Tentative criteria worth discussing: (a) the typed-output schema has not changed across N consecutive weeks of
   operation; (b) the per-role contribution to final-output quality is empirically attributable (i.e., the bull and bear
   debaters' contributions can be teased apart); (c) the multi-agent inference cost is becoming a bottleneck. If two of
   three conditions hold, the skill is a candidate.

7. **Tauric-TR1-DB as a corpus-design template.** The corpus is 100k samples × 14 tickers × 18 months × 5 data sources,
   designed for breadth + depth + robustness-to-variation. For a Linus Phase 6 fine-tuning corpus on a biology or
   scientific skill, what's the analogous design template? The instinct from Dan's domain is that biology corpora are
   more variable in source quality and harder to scale to 100k high-quality examples without synthetic augmentation.
   Should Phase 6 biology fine-tuning explicitly budget for corpus design as a substantial sub-project rather than
   treating it as a data-collection step?
