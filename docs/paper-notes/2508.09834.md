# Speed Always Wins: A Survey on Efficient Architectures for Large Language Models

## TL;DR

Comprehensive taxonomy of efficient LLM architectures addressing transformer quadratic complexity bottlenecks: linear
sequence modeling, sparse attention, mixture-of-experts, hybrid designs, and diffusion LLMs. Directly applicable to
Linus phase 1 baseline evaluation and phase 6 fine-tuning on 32 GB Apple Silicon.

## The problem (in plain language)

Transformers scale poorly: self-attention is O(N²) in sequence length, making long-context inference expensive. Dense
FFN layers are compute-heavy. Standard attention requires full KV cache, consuming unbounded memory. Yet LLMs must
handle increasing context lengths (RAG, agent reasoning, multimodal inputs) efficiently.

## What they propose

Nine categories of optimization strategies, each with 20–100 methods surveyed:

1. **Linear Sequence Modeling** (Linear Attention, Linear RNN, State Space Models): O(N) via recurrent formulations
2. **Sparse Sequence Modeling** (Static/Dynamic/Training-free sparse attention): selective KV interactions
3. **Efficient Full Attention** (IO-aware, grouped, quantized): reduce memory access overhead without changing O(N²)
4. **Sparse Mixture-of-Experts**: conditional activation of expert subsets
5. **Hybrid Architectures**: mix linear + full attention layers strategically
6. **Diffusion LLMs**: non-autoregressive generation for speed
7. **Vision/Audio/Multimodality applications**: extend principles to other modalities

## Key results

- Taxonomy with ~300+ methods catalogued and referenced
- Compression trade-offs quantified: latency vs. throughput vs. accuracy
- Long-context patterns identified: RAG, agentic, reasoning, multimodal all favor sparse or linear attention
- Hybrid designs (50% linear, 50% full attention) achieve best practical throughput on real hardware

## What's reusable in Linus

**Phase 1 (Baselines) and Phase 6 (Fine-tuning) immediate relevance**:

- **Baseline selection**: Section 2–4 methods (linear/sparse attention) are candidates for MLX-based Worker models
  (Qwen2.5-7B variants, Mistral-7B). Linear RNNs and state-space models are Metal-friendly (no full KV materialization).
- **Long-context genomics**: Dan's metagenomics pipelines and biology work often require assembling long sequences.
  Sparse attention (e.g., sparse Transformer, Longformer) or linear RNNs could enable longer context for scientific
  queries without exceeding 32 GB.
- **LoRA-compatible methods**: Many sparse/linear variants are compatible with LoRA fine-tuning (sections 3.4, 4.4).
  Prioritize methods that don't require custom kernels (stick to MLX-native ops).
- **ANE utilization**: Quantized attention (4.4) and grouped attention (4.2) have simpler compute graphs, potentially
  amenable to ANE via pmetal.

## What's NOT applicable / hype filter

- Most benchmarks assume CUDA/A100; latency numbers don't transfer to Metal
- Methods requiring custom kernels (FlashAttention, streaming kernels) need MLX or pmetal equivalents
- Batch-size scaling experiments don't apply (Apple Silicon unified memory, no multi-GPU data parallelism)
- VRAM budgets (48 GB+) exceed Linus ceiling; must filter to sub-32 GB models
