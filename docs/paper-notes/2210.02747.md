# Flow Matching for Generative Modeling

## TL;DR

The paper that named flow matching. Lipman, Chen, Ben-Hamu, Nickel, and Le (Meta AI / FAIR + Weizmann) introduce a
simulation-free training objective for Continuous Normalizing Flows (CNFs): regress a neural-network vector field `v_t`
against a target vector field `u_t` that generates a fixed probability path from a simple noise prior `p_0` to the data
distribution `p_1`. The marginal target is intractable, so the central trick is **Conditional Flow Matching (CFM)** —
condition the path on a single data sample `x_1`, give every sample its own simple Gaussian probability path
`p_t(x | x_1) = N(x | mu_t(x_1), sigma_t(x_1)^2 I)`, and optimize the conditional objective (Theorem 2: identical
gradients in expectation). With this trick, flow matching subsumes the standard diffusion paths (Variance Exploding,
Variance Preserving) as special cases of `(mu_t, sigma_t)` and opens the door to non-diffusion paths — most importantly
the **Optimal Transport (OT) displacement interpolant** with `mu_t(x_1) = t x_1`,
`sigma_t(x_1) = 1 - (1 - sigma_min) t`, which produces straight-line trajectories at constant speed. On ImageNet, FM
with OT paths achieves SOTA likelihood and sample quality, trains ~70 percent faster than diffusion baselines on
ImageNet-64, and samples in roughly 60 percent of the function evaluations needed by score-matching baselines for the
same numerical error.

For Linus, this is a **REFERENCE-category** paper. Linus will not train a flow-matching model. The note exists to give
Dan a durable cite-target when a downstream paper says "we train a flow-matching model on..." — so he can look up what
"flow matching" actually means without redoing the derivation from blog posts or jumping straight into a graduate
textbook. The synthesis-level mathematical treatment lives in the
[Holderrieth & Erives Flow Matching textbook note](2506.02070v3.md); this note is the primary-source companion.

## The problem (in plain language)

Continuous Normalizing Flows (CNFs, Chen et al. 2018) describe generation as solving an ODE: start from a noise sample
`x_0 ~ p_0` (a standard Gaussian), integrate a learned time-dependent vector field `v_t(x; theta)` from `t = 0` to
`t = 1`, and the result is a sample from the data distribution. CNFs are attractive in principle because they are
deterministic, invertible, and can model arbitrary probability paths — including the paths underlying diffusion models
as a special case. But before this paper, the only way known to train them at scale was either (a) maximum-likelihood
training, which requires expensive ODE simulations during the forward and backward pass and does not scale to images, or
(b) diffusion-style score matching (Ho 2020, Song 2020) — which is simulation-free and trains stably, but locks the
researcher into the specific class of probability paths obtainable from Gaussian forward processes.

The cost of being locked into diffusion paths is two-fold. First, diffusion forward processes only approach the noise
distribution as `t -> infinity`; in practice training and sampling are truncated, and the path is curved (the diffusion
trajectory in 2D for a single endpoint pair takes a noticeably bent route, see the paper's Figure 3). Curved paths
require more numerical integration steps to sample accurately. Second, the entire derivation goes through a stochastic
forward process — a thicket of SDE machinery, time-reversal arguments, and Fokker-Planck connections — which is correct
but indirect. Want a different probability path? You first have to design a stochastic process that produces it.

Lipman et al. ask: can we just write the probability path down directly, without going through a forward process at all,
and train the CNF vector field to follow it? Their answer is yes, and the recipe is what the field now calls flow
matching.

## What they propose

The construction proceeds in four named pieces.

**Flow Matching (FM) objective (Section 3).** Given a target probability path `p_t(x)` and the unique vector field
`u_t(x)` that generates it (via the continuity equation), the FM loss is
`L_FM(theta) = E_{t, p_t(x)} || v_t(x) - u_t(x) ||^2`. Simple regression: train a neural-network vector field to match
the target. The catch: `u_t` is generally unknown in closed form, so this objective is intractable as written.

**Conditional probability paths and conditional vector fields (Section 3.1).** Construct the marginal path as a mixture
over per-example conditional paths: `p_t(x) = integral p_t(x | x_1) q(x_1) dx_1`, where each `p_t(x | x_1)` is a simple
distribution (typically Gaussian) conditioned on a single data point. The marginal vector field is then a weighted
average of conditional vector fields (their Equation 8). Their Theorem 1 proves the marginal vector field defined this
way actually generates the marginal probability path — a nontrivial result that lets the construction stand without
going through a forward process at all.

**Conditional Flow Matching (CFM) objective (Section 3.2).** This is the operational payoff. Define
`L_CFM(theta) = E_{t, q(x_1), p_t(x | x_1)} || v_t(x) - u_t(x | x_1) ||^2`. Sample a data point `x_1`, sample `t`,
sample `x` from the conditional path at time `t` for that data point, then regress against the conditional vector field
at that point. Each piece is per-sample and computable in closed form. **Theorem 2** is the central result: `L_FM` and
`L_CFM` have the same gradient with respect to `theta` (up to a constant), so optimizing the tractable conditional
objective is equivalent to optimizing the intractable marginal objective. This is the "denoising score matching" of the
flow-matching world — and the analogy is explicit, since denoising score matching (Vincent 2011) makes the same
per-sample-conditional move for score functions.

**Gaussian conditional probability paths and the OT special case (Section 4).** The general Gaussian family is
`p_t(x | x_1) = N(x | mu_t(x_1), sigma_t(x_1)^2 I)`, parametrized by per-sample mean and standard deviation schedules.
**Theorem 3** gives the unique vector field generating this Gaussian path in closed form:
`u_t(x | x_1) = (sigma'_t(x_1) / sigma_t(x_1)) (x - mu_t(x_1)) + mu'_t(x_1)`. With this, the paper shows two examples.
The Variance Exploding and Variance Preserving diffusion paths reproduce the standard score-matching diffusion targets
(Equations 17 and 19) — proving flow matching subsumes diffusion. The Optimal Transport interpolant (Example II) sets
`mu_t(x_1) = t x_1` and `sigma_t(x_1) = 1 - (1 - sigma_min) t`, producing the OT displacement map between `p_0` and the
Gaussian centered at `x_1`. Particles under the OT map travel in **straight lines at constant speed** (Figure 3, right)
— which is the geometric reason this version trains faster, samples faster, and generalizes better.

The operational consequence is that the same training code now supports any Gaussian probability path the researcher
chooses to write down, including non-diffusion paths that diffusion machinery cannot produce. Forward processes become
optional.

## Key results

Reported on CIFAR-10, ImageNet 32x32, ImageNet 64x64, and ImageNet 128x128, using the same U-Net backbone as Dhariwal
and Nichol (2021):

- **Likelihood and FID (Table 1).** Flow Matching with OT paths achieves the best NLL on ImageNet-32 (3.53 BPD vs VDM's
  3.72) and ImageNet-64 (3.31 vs 3.40), and the best FID on ImageNet-32 (5.25 vs Score SDE's 2.92 — Score SDE wins FID
  on ImageNet-32 — but FM-OT wins NLL there) and ImageNet-64 (14.00 vs 16.06 for FM with diffusion paths). On
  ImageNet-128, FM-OT reaches 2.88 BPD and FID 21.52, competitive with much larger likelihood-free models like BigGAN.
- **Faster convergence.** ImageNet-64 trained with FM-OT reaches comparable quality at 157k iterations (batch 2k) versus
  the Dhariwal-Nichol baseline's 540k iterations — **~70 percent fewer image throughput steps**. ImageNet-128 uses 475k
  iterations vs 4.36m for the diffusion baseline — about 35 percent less compute despite a 25 percent larger model.
- **Sampling efficiency (Table 2, Figure 6).** At fixed numerical-error tolerance 1e-5 (dopri5 adaptive solver), the
  average number of function evaluations (NFE) needed to sample is 156 for FM-OT vs 232 for score matching with
  diffusion on CIFAR-10, 165 for FM-OT vs 157 for FM-diffusion vs 157 for SM on ImageNet-32, and 162 for FM-OT vs 436
  for SM on ImageNet-64. Roughly **60 percent of the NFE budget** for the same error.
- **Training stability ablation.** Comparing FM-with-diffusion-path vs score matching on the same diffusion path and the
  same architecture, FM trains noticeably more stably and produces better samples (Section 6.2; Figure 4 left). Even
  when the path is held fixed, the FM training objective is preferable.
- **Sample-path qualitative behavior (Figure 5).** On ImageNet-64 with identical noise seeds, OT paths begin generating
  recognizable image structure substantially earlier in the trajectory than diffusion paths do — diffusion samples
  remain mostly noise until the final few steps, while OT samples linearly interpolate noise out and structure in.

The empirical claim that survives the reproducibility filter is the **OT displacement path**, not flow matching as a
training objective in isolation. Flow matching with diffusion paths is roughly comparable to score matching with
diffusion paths in headline numbers; the substantial wins (faster training, faster sampling, better likelihood) come
from being able to use straight-line OT paths that diffusion forward-process derivations cannot produce.

## What's reusable in Linus

This section is intentionally short — the paper is REFERENCE-category, not implementation.

- **REFERENCE — flow-matching notation lookup.** When a downstream Wave-3 generative-biology or generative-chemistry
  paper writes "we train a flow-matching model on..." with no further qualification, this paper is the construction
  being cited: conditional probability paths, marginal vector fields recovered via expectation, the CFM objective,
  Theorem 2 gradient equivalence, and the OT path as the natural choice for `(mu_t, sigma_t)`. Open the PDF (or this
  note) when the paper assumes vocabulary that the surrounding context does not define. No phase mapping; reference
  surface only.
- **REFERENCE — sanity-check translation from "diffusion model" notation.** Section 4.1 (Diffusion conditional VFs) and
  Appendix D (full VE/VP derivations) explicitly recover the standard score-matching targets as special cases of
  Theorem 3. When a paper switches between "score function" and "vector field" framings without explanation, this is the
  section to consult to confirm they are the same object up to a known transformation. Pairs with the textbook's
  Appendix E "Guide to the Diffusion Model Literature" — the textbook's appendix is the broader Rosetta stone, this
  paper is the canonical primary source for the flow-matching half of that translation.
- **REFERENCE — CNF training cost intuition.** The CFM objective is simulation-free in the sense that no ODE solve
  appears in the gradient pass; the cost per training step is essentially that of a neural-network forward pass plus a
  closed-form vector-field target. This is the intuition Dan needs when reading later work that touts "simulation-free
  training" — the term is not vacuous; it specifically means not solving ODEs for gradients. Useful as background when
  evaluating what a downstream paper actually contributes.

No Phase 1-8 integration target. The 32 GB unified-memory constraint and the lack of a Linus image-generation or
structure-generation product mean this work surfaces only as cite-target shorthand. If a Phase-5 image-generation or
Phase-7 structure-prediction tool is ever added to Linus, that work would consume a pretrained flow-matching model
through a serving runtime (MLX, Diffusers, MFlux), not train one from scratch — at which point the relevant references
are sampler engineering, quantization, and runtime ports, not this paper.

## What's NOT applicable / hype filter

**Training a flow-matching model from scratch is out of Linus scope.** The ImageNet-64 experiments use 157k iterations
at batch size 2k on a U-Net backbone — order ~3e8 training samples processed, well past anything an M1 Max with 32 GB
unified memory can do in a reasonable wall-clock budget. The ImageNet-128 numbers are an order of magnitude larger. This
is not a Phase 1-8 deliverable. If a future Linus phase wants generated images for figures or visualizations, the only
practical interface is **inference of pretrained models** (FLUX-dev quantized, SD3 medium, AuraFlow on MLX-community
ports if and when those exist) — not training.

**This is the canonical reference Linus reaches for, NOT a target for Linus implementation.** The framing rule from the
[infra-foundations synthesis](../syntheses/infra-foundations-synthesis.md) holds: keep the PDF locally, open it when a
downstream paper's flow-matching notation forces a lookup, do not invest in implementation. The Holderrieth & Erives
textbook ([2506.02070v3](2506.02070v3.md)) is the intended scholarly entrypoint; this paper-note is the cite-target
shorthand for downstream papers that say "Lipman 2022" or "flow matching" without further reference.

**No agentic, retrieval, KV-caching, or LLM-relevant content.** This is a continuous-time generative-modeling paper.
Nothing in it speaks to Ollama, KV cache, agent orchestration, MCP tools, knowledge graphs, or any of the inference-side
concerns dominating Linus Phase 1-2 work. It is in a parallel technical universe to the autoregressive-LLM thread that
Linus actually inhabits — adjacent and consumed, not produced.

**OT here is per-sample, not full Wasserstein-2.** Worth noting because the paper's framing can mislead a casual reader.
The OT displacement path used here is the OT map between the prior Gaussian `N(0, I)` and a single concentrated Gaussian
`N(x_1, sigma_min^2 I)` per data sample — a closed-form straight-line interpolation. Computing the true Wasserstein-2 OT
map between the prior and the full data distribution is intractable; the paper's contribution is showing that the
per-sample OT trick is enough for the marginal vector field to behave well empirically (Section 4.1, final paragraph).
When a later paper says "we use OT flow matching" they generally mean this per-sample construction, not the full
transport problem. Worth verifying when reading.

**No protein-specific or biology-specific math.** The paper is image-only at the experimental level (CIFAR-10 and
ImageNet), and the construction is for `R^d`. Extensions to manifolds (Chen and Lipman 2023 follow-up,
[Riemannian flow matching](https://arxiv.org/abs/2302.03660)) and to discrete state spaces (later work by Holderrieth,
Lipman, and others) build on top of this construction but require additional machinery the paper does not provide. For
SE(3)-equivariant protein-backbone flow matching specifically, this paper is a prerequisite and not a complete answer.

**Honest depth disclaimer.** This paper-note is a chapter map plus an explicit framing of when reaching for the paper is
worth the cost. It cannot replace working through Sections 3-4 carefully when a Linus-relevant downstream paper forces a
deep read. The right way to use the note is: confirm the construction matches what the downstream paper is citing,
identify which of `(mu_t, sigma_t)` the downstream is using (diffusion VE/VP or OT or something custom), and either
accept that as given and move on, or open the paper for a full read of Section 4.

## Open questions for Dan

1. **Which Wave-3 protein paper first forces a deep read of Section 4?** The textbook note's Open Question 1 asks the
   same thing at the textbook level. For this paper specifically: when a Wave-3 paper cites "flow matching" in passing
   and uses the OT path, this note plus the textbook's Section 3 should be enough; when a Wave-3 paper extends flow
   matching to manifolds or discrete state spaces, the answer requires the Chen and Lipman 2023 Riemannian follow-up and
   adjacent work, not this paper alone. Worth recording per-paper which downstream Wave-3 reads required only this note
   vs required deeper reading — that data would refine the REFERENCE-category framing for future curation passes.
2. **Should the Lipman et al. "Flow Matching Guide and Code" (arXiv 2412.06264) be added to the corpus?** It is the
   applied cookbook companion to this primary-source paper, more likely than this paper to be opened when an
   inference-time question about samplers or schedulers arises. The textbook note already raises this; the answer
   ultimately depends on whether Linus ever wants to run flow-matching inference (Phase 5+ image generation) — which is
   itself contingent on whether MLX-community has usable ports of FLUX, SD3, or AuraFlow on Apple Silicon at 32 GB.
3. **Does the OT-path advantage transfer to manifold flow matching?** The straight-line trajectories that win on
   ImageNet are a Euclidean phenomenon — geodesics on a manifold are not straight lines in any Euclidean sense, and the
   analogous "OT-like" path is the geodesic interpolant. Empirical question: do downstream protein-backbone
   flow-matching papers (RFdiffusion, AlphaFold3 in flow-matching framing, Chroma) report similarly large OT-like wins
   on the SE(3) manifold, or do those gains attenuate? Worth tracking when Wave-3 reading lands; the answer informs
   whether the OT story is a deep principle or a Euclidean accident.
4. **How sensitive is the "OT wins" story to evaluation choice?** The paper reports NLL and FID; both are imperfect
   proxies for sample quality and diversity. The straight-line trajectory observation is geometric and likely real, but
   the production gains (FID-only or NLL-only) sometimes invert across rows of Table 1. When a Wave-3 paper cites
   "Lipman 2022 OT flow matching is better" as headline justification, the right move is to read which metric and which
   resolution the citation is leaning on — the paper's own table is more nuanced than the abstract suggests.
