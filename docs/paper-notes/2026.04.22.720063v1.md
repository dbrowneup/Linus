# GenNA: Conditional generation of nucleotide sequences guided by natural-language annotations

## TL;DR

GenNA is a 3.6B-parameter (with a smaller 0.36B variant) decoder-only Transformer — Qwen3-style architecture, GQA, RoPE,
SwiGLU, RMSNorm — pretrained from scratch on a multimodal corpus of ~416 billion characters spanning 2,221 eukaryotic
species. The corpus interleaves nucleotide sequences with natural-language functional annotations, gene names, species
labels, and XML-style structural tags, tokenized through a shared 6,000-token cross-modal BPE vocabulary. The model is
autoregressive, takes a prompt of the form
`[Molecule Type], [Species Name], [Gene Symbol], [Functional Annotation]<seq>` and generates the corresponding
nucleotide sequence. Without task-specific fine-tuning it shows stratified perplexity responses to mutation classes
(synonymous < missense < nonsense < frameshift), forms phylogeny-related embedding clusters from species labels alone,
and generates tRNAs that pass tRNAscan-SE including spontaneously reproduced wobble-pairing behavior. Code and weights
are publicly released. **For Linus, GenNA is the "natural-language-to-biology" bridge the orchestration layer needs: a
Worker that accepts an English prompt and emits a sequence.**

## The problem (in plain language)

Existing nucleotide foundation models — DNABERT, Nucleotide Transformer, HyenaDNA, GenSLMs, DNAGPT, megaDNA, Evo, Evo 2,
GENErator, AlphaGenome — are good at modeling sequence statistics, but their conditioning signals are awkwardly
biological. You prompt them with a sequence prefix, a fixed structural attribute, or a numeric label. None natively
accepts "design me a histone H1 gene from maize" as text.

That mismatch matters. A scientist's intent ("a tRNA-Ala for _Arabidopsis_", "a CDS encoding a basic, high-MW H1
histone") is naturally expressed in language. Translating it into structured attribute schemas is friction: it limits
what can be requested, and it cuts the model off from the semantic context already present in RefSeq's `product` and
`note` fields. OmniNA, the closest prior work, models text and sequence jointly but emphasizes sequence-to-annotation
rather than annotation-to-sequence.

GenNA closes the gap. If the pretraining corpus carries natural-language annotations alongside sequences in a unified
token stream, an autoregressive decoder learns to use those annotations as a generation prior.

## What they propose

The recipe has three load-bearing pieces.

**Corpus.** All eukaryotic GenBank records from RefSeq, restricted to gene-associated regions (intergenic regions are
noisy; they cite an Evo 2 result against whole-genome mixtures), parsed with Biopython. For each valid record they
extract molecule type, species, gene name, and natural-language descriptions from the `product` and `note` fields.
Hierarchical structures (mRNA, CDS, ncRNA, regulatory elements) are wrapped in custom XML-style tags so structural
boundaries survive into the token stream. Genomic DNA samples get a random 0–100 bp upstream/downstream extension. Final
corpus: 24.7M samples / ~416B characters across 2,221 species.

**Tokenizer.** A BPE tokenizer trained on 10,000 sampled records to a 6,000-token vocabulary. Because nucleotides,
English phrases, and XML tags coexist in the training text, the merges produce a vocabulary where
`"transcription factor"`, `"ATGCG"`, and `<cds>` live in one discrete token space. The 6,000-token size is a compromise
— small enough to keep biological motifs as multi-character merges, large enough to capture functional phrases.

**Model.** Decoder-only Transformer on the Qwen3-4B paradigm: 36 layers, hidden size 2,560, 32 query / 8 KV heads (GQA),
RoPE, SwiGLU, RMSNorm pre-norm. Reinitialized from scratch (the custom vocab forecloses checkpoint transfer). Pretrained
on five NVIDIA A800 80GB GPUs, causal LM objective, AdamW, 5k-step linear warmup to 2e-4 then cosine to 10%, effective
batch 60, 400k steps, ~3 months wall-clock. Context window is 4,096 tokens (~20k raw characters), with sequence packing.
A late-training "dynamic information-masking" trick drops the species or gene identifier with p=0.5 to discourage rote
reliance on those fields.

At inference: prompt is `[Molecule Type], [Species Name], [Gene Symbol], [Functional Annotation]<seq>` — species and
gene optional. Default decoding: temperature 0.7, top-p 0.8, repetition penalty 1.3, no-repeat-5-gram.

## Key results

The evaluations are almost entirely _in silico_ — the authors are explicit that this is computational evidence of
learned probabilistic constraints, not wet-lab validation.

**Sequence-function consistency.** Holding the nucleotide sequence fixed and swapping in a mismatched functional
annotation raises average perplexity substantially. Cross-family swaps over 15 genes from four families produce a
structured ΔPPL pattern that follows family-level relatedness. Layer-18 attention analysis on 100 sampled sequences
shows prompt tokens carry 64.4% of attention weight during CDS generation — the prompt is not decoration, it is a live
conditioning signal.

**Zero-shot mutation effects.** In silico single-nucleotide scanning across 100 protein-coding genes (200–3,000 bp)
yields a clean stratification by mutation class: synonymous CDS substitutions and UTR perturbations produce median ΔPPL
of -0.015 to 0.076, missense 0.291 (~4× synonymous), nonsense 1.732, stop-loss 1.558, frameshift (single-base deletion
in CDS) 5.411. The model has internalized genetic-code degeneracy and reading-frame integrity from cross-species
pretraining alone, with no variant-effect supervision.

**Phylogeny-like representations.** Layer-18 hidden-state t-SNE separates fungi/plants/invertebrates/vertebrates and
forms distinct DNA vs. RNA subspaces. A 10×10 ΔPPL matrix from pairwise species-label substitution shows a
diagonal-consistent structure; Ward's hierarchical clustering of that matrix produces a topology broadly consistent with
reference phylogeny (fishes/birds/mammals separate within vertebrates), with some quirks (yeast clusters near
_Chlamydomonas_).

**Generation.** Unconditional generation (modality tag only, model fills in everything) produces 10,000 samples whose
translated CDS regions BLASTP against UniProt; Sentence-Transformers cosine similarity between the model's
self-generated annotation and the BLASTP hit annotations shows good alignment. Sequence-logo of the generated mRNAs
recovers a Kozak-like translation initiation context. Species-conditioned generation (2,000 RNAs each across human,
zebrafish, fly, _Arabidopsis_, maize, yeast) reproduces species-specific GC-content, GC3, codon-usage, and amino acid
frequency distributions — though the generated distributions are systematically _sharper_ than natural ones, a known
top-p truncation artifact.

**Structured-RNA generation.** 30,500 tRNA sequences across 61 anticodon prompts pass tRNAscan-SE with high Cove scores;
the model spontaneously adopts wobble-pairing strategies and reproduces the longer variable-arm characteristic of
Type-II tRNAs (Leu, Ser). 1,000 sequences each across 5S, 5.8S, 18S, 28S rRNA prompts produce Infernal cmsearch
bit-score distributions close to natural rRNA references. 1,000 sequences each across H1/H2A/H2B/H3/H4 histone prompts
(under four temperature/top-p combinations) translate to proteins whose MW × pI distributions track natural histone
families: H1 stays high-MW and basic, H3/H4 cluster tightly. ProGen2 perplexity on the translated histones supports
plausibility. Higher temperature broadens coverage toward the natural distribution at the cost of more off-target
samples.

**Limitations the authors flag.** No scaling-law sweep. Top-p sharpening reduces tail diversity. The 6,000-token BPE may
wash out single-nucleotide-resolution signals needed for cis-regulatory mutational analyses. No wet-lab validation of
any generated tRNA, rRNA, or histone CDS.

## What's reusable in Linus

**Phase 7 candidate skill, direct.** "Design me a [function] sequence for [species]" is exactly the
natural-language-driven workflow Linus is meant to expose. GenNA is a clean fit: prompt is plain English, model is
open-weights, code on GitHub, a 0.36B variant is explicitly provided "to facilitate efficient experimentation and
development in resource-constrained settings" — roughly the M1-Max acknowledgment we needed. The 0.36B is plausibly
runnable on Dan's hardware via MLX int4. The 3.6B is Mistral-7B-class memory footprint at int4 — within the 32 GB
envelope when not competing with other Workers.

**Phase 2 tool registry, direct.** `genna_generate(prompt, species=None, gene=None, temperature=0.7, top_p=0.8, n=1)` is
a one-line tool definition. Wrap as an OpenAI-compatible function-call tool, expose through the orchestration layer.
Maestro translates Dan's intent into the four-field template, dispatches to the GenNA Worker, captures sequences, routes
to validators (tRNAscan-SE, Infernal, BLASTP — Dan's existing toolchain).

**Phase 3 archetype, direct.** Name it in `docs/syntheses/`: **text-conditioned biology generation**. The pattern is
"LLM-style prompt → domain-specific generative model → validator chain → result." GenNA is one instance; DeepSeMS
(secondary metabolites), Trias (codon design), generative phage genomes (whole-genome) plug into the same orchestration
shape. Worth a synthesis note now so future paper-notes can reference a stable abstraction.

**KnowledgeBase integration, direct.** The prompt template is a structured tuple `(modality, species, gene, function)`
paired with a generated sequence — exactly a knowledge-graph node. Every Linus-mediated generation persists as
`(prompt-tuple, sequence, generation-params, validator-results, provenance)`. Generated-sequence provenance is
high-quality KG content because conditioning is fully reproducible from the prompt.

**Phase 1 smoke-test bench, direct.** 50 prompts × {0.36B, 3.6B} × MLX int4, run tRNAscan-SE / Infernal / BLASTP, record
pass rates and wall-clock. Calibrates the right Linus default variant and whether MLX inference is competitive. Plugs
into `benchmarks/dan_tasks/` alongside the LM task suite.

**End-to-end design loop, indirect.** GenNA + Evo 2 + AlphaGenome is a worth-it tool-registry triple: GenNA generates
from a natural-language spec, Evo 2 scores likelihood under a different generative prior, AlphaGenome predicts
regulatory effect of the variant in genomic context. Three inductive biases triangulating on one sequence — the standard
ensemble-validation shape that protein-design pipelines (RFdiffusion → ESM → AlphaFold) made canonical in the protein
world. No comparable pipeline yet exists for nucleotide design; Linus could be where it gets assembled.

**Maestro/Worker fit, direct.** GenNA is an unusually clean Worker because it accepts text prompts natively. No
prompt-engineering acrobatics to coerce a sequence-only model into accepting English. Maestro's role reduces to (1)
clarifying intent into the four-field template, (2) choosing decoding parameters, (3) chaining to validators, (4)
reporting back.

## What's NOT applicable

**Pretraining is permanently out of reach.** Five A800-80GB GPUs for three months is roughly $50K of compute and 10× the
memory bandwidth of an M1 Max. Linus is consuming GenNA, not training it. This is the same posture as with Evo 2 and
AlphaGenome.

**The corpus construction is heavyweight.** 24.7M GenBank records → 416B characters via Biopython requires substantial
disk and a pipeline that Dan probably doesn't want to run unless he's curating his own domain-specific extension corpus.
The released model and weights are sufficient for inference.

**LoRA fine-tuning is plausible but not a given.** Authors flag PEFT/LoRA as future work for downstream task adaptation.
On 32 GB unified memory, LoRA on the 3.6B model is tractable in principle (per the CLAUDE.md hardware note: LoRA/QLoRA
on 7–14B is tractable). But the cross-modal BPE vocabulary means existing Qwen3 LoRA tooling won't work out of the box —
token embeddings are bespoke. This is a Phase 6 consideration, not a Phase 1 priority.

**Distribution sharpening at default sampling.** Generated sequences are systematically more concentrated than natural
distributions. For Dan's design use cases this is mostly fine (you usually want a _typical_ histone H1, not a
long-tail-weird one), but if the task is "explore the rare-variant space," GenNA defaults aren't right and sampling has
to be aggressively detuned (temperature → 1.0, top-p → 0.95, with the cost of more off-target samples).

**No wet-lab validation in the paper.** Everything is in silico. For Linus, that means GenNA outputs should always be
staged through a validator chain (tRNAscan-SE, Infernal, BLASTP, Evo 2 perplexity, AlphaGenome regulatory prediction)
before they reach Dan as "candidate sequences worth thinking about." Naked GenNA output is hypothesis, not result.

**Single-nucleotide resolution is compromised.** The 6,000-token BPE means GenNA isn't the right tool for fine-grained
mutational analysis of cis-regulatory elements at single-bp precision. Use AlphaGenome or Nucleotide Transformer for
that; use GenNA for coarser-scale generation tasks.

## Connections

GenNA is the _text-conditional generation_ corner of the Group B nucleotide-modeling constellation. Read the
constellation as a 2×2 matrix of (representation vs. generation) × (sequence-only vs. text-conditioned), and GenNA
occupies the text-conditioned-generation cell that was previously empty.

- [mCSM-metal](1-s2.0-S0022283626000513-main.md) — mutation-effect prediction on metal-binding proteins; discriminative.
  Complementary: GenNA proposes variants, mCSM-metal-style scorers rank them.
- DISCO (later) — protein structure-sequence co-design; protein-domain analog at a different abstraction level.
- DeepSeMS (later) — secondary metabolite generation; another text-conditioned generative model. If it fits the same
  orchestration archetype, the Linus tool registry gets cleaner.
- Trias (later) — codon design; downstream of GenNA. After GenNA generates a CDS, Trias could re-codonize it for a
  target expression host.
- Generative phage genomes (later) — whole-genome generation; one scale up.

GenNA explicitly cites and builds on DNABERT, Nucleotide Transformer (v3), DNABERT-2, GENA-LM, AlphaGenome, GenSLMs,
DNAGPT, HyenaDNA, megaDNA, Evo, Evo 2, GENErator, ATGC-Gen, and OmniNA. It's a generative descendant of the
nucleotide-FM lineage with a text-modality channel added via shared BPE — not a fork of any specific Group A model
(reinitialized from scratch) but firmly in the same family. Closest conceptual prior: **OmniNA** (ref. 19), which
jointly models nucleotide and text but emphasizes the sequence→annotation direction. GenNA inverts the primary
direction.

The "natural-language → domain model" pattern GenNA embodies is structurally identical to what Linus does at the
orchestration layer between Maestro and any biology Worker. GenNA internalizes the pattern _inside_ a single model — a
single-model proof-of-concept for the multi-component thing Linus is building.

## Open questions for Dan

1. **Synthesis archetype.** Should "text-conditioned biology generation" be a named architectural archetype in
   `docs/syntheses/`? GenNA is the cleanest current example, but DeepSeMS, Trias, and the generative-phage-genome papers
   will all fit the same shape. A short synthesis note now lets later paper-notes reference a stable abstraction.
2. **Linus default variant.** 0.36B vs. 3.6B on M1 Max: which becomes the default Worker? A Phase-1 smoke test (50
   prompts × both variants × MLX int4, measured by tRNAscan/Infernal/BLASTP pass rates and wall-clock) settles it
   empirically.
3. **GenNA + Evo 2 + AlphaGenome triple.** Is end-to-end "text-driven generation → cross-prior likelihood →
   regulatory-effect prediction" a worth-it Phase 2/3 pipeline to scaffold? All three almost certainly can't run
   simultaneously on M1 Max, but sequential dispatch through the orchestration layer with intermediate KG persistence
   makes it tractable.
4. **Concrete design tasks.** What specific tasks from Dan's research program would actually be used through
   Linus-mediated GenNA — histone variants, custom tRNAs for non-canonical amino acid incorporation, species-specific
   reporter constructs, synthetic UTRs for expression tuning? The use-case list determines whether GenNA stays a Phase 7
   candidate or justifies earlier integration.
5. **KnowledgeBase node schema.** Should generated sequences enter the KB as a distinct `GeneratedSequence` node type
   with edges to the prompt tuple, model variant + version, decoding parameters, and validator results? Consistent
   schema from the start beats retrofitting later.
6. **Cross-modal BPE as a wider lesson.** A single 6,000-token BPE usefully tokenizing nucleotides + English + XML is
   interesting beyond GenNA. Are there other Linus contexts where cross-modal BPE would simplify a Worker interface
   (protein sequence + UniProt descriptions, SMILES + IUPAC names)? Possibly a synthesis thread.
