# PAN: A World Model for General, Interactable, and Long-Horizon World Simulation

## TL;DR

PAN is a _visual_ world model: given a video frame and a natural-language action ("drive through a snowy forest"), it
predicts the next several seconds of video, then accepts another action and continues, supporting closed-loop,
long-horizon, action-conditioned simulation. Architecturally it is a **Generative Latent Prediction (GLP)** stack — a
Qwen2.5-VL-7B vision-language backbone autoregresses over a compact 256-token latent world state, and a 14B Wan2.1 video
diffusion decoder (extended with a Causal Shift-Window denoising scheme) renders each predicted latent into a temporally
coherent video chunk. Trained on large video–action pair corpora, PAN beats open-source video world models (Cosmos,
V-JEPA 2, Wan-2.x) on action fidelity, long-horizon stability, and — most interestingly — on **simulative planning**,
where a VLM agent uses PAN to run "thought experiments" over candidate actions and pick the best, lifting
goal-completion success by ~25% over a VLM-only baseline. **For Linus this is a watch-the-field paper, not an action
item**: video simulation is well outside Linus's scope, but the architectural pattern (slow latent reasoning + fast
generative output) and the conceptual frame ("world model as an internal simulator the agent queries before acting") are
worth tracking, especially as they overlap with Kosmos's structured shared world-state and with Coconut's
continuous-latent reasoning.

## The problem (in plain language)

Modern video generators (Sora, Wan, KLING, Gen-3) make beautiful clips from a single prompt, but they're open-loop: you
ask once, you get a fixed video. You cannot stop halfway, inject a new action ("now turn left"), and have the model
continue coherently. They have no persistent state, no notion of action, and they drift visually over long horizons.
Existing "world models" address pieces of this — game world models (Genie 2), driving world models, 3D scene models —
but each is locked to a narrow domain or short horizon.

The deeper claim PAN is making, well beyond video generation, is that an _agent_ needs a world model the way a person
needs an imagination: to mentally rehearse "what happens if I do X?" before committing. If your agent has a reliable
simulator, you can run a tree search over candidate actions in imagination, pick the branch that gets closest to the
goal, and only then act in the real environment. The bottleneck on this kind of planning has historically been that the
simulators (whether physics engines, game engines, or learned world models) only work in narrow domains. PAN's pitch is
that an LLM-backed video world model can be _general_ enough to serve this role across diverse open-domain tasks.

## What they propose

The architecture is **Generative Latent Prediction (GLP)**, formalized as three components: an encoder $h$ mapping
observation $o_t$ to latent state $\hat{s}_t$; a predictive module $f$ rolling the latent forward conditioned on action
$a_t$; and a decoder $g$ that reconstructs the next observation from the predicted latent. Training minimizes the
discrepancy between $\hat{o}_{t+1} = g \circ f(h(o_t), a_t)$ and the ground-truth $o_{t+1}$ — crucially, the supervision
happens _in observation space_, which the authors argue distinguishes GLP from JEPA-style latent-matching objectives
prone to representational collapse.

The instantiation is what makes the paper interesting. The encoder and predictive backbone are both lifted from
**Qwen2.5-VL-7B-Instruct**: the vision tower becomes $h$, the language model becomes $f$, and the multi-turn dialogue
template becomes the rollout structure (`<user>` turns inject visual state + action, `<assistant>` turns emit 256
learnable query embeddings that _are_ the next latent state). This means the world model inherits the VLM's
text-pretrained world knowledge — names of things, rough physics intuitions, action semantics — for free. The decoder
$g$ is **Wan2.1-T2V-14B**, a diffusion transformer, extended with a "Causal Shift-Window Denoising Process Model"
(Causal Swin-DPM) that uses chunk-wise causal attention to stop the per-chunk generation from drifting at boundaries
during long rollouts.

Training data is the other lever: previous video corpora are short, independent clips. PAN's team curated large-scale
video–action pairs spanning embodied tasks, everyday activities, natural environments, and multi-agent scenes, with
shot-boundary detection, motion-quality filtering, and action-text alignment. The supervision is **flow matching**
rather than DDPM-style score matching, which is consistent with the broader Group-G drift toward flow-matching as the
diffusion training objective of choice.

## Key results

The headline benchmark introduces three evaluation tiers: **Action Simulation Fidelity** (does a single action produce
the right next state?), **Long-Horizon Forecast** (does the rollout stay coherent over many steps?), and **Simulative
Reasoning and Planning** (can a VLM agent use the world model to actually plan?).

On Action Simulation Fidelity, PAN scores 70.3% on agent simulation and 47.0% on environment simulation (overall 58.6%),
surpassing all open-source baselines and most commercial ones. On Long-Horizon Forecast, PAN reaches 53.6% Transition
Smoothness and 64.1% Simulation Consistency, beating commercial KLING and MiniMax. On Step-Wise Simulation, PAN scores
56.1%, the best among open-source world models.

The most interesting result is **Simulative Planning**. A VLM agent (OpenAI o3) proposes candidate actions; the world
model simulates each as a "thought experiment"; the agent picks the action whose predicted state most progresses toward
the goal; the loop repeats. With _PAN_ providing the simulations, the agent's task-success rate rises **+26.7% on
Open-Ended Planning** and **+23.4% on Structured Planning** over the VLM-only baseline. Critically, with _other_ world
models (Cosmos, Wan, V-JEPA 2) plugged in, performance is inconsistent — sometimes simulation _hurts_ the agent, because
unreliable rollouts mislead the planner. The takeaway is that "realistic appearance" of a generated video is not the
same as "causal grounding sufficient for planning," and only the latter helps an agent.

## What's reusable in Linus

**The architectural pattern, conceptually.** GLP separates _slow latent reasoning_ (the LLM autoregressing 256-token
world states) from _fast perceptual generation_ (the diffusion decoder). This is the same family of design as Coconut
([2412.06769v3](2412.06769v3.md)) where reasoning happens in continuous latent space rather than emitted tokens, and as
the broader memory-pillar trend toward latent scratchpads. For Linus, the lesson worth banking is: **the unit of
reasoning need not be the unit of output**. A Linus Worker could reason in a compact latent context and emit prose,
code, or tool calls only at decision points. This is far enough away from current Linus MVP work to be Phase 6+
territory, but it's a pattern worth naming.

**The "world model as orchestration primitive" frame.** Kosmos uses a _symbolic-scientific_ shared world model —
structured state passed between specialized agents (a literature agent, a code agent, an analysis agent) so they reason
against the same evolving picture of the experiment. PAN uses a _visual-physical_ world model — pixel-level rollouts
conditioned on language actions. They are very different things, but the abstract role is similar: **a shared,
queryable, evolving representation that lets an agent (or coalition of agents) test hypotheticals before committing**.
For Linus's Phase 3 (Knowledge & Parallel Agents) and Phase 7 (Skills & Autonomy), the question worth asking is whether
the orchestration layer should expose any explicit "world state" object that Workers read from and write to — distinct
from the session store, distinct from the KnowledgeBase, modeled as the agents' shared belief about the task in flight.
Even a text-only, JSON-serialized version of this would be cheap to prototype.

**The planning-loop pattern.** PAN's "VLM proposes action → world model simulates → VLM scores → pick best" loop is
structurally identical to a tree-search agent harness. Linus does not need PAN to implement this — a text-based
equivalent (Worker-A proposes, Worker-B critiques against KnowledgeBase, Worker-A revises) is in scope for Phase 3 and
would cost little. PAN is useful as a reminder that _forward simulation before action_ is a real lever for agent
quality, not just chain-of-thought.

## What's NOT applicable / hype filter

Almost all of the engineering. PAN trains on tens of thousands of GPU-hours of large video corpora, runs a 7B VLM
backbone _plus_ a 14B diffusion decoder, and targets visual-physical simulation. This is multiple orders of magnitude
beyond M1 Max / 32 GB unified memory. There is no plausible Phase 1–7 deliverable that involves training or even running
PAN locally.

The Causal Swin-DPM, the chunk-wise causal attention, the flow-matching loss for video, the shot-boundary detection
pipeline — all of this is video-specific machinery that doesn't translate to text-modality Worker agents. Likewise, the
benchmarks (WorldScore, WM-ABench, Agibot, Language Table) measure things Linus doesn't care about.

PAN is also a research artifact, not a usable backend: there's no released model checkpoint, no quantized variant, no
MLX port. Even if Linus wanted a video world model (it doesn't, in any current phase), PAN is not something to "pull and
run."

The visual world-model frame also doesn't map onto Linus's actual workloads. Dan's tasks are scientific reasoning, code
generation, literature synthesis, and pipeline execution. None of these benefit from pixel-level rollouts. The "thought
experiment" pattern transfers; the visual substrate does not.

## Connections

The GLP architecture sits in the same conceptual family as **Coconut ([2412.06769v3](2412.06769v3.md))**: both move
reasoning into a continuous latent space and emit observable outputs only when needed. PAN does this at the architecture
level (latent backbone + generative decoder); Coconut does it inside a single LLM by recycling hidden states. Worth a
synthesis note pairing them as "latent-space reasoning" exemplars.

The diffusion decoder rests on **Flow Matching** ([2210.02747 / Lipman et al.](2210.02747v1.md) — track via the existing
Group-G flow-matching note if filed; cited heavily here), which has become the default training objective for
high-quality generation. PAN's adoption of flow matching is one more datapoint that this is the right loss for
diffusion-style generation in 2025+.

The **Transformer ([NIPS-2017-attention-is-all-you-need-Paper.md](NIPS-2017-attention-is-all-you-need-Paper.md))** is
the foundational ancestor — the Qwen2.5-VL backbone is a transformer, the diffusion decoder is a DiT, and the
autoregressive rollout is classical next-token prediction reapplied to latent world states.

The **Google environmental-impact paper ([2510.09244v1](2510.09244v1.md))** provides the sustainability framing
PAN-class models demand. A 7B + 14B inference stack producing video at interactive rates is computationally expensive at
every step, and the "agent runs N parallel rollouts to plan" pattern multiplies that cost. If forward simulation becomes
a Linus paradigm, the sustainability cost needs to be reckoned with even at text scale.

The strongest cross-paper resonance is with **Kosmos** (the autonomous-AI-scientist paper noted in this same wave).
Kosmos's "world model" is a _structured, symbolic, shared representation across agents_ — the experimental state of an
investigation, what hypotheses are open, what evidence is in. PAN's "world model" is a _visual generative simulator_.
Same word, very different objects. The interesting design question for Linus is whether the orchestration layer should
adopt the _role_ of a world model (a shared queryable state agents can simulate forward against) without committing to
either Kosmos's symbolic instantiation or PAN's pixel-level one — perhaps a JSON-shaped task-state object with a "what
would happen if I did X?" query interface backed by a Worker.
