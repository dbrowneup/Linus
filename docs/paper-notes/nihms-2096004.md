# The brain works at more than 10 bits per second

## TL;DR

This three-page perspective in Nature Neuroscience directly contests a high-profile claim by Zheng & Meister (Neuron 2025) that human information throughput is bounded at approximately **10 bits/s**. Sauerbrei and Pruszynski argue that the 10 bits/s figure is a ceiling on *conscious cognitive* output, not on the nervous system as a whole, and that unconscious sensorimotor processing — which occupies the majority of CNS neurons and governs real-time motor control — operates at throughput far exceeding this bound. The core reframing: **the 10 bits/s figure is a lower bound on human neural information processing, not an upper bound**. The paper does not propose new experiments; it is a conceptual rebuttal with illustrative examples, most prominently the biomechanics demands of a skilled athlete crossing unpredictable terrain.

## The problem (in plain language)

Zheng and Meister (the paper being rebutted) measured the rate at which people produce abstract symbolic sequences — typing, memorizing binary digits, reading aloud, solving Rubik's cubes — and found that the maximal information rate across these diverse tasks consistently sits around 10 bits/s. They concluded that this is the "information throughput of a human being" and described the ratio of sensory input (~1 gigabit/s at the retina alone) to behavioral output as the brain's "sifting number" (~10^8), framing it as a profound open question in neurobiology.

Sauerbrei and Pruszynski accept that finding for what it actually measures — the rate of conscious, deliberate, symbolic behavior — but dispute the implied scope. The problem they identify is a category error: measuring throughput at one narrow output channel (explicit behavioral choices) and treating it as the throughput of the entire system. An experienced runner navigating rocky terrain every 250 ms is running dozens of reflex arcs in parallel, activating muscles across trunk, neck, arms, and legs with precision, stabilizing the visual scene via the vestibulo-ocular reflex, and continuously updating motor commands from proprioceptive feedback — none of which is captured in the symbolic output measures Zheng and Meister used.

## What they propose / study

This is a commentary, not an empirical study, so there is no experimental design. The argument proceeds in three steps.

First, the authors identify what Zheng and Meister actually measured: the information content of sequences of symbols produced during tasks requiring conscious deliberation. This, they concede, is likely bounded near 10 bits/s, and the measurement methodology (analyzing the degree of randomness in symbolic output sequences) is sound for that purpose.

Second, they argue that Zheng and Meister's proposed "outer brain / inner brain" partition — in which the outer brain (sensory and motor periphery) operates at gigabit rates and the inner brain (cognition, goal-setting) operates at 10 bits/s — is anatomically incoherent. The vestibulo-ocular reflex, a prototypical outer-brain reflex, runs through the brainstem and cerebellum, both of which are unambiguously part of the central nervous system. Cortical neurons generating voluntary motor commands receive strong and direct inputs from somatosensory receptors — the inner/outer boundary dissolves under scrutiny. The cerebellum alone contains approximately half of all neurons in the brain and is dedicated primarily to unconscious sensorimotor computation.

Third, they make the positive claim: the nervous system does not discard its gigabit sensory input nor passively relay it. It uses that information to compute moment-to-moment motor adjustments that are small, unconscious, and individually unremarkable but collectively indispensable for stable behavior in an unpredictable physical environment. Accounting for this sensorimotor processing dissolves the "paradox" of the enormous sensory-to-behavior information ratio that Zheng and Meister frame as mysterious.

## Key results

Because this is a perspective, there are no new empirical results. The key quantitative claims are inherited from Zheng & Meister and then recontextualized:

- Zheng & Meister found maximal behavioral information rate ~**10 bits/s** across typing, memorization, reading, Rubik's cube, and speech tasks.
- Sensory input to the retina alone exceeds **1 gigabit/s**, implying a sifting ratio of roughly **10^8**.
- A single muscle's phase, amplitude, and duty cycle across a 250 ms stride cannot be specified with the ~3 bits that a 10 bits/s rate would allocate to that window.
- The cerebellum contains ~**half the neurons in the human brain** and is dedicated primarily to unconscious sensorimotor processing — a structural argument against treating 10 bits/s as a whole-brain throughput limit.

The authors' central inferential claim: 10 bits/s is an upper bound on the *conscious cognitive channel* and simultaneously a **lower bound on total neural information processing**, because the motor system demonstrably processes more than 3 bits per 250 ms stride at the single-muscle level.

## What's reusable in Linus

**Indirect — KnowledgeBase domain content and AI architecture analogy (Phase 3, 6, 7):**

The paper's architecture distinction — a narrow conscious channel bottlenecked at ~10 bits/s sitting atop a massively parallel, high-bandwidth unconscious substrate — maps remarkably well onto the Maestro/Worker model in Linus. The "inner brain" cognitive bottleneck is Dan + Claude: low-throughput, deliberate, strategically powerful. The sensorimotor substrate is the Worker layer: high-throughput, parallel, unconscious execution of well-specified tasks. This is not a computational metaphor the paper draws explicitly, but it is a clean one for thinking about where to put cognitive effort in an agentic system — and for justifying why the Worker layer should operate in parallel by default without waiting for Maestro attention.

**For Dan's KnowledgeBase domain content specifically:** as a genomics/computational biology researcher, Dan works with data that has a similar structure — a massive high-dimensional input space (sequencing reads, protein structures, transcriptomes) that must be "sifted" down to a narrow set of actionable biological conclusions. The Zheng-Meister sifting number of 10^8 has a direct analog in genomics: the ratio of sequencing data to biologically interpretable signal is similarly enormous. The conceptual vocabulary here (bandwidth, throughput, bottleneck, sifting) is useful framing for the KnowledgeBase design decisions about when to do dense embedding retrieval versus sparse symbolic query — high-bandwidth substrate search versus narrow deliberate synthesis.

**Brain-computer interface note:** the paper briefly mentions that the 10 bits/s ceiling has implications for BCI design — specifically that the conscious output channel is the bottleneck, not sensorimotor capacity. This is directly relevant if Linus ever develops a voice/gestural interface layer, since it bounds what the user can explicitly express per unit time regardless of how fast the model responds.

## What's NOT applicable

This is a short theoretical commentary with no new methods, code, models, or datasets. There is nothing to implement, replicate, or benchmark. The argument is conceptual and anatomical, not computational. It does not provide a positive theory of how to measure sensorimotor throughput, nor does it propose experimental designs that would resolve the debate — it simply identifies a logical gap in the Zheng-Meister framing. The 10 bits/s figure for the conscious channel is not disputed and remains a useful (if narrow) constraint for thinking about human-facing interface design. The paper also does not engage with whether artificial systems face analogous bottlenecks.
