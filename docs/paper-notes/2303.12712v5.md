# Sparks of Artificial General Intelligence: Early experiments with GPT-4

## TL;DR
A 155-page Microsoft Research field report on a pre-release, text-only GPT-4, arguing that the model exhibits "sparks" of general intelligence — coherent behaviour across language, code, math, vision (via TikZ), medicine, law, and theory of mind — that go well beyond pattern-matched memorization. The paper is mostly observational; its load-bearing contribution for Linus is the existence proof that *enough exposure to human writing internalizes structured cognitive patterns* (state-transition functions, planning sketches, common-sense physics) that can be elicited as latent skills. Section 8, in turn, is the existence proof for the converse: even at this scale the architecture has no inner scratchpad, so working memory and look-ahead planning fail predictably without external memory scaffolding.

## The problem (in plain language)
The standard machine-learning playbook for evaluating a model is benchmark accuracy on held-out test sets. The authors argue that this method is structurally inadequate for a model trained on essentially the open internet — there is no held-out set you can be confident the model has not already seen — and is also unsuited to assessing *generality*, the property they take to be the heart of the AGI question. Their alternative is closer to clinical psychology than to MLPerf: pose novel, often whimsical tasks that require composing skills the training data does not directly teach (write a proof of the infinitude of primes as a rhyming poem; draw a unicorn in TikZ; stack a book, nine eggs, a laptop, a bottle, and a nail; play Plato writing a dialogue against autoregressive language models), and report what happens.

The deeper question Linus inherits from this paper is not "is GPT-4 AGI?" — that framing has aged poorly — but rather: *what cognitive structure does a model acquire purely as a side effect of next-word prediction over a sufficiently large slice of human writing?* The answer the paper documents, across hundreds of examples, is "more than expected, and structured in surprisingly transferable ways."

## What the paper proposes / observes
There is no new architecture, no new training method, no new dataset. The contribution is a *taxonomy of capabilities and limitations*, organized around: multimodal/interdisciplinary composition (Section 2), coding (3), mathematics (4), interaction with the world via tool use and embodied games (5), interaction with humans via theory of mind (6), discriminative tasks (7), and — the inverse — limitations of the autoregressive architecture (8). The authors deliberately use a "psychological probe" methodology: vary a known prompt slightly and see if the response degrades; remove the horn from the unicorn TikZ code, randomize coordinates, and ask GPT-4 to add it back, to verify the model is reasoning about the geometry rather than copying.

The framing that matters most for Linus is the contrast between Sections 1–7 (what the model can do) and Section 8 (what it cannot, for principled architectural reasons). Section 8 explicitly argues that next-word prediction supplies fast pattern completion but no inner dialogue, no scratchpad, no backtracking — the model performs Kahneman's "fast thinking" extraordinarily well and lacks the "slow thinking" overseer that requires working memory and an organized thinking scheme.

## Key results
The TikZ unicorn is the iconic example, and the one Garrison reaches for. Asked to "draw a unicorn in TikZ," a pure-text model produced LaTeX that compiled to a recognizable (if rotund) unicorn — body, legs, horn, eye, mane — while ChatGPT produced a few disconnected polygons. The follow-up is what makes it load-bearing: the authors took the working code, *deleted the horn block*, *applied random coordinate transformations*, *stripped comments*, and asked GPT-4 to add the horn back. It correctly located the head and attached a horn. A pure language model, given no pixels, had internalized a state-transition function from natural-language description to spatial geometry. Across a month of probing, the unicorn drawings *visibly improved* (Figure 1.3) — the same training trajectory was producing better internal world models.

Other concrete results: GPT-4 scores 10/10 on a LeetCode mock on-site interview (beating 100% of human users on that round), passes the Multistate Bar Exam at >70% and USMLE Steps 1–3 at ~80%, correctly stacks the book/eggs/laptop/bottle/nail puzzle in a physically plausible order, and writes a Plato dialogue criticizing autoregressive language models that the authors themselves judge a "slightly higher grade" than ChatGPT's. Section 8 then catalogues the failures: 58% accuracy on `2*8+7*6`-style four-term integer arithmetic (collapsing to 0% as digit counts grow), wrong answer on Tower of Hanoi with two disks on Rod A and one on Rod B, broken constraint satisfaction when the first and last sentence of a poem must be word-reverses of each other. All of these recover dramatically — arithmetic to 100% in a small range, planning tasks to usable accuracy — when the prompt provides external scratchpad space ("let's think step by step, write down all the intermediate steps"). The capability is latent; the architecture cannot evoke it without help.

## What's reusable for Linus — through the memory lens
The paper is most valuable to Linus not as a capability list but as evidence for a thesis: *what gets transferred when a model is exposed to enough human writing is the shape of human cognition, including its state-transition functions, but not the memory substrate that makes those functions useful across long horizons*. This is the same claim Garrison makes in [garrison_memory_makes_computation_universal.md](../../context/notes/garrison_memory_makes_computation_universal.md), and Sparks is the anchor he cites. The unicorn experiment is the crisp version: a text model has internalized a function from "unicorn" to spatial geometry it has never seen rendered. By induction, a model trained on enough scientific writing should internalize functions from "experimental design" to "expected confound," from "kinetic curve shape" to "candidate mechanism," from "Rust borrow checker error" to "lifetime fix." These are the latent skills Linus is trying to elicit from a Worker model in domains where Dan needs help.

The operational consequence for the Linus orchestration layer is direct. Section 8 says, in effect, that any local Worker — Qwen2.5-Coder, Mistral, the eventual fine-tuned Linus — will have the *same* architectural deficit GPT-4 had: latent competence, no native scratchpad, no native cross-turn memory. The fix at frontier scale has been compute-purchased memory (o3 paying ~$1.15M per ARC pass; chain-of-thought generally), but for Linus the fix has to be *architectural*: an explicit short-term scratchpad in the session layer, an explicit episodic store across sessions, explicit history-access primitives that meet the four sub-requirements Garrison lists. Sparks is the empirical reason to take that design pressure seriously now rather than after Phase 3, because the ceiling on small-Worker usefulness will be set by how well Linus externalizes what the model cannot internalize.

The unicorn-as-state-transition framing also lands on the [flash_moe.md](flash_moe.md) and Phase 1 evaluation work: when Dan benchmarks a Worker model, the question is not just "what's the perplexity" but "which human cognitive patterns has this model acquired sharply enough to be a useful tool transfer target." That is a qualitative axis the Sparks methodology makes legible.

## What's NOT applicable / hype filter
Almost all of the experimental setup is Linus-irrelevant. This is a frontier closed-weights model, accessed through Microsoft Research's privileged pre-release, on tasks that require the full GPT-4 parameter count and OpenAI's unpublished training mix. None of the specific results — the LeetCode score, the USMLE score, the bar exam score, the unicorn drawing quality — should be expected to reproduce on any local 7B/8B model Linus will run. Treat the *capability ceilings* as upper bounds achievable by a much larger frontier system, not as deliverables.

The "sparks of AGI" framing itself has aged into mostly-marketing. The paper's most enduring claims are the small ones: text models can internalize spatial structure (unicorn), the autoregressive architecture has no scratchpad (Section 8 arithmetic), planning fails predictably on tasks with global constraints (Section 8 reverse-poem). The grand claim — that GPT-4 is an early AGI — is not load-bearing for any Linus design decision and should be filtered out.

The methodology is qualitative and deliberately not reproducible: the authors note throughout that the exact responses depend on a model version that no longer exists. There is nothing here to integrate into `benchmarks/dan_tasks/` directly. The paper's contribution to Linus benchmark design is at the level of *task style* — open-ended composition under constraints, perturbed-input probes, theory-of-mind scenarios — not specific items.

Finally, nothing in the paper addresses the hardware story. There is no Apple Silicon, no quantization, no inference engineering content. The lessons here are about cognition and architecture, not about how to fit an inference workload in 32 GB of unified memory.

## Connections
The primary connection is to [garrison_memory_makes_computation_universal.md](../../context/notes/garrison_memory_makes_computation_universal.md). Sparks Section 8 is the symmetric anchor: GPT-4 fails exactly where Garrison's two requirements (recursive state maintenance, reliable history access) are missing. Sparks supplies the *what* gets transferred; Garrison supplies the *what is still missing*. The paper also threads into the cognitive-throughput notes [PIIS0896627324008080.md](PIIS0896627324008080.md) and [nihms-2096004.md](nihms-2096004.md), which estimate the bandwidth of human conscious thought at roughly 10 bits per second against ~10⁹ bits per second of sensory input. Sparks is the AI-side mirror: a model can internalize an enormous vocabulary of human-derived cognitive patterns (high-bandwidth absorption) but is bottlenecked on serial deliberate reasoning (Section 8) in the same way humans are. Both literatures point to the same Linus design implication: serial reasoning is the scarce resource; an orchestration layer's job is to spend that scarcity well, partly by externalizing the memory substrate so the deliberate step does not have to be re-derived from scratch each turn.

## Open questions for Dan
1. **Memory substrate decision, forced by Sparks Section 8.** A local 7B/8B Worker will fail at the same class of multi-step reasoning the paper shows GPT-4 failing at, only worse. Should the Phase 2 MVP commit upfront to an explicit scratchpad protocol — every Worker invocation gets a writable, durable scratch region the orchestration layer manages — rather than relying on the chat template? Sparks suggests this is not optional polish; it is what closes the gap between latent and elicited capability.
2. **Which of Dan's domains has the most useful transferred priors?** The unicorn is impressive because TikZ-of-a-unicorn is rare in training data. The analogue for Linus would be domains where Dan's Worker tasks plausibly sit at intersections the model has *not* seen but whose components it has — e.g., "write a Snakemake rule that fits this kinetic model to nanopore methylation data." Is it worth running a small Sparks-style qualitative probe on three or four such cross-domain prompts against current Ollama models, just to map where transferred priors are sharp vs. hollow?
3. **Episodic memory as the o1 anti-pattern.** Garrison reads o1 as violating reliable-history-access by discarding reasoning tokens; Sparks shows the model's working memory is too small even within a single response. For Linus, does the episodic store need to persist not just final outputs but the *intermediate reasoning traces* of a Worker — so that next session can build on the deliberation, not just the conclusion? Cheap to do at small scale, painful to retrofit later.
4. **Evaluation philosophy.** The paper makes a serious case that benchmark accuracy is the wrong axis for assessing generality on a model whose training data overlaps every benchmark. For Linus, where Dan will be the actual user and the actual evaluator, should `benchmarks/dan_tasks/` lean explicitly into the Sparks methodology — small numbers of perturbed, open-ended tasks judged by Dan — alongside the quantitative tok/s and task-completion-time measurements from [2502.16721v1.md](2502.16721v1.md)? They are answering different questions and both are needed.
