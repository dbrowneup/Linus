# Kosmos: An AI Scientist for Autonomous Discovery

## TL;DR
Kosmos is the most ambitious agentic-system paper in the current wave: an autonomous research agent that runs for **up to 12 hours per task**, executing **~200 agent rollouts**, writing **~42,000 lines of code**, and reading **~1,500 full-length papers** in pursuit of a single open-ended research objective. The architectural keystone is a **structured "world model"** — a continuously updated shared representation that lets parallel data-analysis agents and parallel literature-search agents coordinate without colliding or losing coherence over hundreds of cycles. Independent expert evaluators rated **79.4% of statements** in Kosmos reports as accurate (85% for data analysis, 82% for literature, 58% for synthesis); collaborating academic groups estimated that a single 20-cycle Kosmos run produced the equivalent of **~6 months of their own research time** and that valuable findings scale roughly linearly with cycle count. The paper highlights seven validated discoveries spanning metabolomics, materials science, connectomics, statistical genetics, proteomics, and transcriptomics, including three reproductions of unpublished or post-cutoff work and four genuinely novel contributions. **For Linus, this is the closest existing instantiation of the long-term vision** — and simultaneously a sober reminder that the capability ceiling Kosmos demonstrates almost certainly depends on hosted frontier-model APIs, not on the local-Worker substrate Linus is building toward.

## The problem (in plain language)
Data-driven scientific discovery is iterative: read the literature, look at the data, form a hypothesis, run an analysis, update beliefs, and repeat. Humans do this on timescales of weeks to years. Earlier "AI scientist" systems — Sakana's AI Scientist (limited to ML research), Google's AI co-scientist (no actual data analysis), Robin (the authors' own prior system, narrow to therapeutics), the Virtual Lab (nanobody design) — could each automate a slice, but all hit the same ceiling: **agents lose coherence after a few dozen actions**. They forget what they already tried, redo work, contradict their own conclusions, and decay into noise. The depth of any single investigation is bounded by how long the agent can stay coherent.

The deeper diagnosis is a context-management problem. A naive "long-running agent" stuffs everything it has done into a growing scratchpad; that scratchpad blows past context windows, confuses retrieval, and forces summarization that loses the load-bearing details. A naive "fan-out" architecture spawns parallel sub-agents but has no mechanism to combine their findings into a single coherent worldview — so the parent agent ends up with N partial summaries it can't reconcile.

Kosmos's wager is that this is fundamentally a **memory architecture problem**, not a capability problem of the underlying LLM. If you build the right structured artifact for agents to write into and read from, a frontier model can sustain coherent multi-day investigations. If you don't, even the best model degrades after twenty turns.

## What they propose
The core design is three-layered. At the bottom are **two general-purpose worker agents**: a data-analysis agent (writes Python in Jupyter notebooks against a provided dataset) and a literature-search agent (an evolution of the authors' PaperQA2 system, which reads full-text papers rather than abstracts). Both are stateless per-rollout. In the middle is the **world model**: a structured, continuously updated representation of everything the system currently believes, has tried, has ruled out, and intends to investigate. At the top is a **planner-orchestrator** that, on each cycle, queries the world model to propose up to ten parallel tasks (some analytical, some literature-focused), dispatches them to fresh agent rollouts, ingests their summaries, and updates the world model.

A "cycle" is one round of plan → fan-out (≤10 parallel rollouts) → ingest → world-model update. A typical run is 20 cycles, producing on average **166 data-analysis rollouts and 36 literature-review rollouts** per run. Once the orchestrator decides the objective is satisfied, a final synthesis pass produces three or four scientific reports, where every statement and every figure is hyperlinked to either the originating Jupyter notebook (for data claims) or the cited paper (for literature claims). This **end-to-end provenance** is what makes the 79.4% accuracy number auditable — evaluators can chase any statement back to its source.

The paper is sparse on engineering specifics of the world model itself (schema, storage, update primitives) — it is described functionally rather than mechanically — but the behavioral claim is precise: the world model is what enables 8× more iterations than the prior state of the art and 9.8× more code generation than Robin without coherence collapse.

## Key results
**Accuracy.** 102 statements sampled from three representative reports were independently evaluated by domain experts. Overall: 79.4% supported. By type: 85.5% of data-analysis statements were reproducible by the expert's own analysis; 82.1% of literature statements were verifiable in primary sources; 57.9% of synthesis (interpretation across data and literature) statements were judged sound. The gap is informative — Kosmos is good at *doing* and at *citing*, weaker at *interpreting*.

**Throughput equivalence.** Two estimates of human-equivalent research time. By tallying the work product (papers read at 15 min/paper; notebooks at 2 hr/notebook; 174 working hours/month), each Kosmos run is ~4.1 expert-months. Asked directly, leading academic collaborators rated a 20-cycle run at **6.14 expert-months** on average (n=7, σ=2.49). The two estimates agree in order of magnitude.

**Scaling.** Equivalent expert time roughly doubles from cycle 5 to cycle 20, and the count of "valuable findings" reported by collaborators scales approximately linearly with cycle count over the tested range. This is the most consequential empirical result in the paper for Linus's long-term planning: **autonomous-research compute appears to translate linearly into research output**, at least up to 20 cycles.

**Discoveries.** Seven validated findings: nucleotide-salvage metabolism in hypothermic neuroprotection (reproduces unpublished WashU work); a "fatal filter" environmental-parameter pattern in perovskite spin-coating (reproduces a post-cutoff preprint); cross-species scaling laws in connectomics; SOD2 as a Mendelian-randomization-supported causal candidate for myocardial fibrosis; a T2D regulatory mechanism via TFBS-disruption analysis; a novel segmented-regression method for ordering events along an Alzheimer's pseudotime axis; and an age-dependent collapse of neuronal flippase machinery linked to microglial phagocytosis in entorhinal cortex.

**Honest limits the authors flag.** Kosmos handles datasets up to ~5 GB; cannot process raw images or sequencing reads; cannot autonomously fetch external data; is stochastic (independent runs converge differently); is sensitive to objective phrasing; and provides no mid-run scientist intervention. Humans must still do the discovery-triage step — finding which of the 3-4 narratives × 25 claims actually matters is itself expert-time-intensive.

## What's reusable in Linus

**Phase 3 (Knowledge & Parallel Agents) — the world-model artifact.** The single highest-value transplant from Kosmos is the **structured world model as a first-class artifact distinct from the KnowledgeBase**. Linus's memory pillar already specifies cross-session episodic and semantic-knowledge layers; Kosmos validates that an *active*, *task-scoped* world model — written by agents, read by the orchestrator, updated every cycle — is the missing layer between scratchpad (intra-step) and KnowledgeBase (cross-task). This connects directly to the "schema is the product" principle from the LLM-wiki synthesis. The schema deserves an ADR before Phase 3 implementation; it should be designed as the coordination point for parallel Workers, not retrofitted later.

**Phase 3 — multi-agent fan-out validated.** Kosmos's "≤10 parallel rollouts per cycle" is essentially the Maestro/Worker fan-out Linus is planning, made concrete. The agent-spawner should target the same pattern: planner proposes a batch per cycle, each task gets a stateless Worker rollout, orchestrator ingests summaries, world model updates atomically before the next cycle. Validated reference architecture; do not reinvent.

**Phase 3 — agent specialization at the right granularity.** Kosmos has exactly two worker types (data analysis, literature search) and does not over-decompose into a zoo of micro-agents. Directly applicable: Linus should start with two general-purpose Workers (a code/analysis Worker, a knowledge-retrieval Worker over the KnowledgeBase) coordinated through the world model. Specialization can be added later if measurement justifies it.

**Phase 3 / Phase 7 — provenance as a first-class output.** Every Kosmos statement cites a notebook or a paper. This is what makes 79.4% defensible and what makes humans trust the system. Linus should bake provenance into the audit log from day one: every agent claim carries pointers back to the executing tool call. Dan's bench-science instinct expressed in software — claims without methods sections are not claims.

**Phase 7 (Skills & Autonomy Graduation) — "Linus Kosmos-mode" as a benchmark target.** The seven Kosmos discoveries map cleanly onto Dan's domains. A concrete Phase 7 milestone: **"a 12-hour autonomous Linus run on a Dan-supplied multi-omics dataset that produces a provenance-cited report a domain expert rates as ≥6-month-equivalent work."** Aggressive but well-defined; directly inherits Kosmos's expert-rater evaluation framework.

**Evaluation methodology.** The supported/refuted/unsure rubric, separated by statement type (data-analysis / literature / interpretation), is a clean reusable protocol. Adopting it even at small scale (10 statements per run, evaluated by Dan) makes the weak link (synthesis at 58%) visible rather than letting it hide inside an overall percentage.

## What's NOT applicable

**The model substrate.** The paper does not specify which LLMs power Kosmos's agents, but the throughput numbers (42k LoC per run, 1,500 full papers per run, 200 rollouts at hours of wall-clock each) are only economical and only effective at frontier-model capability levels — almost certainly hosted Anthropic or OpenAI APIs, not Qwen2.5-Coder-7B or Mistral-7B on M1 Max. Linus's local Workers will not match Kosmos's per-rollout reasoning depth in the near term. **The right framing: Kosmos is the capability ceiling Linus aspires to, not a recipe Linus can deploy on current hardware.**

**Scale of compute.** A 12-hour run with 200 parallel rollouts implies non-trivial cloud compute. Even the orchestration overhead (10 parallel Workers for 20 cycles, each holding state and doing real I/O against the KnowledgeBase) presses against the 32 GB unified-memory budget. Linus's first Phase 3 multi-agent runs should target small-fan-out (2-4 parallel Workers, 5 cycles, single-hour wall-clock), not full Kosmos-scale.

**Engineering opacity.** The world-model schema, update semantics, and storage layer are not described in implementable detail. Linus must design them from scratch — also an opportunity to make them biomedical/genomics-flavored rather than fully general.

**Discovery-triage problem.** Kosmos generates 3-4 reports × ~25 claims per run; finding which claims matter is human-bottlenecked. Linus inherits this problem unchanged. For a single user (Dan), the bottleneck is sharper than for a research team.

**Mid-run interaction.** Kosmos has no scientist-in-the-loop during a run. For Linus this is a feature not to copy — Dan wants to nudge mid-stream, and the Maestro/Worker protocol assumes ongoing Maestro presence. The Linus world model should expose mutation hooks between cycles.

## Connections

**Kosmos vs. Boiko/Gomes (Coscientist, 2023).** Boiko's chemistry agent was the first widely-noted multi-step scientific agent — narrower in scope (single domain, short horizons, no shared world model) but the same architectural family. Kosmos is what you get when that idea meets two more years of model capability and serious engineering on the coordination layer.

**Kosmos vs. BioGuider, Sketch2Sim, TradingAgents.** These are domain-specific multi-agent systems for documentation, chemical-process simulation, and trading respectively. The contrast clarifies that Kosmos's contribution is not "use multiple agents" (everyone is doing that) but **"use a structured shared state to make multiple agents cohere over hundreds of rollouts"** — and that the world-model abstraction is what gives Kosmos cross-domain generality those systems lack.

**Kosmos vs. "Fundamentals of LLM Agents" survey and the Practical Guide for LLM Eval.** The survey formalizes the planner/executor/memory/tool-use patterns as theoretical types; Kosmos is what those patterns look like at industrial scale on a real scientific objective. The Practical Guide's evaluation taxonomy maps cleanly onto Kosmos's data/literature/synthesis statement-type rubric.

**Kosmos vs. PaperQA2 (cited internally as the literature-search agent).** PaperQA2 is one of Kosmos's two worker components. The lesson is that Kosmos's novel contribution is *not* its individual agents but the orchestration layer that turns them into a sustained investigator — exactly the Linus-orchestration-layer thesis: value accrues at the coordination layer, not in any single tool.

**Kosmos vs. Linus's memory architecture.** Linus's memory pillar lists intra-step latent / scratchpad / episodic / semantic-knowledge layers. Kosmos's world model fits between *episodic* and *semantic-knowledge*: **task-scoped working memory shared across agents**, with a lifetime of one investigation. Linus should consider naming this layer explicitly — perhaps "investigation memory" — and giving it its own component in the orchestration layer. The KnowledgeBase submodule is the corresponding semantic-knowledge layer; its existence is *prerequisite* for any Linus-Kosmos-mode deliverable.

## Open questions for Dan

1. **World model as a first-class component.** Should Linus's Phase 3 architecture explicitly add a "world model" / "investigation memory" component to the orchestration layer, distinct from both the scratchpad and the KnowledgeBase? An ADR before Phase 3 implementation would force a clear schema decision; deferring it risks retrofitting later. Recommendation: write the ADR; the Kosmos paper is strong evidence the layer is load-bearing rather than nice-to-have.

2. **Phase 7 deliverable benchmark.** Is "12-hour autonomous Linus run on a Dan-supplied dataset producing a provenance-cited report rated ≥6-month-equivalent by an external collaborator" the right north-star benchmark for Phase 7? It is concrete, falsifiable, directly inherits Kosmos's evaluation method, and forces the whole orchestration + memory + KnowledgeBase + Worker stack to function end-to-end. Or is this premature, and Phase 7 should target a smaller-scope autonomous-investigation benchmark first?

3. **Worker substrate honesty.** Given Kosmos's likely dependence on frontier hosted models, should Linus explicitly support a "hybrid mode" where the planner-orchestrator is allowed to call hosted Claude (the Maestro) for high-stakes synthesis steps while keeping bulk rollouts on local Workers? This is a meaningful departure from "no paid APIs required for operation" — defensible if local-only is preserved as a separate operating mode, problematic if it becomes the only path to capability. Worth deciding before implementation rather than discovering it as a regression.

4. **Evaluation protocol adoption.** Should Linus formally adopt Kosmos's three-statement-type evaluation rubric (data-analysis / literature / interpretation, supported/refuted/unsure) as the standard for evaluating Linus outputs in `benchmarks/`? Even at small scale (10 statements per run, evaluated by Dan personally), it would surface the same synthesis-quality gap Kosmos found and prevent overall-accuracy numbers from hiding the weak link.

5. **Discovery-triage tooling.** Kosmos generates more candidate findings per run than its users can evaluate. For Linus on Dan's single-user workload, this bottleneck is sharper. Is a "candidate-finding ranker" — a small Worker that pre-scores a Kosmos-mode run's outputs by novelty × surprise × Dan-relevance before Dan reviews them — itself a Phase 7 component worth specifying? It would be a Linus-specific addition Kosmos does not provide.

6. **World-model schema design — generic or biomedical-first?** Kosmos's world model is described functionally and appears domain-agnostic. Should Linus's first world-model schema be intentionally biomedical/genomics-flavored (entities = genes, proteins, pathways, datasets, experiments; relations = upregulates, correlates-with, replicates, contradicts), trading generality for fit to Dan's domain? The Algorithm says yes — design for the use case you actually have, not the use case you might want. Worth confirming.
