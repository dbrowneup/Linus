# ProtHGT: Heterogeneous Graph Transformers for Automated Protein Function Prediction Using Biological Knowledge Graphs and Language Models

## TL;DR
ProtHGT predicts Gene Ontology (GO) annotations for proteins by treating protein function prediction as **link prediction over a large heterogeneous biological knowledge graph**. The graph (CROssBAR KG) has 542k nodes spanning nine entity types — proteins, GO terms, EC numbers, domains, pathways, drugs, compounds, diseases, phenotypes — and 3.79M edges across 17 relation types. Each node type carries a type-specific embedding (ProtT5/TAPE/ESM2 for proteins, anc2vec for GO terms, dom2vec for domains, RXNFP for EC numbers, doc2vec for diseases, TransE for pathways, node2vec for phenotypes, SELFormer for compounds). A Heterogeneous Graph Transformer (HGT) — Hu et al. 2020 — propagates messages with **per-edge-type attention matrices and per-node-type linear projections**, then an MLP scores protein–GO term pairs. ProtHGT-ProtT5 achieves SOTA on the DeepHGAT time-based benchmark across all three GO sub-ontologies (MF/CC/BP) and is competitive with the top 10 CAFA3 methods. Ablations show pathway and EC-number nodes contribute disproportionately, and the model's KG-path explanations make individual predictions traceable.

## The problem (in plain language)
Protein function annotation is a foundational task in Dan's domain. UniProtKB has hundreds of millions of sequences, but only a fraction are experimentally annotated, and experimental annotation is slow, expensive, and biased toward well-studied organisms. Computational predictors fill the gap, but the field has bifurcated unhelpfully: **feature-based methods** look at sequences and structures (BLAST, PFAM, more recently DeepFRI on AlphaFold structures, and protein language models like ESM/ProtT5), while **graph-based methods** look at protein-protein interaction networks or GO term hierarchies. Each captures a slice of biology and ignores the rest. A protein's function is jointly determined by its sequence, its structural domains, the pathways it participates in, the diseases it's implicated in, the compounds it binds, and the phenotypes its perturbation produces. Most predictors look at one or two of those sources at a time.

The ProtHGT authors argue that the right substrate for protein function prediction is a **biological knowledge graph integrating all of those entity types simultaneously**, and that a graph neural network designed for heterogeneous graphs (HGT) is the right learner for that substrate. The architectural intuition is that an attention mechanism that knows the *type* of edge it's traversing — protein-domain vs protein-pathway vs disease-phenotype — can weigh different relational paths differently when scoring whether a candidate (protein, GO-term) pair should hold. This is a direct instantiation of the heterogeneous-KG-with-typed-edges design that the Hogan KG survey theorizes about; ProtHGT is what happens when you actually build and train one for a high-value biological prediction task.

## What they propose
The construction has three parts. First, a **knowledge graph**: CROssBAR KG, integrating 14 source databases — UniProtKB/Swiss-Prot (proteins), GO + GOA (function annotations, IEA evidence excluded), InterPro + InterPro2GO (domains), Reactome + KEGG (pathways), OMA (orthology, 29 organisms), Expasy ENZYME (EC numbers), DrugBank-class sources (drugs/compounds), and disease/phenotype sources via PrimeKG. UniRef90 clustering removes proteins with >90% sequence similarity from crossing train/test splits. Final graph: 9 node types, 17 edge types, 542,465 nodes, 3,792,832 edges.

Second, **type-specific node features**. Each node type gets an embedding from a method appropriate to its modality: proteins from ProtT5, TAPE, ESM-2, or APAAC (ablated to compare); GO terms from anc2vec (which preserves DAG ancestral relationships); domains from dom2vec (word2vec over domain architectures); EC numbers from RXNFP applied to SMILES of catalysed reactions; diseases from doc2vec over textual descriptions; pathways from TransE over gene-pathway networks; phenotypes from node2vec over gene-phenotype networks; compounds from SELFormer over SELFIES. The per-type embedding step is itself a small lesson — the authors don't try to learn all node features end-to-end from the KG; they bootstrap from modality-appropriate pretrained encoders and let the HGT do the relational refinement.

Third, the **HGT architecture**. Each HGT block has three sub-modules: (1) attention, where source/target nodes are projected with type-specific Key/Query linear layers and the similarity is computed through an edge-type-specific attention matrix, with a triple-importance tensor μ weighting each (source-type, edge-type, target-type) combination; (2) message passing, also with edge-type-specific message matrices; (3) feature aggregation with residual connection and target-type-specific output projection. Stacking blocks propagates information across the heterogeneous graph. Function prediction is framed as **link prediction**: concatenate the final-layer protein and GO-term embeddings, push through an MLP, sigmoid-activate.

Three independent models are trained — one per GO sub-ontology (MF, BP, CC) — because the authors found the sub-ontologies differ enough in scope and structure that joint training underperforms. Negative sampling at 100:1 negative:positive, with weighted binary cross-entropy. Implementation uses PyTorch Geometric's HGTConv. Random hyperparameter search over hidden-channel size, learning rate, attention heads, layer count, batch size, neighbour sampling size, weight decay. Splits are at the protein level (80/10/10) with all edges of a given protein appearing in only one split, simulating cold-start prediction for newly sequenced proteins.

## Key results
On the **DeepHGAT time-based benchmark** (Human, train ≤ 2021-09-01, test 2021-09-01 to 2023-04-01), ProtHGT-ProtT5 achieves the best score across every metric (Micro AvgF1, Macro AvgF1, AUC, AUPR, Fmax) and every GO sub-ontology (MF, BP, CC), beating DeepHGAT, PSPGO, DeepGraphGO, PFresGO, TALE+, PANDA2, DeepFRI, and several PPI-network baselines. ProtHGT-TAPE and ProtHGT-ESM2 are close behind. Sample numbers from Table 3: ProtHGT-ProtT5 reaches AUPR around 0.97 (CC), 0.96 (MF), 0.96 (BP); Fmax around 0.91 (CC), 0.89 (MF), 0.90 (BP). The lowest-performing variant (ProtHGT-ESM2 on BP, Macro AvgF1 = 78.52) sometimes lags TAPE and ProtT5, suggesting the choice of PLM matters even given the relational refinement.

On **CAFA3**, ProtHGT variants outperform the Naive and BLAST baselines across all sub-ontologies, achieve top Smin scores in MF and BP (meaning they're good at predicting *informative* terms, not just generic ones), and the highest Fmax in CC. They are competitive with the top 10 CAFA3 methods, though not always dominant. APAAC (the traditional non-PLM embedding) lags clearly — confirming that modern PLM embeddings are essential as input features.

The **ablation analyses** are the most architecturally informative. Removing EC-number nodes causes the largest MCC drop in MF (0.697 → 0.509) and BP (0.602 → 0.480). Removing pathway nodes (KEGG and Reactome) hurts every sub-ontology substantially. Domain nodes are critical for MF and CC. Conversely, removing phenotype nodes actually *improves* CC (0.684 vs the all-included baseline), suggesting phenotype edges add noise for cellular-component prediction. The retention ablation (train with only one node type) shows that BP nodes alone are nearly sufficient for MF prediction, that pathway-only graphs give moderate MF/CC performance, and that KEGG-only graphs reach MCC 0.552 for BP.

The **embedding-space visualisation** (t-SNE/UMAP) shows that initial PLM embeddings already cluster non-enzyme protein superfamilies cleanly; HGT refinement tightens the clusters and improves separation, particularly for non-enzyme families and CC GO terms.

The **use case**: ProtHGT correctly recovers known annotations for Keratin-associated protein 23-1 (cytosol GO:0005829, protein binding GO:0005515) at probability >0.99, and predicts a novel "structural constituent of bone" annotation. The interpretability layer surfaces a 4-step and a 5-step KG path explaining the prediction — through Keratinization and Developmental Biology pathways and shared domain (Keratin-associated protein, PMG type IPR007951) with bone-related proteins (Collagen alpha-1(II), Bone morphogenetic protein 6). The predicted annotation is technically a false positive against current curated databases but is biologically plausible and hypothesis-generating.

## What's reusable in Linus
**KnowledgeBase architecture (most important).** ProtHGT's CROssBAR KG is a concrete, working template for the protein layer of Linus's KnowledgeBase: nine biological entity types, seventeen typed relations, type-specific node embeddings bootstrapped from modality-appropriate pretrained encoders. The KnowledgeBase already uses rdflib + networkx; adopting CROssBAR schema fragments would give immediate interop with downstream tools (DIAMOND DeepClust, Horizyn-1, ProteinReasoner, BioReason).

**Phase 7 candidate skill.** Protein function annotation is the kind of focused, high-value task Linus's tool registry should expose. The ProtHGT GitHub repo and Hugging Face space provide both a programmatic interface and a web UI. Input: protein sequence or UniProt ID. Output: ranked GO terms across MF/BP/CC with probabilities, plus KG-path explanations.

**Provenance layer template.** The use-case study's KG-path explanation (the protein, the four/five edges connecting it to the predicted GO term, the intermediate nodes) is exactly the audit-log shape Linus should aim for. Any KnowledgeBase-derived prediction should be accompanied by a traceable graph path explaining *why* it holds. ProtHGT proves the pattern works at scale and is human-interpretable in practice.

**Embedding-bootstrap pattern.** The authors don't train node embeddings end-to-end. They bootstrap with modality-appropriate pretrained encoders (PLM for proteins, anc2vec for GO, RXNFP for chemistry) and let the HGT refine the relational structure. A useful default for Linus: bootstrap each modality with the best available pretrained encoder, then refine relationally.

**Evaluation methodology.** The time-based split (train pre-cutoff, test post-cutoff) and the protein-level split (all edges of a given protein in one split) are the right defaults for any KnowledgeBase-resident predictor — they simulate real cold-start.

## What's NOT applicable
ProtHGT is trained on **NVIDIA hardware via PyTorch Geometric**, not Apple Silicon. Inference might run on CPU or via PyTorch's MPS backend on M1, but the published checkpoints and training pipeline assume CUDA. Reproducing training on the M1 Max would require porting HGTConv to MLX or accepting a slow CPU/MPS run. Inference on a single protein is likely tractable; full retraining with a 542k-node graph and 3.79M edges on 32 GB unified memory is at the edge.

The **paper does not measure inference cost**. We don't know how many seconds per protein, batch size limits, or memory footprint at inference. Before treating ProtHGT as a routine Linus tool, the inference profile on Dan's hardware needs measurement.

The **CC sub-ontology results** are weaker on highly informative terms (higher Smin on CAFA3), which the authors attribute to sparse domain-content annotations. Dan should expect CC predictions to be less reliable than MF/BP for niche localisation calls.

The **PLM choice (ProtT5)** is heavy: ProtT5 is a 3B-parameter encoder-only T5 variant. Using ProtT5 embeddings means either pre-computing them (and storing 1024-dim vectors per protein) or running ProtT5 inference on demand. ESM-2 variants (smaller and Apple-Silicon-friendlier) are slightly behind ProtT5 in the paper but viable substitutes.

This is a **predictive model trained on a fixed-time KG snapshot**. The graph schema is static; new entity types or new data sources require re-engineering and retraining. ProtHGT is not the right substrate for Linus's *living* KnowledgeBase if Dan wants entities and edges to grow continuously without periodic retrains. It's the right substrate for a periodically refreshed annotation snapshot.

## Connections
**[Hogan KG survey (2003.02320v6)](2003.02320v6.md)** — ProtHGT is a concrete instantiation of the heterogeneous-KG-with-typed-edges design Hogan theorizes about. The Hogan paper sets up the abstractions (entities, types, relations, schemas, embeddings, GNNs over KGs); ProtHGT shows what it looks like in production for a domain Dan cares about. Together they bracket the design space: Hogan from the theory side, ProtHGT from the implementation side.

**[Horizyn-1 (rocks-et-al-2026-dual-encoder-contrastive-learning-accelerates-enzyme-discovery.md)](rocks-et-al-2026-dual-encoder-contrastive-learning-accelerates-enzyme-discovery.md)** — both fuse PLM embeddings with structured biological knowledge for function-related prediction, but the architectures bet differently. Horizyn-1 uses a dual-encoder contrastive retrieval framework (sequence ↔ EC number / reaction text); ProtHGT uses a heterogeneous graph transformer with link-prediction classification. They are competing paradigms for closely related problems. Horizyn-1 will scale better to vocabulary growth (just embed and retrieve); ProtHGT integrates richer multi-entity context but requires schema design and retraining. Linus should consider hosting both and routing per task.

**[DIAMOND DeepClust (s41592-026-03030-z.md)](s41592-026-03030-z.md)** — DeepClust produces evolutionary cluster nodes that could enrich ProtHGT's KG with another node type (clusters) and edges (protein-cluster, cluster-cluster). The two papers are architecturally complementary: DeepClust as a substrate for evolutionary structure, ProtHGT as a learner over relational structure that includes evolutionary edges.

**BioReason / BioReason-Pro (this batch)** — all three are PLM+structure approaches to function-related prediction. BioReason adds explicit reasoning chains via LLM; ProtHGT surfaces interpretability via KG paths; Horizyn-1 leans on retrieval. Three different architectural bets to compare side by side once notes for all three exist.

**[LucaOne (s42256-025-01044-4.md)](s42256-025-01044-4.md), [ProteinReasoner (2025.07.21.665832v2.md)](2025.07.21.665832v2.md)** — Group A foundation models that produce the kind of input embeddings ProtHGT consumes. ProtHGT is agnostic to which PLM provides the embeddings (it ablates ProtT5/TAPE/ESM-2); LucaOne or ProteinReasoner embeddings could in principle be drop-in substitutes if Linus wants to use its own preferred PLM as the substrate.

**Maestro/Worker fit.** GO function annotation is a focused, well-defined Worker task. Spec: input UniProt ID or sequence, output ranked GO terms with KG-path explanations. The Worker just calls ProtHGT; Maestro decides when to invoke and how to integrate the output into Dan's research thread. This is also a candidate for the synthesis "PLM + heterogeneous KG + graph transformer = a Linus architectural archetype" — three independent recent papers (ProtHGT, Horizyn-1, DeepHGAT) cluster around variants of this pattern, and naming it explicitly in `docs/syntheses/` would help future Maestro decisions.

## Open questions for Dan
1. **KnowledgeBase schema.** Should the protein layer of Linus's KnowledgeBase be modeled directly on CROssBAR KG's nine-entity, seventeen-relation schema? Adopting it now (or pulling the rdflib subset) gives interop with ProtHGT and likely with future KG-native bioinformatics tools. The cost is committing to a specific ontology before exploring alternatives like PrimeKG (Chandak et al. 2023, cited heavily here) or Hetionet (Himmelstein et al. 2017).
2. **ProtHGT vs Horizyn-1 vs BioReason-Pro.** All three address protein-function-adjacent tasks with different architectural bets (graph transformer + classification, dual-encoder retrieval, LLM reasoning chains). Which one becomes the first Linus tool? They're not mutually exclusive — but pick-order matters for Phase 7 sequencing.
3. **Architectural archetype.** Is "PLM input embeddings + heterogeneous biological KG + graph transformer over typed edges" worth naming as a Linus archetype in `docs/syntheses/`? At least three papers in the corpus (ProtHGT, DeepHGAT, PSPGO) are variants of this pattern. Naming it gives future Maestro decisions a vocabulary.
4. **Provenance layer.** ProtHGT's KG-path explanations (4-step, 5-step paths annotated with edge probabilities) are exactly the audit-log shape Linus should aim for. Should the orchestration layer's audit log adopt a graph-path representation natively for any KnowledgeBase-derived prediction? The alternative (free-text rationales) is easier to log but harder to verify.
5. **Inference cost on M1 Max.** What's the per-protein inference latency if we run the published ProtHGT-ProtT5 checkpoint via PyTorch MPS on Dan's hardware? Worth a 30-minute spike before scoping a real Linus tool wrapper. ProtT5 embedding generation might dominate; ESM-2 may be a more practical default.
6. **Retraining on Dan's data.** Could Linus retrain ProtHGT (or fine-tune the HGT layers) on a Dan-specific subgraph — for example, only the proteins relevant to a given research thread, with custom edges added from Dan's notes? The 542k-node, 3.79M-edge full retrain is heavy on 32 GB; a subgraph retrain is plausible.
7. **Hybrid with Horizyn-1.** Horizyn-1's contrastive embeddings could become an additional protein-protein edge type in ProtHGT's KG (link proteins that retrieve to similar EC numbers). Worth a synthesis note on whether this cross-architecture borrowing makes sense.
