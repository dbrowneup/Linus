# Root Mean Square Layer Normalization

## TL;DR

Zhang and Sennrich propose RMSNorm, a simplified layer normalization that removes the mean-centering step and normalizes
only by the root mean square of the input vector. RMSNorm achieves comparable (or better) training stability and model
quality compared to LayerNorm while reducing computation by ~7-64% across different models. Modern LLMs (Llama, Mistral,
Qwen) use RMSNorm instead of LayerNorm, making this substitution foundational for Linus Worker efficiency on Apple
Silicon's unified memory budget.

## The problem (in plain language)

Vaswani's Transformer uses LayerNorm to stabilize training by normalizing both the mean and variance of hidden states
within each layer. While LayerNorm works well, it has two costs: (1) computing mean and variance over the full vector,
and (2) using both statistics in two separate operations. For deep networks and long sequences, this overhead compounds.
Zhang and Sennrich hypothesize that the mean-centering is not the critical component—only the re-scaling invariance
matters. If true, removing mean-centering would cut computation in half without sacrificing training stability.

## What they propose

RMSNorm normalizes summed inputs to a neuron using only the root mean square (RMS) statistic:

$$\bar{a}_i = \frac{a_i}{\text{RMS}(a)} g_i, \quad \text{where } \text{RMS}(a) = \sqrt{\frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^{n} a_i^2}$$

LayerNorm, in contrast, computes:

$$\bar{a}_i = \frac{a_i - \mu}{\sigma} g_i, \quad \mu = \frac{1}{n}\sum_i a_i, \quad \sigma = \sqrt{\frac{1}{n}\sum_i (a_i - \mu)^2}$$

The key insight is that RMS maintains the re-scaling invariance property of LayerNorm. Table 1 in the paper shows that
RMSNorm is invariant to scaling both weight matrices and inputs (both scaling the weight matrix W by δ and the input by
δ leaves the output unchanged), which preserves the stabilizing effect on model output. The mean term is not necessary
for this property, and removing it saves 2 mean computations per normalization.

## Key results

**Machine Translation (newstest2014, RNNSearch + Transformer):**

- RMSNorm achieves comparable or better BLEU scores compared to LayerNorm baseline.
- Training converges faster: LayerNorm reaches loss 5.4 at 65,536 steps; RMSNorm reaches 5.4 at ~50k steps (20% faster
  convergence).
- Wall-clock speedup: 7%-64% faster depending on model size and framework.

**Table 2 (SacreBLEU, Tensor Transformer):**

- LayerNorm Test14: 22.6, Test17: 23.6
- RMSNorm Test14: 22.4, Test17: 23.7 (comparable, slightly better on Test17)
- Time per training step: LayerNorm 665ms, RMSNorm 501ms (24.7% faster)

**Ablations:**

- pRMSNorm (partial RMS, estimated from p% of inputs): achieves competitive performance with even lower compute at
  p=6.25%.
- RMSNorm with L2-Norm baseline: RMSNorm outperforms L2-Norm, confirming that RMS is the right statistic.
- Experiments on image classification, summarization, and question answering show consistent speedups with comparable
  or better quality.

## What's reusable in Linus

RMSNorm is the standard normalization in every modern Worker model:

- **Qwen2.5-Coder, Mistral-7B, Llama 3.x:** All use RMSNorm, not LayerNorm. Any fine-tuning, LoRA, or inference code
  for Linus assumes RMSNorm.
- **Apple Silicon efficiency:** On M1 Max unified memory, every saved FLOP matters. RMSNorm's ~25% speedup vs LayerNorm
  is especially valuable when running inference at 32 GB capacity with large KV caches. Faster normalization → more
  tokens per second on fixed memory.
- **Pre-norm vs post-norm interaction:** Modern models use pre-norm (LayerNorm/RMSNorm applied before the sublayer,
  then residual add), which improves training stability at depth. RMSNorm + pre-norm is the standard architecture.
  Linus models will all use this pattern.
- **BitNet quantization:** RMSNorm's simpler statistic (only variance, no mean) makes quantization-friendly kernels
  easier to write. The mean is information-rich; removing it simplifies the quantization search space.
- **Streaming inference:** RMSNorm is applied per-layer and per-token. In streaming weight-loading (mlx-flash), the
  normalization parameters (gain g_i) are small and can be kept in Metal memory while weights stream from SSD. RMSNorm's
  fewer statistics reduce register pressure.

## What's NOT applicable / hype filter

The partial RMSNorm (pRMSNorm) from Section 5 is a research ablation showing that even p=6.25% of summed inputs
suffices for comparable performance. This is not used in production models and is likely a quirk of the specific task
and batch statistics.

The paper experiments on machine translation and image classification, not scientific computing or code tasks. However,
the efficiency gain is model-agnostic: RMSNorm is faster on any task.

The 2019 paper pre-dates pre-norm (pre-norm became standard between 2019-2021). Modern usage of RMSNorm assumes pre-norm,
not the post-norm LayerNorm pattern shown in Vaswani. The interaction is subtle but worth understanding when reading
recent model cards.

interaction with training stability at scale.
