# Advancing regulatory variant effect prediction with AlphaGenome

## TL;DR

AlphaGenome is a single deep learning model that takes **1 Mb of DNA sequence** as input and predicts **5,930 human (or
1,128 mouse) functional genomic tracks at up to single-base-pair resolution** across eleven modalities — RNA-seq, CAGE,
PRO-cap, splice sites/usage/junctions, DNase, ATAC, histone ChIP-seq, TF ChIP-seq, and 3D contact maps. It resolves the
long-standing length-vs-resolution trade-off in sequence-to-function modelling: prior models gave 200–500 kb context at
32–128 bp resolution (Enformer, Borzoi) or base resolution at ≤10 kb context (SpliceAI, BPNet). AlphaGenome **matches or
exceeds the strongest external SOTA on 22 of 24 genome track tasks and 25 of 26 variant-effect benchmarks**, including
+25.5% on eQTL sign prediction over Borzoi, beating Pangolin on splicing, beating ChromBPNet on accessibility/binding
QTLs, and beating Orca on contact-map cell-type differences by +42%. Crucially for Linus: **weights, source code, and
variant scoring implementations are released openly at `github.com/google-deepmind/alphagenome_research`**, while the
hosted Python SDK / API is non-commercial-only. This is the most directly clinically actionable DNA foundation model in
the current wave.

## The problem (in plain language)

More than 98% of human genetic variation falls outside protein-coding regions. These non-coding variants don't change
amino acids; they change _regulation_ — when, where, and how strongly genes turn on, how RNA is spliced, which
transcription factors bind, how chromatin folds. For Dan's domain (regulatory genomics, variant effect prediction), this
is the central interpretive bottleneck: a clinical sequencing report turns up a variant of uncertain significance in an
enhancer, and the question is whether it disrupts a transcription factor footprint, shifts splice site choice, alters
expression of a distant target gene through a long-range loop, or does nothing at all. Wet-lab characterization at scale
is intractable. So the field builds _sequence-to-function_ models: feed in DNA, predict the experimental measurements.

These models have been wedged on two trade-offs. The first is **length versus resolution**. Splicing decisions live at
single nucleotides — a one-base shift in a donor motif rewrites the transcript — but enhancers regulate genes hundreds
of kilobases away, so the model needs both a wide window and a fine ruler. Models like SpliceAI saw single bases but
only ~10 kb of context. Enformer and Borzoi widened to ~200–500 kb but had to coarsen output to 32–128 bp bins, blurring
the very motifs that define splicing and TF binding. The second trade-off is **multimodal versus specialist**. SpliceAI
does splicing well; ChromBPNet does accessibility well; Orca does 3D contact maps well; but to interpret a non-coding
variant you often need _all_ of these simultaneously, because the variant's mechanism is unknown until you score it
everywhere. Generalist multimodal models existed (Enformer, Sei, Borzoi) but lagged specialists on individual tasks.

AlphaGenome attacks both trade-offs at once.

## What they propose

The architecture is a **U-Net-inspired backbone** with convolutional encoders/decoders bracketing transformer blocks.
The 1 Mb input is split into 131 kb chunks distributed across eight TPU v3 devices via sequence parallelism — that's the
engineering trick that makes base-resolution training feasible at megabase context. The model produces two embedding
types: 1D embeddings at 1 bp and 128 bp resolution (used for linear-genome track outputs), and 2D embeddings at 2,048 bp
resolution (used for pairwise contact-map predictions). Splice junction prediction uses a separate mechanism that
captures interactions between the 1D embeddings of donor–acceptor pairs.

Training has two stages. First, **pretraining**: 4-fold cross-validation models (for held-out evaluation) plus all-fold
models (which become teachers for distillation), trained with random shifts and reverse-complement augmentation against
observed experimental targets. Second, **distillation**: a single student model with the same architecture is trained to
reproduce the predictions of an ensemble of all-fold teachers under augmented and randomly mutated inputs. The distilled
student is what ships — it's a single forward-pass model that takes **less than 1 second per variant on an NVIDIA
H100**, versus the alternative of running an ensemble for every prediction.

The release strategy is mixed and important to read carefully. **Source code, model weights, variant scoring
implementations, and a selection of variant evaluation datasets and predictions are openly available** at
`github.com/google-deepmind/alphagenome_research`. A hosted **API and Python SDK** at
`deepmind.google.com/science/alphagenome` provide the convenient managed interface, but the API is licensed for
**non-commercial use only**. So unlike AlphaFold-2 (open weights, broad reuse) or AlphaFold-3 (early hosted-API-only
era), AlphaGenome lands in a hybrid posture: weights are available for anyone to run locally, but the polished tooling
is gated. For Linus this is good news.

## Key results

The headline is the **22-of-24 sweep on track prediction and 25-of-26 sweep on variant effect prediction** against the
best external model per task. Notable margins:

- **Gene expression**: +14.7% relative on cell-type-specific gene-level log-fold-change RNA-seq vs Borzoi. eQTL sign
  auROC 0.75 → 0.80 (+25.5% relative); coefficient Spearman ρ 0.39 → 0.49. At a calibrated 90% sign-accuracy threshold,
  AlphaGenome recovers 41% of GTEx eQTLs vs Borzoi's 19% — more than double the practical yield.
- **Splicing**: SOTA on 6 of 7 splicing-variant benchmarks. The novel splice-junction-level scorer alone beats Pangolin,
  SpliceAI, and DeltaSplice on most fine-mapped sQTL tasks. ClinVar pathogenic-vs-benign improves on deep
  intronic/synonymous (auPRC 0.66 vs 0.64), splice region (0.57 vs 0.55), and missense (0.18 vs 0.16). One loss: MFASS
  minigene MPRA (0.51 vs Pangolin's 0.54).
- **Chromatin and TF binding**: beats both Borzoi and the specialist ChromBPNet across caQTL, dsQTL, and bQTL benchmarks
  for both causality and effect-size correlation across African, European, and Yoruba ancestries. Pearson r = 0.74 with
  observed effect sizes for causal caQTLs in GM12878.
- **Enhancer–gene linking**: zero-shot within 1% auPRC of ENCODE–rE2G extended, a model explicitly trained on this
  CRISPRi task. Strongly outperforms Borzoi for distal enhancers (>10 kb from TSS).
- **Contact maps**: +6.3% Pearson r on Orca's task; +42.3% on cell-type-specific contact differences.
- **Polyadenylation**: SOTA APA prediction (Spearman ρ = 0.894 vs Borzoi's 0.790) without any explicit training on
  polyadenylation data.
- **Multimodal cross-modality interpretation**: on the TAL1 oncogene locus, AlphaGenome recapitulates the mechanism of a
  known T-ALL oncogenic insertion (chr1:47239296: C>ACG) — predicting H3K27ac/H3K4me1 increase at the variant
  (neo-enhancer formation), H3K9me3/H3K27me3 decrease at the TAL1 TSS (de-repression), H3K36me3 elevation across the
  gene body, and a corresponding mRNA increase, all in one forward pass. ISM localizes the gain to a created MYB motif
  plus an ETS-like motif of previously unknown role.

The ablations (Fig. 7) are clean: 1-bp target resolution wins for fine-scale tasks and is neutral elsewhere; 1-Mb
training context strictly dominates shorter-context training even when both are evaluated at 1 Mb; a single distilled
student from 64 teachers approaches ensemble performance at a fraction of inference cost; multimodal training helps most
modalities relative to single-modality training, with diminishing returns past a few modality groups.

## What's reusable in Linus

**Direct, Phase 7 (Skills & Autonomy)**: AlphaGenome is a strong candidate for a Linus _regulatory variant
interpretation skill_. The use case maps onto Dan's domain almost perfectly — feed a VCF entry, get back structured
predictions across expression, splicing, chromatin, TF binding, and 3D contact, with mechanistic interpretation via ISM.
Because **weights are released**, this can plausibly be a Linus-internal Worker rather than an external API wrapper,
subject to the local-deployability check below. The single-inference-per-variant design (~1 s on H100) is operationally
clean; the open question is what that becomes on M1 Max via MLX or Metal-converted CoreML.

**Direct, Phase 7 — local inference engineering**: training targets TPU v3 sequence parallelism, but inference uses a
single distilled student. Realistic engineering paths: (a) PyTorch on MPS, (b) JAX on Metal (if the released code is
JAX), (c) port to MLX. Memory footprint isn't quoted, but a U-Net + transformer that ate 8 TPU shards for 1 Mb of
one-hot DNA is a non-trivial 32 GB budget. Worth a Phase 1 spike: clone, inspect model size, attempt one local inference
on a 1 Mb interval. Smoke-test before committing.

**Direct, Phase 4 (Data Sovereignty)**: training and evaluation datasets are publicly accessible — ENCODE, GTEx, 4D
Nucleome, ClinVar, gnomAD — with a comprehensive manifest in Supplementary Table 2, plus a selection of variant
evaluation datasets and predictions in the GitHub repo. The fine-mapped GTEx eQTL set, ENCODE–rE2G CRISPRi enhancer–gene
links, CAGI5 MPRA, and ClinVar splicing benchmarks are landmark evaluation sets that generalize across the whole Group A
constellation — worth mirroring into a versioned local store.

**Direct, Phase 3 (Knowledge & Parallel Agents)**: AlphaGenome's multimodal output already runs in a single internal
inference pass, but at orchestration level the _interpretation_ of those outputs (literature retrieval, KnowledgeBase
paper lookup for the affected gene, GWAS catalog cross-reference) is exactly what parallel Workers are for.

**Indirect, KnowledgeBase**: ISM outputs identify the specific motif a variant creates or disrupts and link to JASPAR
matrix IDs for known transcription factors. That's an obvious join key into a regulatory-genomics knowledge graph:
variant → motif → TF → pathway → disease. Indexing regulatory genomics papers gets far more powerful when mechanism is a
structured predicate, not just free text.

**Indirect, Phase 2 (MVP) tool registry**: AlphaGenome is the motivating test case for a generic _external/local hybrid
model_ tool class — register a tool that prefers local inference but can fall back to a hosted API, with the policy
explicit at registration time. Designing this abstraction with AlphaGenome in mind avoids the trap of bolting on
"external API wrapper" categories one-off later.

## What's NOT applicable

**Training from scratch is not on the table.** TPU v3 sequence parallelism across 8 devices for an unspecified but large
compute budget — M1 Max with 32 GB cannot do this. Phase 6 (Fine-Tuning) ambitions for AlphaGenome are limited to
fine-tuning the released student on small task-specific datasets, not full retraining.

**Mouse and human only.** If Dan's work touches non-human/non-mouse organisms, AlphaGenome is silent — explicitly called
out in the discussion. Evo 2's all-domains-of-life coverage is the alternative.

**Personal genome prediction is unbenchmarked.** A known weakness across the field: sequence-to-function models trained
on reference genomes don't necessarily transfer to individual phenotype prediction. Matters for clinical interpretation
in patient-genome context rather than reference-relative scoring.

**Trait-level prediction is out of scope.** AlphaGenome predicts molecular consequences, not phenotypes. Supplementary
Note and Fig. 6f explicitly walk back any phenotype-level interpretation. For complex-trait GWAS work, AlphaGenome is
one input to a downstream model.

**The hosted API is non-commercial-only.** If Linus is ever offered as a service to others, the API path is closed and
the only legal path is the released weights run locally. Check the GitHub license carefully before using either.

## Connections

**Group A constellation — central comparison: AlphaGenome vs Evo 2** (`paper-notes/s41586-026-10176-5.md`, the other
DNA-FM headliner in this batch). These are complementary, not competitive. Evo 2 is a **generative DNA foundation
model** trained across all domains of life with very long context (~1M tokens), suited to general sequence
understanding, sequence generation, and broad species coverage. AlphaGenome is a **discriminative regulatory
specialist** trained only on human and mouse with fine-grained multimodal supervised heads, suited to variant scoring
and mechanism inference. The likely Linus default: **AlphaGenome for clinically actionable regulatory variant
interpretation** in human/mouse, **Evo 2 for general DNA reasoning, sequence generation, and non-human/non-mouse work**.
The tool-registry pattern is to surface both with clear use-case docstrings rather than picking one.

**LucaOne** (`paper-notes/s42256-025-01044-4.md`) sits in a different niche — joint DNA/RNA/protein modelling with a
unified embedding space. Where LucaOne aims at cross-modality biological reasoning, AlphaGenome aims specifically at the
regulatory-variant-effect leaf of that tree. They could compose: LucaOne for "what does this gene do, what protein does
it make"; AlphaGenome for "what does this variant do to that gene's regulation."

**Specialist baselines named in the paper** (Borzoi, Enformer, SpliceAI, Pangolin, ChromBPNet, Orca, ENCODE–rE2G) are
all candidate fallback models for Linus's variant-scoring pipeline, particularly if AlphaGenome turns out hard to run
locally on M1 Max. ChromBPNet and SpliceAI are smaller specialists with mature open releases.

**AlphaFold-lineage architectural echoes**: U-Net + transformer hybrid, distillation from teacher ensembles into a
single deployable student, careful ablations published alongside the result. The 64-teacher → 1-student distillation
pattern is a generally useful technique potentially applicable to Linus's own future fine-tuned models.

**KnowledgeBase indexing of regulatory genomics**: AlphaGenome's structured biological annotations (cell types, tissues,
modalities, ENCODE assay accessions, JASPAR motif IDs) and ISM outputs (variant → disrupted/created motif → TF) are
natural join keys into a regulatory-genomics knowledge graph. Connecting "papers about TAL1 super-enhancers" to
"AlphaGenome's TAL1 oncogenic-variant scores in CD34+ CMP" is the kind of join that justifies the knowledge-graph layer.

## Open questions for Dan

1. **Local deployability check (Phase 1 spike)**: can the released distilled student run on M1 Max via MPS or MLX, and
   what's the per-variant inference latency? The paper quotes <1 s on H100. If we can hit 10–60 s on M1 Max that's
   operationally fine; if we OOM or hit 10+ minutes, the realistic paths are the non-commercial API or specialist
   fallbacks (ChromBPNet, SpliceAI, Borzoi). Worth a one-day clone-and-run experiment before any further commitment.

4. **Benchmark mirroring (Phase 4)**: the variant evaluation datasets in the AlphaGenome research repo (fine-mapped GTEx
   eQTLs, ENCODE–rE2G CRISPRi, CAGI5 MPRA, ClinVar splicing) are landmark sets that generalize beyond AlphaGenome
   itself. Should `benchmarks/dan_tasks/genomics/` adopt these as a starting variant-effect benchmark suite with
   AlphaGenome predictions as a reproducible reference baseline?

5. **Fine-tuning realism (Phase 6)**: can we LoRA-fine-tune the released student on a Dan-specific perturbational
   dataset within 32 GB, or is the realistic scope just ensembling its outputs with a downstream classifier? Parameter
   count isn't cleanly quoted but the 8-TPU-shard training and 1 Mb context suggest substantial size — the answer here
   gates the Phase 6 plan for this model class.

6. **Mechanism-as-predicate KnowledgeBase experiment**: AlphaGenome's ISM outputs make mechanism a structured predicate
   (variant → motif → TF), not just free text. Worth a small experiment to index a chunk of regulatory genomics papers
   with mechanism-aware predicates derived from AlphaGenome runs on the variants they discuss? If it works, that's a
   flagship demo of why Linus + KnowledgeBase is more than the sum of its parts.
