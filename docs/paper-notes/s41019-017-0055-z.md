# Keyphrase Extraction Using Knowledge Graphs

## TL;DR

This paper proposes KGRank, an unsupervised keyphrase extraction method that replaces the standard word co-occurrence
graph with a semantically enriched keyterm graph built from DBpedia. By linking noun-phrase keyterms to knowledge graph
entities and constructing an h-hop subgraph capturing latent semantic relations, then ranking with Personalized
PageRank, **KGRank outperforms all seven baseline methods on the DUC2001 benchmark across precision, recall, and
F-measure**, with best F-measure around 0.33 at K=10. The key insight is that two terms can be semantically related even
when they never co-occur within a fixed window — a failure mode of TextRank and ExpandRank that knowledge graph
connectivity sidesteps entirely.

## The problem (in plain language)

Keyphrases are the handful of phrases that capture what a document is about. Extracting them automatically is useful for
search, clustering, summarization, and — directly relevant to Linus — indexing a knowledge base so that retrieval
actually surfaces the right papers. The two dominant unsupervised approaches each have a structural blind spot.
Co-occurrence methods (TextRank, ExpandRank, SingleRank) build a graph of words that appear near each other in a sliding
window; two semantically related terms that happen to be far apart in the text get no connecting edge. Statistic-based
clustering methods (SC, TPR) pull in external corpora like Wikipedia to enrich similarity signals, but the most common
surface meaning of a word in Wikipedia often differs from its meaning in the target document — "house" in a political
news article means something different from "House Music," and the external statistics cannot easily disambiguate.

## What they propose / study

KGRank is a five-stage pipeline, all operating on a single document without requiring labeled training data.

First, keyterms are extracted — noun words and named entities only (no adjectives), identified via Stanford CoreNLP.
Adjectives are deliberately excluded; a human annotator study confirmed that 70% agreement on keyphrases holds when
phrases are stripped to nouns, versus only 44% for adjective-inclusive phrases.

Second, keyterms are clustered by semantic similarity using Word2Vec embeddings (Google Word2Vec) and one of three
algorithms — K-Means, spectral clustering, or affinity propagation. The cluster count r corresponds roughly to the
number of distinct topics in the document. Spectral clustering performs best in the ablation because it handles the
non-Euclidean geometry of the keyterm embedding space more gracefully than K-Means.

Third, each keyterm is linked to an entity in DBpedia. Because a surface form like "Philadelphia" maps to multiple
candidate entities, the system computes a prior probability from Wikipedia anchor co-occurrence statistics and combines
it with cosine similarity between the entity's DBpedia abstract and the input document. The entity with the highest
combined score is selected — effectively a lightweight word sense disambiguation step.

Fourth, for each cluster, an h-hop keyterm graph is built by extracting all paths of length ≤ h between anchor nodes in
DBpedia (h=2 in experiments given DBpedia's large vertex degree), then filtering expanded (intermediate) nodes whose
Word2Vec-measured semantic relatedness to the full anchor set falls below a threshold τ. This produces a compact,
document-specific subgraph that captures latent relations — for example, "disaster" and "Hurricane Andrew" are connected
through their DBpedia entity paths even though they never appear within a co-occurrence window of 10 in the example
document.

Fifth, all per-cluster keyterm graphs are merged into a document keyterm graph. Personalized PageRank (PPR) is applied,
with jump probability proportional to term frequency so that important terms get higher base scores. Edge weights are
derived from predicate frequency via an IDF-style formula: rare predicates in the knowledge graph carry higher weight,
since a specific semantic relation is more informative than a ubiquitous one. Final phrase scores combine PPR score,
phrase frequency, and inverse first-occurrence position.

## Key results

On DUC2001 (308 news articles, ~700 words each, ~10 manually labeled keyphrases per article), KGRank is compared against
SingleRank, ExpandRank, SCCooccurrence, SCWiki, TF-IDF, and PTF-POS at K = 5, 10, 15, 20. **At K=10, KGRank achieves
precision ~0.30, recall ~0.40, and F-measure ~0.33**, besting all baselines. PTF-POS (which also uses phrase frequency
and first-occurrence position but without the knowledge graph) is the closest competitor, confirming that those two
phrase features are highly valuable independently of the graph structure. SCCooccurrence consistently beats SCWiki,
suggesting that within-document statistical information is more reliable than external corpus statistics for
single-document extraction. Among clustering algorithms, spectral clustering (SCKGRank) slightly edges out K-Means and
affinity propagation on F-measure. Edge weighting (EWKGRank) offers small but consistent gains in recall over unweighted
KGRank. The ablation on noun phrases versus adjective noun phrases (Table 5) shows a clear win for pure noun phrases:
precision 0.205 vs. 0.165, F-measure 0.244 vs. 0.184.

## What's reusable in Linus

**Phase 3 (KB/Parallel Agents) — direct.** The KnowledgeBase submodule is exactly the use case this paper targets: a
corpus of scientific papers that needs to be indexed so that retrieval actually surfaces the right document. KGRank's
pipeline — extract noun-phrase keyterms, embed with Word2Vec or a modern sentence encoder, cluster by topic, link to a
knowledge graph, rank with PPR — could be applied to each paper during ingestion to generate high-quality index terms
that go beyond TF-IDF bag-of-words. Dan's corpus covers biochemistry, genomics, environmental science, and ML; a
domain-appropriate knowledge graph (UniProt, Gene Ontology, ChEBI, or even a subset of DBpedia filtered to biology)
could replace the general DBpedia used here.

**Phase 2 (MVP) — indirect.** Even without a full KG integration, the paper's observation that spectral clustering on
Word2Vec keyterm embeddings meaningfully improves topic coverage suggests a lightweight enhancement to whatever
chunking/indexing strategy Linus adopts: cluster candidate keyterms before selecting index entries rather than simply
ranking them.

**Phase 6 (Fine-tuning) — informative.** The entity-linking disambiguation step (prior probability from co-occurrence +
cosine similarity against document context) is a clean, interpretable pattern for grounding ambiguous terms to canonical
identifiers, which will matter when Linus tries to link fine-tuned knowledge to structured references like PubMed or
UniProt IDs.

The Python preprocessing code and sklearn clustering modules (used verbatim in the paper) are directly compatible with
the Linus conda environment. Stanford CoreNLP can be replaced with spaCy's noun-chunk extractor for a lighter
dependency.

## What's NOT applicable

KGRank is designed for single short-to-medium documents (~700 words of news). Performance on longer scientific papers,
which have deeper nested terminology and domain-specific compound nouns absent from DBpedia, is untested. The use of
DBpedia as the knowledge graph is a significant constraint for scientific domains: most biochemistry and genomics
entities are poorly represented there relative to domain ontologies like GO or MeSH. The paper benchmarks only on
DUC2001 (news), so generalization claims to scientific literature must be treated as hypotheses, not findings. The
computational cost of BFS path extraction over DBpedia at h=2 is non-trivial at scale; the paper does not report
runtime, and for a corpus of thousands of papers this would require either a local DBpedia mirror or a domain-specific
KG substitute. Finally, the method is fully unsupervised and optimized for recall of manually labeled keyphrases — it
will not necessarily outperform a fine-tuned neural tagger on in-domain scientific text.

## Connections

The knowledge graph foundation here connects directly to **2003.02320v6** (Knowledge Graphs survey) — the entity-linking
and h-hop subgraph construction steps are concrete instantiations of the KG traversal patterns that survey describes.
The Word2Vec similarity used for clustering and semantic relatedness filtering is an earlier-generation analogue of the
sentence embedding approaches in **2408.08073v2** (sentence embeddings); a modern reimplementation of KGRank would
almost certainly substitute a sentence transformer for Word2Vec and see improved disambiguation. The PPR ranking on a
graph whose edge semantics matter also resonates with the dimensionality concerns in **2401.00422v3** (curse of
dimensionality): the paper's finding that spectral clustering beats K-Means is consistent with K-Means being sensitive
to Euclidean geometry in high-dimensional embedding spaces.

## Open questions for Dan

1. Dan's KnowledgeBase holds biochemistry and genomics papers. Which controlled vocabulary — Gene Ontology, MeSH, ChEBI,
   UniProt keywords — would serve as the most practical KGRank-style knowledge graph for his domain, and how would the
   h-hop path extraction behave on GO's DAG structure versus DBpedia's general graph?

2. The paper excludes adjectives from keyterms deliberately, citing low annotator agreement. In scientific writing,
   adjectives frequently carry domain meaning ("oxidative stress," "transcriptional regulation"). Does the noun-only
   heuristic hold for scientific text, or does it discard too much signal?

3. The entity-linking disambiguation step uses document-level cosine similarity between a DBpedia abstract and the full
   document. For long papers with multiple distinct methods sections, would section-level disambiguation (matching
   entity abstracts against the methods or results section independently) outperform whole-document matching?

4. KGRank is fully unsupervised and produces keyphrases that cover document topics. For Linus's RAG use case, the goal
   is slightly different: keyphrases should maximize retrieval recall for queries Dan is likely to ask, not just topic
   coverage. Is there a way to incorporate Dan's past query patterns as a supervision signal for the PPR jump
   probabilities?

5. The paper uses Stanford CoreNLP for named entity recognition and noun phrase chunking. For Apple Silicon without JVM
   overhead, spaCy's `en_core_sci_lg` (scispaCy) model is a drop-in replacement tuned for biomedical text. Is scispaCy's
   NER quality sufficient for accurate entity boundary detection in genomics papers, or would a domain-specific
   fine-tune be needed?
