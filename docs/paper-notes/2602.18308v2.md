# JPmHC: Dynamical Isometry via Orthogonal Hyper-Connections

## TL;DR

A successor to **Hyper-Connections (HC)** — the 2024 trick that generalizes residual streams by splitting the hidden
state into `n` parallel streams and mixing them via a small `n × n` matrix. The paper's predecessor (mHC) constrained
that mixer to **doubly-stochastic** (Birkhoff polytope) matrices via Sinkhorn iteration, which caused **vanishing
gradients** at depth — the paper diagnoses this analytically using operator-valued free probability, names it _spectral
collapse_, and proposes replacing the Birkhoff constraint with the **orthogonal group `O(n)` via the Cayley transform**
on the Stiefel manifold. Validated on **ARC-AGI-1** with a tiny 7M-parameter recursive transformer: Cayley beats
Sinkhorn 31.4% → 27.9% exact match, 40.5% → 36.5% pass@1, and converges in 40–58% of the training budget.

## The problem (in plain language)

Residual connections (`x_out = x + F(x)`) are the workhorse of modern deep learning — they're what made it possible to
train networks thousands of layers deep without gradients exploding or vanishing. Hyper-Connections generalize this:
instead of one "stream" carrying the residual, you have `n` parallel streams, mixed at every layer by a small `n × n`
matrix `H`. The classic result for HC was that on MoE models like DeepSeek-27B, this halves the training tokens needed
and adds +7 points on benchmarks like BBH and GSM8K.

But the matrix `H` matters enormously. If it's unconstrained, training diverges (signal gains of 3000× at 27B were
observed). The previous fix (mHC) constrained `H` to be **doubly stochastic** — every row and column sums to 1 — by
projecting onto the Birkhoff polytope using Sinkhorn iteration. That bounds the operator norm at 1 (no explosion), but
it doesn't bound the _minimum_ singular values. After 50 layers of composition, most of the singular values drift toward
zero — the network's effective capacity collapses to a thin slice of its parameter count. The paper calls this
**spectral stalling**: gradients pour into directions that don't move the function, training stops making progress.

The biology analogue: think of a long signaling cascade where each step's gain matrix has subunit-norm. By the 50th
step, all but a few information dimensions have decayed below detection threshold. The cascade _runs_, but most of what
it could have transmitted is gone.

## What they propose

**Replace the Birkhoff constraint with the orthogonal group `O(n)`**. Orthogonal matrices have all singular values
exactly 1, so they preserve the full spectrum across composition. Implementation:

**Cayley parametrization**: an orthogonal `n × n` matrix can be written as `(I − S)(I + S)⁻¹` where `S` is a small
skew-symmetric matrix. The Cayley iteration takes a starting point and converges quadratically; with `s = 2` iterations
it produces an essentially-exact orthogonal projection. Each application is `~256 FLOPs/module` for n=4 (vs `576` for
Sinkhorn).

**Three JPmHC variants** evaluated:

- **Sinkhorn** (Birkhoff polytope, baseline) — 576 FLOPs/module, contractive, the prior art.
- **Cayley** (Stiefel manifold = orthogonal group) — 256 FLOPs/module, norm-preserving, the recommended variant.
- **Grassmann** (rank-p orthogonal projector `U Uᵀ`) — even cheaper (72 FLOPs/module), middle-ground expressivity,
  preliminary results.

**Theoretical contributions** (the half of the paper aimed at researchers, not practitioners):

- A free-probability analysis predicting Jacobian spectra of structured skip connections — including the operator-valued
  generalization (operator-valued Dyson pipeline) needed for the Kronecker structure of HC.
- An **implicit-differentiation backward pass** for fixed-point projections (Sinkhorn, Cayley) that reduces activation
  memory from `O(T)` (number of iterations) to `O(1)` and eliminates `128K` autograd nodes that were causing
  distributed-training synchronization stalls.

## Key results

On **ARC-AGI-1** (1000 tasks, exact-match all-or-nothing scoring; 7M-parameter Tiny Recursive Model with 12-fold
weight-tied recursion — a deliberately stringent test of mixer composition):

- **Exact accuracy**: Cayley **31.4%** vs Sinkhorn 27.9% (1.13×).
- **Pass@1**: Cayley **40.5%** vs Sinkhorn 36.5% (1.11×).
- **Pass@1000**: Cayley **62.7%** vs Sinkhorn 56.1% (1.12×).
- **Eval LM loss**: Cayley **0.643** vs Sinkhorn 0.817 (1.27× lower loss).
- **Convergence**: Cayley reaches Sinkhorn's best pass@1 at **40% of the budget**.
- **FLOPs**: Cayley uses 2.25× fewer FLOPs per module.
- **Gradient norms**: Sinkhorn's gradient norms are ~4× larger than Cayley's despite worse loss reduction — direct
  empirical evidence of "spectral stalling" (gradient energy poured into low-singular-value directions that don't move
  the function).
- **Grassmann (preliminary, 111K steps vs 500K+ for the others)**: 12.8% exact match, 27.5% pass@1 — tracking between
  Cayley and Sinkhorn, consistent with theory.

Scale: small (7M params) but rigorous. The TRM architecture's 12-fold weight-tied recursion intentionally exposes mixer
composition to amplify any spectral pathology — successful technique on TRM should compose cleanly to bigger models.

## What's reusable in Linus

**Indirect (Phase 6 architecture):** This is the "right way" to do parallel residual streams as of early 2026. If Linus
ever trains, fine-tunes, or distills a transformer with structured residual streams (any HC-derived architecture,
including future BitNet-MoE variants), the Cayley constraint should replace the Sinkhorn one by default. Adoption
depends on whether MLX has a Cayley parametrization layer ready or whether someone has to write one.

**Indirect (training stability principles):** The paper's framing — "operator norm ≤ 1 is necessary but not sufficient;
you need the full singular spectrum near 1, not just the maximum" — is a transferable rule for any deep-stack
architecture. Worth holding in mind whenever a Linus model design depends on signal propagating through many composed
layers.

**Indirect (implicit-differentiation pattern):** The `O(T) → O(1)` activation memory trick for fixed-point operators
applies generically to any iterative procedure inside a backward pass. If Linus ever wires up RAG iteration, plan-act
loops, or any other fixed-point structure into a differentiable path (unlikely near-term, possible later), this is the
technique.

**Direct (small model, ARC-AGI test bed):** The TRM architecture this paper uses (7M params, ACT halting, 12-fold
recursion) is _small enough to run on M1 Max_, possibly even to _fine-tune_. ARC-AGI-1 itself is small (1000 tasks). A
Phase 1 spike to reproduce the basic JPmHC ARC-AGI experiment in MLX would be ambitious but not absurd, and would be the
kind of "evidence beats intuition" exercise CLAUDE.md endorses.

## What's NOT applicable

- **Production-scale claim is not validated**. All experiments are at 7M parameters. The HC papers cited (Zhu et al.
  2024, Xie et al. 2025) showed gains at 27B; whether JPmHC generalizes there is "future work."
- **Pre/post architecture confound**: Cayley uses LayerNorm + softmax-constrained mappings; Sinkhorn uses RMSNorm +
  sigmoid. Some of the gap could be from those secondary differences, not just the manifold choice.
- **Distributed-training infrastructure focus**: a major contribution is fixing DDP synchronization stalls that arise
  when the Sinkhorn iteration's autograd graph has 128K nodes. Linus is single-machine; this contribution doesn't apply.
- **8× B200 GPU training** for the experiments — not Linus territory for from-scratch training.
- **The "hypercube" word in this paper has a different meaning** than in the [Horiike-Fujishiro
  paper](Horiike-Orthogonal projections of hypercubes-2025-Physical Review E copy.md). HC's "hyper-" refers to the
  parallel-streams structure, not high-dimensional binary state spaces.
