# QuantAgent: Seeking Holy Grail in Trading by Self-Improving Large Language Model

## TL;DR
QuantAgent proposes a **two-loop self-improving agent framework** for domains where building a knowledge base by hand is impractical. The **inner loop** is a writer/judge dialog grounded in a domain knowledge base (KB): the writer drafts an answer using KB-retrieved hints, the judge scores it using KB-retrieved evaluation hints, the score+critique go back into a shared context buffer, and the loop repeats until a threshold is met or T iterations are spent. The **outer loop** runs the inner loop's final answer against the *real* environment (here, financial backtesting), takes the real-world score and review, and folds them back into the KB so the next iteration starts with richer evidence. The authors give a Bayesian-regret analysis (modeled as an MDP, leaning on Liu et al. 2023 and Jin et al. 2021's pessimistic offline-RL bound) showing the combined regret is sublinear in KT under linear-MDP-style assumptions. The instantiation is QuantAgent: a financial alpha-mining agent built on GPT-4 (gpt-4-0125-preview) over 500 Chinese A-share stocks for 2023, where backtesting Information Coefficient (IC) and Sharpe serve as the real-world feedback. Empirically, alpha quality and predictive power both improve with outer-loop iterations; ablations show inner and outer loops each contribute. The paper is interesting to Linus less for its trading specifics and more as a **clean reference architecture for self-improving Workers whose KB grows from real-world feedback**.

## The problem (in plain language)
In specialized domains (finance, biochemistry, genomics) the experience that lets a human expert do good work isn't sitting in any one document — it's accreted over years of running experiments, seeing what failed, and building a private notebook of patterns that worked. If you want an LLM agent to operate in such a domain, you have two stock options. You can fine-tune on a curated corpus, which costs labor and capital and freezes the knowledge at training time. Or you can do RAG against an external database, which only works if the database already exists. For "design a profitable trading signal" or "design a primer pair that amplifies cleanly across these strains", neither answer is satisfying: there is no pre-existing corpus of unambiguously correct examples.

The paper's framing question is: can the agent **build its own domain KB by repeatedly trying things in the real world and writing down what happened**? And can we ground that loop in something stronger than "looked good in the demo" — specifically, a regret bound that says the policy converges to optimal as the KB grows?

## What they propose
The architecture is two nested loops sharing a knowledge base.

**Inner loop (Algorithm 1).** Two LLM agents, **writer** and **judge**, with a **shared context buffer** that starts as just the user's problem. Each iteration: the writer queries the KB (embedding-based retrieval) for hints relevant to its current context and drafts an answer; the judge queries the KB for evaluation hints, reviews the writer's draft, and emits a (score, advice) pair; the review is appended to the shared buffer. Loop until score ≥ β or t = T. The KB here functions as the *simulated environment*: cheap, fast, partial. The buffer is essentially a per-task scratchpad; the writer/judge dialog is iterative refinement under retrieval grounding.

**Outer loop (Algorithm 2).** Run the inner loop to produce a final answer, then submit that answer to the *real* environment (in QuantAgent: backtest the alpha against actual market data and compute IC, Sharpe, validity counts). Take the real-world feedback — score plus qualitative review from a sophisticated reviewer LLM — and update the KB. Sanity checks during the update keep the KB diverse: both successes and failures get stored, with metadata. Repeat for K iterations.

**The key conceptual move** is the asymmetry between the inner judge and the outer reviewer. The judge is fast, cheap, and approximate (LLM evaluating against a partial KB). The reviewer is slow, expensive, and high-fidelity (real backtest, or an LLM working with full ground-truth tooling). As outer-loop iterations accumulate, the KB becomes a better simulator of the real environment, and the inner-loop judge converges toward the outer-loop reviewer.

**The analysis** formalizes the inner loop as an MDP where state = information in the context buffer, action = writer's answer or KB query, transition = KB response or judge review, reward = judge score. Two assumptions matter: (4.1) the LLM does implicit Bayesian inference over the environment parameter during in-context inference (citing Xie et al. 2021), and (4.4) the optimal policy on the simulated KB-environment can be obtained via pessimistic value iteration (Jin et al. 2021). Lemma 4.3 gives the inner-loop regret as sublinear in T. Lemma 4.5 bounds outer-loop suboptimality by KB coverage uncertainty. Theorem 4.6 stitches them: total regret R(TK) is sublinear in KT.

**Cost analysis.** Per outer iteration the inner loop is O(T²H) tokens with planning horizon H (the T² comes from the cumulative buffer growing each round). Training over K outer iterations is O(KT²H) tokens, O(KTH) wall time. At inference (no outer loop), it's O(T²H) tokens, O(HT) time. The T² is the main scaling pain.

**QuantAgent instantiation.** 500 Chinese A-share stocks, 2023 daily OHLCV data; trading ideas sampled from another LLM; alphas implemented as Python functions matching a fixed schema (similar to FunSearch, Romera-Paredes et al. 2023). The KB stores each alpha with its code, the trading idea behind it, backtest metrics, and reviewer commentary. Foundation model: gpt-4-0125-preview throughout.

## Key results
Three pieces of evidence are reported.

**Self-improvement (Figure 5).** As the agent accumulates alphas across outer iterations, an XGBoost regression trained on the KB-as-features shows monotonically decreasing MSE on future-return prediction. The KB itself becomes a better feature set as it grows.

**Single-alpha quality trends (Figure 3).** Per-alpha entry-valid-ratio, IC, return, and Sharpe all trend upward across iterations, though the per-alpha trends are noisy.

**Inner+outer ablation (Figure 4).** Pairwise win-rate matrices comparing alphas from the same trading idea generated under four conditions: full system, outer-only, inner-only, neither. The full system's win-rates concentrate in the upper-right (later iterations beat earlier), the inner-only and outer-only ablations show weaker patterns, and the neither condition shows no improvement structure. The inner and outer loops both contribute and the effect is multiplicative rather than additive.

The paper does **not** publish absolute IC or Sharpe headline numbers in the body, and it does not run on out-of-sample years. The contribution is methodological more than benchmark-topping.

## What's reusable in Linus
**Phase 3 (Knowledge & Parallel Agents) — writer/judge inner loop with KB grounding.** Linus' agent spawner can directly adopt the writer/judge pattern as a **role-pair primitive**. A "writer" Worker drafts; a "judge" Worker scores against domain-specific criteria; both retrieve from a shared KB; both write to a shared context buffer that serves as the per-task scratchpad memory tier (see [memory synthesis](../memory-synthesis.md) and the scratchpad component in [Memory Architecture](../memory-architecture.md)). This is a cleaner pattern than free-form multi-agent debate for tasks where evaluation is cheap and well-defined, and it composes naturally with the role-specialization template from [TradingAgents (2412.20138v7)](2412.20138v7.md).

**Phase 3 — KB-grows-from-real-feedback as a Linus design pattern.** The outer loop is the architecturally interesting half. Most agentic systems have static KBs; QuantAgent's KB is *append-only experiential memory* keyed by (task, attempt, real-world-outcome, review). For Linus this maps onto the **episodic memory tier**: each Worker run produces an episode (problem, attempt, outcome, critique) that gets stored, then later Workers retrieve relevant past episodes when starting similar tasks. This is the same shape as Voyager's skill library but with explicit reviewer-generated metadata for retrieval ranking.

**Phase 3 — judge/reviewer asymmetry as a Maestro/Worker discipline.** The cheap-fast-approximate judge / expensive-slow-truthful reviewer split maps cleanly onto Linus' Maestro/Worker separation. Local Workers (Qwen2.5-Coder, BitNet, future fine-tuned Linus) play the inner-loop judge role: cheap, available, partial. Hosted Claude or a deterministic external check (compiler, test suite, KnowledgeBase ground-truth lookup) plays the outer-loop reviewer role: scarce, slow, high-fidelity. The outer-loop frequency is the budget knob.

**Phase 7 (Skills & Autonomy) — the pattern is domain-portable.** The authors note healthcare and logistics as transfer targets; for Dan, the natural targets are **bioinformatics pipeline design** (KB of pipeline configs + their actual run outcomes on KnowledgeBase corpora), **literature triage and synthesis** (KB of paper-notes + their downstream usefulness), and **code refactor patterns** (KB of refactors + their test/lint outcomes). Each is a domain where ground-truth feedback is programmatic but a static KB doesn't exist.

**Indirect, Phase 1 (Recon & Baselines) — Bayesian-regret framing for evaluation.** The MDP formalization gives a vocabulary for thinking about benchmark design: what is the action space, what is the reward, where does the simulated/real-environment gap close? When designing `benchmarks/dan_tasks/` it's worth asking which tasks have programmatic real-world feedback (so an outer loop is meaningful) versus which only have human-judge feedback (so the loop collapses to inner-only iterative refinement). The taxonomy matters for which tasks Linus can self-improve on later.

**Indirect — context buffer caveat.** The O(T²H) token cost per inner loop is real and grows fast. The shared context buffer grows monotonically; this is the same problem the [memory synthesis](../memory-synthesis.md) anticipates. Linus' implementation should plan for buffer compression — Coconut-style latent thoughts ([2412.06769v3](2412.06769v3.md)), TTT scratchpad rollups ([2411.07279v2](2411.07279v2.md)), or summarization checkpoints — rather than naively concatenating every turn.

## What's NOT applicable
The financial domain is the *example*, not the contribution; the absolute trading numbers should not be transplanted into any decision Linus makes. Backtest results from Q1–Q4 2023 on Chinese A-shares with 500 names tell us nothing about the framework's transferability outside that distribution, and the paper doesn't claim otherwise.

The system runs on GPT-4 (gpt-4-0125-preview) throughout. Both writer and judge are large hosted models with strong instruction-following and JSON-mode reliability. Whether a 7B local Worker (Mistral-7B, Qwen2.5-Coder-7B, BitNet-derived) can play either role with comparable inner-loop convergence is an empirical question — and a particularly important one for Linus, where the whole point is that Workers are local. Expect the inner loop to need more iterations T to compensate for weaker per-turn judgment.

The Bayesian-regret theorem leans on two strong assumptions: (4.1) implicit Bayesian inference during in-context inference, and (4.4) PEVI-trainable optimal policies on the offline KB. The first is suggestive but not proven for arbitrary LLMs and arbitrary tasks; the second is a linear-MDP-class assumption that is unlikely to hold cleanly in messy real-world domains. The proofs are useful as design intuition (more KB coverage → tighter regret bound), not as performance guarantees.

The framework assumes a programmatic real-world reward signal (backtest score). For domains where the real-world signal is human judgment (writing quality, research direction), the outer loop becomes much more expensive and the convergence story degrades — Dan's Maestro time becomes the bottleneck the moment the outer loop fires.

The paper does no comparison with, e.g., simple iterative-refinement-only baselines on the same finance task, no ablation of judge model size vs. writer model size, and no analysis of KB pruning or staleness. All of those are open engineering questions Linus would have to answer before deploying this pattern at scale.

## Connections
The closest neighbor in the corpus is [TradingAgents (2412.20138v7)](2412.20138v7.md) — same finance-as-substrate framing, both papers' real value is architectural. TradingAgents gives the *role specialization and structured-vs-free-form communication* template; QuantAgent gives the *self-improvement-via-KB-update* loop. The two compose: a Linus agent firm could use TradingAgents-style role specialization for the writer/judge/reviewer assignments, and QuantAgent-style outer-loop KB updates for the firm's institutional memory. [Boiko (2304.05332v1)](2304.05332v1.md) is the chemistry-lab analog of self-improving agents, but with physical-world feedback rather than backtests; the same outer-loop pattern applies with much slower iteration cycles.

The inner loop is structurally a writer/critic refinement loop, the same family as Madaan et al. 2023 Self-Refine and Voyager (Wang et al. 2023a) — the paper explicitly notes both as special cases of its framework. For Linus' KnowledgeBase integration this maps onto the [Hogan KG survey (2003.02320v6.md)](2003.02320v6.md) and the [embeddings paper (2408.08073v2.md)](2408.08073v2.md): the KB retrieval step is exactly the "structured search over a KG/vector store with task-conditioned queries" problem those papers describe.

The outer-loop KB-update mechanism is functionally **episodic memory** in the [memory architecture](../memory-architecture.md): each (problem, answer, real-feedback) triple is an episode, retrieved later via embedding similarity to inform future writer/judge runs. This is the same shape as ARC-style task buffers ([2412.04604v2.md](2412.04604v2.md)) and minGRU-style streaming state ([2410.01201v3.md](2410.01201v3.md)), albeit at a much coarser temporal granularity.

The Bayesian-inference framing of in-context learning (Xie et al. 2021, leveraged in Assumption 4.1) is the same conceptual lens [Coconut (2412.06769v3.md)](2412.06769v3.md) and [TTT (2411.07279v2.md)](2411.07279v2.md) apply to latent-thought reasoning. The methodological evaluation argument from [Eval guide (2506.13023v1.md)](2506.13023v1.md) and the speed/throughput cautions from [2502.16721v1.md](2502.16721v1.md) both apply to deploying QuantAgent-style loops on local Workers — the O(T²H) inner-loop cost will manifest as wall-clock time, not just tokens, on a 32 GB M1 Max.

## Open questions for Dan
1. **Worker-as-judge feasibility.** Can a local 7B Worker (Qwen2.5-Coder, Mistral) play the inner-loop judge role with enough fidelity that the writer's score signal is informative, or does the judge need to be hosted Claude / a hardcoded checker (test suite, type checker, KnowledgeBase fact lookup)? A 30-item smoke-test on a code-refactor task would answer this cheaply.
2. **Episodic-memory key design.** If Linus adopts the outer-loop pattern, what is the key schema for KB entries? QuantAgent uses (trading idea, code, metrics, review). For Linus' use cases the natural keys differ per domain (paper-triage: query, retrieved set, downstream-usefulness; pipeline-design: input data shape, pipeline config, run outcome). Should the agent spawner expose a per-role KB schema as part of the role definition?
3. **Outer-loop trigger policy.** When does the outer loop actually fire? Every Worker run? Only on flagged successes/failures? On a sample? The cost asymmetry (outer loop fires the expensive reviewer) means the policy matters a lot for Maestro budget, and the paper doesn't address this — they fire the outer loop every iteration.
4. **Bioinformatics analog as Phase 3 demo.** The cleanest Linus analog of QuantAgent is a self-improving primer-design or pipeline-tuning agent over the KnowledgeBase corpus, where backtest-equivalent feedback is "did the pipeline run cleanly and produce expected outputs on a held-out genome." Worth scoping as a Phase 3 deliverable, or premature?
5. **Buffer compression policy.** The O(T²H) inner-loop token cost is the immediate practical scaling concern. Should the writer/judge buffer get summarized every N turns (cheap, lossy), compressed via Coconut-style latent thoughts (research-grade, novel), or simply truncated with KB retrieval re-grounding each turn (simple, may regress)? The choice changes how aggressive T can be.
