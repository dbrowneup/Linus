# Geometry-Preserving Neural Architectures on Manifolds with Boundary

## TL;DR

A unified theoretical framework for **geometry-aware neural architectures** whose layers, _by construction_, keep
predictions on a prescribed constraint set `M ⊂ ℝᵈ` — including manifolds with boundary, not just smooth closed
manifolds or Lie groups. The construction views deep networks as discretizations of **projected dynamical systems**: an
ambient-space update is interleaved with a constraint-preserving step (analytic projection, Riemannian exponential map,
or a learned projection inferred via flow matching) back onto `M`. The framework yields two architecture families —
**Intermediate Augmented Architectures (IAA)** that enforce the constraint at every layer, and **Final Augmented
Architectures (FAA)** that enforce it only at the output — and proves **universal approximation** for both, including
for **constrained neural ODEs** (the first such result the authors are aware of that uses projected ODEs to guarantee
invariance of `M` under the learned dynamics). When `M` is an unknown constraint set, they learn the projection by
fitting a flow-matching velocity field whose small-time backward flow recovers the metric projection up to `O(t^(1/2))`
error — the first instance, to the authors' knowledge, of **diffusion / flow matching used to enforce geometric
constraints**. Empirical study on five constraint sets — the sphere `S²`, the closed unit disk in `ℝ²`, the special
orthogonal group `SO(3)`, Cucker–Smale consensus dynamics on `SO(3)¹⁰`, and a real ProteinNet tertiary-structure dataset
on `SE(3)` — shows that projected variants achieve a favorable Pareto tradeoff (low test MSE _and_ low
distance-to-manifold), while exponential variants reduce off-manifold drift relative to the unconstrained ResNet
baseline. **For Linus, this paper is a watch-the-field sibling to [JPmHC (Sengupta et al., 2026)](2602.18308v2.md), not
a near-term integration target** — the synthesis home is `infra-foundations-synthesis.md`'s "manifold ML — a
watch-the-field footnote" section, and almost every reusable thread sits in Phase 6+ research-direction territory.

## The problem (in plain language)

Many learning problems impose **hard geometric constraints** on their outputs. Protein backbone modeling lives on
`SO(3)` (rotation group); covariance matrices live on the symmetric-positive-definite cone with fixed trace;
visual-inertial pose estimation outputs live on `SE(3)`; orientations live on `S²`. Standard ML architectures — MLPs,
transformers, ResNets — provide **no guarantee** that their outputs (or their intermediate representations) respect
these constraints. The conventional fix is a **post-hoc projection**: produce something in the ambient space `ℝᵈ`, then
project onto `M` at the end. This works but is unprincipled — there is no theory connecting the expressivity of the
unconstrained network to the expressivity of the projected one, and intermediate representations may wander far
off-manifold in ways that break downstream geometric reasoning.

The biology analogue is direct. Imagine modeling a protein conformational trajectory: the backbone's residue frames live
on `SE(3)` at every timestep. A vanilla LSTM that "kind of" produces rotation-like outputs requires a projection step to
be physically meaningful, but nothing in its training prevents the hidden state from drifting into rotation-impossible
regions where small numerical perturbations push the projection toward a discontinuity. You want an architecture where
_the geometry is enforced by the layer structure itself_, not patched on at the end.

Two gaps in the prior literature motivate the paper. First, **neural ODEs have not previously been studied in a form
that guarantees invariance of a prescribed set `M` (including manifolds with boundary) under the learned dynamics**.
Second, existing constraint-preserving architectures either (a) target specialized geometric operations like Lie-group
exponential maps that work only when `M` is a smooth Lie group (Katsman et al., 2023; Elamvazhuthi et al., 2023), or (b)
use projection / repair mechanisms tailored to restricted constraint families that have closed-form or convex-analytic
projections (HardNet, OptNet, DC3). No unified framework supports both analytic and data-driven constraint-preserving
updates in the same architectural language.

## What they propose

A **unified class of geometry-preserving architectures** built on the perspective that deep networks are discretizations
of dynamical systems, where the dynamical system is a **projected ODE** that keeps `M` invariant by construction. Three
architectural variants and one core algorithmic novelty:

**Projected Neural ODEs (the core machinery).** Replace the standard neural ODE `ẋ = f_θ(t, x)` with a projected variant
`ẏ = P_{T_M(y)} f_θ(t, y)`, where `P_{T_M(y)}` is the orthogonal projection onto the tangent cone of `M` at the current
state. By construction, the solution remains in `M` for all time. Theorem 2.2 establishes universal approximation: any
sufficiently regular target end-point map `Φ_F : M → M` (where `F` is a `ℒ`-Lipschitz vector field on a uniformly
prox-regular `M`) can be approximated arbitrarily well by a projected neural ODE with parameter `θ`. The error scales as
`(δ/ℒ_α)(e^{ℒ_α T} − 1)` where `δ` is the sup-norm distance between the target and learned vector fields and `α` is the
reach of `M`.

**Intermediate Augmented Architectures (IAA).** Enforce the constraint at **every layer** by interleaving the
ambient-space update with a constraint-preserving step. Three IAA flavors:

- **Projected IAA**: `h^{ℓ+1} = h^ℓ + Δt · P_M(f_θ(ℓ, h^ℓ))` — uses the closed-form metric projection `P_M`. Works
  whenever `P_M` has a closed form (e.g., `S²` embedded in `ℝ³`, the closed disk).
- **Exponential IAA**: `h^{ℓ+1} = exp_{h^ℓ}(Δt · f_θ(ℓ, h^{ℓ-1}))` — uses the **Riemannian exponential map** along a
  tangent-vector update. Avoids ambient-space excursions entirely.
- **Lie-group IAA (specialization)**: when `M` is a matrix Lie group `G`, the network directly outputs **Lie-algebra
  coordinates** in a fixed basis `{E_1, ..., E_n}` of `g = T_eG`, with the update
  `g^{ℓ+1} = exp_e(Δt · Σ f̂_θ(ℓ, g^ℓ)_i E_i) g^ℓ`. This avoids the explicit tangent-space projection at every layer —
  significantly simplifying implementation versus prior Lie-group neural ODEs.
- **Flow IAA**: when `M` is unknown / no closed-form `P_M` exists, replace `P_M` with a learned approximation `P̂_M`
  obtained from a flow-matching velocity field (see "Learning projections via flow matching" below).

**Final Augmented Architectures (FAA).** Enforce the constraint **only at the output layer** via a single projection or
exponential step: `f̃_θ = P_M ∘ f_θ` (Projected FAA), or `f̃_θ = exp_p ∘ f_θ` for some fixed base point `p ∈ M`
(Exponential FAA). Cheaper than IAA — intermediate representations may drift — but Theorem 2.3 proves a separate
universal approximation result for prox-regular constraint sets (which can be non-convex and need not be smooth
manifolds in the strict sense, as long as the reach is positive). FAA results generalize HardNet (Min & Azizan, 2024),
which assumed the constraint was input-dependent affine inequalities.

**Learning projections via flow matching (the headline algorithmic contribution).** When `M` is unknown — only samples
from `M` are available — the projection map cannot be written down. The paper recovers `P_M` from data by: (i) treating
samples of `M` as a probability measure `μ`; (ii) fitting a flow-matching velocity field `v_θ(t, x)` to the
perturbed-sample distribution `Y_t = X + tv` with `v ~ N(0, I)`; (iii) integrating the **reverse-time** ODE
`ẋ = -v_θ(t, x(t))` from small `t` to recover an approximate projection `P̂_M`. Theorem 2.5 proves
`‖P_M(x) − [x + t∇_x log μ_t(x)]‖ ≤ C_K · O(t^{1/2})` uniformly on tubular neighborhoods of `M`, which makes the
score-induced map a valid data-based projection in the small-time limit. To the authors' knowledge, this is the **first
use of diffusion models to enforce constraints in neural network architectures** — a non-trivial inversion of the usual
flow-matching framing where diffusion is the generator, not the constraint-enforcer.

## Key results

The paper is structured as theory-first with a focused empirical study illustrating the theory's predictions.

**Theory:**

- **Theorem 2.2** (universal approximation for projected neural ODEs). On a uniformly prox-regular `M`, projected neural
  ODEs are universal approximators of end-point maps `Φ_F` for sufficiently regular `F`. Error scales with the sup-norm
  distance between target and learned vector fields, modulated by the Lipschitz constant and the reach of `M`.
- **Theorem 2.3** (universal approximation for projected FAA). For prox-regular `M`, the projected FAA satisfies
  `‖F(x) − P_M(f_θ(x))‖ ≤ 2ε` whenever `f_θ` is `ε`-close to the target `F` and the image of `f_θ` lies within the reach
  of `M`. Generalizes prior HardNet-style results to non-convex / non-affine constraint sets.
- **Theorem 2.4** (universal approximation for exponential FAA). On geodesically complete finite-dimensional Riemannian
  manifolds, any continuous `F : Ω → M` admits a measurable lift `f : Ω → T_pM` with bounded range such that
  `F = exp_p ∘ f`. With `f_θ` `δ`-close to `f` in `L²_μ`, the composition `exp_p ∘ f_θ` recovers `F` up to a Lipschitz
  factor times `δ` in `L²`-distance on `M`.
- **Theorem 2.5** (small-time heat-kernel projection). The score-induced map `x ↦ x + t∇_x log μ_t(x)` recovers the
  metric projection `P_M(x)` with `O(t^{1/2})` error uniformly on tubular neighborhoods.

**Empirical study** (Table 1 in the paper, summarized):

Five datasets — sphere `S²`, closed unit disk in `ℝ²`, `SO(3)`, Cucker–Smale dynamics on `SO(3)¹⁰`, real ProteinNet
tertiary-structure dataset on `SE(3)`. Eight model classes — unconstrained `Regular ResNet`, `Proj IAA` / `Exp IAA` /
`Flow IAA`, probabilistic constrained baseline `Prob` (Kratsios et al., 2022b), `Proj FAA` / `Exp FAA` / `Flow FAA`.
Sweep over depths {4, 6, 8} and weight-decay {on, off}, 10000 epochs, AdamW with LR-on-plateau. Robustness probed by
Gaussian noise injection at `σ ∈ {0.2, ..., 1.5}`.

Headline observations from the discussion:

- **Projected variants display a favorable Pareto tradeoff**: simultaneously low test MSE and low distance-to-manifold.
  Other methods improve one metric but not both.
- **Exponential variants reduce off-manifold drift** relative to the regular ResNet baseline — confirming that intrinsic
  updates can keep intermediate representations near `M` even without explicit projection.
- **Methods that don't explicitly encode constraint-set knowledge** (regular, flow, probabilistic baselines) all perform
  similarly — without geometric structure the architectures behave indistinguishably.
- **Flow-matching projection (Flow IAA / Flow FAA) underperforms analytic projection** when an analytic projection is
  available. The likely cause is the `O(t^{1/2})` error term: as `t → 0` the projection becomes accurate but the flow
  integration becomes unstable; finite `t` gives stable integration with biased projection. This is the practical
  limitation of the flow-matching approach.
- **Projected IAA performs better than exponential IAA**, and the two are comparable in the FAA case. FAA enjoys a
  significant computational advantage over IAA (one projection per forward pass, not `L`).

**Scope.** All experiments are at small scale — synthetic dynamics on small manifolds plus one real protein dataset. No
transformer- or LLM-scale demonstrations. The Lie-group IAA is exercised on `SO(3)` and `SO(3)¹⁰`; the flow-matching
projection is exercised on the closed disk and protein data where analytic projection is awkward.

## What's reusable in Linus

Almost everything below is **Phase 6+ research-direction territory**. Manifold-aware architectures are theoretically
attractive but the practical bridge to Linus's near-term phases is uncertain. Be honest about that distance.

**Phase 6+ (architecture choices for fine-tuned / distilled models, speculative).** If a future Linus model — a
biology-skill Worker, a protein-structure encoder, a domain-specific head bolted onto a base LLM — needs to emit
geometry-constrained outputs (rotations, frames, simplices, covariance matrices), the projected-IAA / exponential -IAA
recipe in this paper is the right architectural starting point rather than ambient ResNet + post-hoc projection. The
Lie-group specialization (network outputs Lie-algebra coordinates directly, exponential map back to the group) is
particularly clean for `SO(3)` / `SE(3)` outputs in protein modeling. **Status: research direction, no near-term
commitment.** A Phase 6 spike spec would be the right artifact if a concrete biology skill ever needs it.

**Phase 6+ (sibling architectural pattern alongside JPmHC).** This paper and [JPmHC](2602.18308v2.md) make the same
broad claim — _the geometry of the prediction space deserves first-class architectural treatment, not a post-hoc
projection_ — from different angles. JPmHC works inside the residual-stream mixer (a tiny `n × n` constraint), uses the
orthogonal-group `O(n)` via the Cayley transform, and validates at 7M parameters on ARC-AGI-1. This paper works on the
entire output trajectory (ambient `ℝᵈ`-to-`M` constraint), uses projected ODEs and exponential maps, and validates on
synthetic dynamics + protein structure. The threads converge if Linus ever wires _both_ orthogonal-mixer residual
streams _and_ geometry-constrained heads into the same architecture — for example, a JPmHC-stabilized transformer with a
geometry-preserving SE(3) output head for protein structure. **Status: speculative; no current Linus phase requires
it.**

**Phase 6+ (flow-matching as a constraint-enforcement primitive).** The headline algorithmic contribution — using
small-time flow-matching backward dynamics to recover an approximate metric projection from data — is generic. For any
Linus task where the target distribution is known only by samples and an explicit projection is unavailable (a learned
manifold of "in-distribution" embeddings, a learned protein-structure manifold from a biology dataset, a learned style
manifold from a corpus of writing samples), this is a concrete recipe for constraining outputs to that manifold.
**Status: speculative; would need a concrete use case to be worth implementing in MLX.** The empirical caveat — Flow IAA
/ FAA underperform analytic projection when one exists — matters: this technique is only worth deploying when the
alternative is _no_ projection, not when a closed-form projection is available.

**Phase 7 (biology-skills roadmap, indirect).** Generative biology in Dan's domain — protein structure, RNA structure,
conformational dynamics — is the natural application surface. The paper's `SO(3)` and `SE(3)` demonstrations, including
a real ProteinNet tertiary-structure dataset, sit directly on top of the geometric substrates Phase 7 biology skills
will want. If Phase 7 ever produces a Linus-native protein-structure or backbone-modeling skill, this paper is one of
the references the spec should cite. **Status: tag for the Phase 7 biology-skills roadmap; do not block current work on
it.**

**Phase 1+ (training-stability principle, transferable rule).** The paper's framing — "intermediate representations
should respect the geometry of the prediction space, not just the output" — is a transferable design principle even
outside manifold-ML. It generalizes the JPmHC observation about preserving the full singular spectrum (not just the
operator norm) of residual-stream mixers: in both papers, the lesson is that weak constraints at the output do not
ensure good intermediate representations. **Status: candidate for a one-paragraph note in CLAUDE.md §Engineering
conventions, but only if a third or fourth instance of the same principle lands.** Two papers is not a corpus-wide
convention.

## What's NOT applicable / hype filter

- **No transformer- or LLM-scale validation.** All experiments are at small scale: synthetic dynamics on small manifolds
  plus one ProteinNet dataset. Whether the IAA / FAA recipes generalize to billions-of-parameters transformer
  architectures is "future work." For Linus's Phase 1–5 needs (Worker orchestration, KB integration, sandbox,
  harnesses), this paper has nothing to say.
- **The flow-matching projection underperforms when an analytic projection is available.** The headline algorithmic
  contribution is a fallback for unknown constraint sets, not a replacement for closed-form projections. Empirically,
  the analytic-projection variants beat the flow-learned variants on every dataset where both were tested.
- **Manifold-aware ML is theoretically attractive but practically narrow.** The applications that actually benefit from
  this machinery — protein structure, pose estimation, covariance estimation, orientation prediction — are
  domain-specific and not on the critical path for Linus's general-purpose orchestration backend. Even within Dan's
  domain, mainstream protein-structure tools (AlphaFold3, RFdiffusion) hard-code SE(3)-equivariance via specialized
  layers rather than the unified projected-ODE framework — so even the application surface is a contested design space,
  not a settled one.
- **No code release at submission.** The paper points to "Anonymous Github" for code; an MLX port would be from scratch
  (or from PyTorch, once the de-anonymized code lands). Adoption cost is multi-day, not one-line drop-in — comparable to
  the JPmHC adoption cost.
- **The synthesis framing already says this is a footnote, not a cluster.** Per
  `docs/syntheses/infra-foundations-synthesis.md`, two papers is not a cluster — both this paper and JPmHC live as a
  watch-the-field footnote, and the connection between them is the load-bearing link rather than either paper
  independently. If a third or fourth manifold-ML paper enters the corpus and an MLX Cayley layer or exponential-map
  layer lands in the broader ecosystem, this picture upgrades; until then, the sober reading is "interesting theory; no
  Linus action required."
- **Universal-approximation theorems are existence proofs, not constructions.** Theorems 2.2 / 2.3 / 2.4 say "for any
  target there exists a sufficiently expressive `f_θ` such that the projected architecture approximates it." They do not
  say what depth, width, or training budget actually achieves a given error tolerance, nor do they give
  optimization-theoretic guarantees. Empirical performance still depends on the usual hyperparameter sweep.
- **Prox-regularity is a non-trivial assumption.** The theorems require the constraint set `M` to be uniformly
  prox-regular with positive reach. `C²` manifolds satisfy this, but practical constraint sets that arise in
  applications (manifolds with corners, stratified spaces, intersections of constraint families) may not — and the paper
  does not address what happens when the assumption is violated.

1. **Sibling-paper convergence: when does it stop being a footnote?** The synthesis frames this paper and JPmHC as a
   watch-the-field footnote, with the explicit upgrade trigger of "a third or fourth paper cited by something Dan reads
   in earnest, an MLX implementation of a Cayley layer landing, or a generative-biology paper that hinges on a
   non-Lie-group constraint set." Is the right policy to set a calendar reminder (next quarterly curation review,
   2026-08-01) to re-check the manifold-ML literature, or to wait passively for one of the trigger conditions to
   surface?
2. **Phase 7 biology-skills implications.** The paper validates on a real ProteinNet tertiary-structure dataset on
   `SE(3)`, which sits directly on top of the geometric substrates a Phase 7 biology-skill Worker (protein structure,
   backbone modeling, conformational dynamics) would want. Is it worth tagging
   [`docs/specs/biology-phase7-roadmap.md`](../specs/biology-phase7-roadmap.md) with a note that any future
   protein-structure skill should consider projected-IAA / exponential-IAA architectures rather than ambient ResNet +
   post-hoc projection? The cost is a one-line spec edit; the value is a single canonical reference when the spike
   eventually happens.
3. **Flow-matching projection as a constraint-enforcement primitive.** The headline algorithmic contribution — using
   small-time flow-matching backward dynamics to recover an approximate metric projection from data — is generic enough
   that it could in principle apply to any Linus task with a learned manifold of valid outputs (in-distribution
   embedding manifolds, learned style manifolds from a writing corpus, etc.). The empirical caveat is that it
   underperforms analytic projection when one exists. Is this worth a Phase 6 spike spec, or too speculative until a
   concrete use case surfaces?
4. **MLX implementation status for projected / exponential layers.** As with JPmHC's Cayley layer, adoption depends on
   whether MLX (or mlx-lm, or a sibling library) has a ready projected-ODE / Lie-group exponential map layer or whether
   someone has to write one. The paper points to "Anonymous Github" for code — once that de-anonymizes, is a short scout
   worth doing now (alongside the JPmHC Cayley scout) to understand the porting cost, or wait until a concrete Phase 6+
   commitment surfaces?
5. **Relationship to SE(3)-equivariant networks (RFdiffusion, AlphaFold3).** Mainstream protein-structure tools
   hard-code SE(3)-equivariance via specialized equivariant layers rather than the unified projected-ODE framework. Both
   approaches enforce the same constraint set; they differ in expressiveness and computational cost. If a Phase 7
   protein-structure skill ever happens, is the right architectural starting point the specialized-equivariant-layer
   route (mature, deployed, well-validated) or the geometry-preserving framework (more general, theoretically cleaner,
   less battle-tested)? This is a real architectural choice the spec should resolve, not just a citation question.
6. **Universal-approximation theorems vs. practical performance.** The paper's universality theorems are existence
   proofs, not constructions — they say "for sufficiently expressive `f_θ` the constrained architecture approximates the
   target," but give no width / depth / training-budget bounds. The empirical study at small scale is consistent with
   the theorems but does not validate them at LLM scale. Is the appropriate Linus stance "treat universal approximation
   as a sanity check, not a guarantee, and rely on empirical validation at the scale that matters"? That stance
   generalizes beyond this paper to any universal-approximation result Linus encounters.
7. **Prox-regularity as an unstated practical constraint.** The IAA / FAA theorems require `M` to be uniformly
   prox-regular with positive reach. `C²` manifolds satisfy this, but practical constraint sets in applications
   (manifolds with corners, intersections of constraint families, stratified spaces) may not. For any Phase 6+ adoption,
   is the right discipline "verify prox-regularity of the constraint set as part of the design phase, and document a
   fallback if the constraint set violates the assumption" — analogous to the smoke-test gate convention in `CLAUDE.md`?
