# BitNet v2: Native 4-bit Activations with Hadamard Transformation for 1-bit LLMs

## TL;DR

BitNet b1.58 had ternary weights but kept activations at 8 bits. BitNet v2 pushes activations down to **4 bits** by
inserting a **Hadamard transformation** before activation quantization in the two layers where outliers cluster
(attention output projection `Wo` and FFN down-projection `Wdown`). Result: a model that's effectively trainable at
**W1.58A4** with negligible accuracy loss vs. the W1.58A8 BitNet b1.58 baseline, and that can use 4-bit KV cache too —
unlocking the 4-bit compute paths on next-gen GPUs (GB200) and equivalent low-bit hardware lanes elsewhere.

## The problem (in plain language)

When you quantize neural network activations down to 4 bits, the lossy step isn't the average value — it's the **outlier
channels**. A handful of dimensions in attention's `Wo` input and the FFN's `Wdown` input have activations dozens or
hundreds of times larger than the rest. Quantizing them to 4 bits forces the quantization scale wide enough to cover
those outliers, which means every "normal" activation gets crammed into 2–3 of the 16 available bins. The signal is
destroyed.

The Hadamard transformation is a classical orthogonal matrix (built recursively from `[[H, H], [H, -H]] / √2`) that —
when applied to a vector — _spreads_ concentrated energy across all dimensions. A vector with one outlier becomes a
vector with the outlier's energy smeared roughly Gaussian across all components. Apply it before quantization, quantize
the smeared vector cheaply, then (because Hadamard is orthogonal and self-inverse up to scale) the math downstream still
works out.

The biochemistry analogue: Hadamard is doing what beam-shaping or signal-spreading does in spectroscopy — taking a peaky
distribution that breaks dynamic range and turning it into a flat one that the detector (here: 4-bit quantizer) can
actually capture.

## What they propose

**H-BitLinear**: BitLinear + an online Hadamard transform applied to the input activations before quantization. Used
only for the two outlier-prone layers (`Wo`, `Wdown`); other linears keep the regular BitLinear because their inputs are
already roughly Gaussian.

The forward path:

1. RMSNorm the input.
2. Apply Hadamard transform via a fast O(n log n) algorithm (using `Dao-AILab/fast-hadamard-transform`).
3. Absmean-quantize to INT4 (or absmax to INT8, depending on stage).
4. Multiply against the ternary weights (1.58-bit absmean quantization, same as BitNet b1.58).

Backward pass exploits Hadamard orthogonality: gradient of input = Hadamard of gradient w.r.t. transformed input. No new
training infrastructure beyond what BitNet already needed (STE + latent FP weights).

**Training recipe**: train W1.58A8 from scratch for 95B tokens; continue-train with INT4 activations for 5B tokens. The
4-bit variant requires almost no extra training.

## Key results

- **W1.58A8 BitNet v2 ≈ BitNet b1.58** at 1.3B / 3B / 7B (slightly _better_ on average — +0.16 / +0.49 / +0.61%
  accuracy).
- **W1.58A4 BitNet v2** loses only ~0.1–0.2 PPL vs. W1.58A8 — i.e., dropping activations from 8 to 4 bits is essentially
  free.
- Beats post-training-quantization baselines (QuaRot, SpinQuant) by **~4 PPL points** at 1.3B at the same W1.58A4
  budget.
- Works with **4-bit KV cache** too, with negligible accuracy loss at 3B and 7B — relevant for long-context inference
  where KV is the memory bottleneck.
- **Ablation**: removing the Hadamard transform causes training to diverge entirely. It's not an optimization — it's
  necessary for stability at 4-bit.

Scale: 400M to 7B params, RedPajama 100B tokens. Mid-scale relative to BitNet b1.58, but every claim is on real trained
checkpoints, not toy models.

## What's reusable in Linus

**Direct (Phase 6 fine-tuning, batched inference):** If we ever serve a BitNet from Linus to multiple Workers in
parallel, batched inference is where 4-bit activations matter most — that's where memory bandwidth on activations stops
being amortized across the batch and starts dominating. H-BitLinear is what you'd want under the hood. Reference impl in
[BitNet/](../../repos/BitNet/) (Microsoft) likely tracks this.

**Direct (KV-cache compression):** The 4-bit KV cache result is the most immediately useful: _long context windows on
Apple Silicon are memory-bound, not compute-bound_. KV cache at FP16 doubles every doubling of context. 4-bit KV cuts it
4×, with the paper showing essentially zero accuracy cost. This is a much shorter path to "Linus handles 32K-context
queries" than buying more RAM.

**Indirect (M1 4-bit lanes):** Apple Silicon has INT4/INT8 paths in the Neural Engine (less so on Metal/MPS). Models
trained natively at low precision should map onto ANE more efficiently than retrofitted INT8 quantizations of FP16
models. This paper validates that "trained at low precision ≠ degraded model."

**Aspirational:** Hadamard outlier suppression is a general trick — it shows up in QuaRot, SpinQuant, BitNet v2, and
likely future quantization schemes. Worth knowing about even if we don't implement it directly.

## What's NOT applicable

- The headline 4-bit-compute speedup is GPU-side (GB200 native FP4). Apple Silicon doesn't have FP4 today; gains on M1
  Max would come from memory bandwidth, not compute throughput.
- Continue-training requires 5B tokens of compute. That's small for a research lab; still significant for Linus. We'd be
  downstream of someone else releasing the 4-bit BitNet v2 checkpoint.
