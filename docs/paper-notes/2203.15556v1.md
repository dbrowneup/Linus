# Training Compute-Optimal Large Language Models

## TL;DR

"Training Compute-Optimal Large Language Models" — Hoffmann et al. (DeepMind, March 2022) — is the canonical "Chinchilla
scaling laws" paper. Across more than 400 models from 70M to 16B parameters trained on 5B–500B tokens, the authors show
that under a fixed FLOPs budget, **model size and training-token count should scale in roughly equal proportion** — a
doubling of compute should double both, not (per Kaplan et al. 2020) increase parameters 5.5x while only growing data
1.8x. The empirical proof is _Chinchilla_, a 70B model trained on 1.4T tokens at the same compute budget as the 280B
_Gopher_; Chinchilla beats Gopher, GPT-3 (175B), Jurassic-1 (178B), and MT-NLG (530B) across the board, including a
67.5% MMLU average (a >7-point jump over Gopher). The corollary the field internalized: most frontier LLMs of that era
were _significantly undertrained_.

## The problem (in plain language)

If a lab has a fixed amount of GPU-hours to spend training one large language model, how should it split that budget
between making the model bigger (more parameters N) and feeding it more data (more training tokens D)? The previous
reigning answer, from Kaplan et al. (2020), said: spend most of the new compute on parameters; data needs to grow only
mildly. The field followed that advice — GPT-3, Gopher, Jurassic-1, and MT-NLG were all trained on roughly 300B tokens
regardless of their parameter count, because the scaling-law fit told them more parameters were the better marginal
investment.

Hoffmann et al. argue this was wrong, and that the error came from an experimental artifact: Kaplan et al. fixed the
learning-rate cosine schedule length while varying training duration, which systematically overestimated the loss of
models trained on less data and therefore made parameters look more valuable than they were. With the schedule corrected
and more than 400 training runs spanning a wide grid of (N, D) configurations, a different curve falls out — one where
data and parameters deserve equal marginal compute.

## What the paper proposes

Three independent methodologies — fitting curves through IsoFLOP slices, fitting a parametric loss function L(N, D), and
fitting power laws to the loss-versus-FLOPs frontier — all converge on the same compute-optimal scaling relationship.
Concretely, the optimal parameter count N_opt(C) and token count D_opt(C) for a compute budget C scale as:

N_opt ∝ C^a, D_opt ∝ C^b, with a ≈ b ≈ 0.5

In other words: **for every doubling of compute, double both the model and the data.** This is the headline equation. It
implies that the optimal tokens-per-parameter ratio is approximately constant across scales (~20 tokens per parameter
for the regime they study), making it a usable rule of thumb for pretraining budget allocation.

## Key results

The verification experiment is _Chinchilla_: a 70B-parameter model trained on 1.4T tokens, sized exactly to match
Gopher's compute budget under the new scaling law. Gopher is 280B parameters trained on 300B tokens — four times the
parameters, one-quarter the data. The Chinchilla recipe predicts that swap should win, and it does: Chinchilla beats
Gopher on the large majority of language-modeling, reading-comprehension, common-sense, and question-answering
benchmarks evaluated, posts 67.5% on MMLU (vs. Gopher's ~60%), and uses substantially less compute at inference time
because it's a quarter the size. The effect is not marginal — it is uniform across task suites and statistically robust.

The other large dense models of the era — GPT-3 (175B/300B tokens), Jurassic-1 (178B/300B), MT-NLG (530B/270B) — sit on
the wrong side of the same frontier. Per the paper's fits, each was trained on roughly an order of magnitude too little
data for its size. The figure that became famous (Figure 1 in the paper) shows the Kaplan et al. extrapolation as a
steeply parameter-biased dashed line, with the new IsoFLOP fits as gentler lines crossing through the actual training
points; the existing 175B+ models all sit far above the corrected curve.

## What's reusable for Linus

Linus will not pretrain a frontier model. The 32 GB unified memory of the M1 Max cannot host the optimizer state for a
70B run, let alone the data pipeline. The paper's _operational_ recommendations are therefore not directly actionable.
What is actionable is the _implication chain_, and reading it through the
[memory-as-load-bearing lens of Garrison](../../context/notes/garrison_memory_makes_computation_universal.md) sharpens
which parts to keep.

The first-order takeaway is that **data quality and quantity are the underweighted axis**. For Phase 6 fine-tuning, this
argues against the reflex to chase a larger base model when a smaller one with better-curated domain data would do more
work per compute dollar. A Qwen2.5-Coder-7B or Mistral-7B fine-tuned on a high-quality, well-de-duplicated,
well-filtered slice of Dan's papers, threads, and notes is, by the Chinchilla logic, a better use of M1 Max LoRA hours
than a marginal larger model on a thinner corpus. Phase 1's KnowledgeBase work — corpus curation, dedup, keyphrase
indexing — is the same activity that pretraining labs eventually had to take seriously, and it pays the same kind of
dividend.

The second-order takeaway runs through Garrison: Chinchilla is the high-water mark of the _parameter-and-data_ axis of
intelligence, and it still has to bolt chain-of-thought and external retrieval on at inference time to do anything
genuinely sequential. Even an optimally-trained dense transformer is a TC0 pattern matcher per forward pass; the
universality wall is not where Chinchilla looks for it. So while Chinchilla is the right answer to the question "given a
fixed pretraining budget, what should you train?", it does not contradict the architectural argument that **memory
mechanisms, not parameter count, are where future capability comes from**. Linus should treat Chinchilla as evidence for
_spending compute on data curation now_ and as backdrop for _spending architecture effort on memory next_.

## What's NOT applicable / hype filter

The entire training-compute regime of the paper — 5.76 × 10²³ FLOPs for the Chinchilla run, distributed across
DeepMind's accelerator clusters — is irrelevant to a single M1 Max. None of the headline numbers (1.4T tokens, 70B
parameters, MMLU at 67.5%) translate to any operation Linus will perform. The paper is also strictly about _dense_
transformer pretraining loss; it says nothing about MoE, about quantization-aware training (see
[BitNet](2402.17764v1.md)), about RLHF, about instruction tuning, or about LoRA-style fine-tuning on top of an existing
checkpoint. A common misreading is to apply the 20-tokens-per-parameter rule of thumb to fine-tuning data sizing — there
is no support in the paper for that extrapolation, and the fine-tuning regime is governed by entirely different dynamics
(catastrophic forgetting, capacity for new behavior, learning-rate sensitivity).

The fits also assume a fixed architecture family and a fixed training objective. Llama 3, by training a 7B/8B model on
~15T tokens, deliberately overshot Chinchilla-optimal in the tokens direction to get a smaller model that is cheap at
inference — a perfectly valid strategic deviation that Chinchilla scaling doesn't forbid but doesn't predict either. The
paper is about pretraining loss at fixed compute, not about deployment economics.

## Connections

The most important connection is to the
[Garrison memory note](../../context/notes/garrison_memory_makes_computation_universal.md): Chinchilla is the canonical
scaling-laws reference that Garrison's argument _answers_. Garrison's claim is not that scaling is wrong but that it is
bounded by the architectural ceiling of attention's quadratic cost and by the absence of robust memory mechanisms;
Chinchilla is what _optimal_ looks like inside that bounded regime, and the existence of a clean optimum makes the
ceiling argument sharper, not weaker.

The data-quality corollary connects directly to the [FineWeb paper (2406.17557v2)](2406.17557v2.md) — once Chinchilla
established that more, better tokens dominate, the next generation of work was forced to take corpus curation seriously,
and FineWeb is one of the artifacts of that turn. Llama 3's training recipe (15T tokens on a 7-8B model) is a deliberate
post-Chinchilla overshoot, trading pretraining-optimality for inference economics, and the same logic underlies the
[BitNet line (2310.11453v1, 2402.17764v1, 2504.18415v2)](2402.17764v1.md): once you accept that
smaller-but-better-trained beats larger-but-undertrained, aggressive quantization of the smaller winner is the natural
follow-on. The "Sparks of AGI" framing of GPT-4-class capability also lives downstream of Chinchilla — those
capabilities emerged in models trained on much more data per parameter than the pre-2022 norm.

For Linus phases, this paper is most relevant to **Phase 2 (KnowledgeBase v1)** and **Phase 6 (fine-tuning)**, in both
cases as an argument for spending the marginal hour on data work rather than on parameter-count chasing.

## Open questions for Dan

1. **Data axis for fine-tuning.** Chinchilla's lesson is that data is the underweighted half. For Phase 6, what does
   Linus's "1.4T tokens equivalent" look like — what is the realistic upper bound on high-quality, well-filtered tokens
   we can assemble from Dan's papers, threads, code, and notes, and is it large enough relative to a 7B LoRA target to
   matter? Worth a back-of-envelope before committing to a fine-tuning run.
2. **Memory vs. scaling, in concrete terms.** If Garrison's framing is right that memory is the load-bearing capability
   and Chinchilla is right that compute-optimal pretraining is data-balanced, then Linus's bet is "use a small
   Chinchilla-era model + strong episodic/semantic memory" instead of "use a bigger model with weaker memory." Is that
   the bet you want to commit to architecturally in Phase 2, and what is the test that would falsify it?
3. **Inference-economics overshoot.** Llama 3 trained well past Chinchilla-optimal to make the _inference_ model small.
   Should Linus prefer Llama-3-style overtrained smaller models as the default Worker substrate, since inference cost on
   the M1 Max is the binding constraint, not pretraining cost (which we don't pay)?
4. **Curation as a first-class Linus activity.** If data quality is what made Chinchilla possible and FineWeb practical,
   should the KnowledgeBase ingestion pipeline explicitly version, hash, and quality-score corpus subsets the way
   pretraining labs version their data mixes — making "corpus version 0.3 with stricter dedup" a first-class Linus
   artifact alongside model checkpoints?
