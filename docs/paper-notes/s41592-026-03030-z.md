# Clustering the protein universe of life using DIAMOND DeepClust

## TL;DR
DIAMOND DeepClust is a cascaded, ultra-fast protein clustering tool — not a foundation model — that scales sensitive sequence clustering to the full assembled biosphere. The authors used it to cluster ~19 billion deduplicated protein sequences in 18 days on 27 compute nodes (~255,000 CPU hours total), producing ~1.70 billion clusters; the 335 million representatives of clusters with at least three members capture ~92% of the input space and represent a 5.5-fold increase in sequence diversity over AlphaFold2's Big Fantastic Database (BFD). On the NCBI NR benchmark (~546 million sequences) DIAMOND DeepClust runs **36-fold faster than MMseqs2** (19 hours vs. 29 days) at comparable precision and slightly higher Pfam-based sensitivity (68.6% vs. 62.3%); a linear mode runs 102-fold faster than FLSHclust at higher sensitivity. The full clustered DeepClust database is released for download under GPL3-licensed tooling, and substituting it for BFD/MGnify in AlphaFold2 raised the mean pLDDT for 473 difficult, low-MSA-depth targets from 52.9 to 62.6. For Linus this is **infrastructure, not a model**: the relevant action is to consume the released artifacts, not reproduce the pipeline.

## The problem (in plain language)
Modern biology has stopped struggling to *generate* protein sequence data and has started struggling to *organize* it. The Earth BioGenome project alone aims to assemble ~1.8 million eukaryotic species, projected to yield roughly 27 billion protein sequences, on top of metagenomic catalogs that already contribute tens of billions more. Almost everything downstream — comparative genomics, function annotation, AlphaFold-class structure prediction, protein language model pretraining — depends on first reducing this redundant, noisy, unevenly sampled space to a tractable set of representative sequences with their evolutionary neighborhoods attached.

The standard tools — CD-HIT, UClust, MMseqs2, MMseqs2/Linclust, FLSHclust, HipMCL — weren't designed for this scale. CD-HIT and UClust degrade past ~50% identity. MMseqs2 has good sensitivity but clustering NR (half a billion sequences) takes nearly a month on a 64-core server. Linclust scales linearly but tops out at 4 billion sequences (32-bit IDs), needs the database to fit a single node's RAM, and cannot parallelize across nodes. FLSHclust scales but trades away sensitivity. The field has been forced into a Pareto choice between speed, sensitivity, and dataset size — exactly the choice that prevents systematic clustering of the assembled biosphere.

The functional cost of that choice is concrete: AlphaFold2's accuracy is bounded by the depth and diversity of the multiple-sequence alignment (MSA) it can build for a query, and MSA depth is bounded by the underlying clustered protein database. A bigger, deeper, more diverse cluster database raises the floor for everything that depends on evolutionary signal.

## What they propose
DIAMOND DeepClust is a clustering pipeline built on top of DIAMOND v.2's sensitive protein aligner. The architecture is a cascade: several rounds of all-versus-all alignment at progressively higher DIAMOND sensitivity (`--faster`, `--fast`, default, `--sensitive`, optionally `--very-sensitive`/`--ultra-sensitive`), each round taking the representatives from the previous round as input. Inside each round, alignments are encoded as a directed graph and clusters are picked by greedy vertex cover (search depth one for early rounds, three for late rounds). The earliest rounds use a "linear-stage" algorithm with minimizer subsampling (window size 12) — a generalization of the Linclust trick. The pipeline ends with a clustering criterion expressed as a tuple of sequence-identity threshold, coverage threshold, and e-value, with thresholds tightened in earlier rounds (+7% identity/coverage up to default mode, +5% coverage at `--sensitive`) and relaxed in the final round.

Two design choices distinguish DIAMOND DeepClust from MMseqs2/Linclust. First, it supports **bi-directional coverage clustering** — both representative and member must satisfy the coverage threshold — which is critical for building clusters of conserved families rather than ragged gluings. A length-ratio bound lets the linear-stage algorithm exclude impossible pairs cheaply by processing only "median-length" comparisons within feasible windows of a length-sorted seed table. Second, the linear mode is engineered for **multi-node distributed execution**: 64-bit sequence IDs, parallel external radix sort over distributed file systems (256 radix buckets per table), no requirement that the whole database fit in a single node's RAM. Near-ideal scaling: one node 15.3 h → 32 nodes 35 min on a 22B-sequence dataset.

Other ingredients: empirically learned multiple spaced seeds (30 shapes of weight 10, derived greedily from 1.47M real DIAMOND alignments) for the linear mode; a new vectorized Smith-Waterman generalizing SWIPE to compute alignments of independent query-target pairs in the same SIMD register; self-alignment optimizations exploiting query/target symmetry. A planned extension is Markov clustering on the alignment graph to detect false-positive edges between dense communities.

The artifact released alongside the paper is a clustering of ~19 billion (deduplicated from ~22.8 billion collected) protein sequences pulled from JGI IMG, SRC/MERC, MGnify, Metaclust, NCBI NR, AGNOSTOS, MetaEuk, SMAGs, TOPAZ, GPD, NovelFams, and MGV, clustered at 30% identity and 90% uni-directional coverage to match BFD's parameters. Outputs are made available as a BLAST database, FASTA of representatives, TSV mapping members to representatives, BLAST database and Parquet files for all members, fast-extraction indices, and a ColabFold-ready package — hosted at `objectstore.hpccloud.mpcdf.mpg.de/deepclust/index.html`.

## Key results
Clustering the full ~19 billion deduplicated sequence dataset at 30% identity / 90% uni-directional coverage produced **~1.70 billion clusters**, of which 32% are non-singletons and 68% are singletons (the singletons amount to only ~6% of total sequence mass). 335 million clusters of size ≥3 cover 92% of input sequences (~17.48 billion). Total compute: ~255,000 CPU hours over 18 days on 27 nodes, run as four cascaded rounds (6.38 days on one 72-core node; 36.8 hours on 16 nodes; 20.1 hours on 16 nodes; 9.63 days on up to 27 nodes).

Compared to AlphaFold2's BFD (61M clusters of size ≥3), the DeepClust database represents a **5.5-fold increase in sequence diversity at matched clustering parameters**, with an estimated 118 million new families not annotatable in BFD at ≥60% query coverage by HHblits.

NCBI NR benchmark (546M sequences, bi-directional coverage, no identity threshold, 64-core / 2 TB RAM server):
- **DIAMOND DeepClust:** 19.0 h, sensitivity 68.6%, precision 95.5%
- **MMseqs2:** 29 days (36-fold slower), sensitivity 62.3%, precision 95.5%
- **FLSHclust:** 17 days (21-fold slower), sensitivity 49.7%, precision 96.5%
- **DIAMOND DeepClust linear mode:** 3.9 h (102-fold faster than FLSHclust), sensitivity 53.8%, precision 95.9%
- **MMseqs2/Linclust:** 2.8 h (fastest), sensitivity 21.6%, precision 97.5%

Multi-node scaling on the 22B-sequence dataset using linear mode showed near-ideal speedup (15.3 h → 35 min, 1 → 32 nodes).

The AlphaFold2 evaluation: from 10,000 randomly sampled clusters of 30–100,000 members, they kept 732 representatives with fewer than 30 hits across BFD/MGnify/UniRef30 by HHblits/JackHMMER, then narrowed to 473 with AF2 MSA depth <30. Re-running AF2 with the DeepClust database in place of BFD+MGnify raised pLDDT for **366 of 473 sequences** and decreased it for 107; mean pLDDT improved from 52.9 to 62.6 (+7.73 absolute, Wilcoxon P < 2.2 × 10⁻¹⁶). The cost was high enough (~100,000 CPU hours for the 473-protein analysis) that a full-corpus re-prediction was out of scope.

## What's reusable in Linus
**Phase 4 (Data Sovereignty), direct.** The released DeepClust artifacts are textbook Phase 4 candidates: a public, GPL3-tooled, downloadable scientific dataset that is large, expensive to regenerate, and likely to be cited heavily in Dan's neighborhood for years. Mirroring it locally — or at minimum mirroring the representatives FASTA and the member-to-representative TSV — gives Linus durable access independent of MPCDF object-store availability. The Parquet member-sequence files for ~19B sequences are likely low TB, which puts them at the boundary of "mirror in full" vs. "mirror representatives + cluster-on-demand member retrieval."

**KnowledgeBase, direct.** Protein clusters map cleanly onto KG nodes: a representative sequence is a canonical entity (length, source database, provenance, Pfam clan if annotated as attributes), and member sequences are instances linked by a "member-of" edge. This gives the protein layer of the KnowledgeBase a principled set of node anchors that scales — 335M canonical entities is large but tractable for SPECTER2/BGE-style embedding indexes already used in KnowledgeBase. The cluster graph itself is a derived edge set ("co-clustered with") that compresses the implicit homology relation across 19B sequences into something a graph algorithm can traverse.

**Phase 7 (Skills & Autonomy Graduation), direct.** Any Linus skill that wraps an AlphaFold-class structure predictor (ColabFold being the practical local handle, since AF2 itself wants serious GPU and isn't well-suited to M1 Max) should consider DeepClust as the MSA-prior database. The authors provide a ColabFold integration (`drostlab/deepclust_colabfold`) that's near drop-in. The expected lift — from the paper's 473-sequence demo — is biggest exactly for sequences underrepresented in BFD/MGnify, i.e., the cases where Dan would most want extra signal.

**Phase 1 (Recon & Baselines), indirect.** This paper is a useful concrete benchmark for what "infrastructure-grade scientific tooling" looks like in Dan's domain: a single C++ tool, GPL3-licensed, distributed via GitHub, that does one thing extraordinarily well. When Linus grows skills that *invoke* such tools (DIAMOND v.2, mmseqs2, HHblits), DIAMOND is a strong first candidate to wrap — Buchfink's tooling has the cleanest CLI in this corner of bioinformatics and the fastest path from "Worker has a FASTA" to "Worker has annotations."

## What's NOT applicable
**The pipeline itself is not reproducible on Linus.** 18 days × 27 compute nodes × ~255,000 CPU hours is hyperscale HPC, not M1 Max. Even the single-server NR benchmark (19 hours, 64-core, 2 TB RAM) requires roughly twice the wall time and an order of magnitude more RAM than Dan's MacBook Pro can offer. Running DIAMOND DeepClust **locally** is feasible only on small inputs (subsets of UniProt, single-genome proteomes, lab-scale metagenomes) — useful for skill development and testing, not for biosphere-scale anything. The relevant Linus action is **download, index, query**.

**The AlphaFold2 demo is also not locally reproducible.** 100,000 CPU hours for 473 predictions implies AF2 itself, which on commodity hardware needs a CUDA GPU and can't be straightforwardly run on Metal/MLX without significant porting effort (and even then is slow). The realistic Linus path is ColabFold (which is more memory-flexible) and accepting that protein structure prediction at meaningful throughput remains a Mac Studio / cloud-burst capability, not an M1 Max capability.

**No model weights, no embeddings.** This is an alignment-and-clustering paper. There is no neural network, no pretrained representation, no embeddings to download. If Linus wants vector embeddings of the DeepClust representatives — e.g., for retrieval over the protein universe analogous to what KnowledgeBase does over papers — those would need to be computed separately using a PLM (ESM-2, ProtT5, etc.), and 335M sequences × even a small PLM is its own significant compute project.

**License caveats.** DIAMOND DeepClust is GPL3 — fine for personal Linus, but GPL3-derived components in any future shared/distributed skill stack invoke copyleft considerations. The cluster database itself appears CC-BY-4.0 per the article's open-access notice; the hosting page should be checked for the exact data license.

## Connections
**Group A foundation models (paired data substrate).** Better clustering raises the ceiling for PLM pretraining diversity. RiNALMo ([s41467-025-60872-5.md](s41467-025-60872-5.md)) used MMseqs-style clustering for RNA diversity sampling; the Group A protein FM papers (Evo 2, LucaOne, ProteinReasoner, METL) all depend on an upstream sequence-deduplication/clustering step. DIAMOND DeepClust is the most credible current candidate to be the *protein-side* counterpart to OpenGenome2 (Evo 2's training corpus, [s41586-026-10176-5.md](s41586-026-10176-5.md)) — if Linus mirrors OpenGenome2 as a Phase 4 asset for nucleotide work, the DeepClust artifacts deserve symmetric treatment for protein work.

**Group C constellation.** This sits at the *infrastructure / clustering* end of Group C, upstream of the function-annotation and reasoning papers. Horizyn-1 ([rocks-et-al-2026-dual-encoder-contrastive-learning-accelerates-enzyme-discovery.md](rocks-et-al-2026-dual-encoder-contrastive-learning-accelerates-enzyme-discovery.md)) does retrieval over a related substrate; ProtHGT does function prediction on protein KGs that could in principle be node-anchored on DeepClust representatives; BioReason reasons over DNA but the protein-side analog would benefit from a clustered substrate. DIAMOND DeepClust is Group C's quietest paper but plausibly the most foundational — it's the substrate the others would cluster *over* if they were honest about their data-prep dependency.

**KnowledgeBase pipeline.** The KB submodule's RaKUn 2.0 + SPECTER2/BGE + rdflib/networkx stack was designed for documents. Adapting it to a protein layer (entities are sequences not phrases, embeddings come from a PLM not BGE, edges come from sequence homology not citation) is non-trivial, but DIAMOND DeepClust gives a clean answer to the *node-identification* problem, which is the hardest part. Pfam clans (used in the paper's precision metric) give an off-the-shelf categorical type system on top.

**Phase 8 (Beyond MacBook).** When Linus eventually graduates to Mac Studio, running DIAMOND DeepClust on a Dan-curated corpus — proteins from organisms in his research domain — becomes a feasible Worker task at lab scale rather than biosphere scale. Mac Studio with 192 GB unified memory could plausibly handle it.
