# ARC Prize 2024: Technical Report

## TL;DR

"ARC Prize 2024: Technical Report" by François Chollet, Mike Knoop, Gregory Kamradt, and Bryan Landers documents the
2024 edition of the ARC-AGI competition: a $600K-grand-prize contest where 1,430 teams submitted 17,789 entries against
a benchmark designed to be hard for AI and easy for humans. The state-of-the-art on the private evaluation set jumped
from 33% (2023) to 55.5% (MindsAI, end of 2024), with the top open-sourced winner ("the ARChitects") at 53.5% — driven
primarily by **test-time training (TTT)** and **deep-learning-guided program synthesis**, not by raw model scale. The
Grand Prize (85%) was not claimed; the headline o3 number lives on a separate compute-rich track and is the cost story
Garrison's [memory note](../../context/notes/garrison_memory_makes_computation_universal.md) is reading against.

## The problem (in plain language)

ARC-AGI is a benchmark that tries to measure _generalization to novel problems_, not _skill at problems you've seen_.
Each task is a small grid puzzle: a few input/output demonstration pairs (median three), and a held-out test input whose
output you must produce. Grids are up to 30×30, with 10 cell colours. The trick is that every task in the dataset
follows a _different_ logic — there is no shared template to memorize. The only assumed prior knowledge is "Core
Knowledge" (objectness, basic topology, elementary counting) — concepts that human four-year-olds already have.

Two facts give the benchmark its bite. First, humans are nearly perfect on it: NYU's Mechanical Turk study showed 99% of
public evaluation tasks are solved by at least one of ten workers. Second, classical deep learning is nearly useless on
it: GPT-3 scored 0% via direct prompting in 2020, no pure deep-learning approach exceeded 1% in the first Kaggle
competition, and the benchmark has remained mostly intact through the entire 2022–2024 LLM scale-up. Chollet's framing
is that this is not an accident — it's the difference between _interpolation over training distribution_ (what scaled
transformers do) and _recombining priors at test time to solve a problem you've never seen_ (what general intelligence
requires).

ARC-AGI-1 is split into 1,000 tasks: 400 public training (easy, demonstrate the format), 400 public evaluation (hard,
local debugging), 100 semi-private (for commercial-API entries), and 100 fully private (for the Kaggle leaderboard).
Only the private set is the headline number, to limit overfitting and contamination.

## What the paper proposes / reports

This is a competition retrospective, not a methods paper. The authors do four things: survey the top approaches, present
scores, diagnose ARC-AGI-1's known flaws, and pre-announce ARC-AGI-2 for 2025. They group the year's progress into three
approach families:

- **Deep-learning-guided program synthesis.** LLMs generate or guide search through candidate Python (or DSL) programs
  that map demonstration inputs to outputs. Greenblatt's GPT-4o-driven approach generates thousands of programs per task
  and selects by demonstration fit; the authors estimate that _brute-forcing 85% this way_ would cost ~10⁸ programs/task
  — a multi-million-dollar compute budget for 100 tasks.
- **Test-time training (TTT).** Fine-tune a pretrained LLM on the demonstration pairs of _each individual test task_,
  producing a per-task specialized model. Pioneered for ARC by MindsAI in 2023, popularized by ARC Prize 2024. All top
  transduction-based entries used TTT; no static-inference transduction approach scored above 11%.
- **Hybrids that combine induction (program synthesis) with transduction (direct LLM prediction).** Li et al.'s
  award-winning paper showed the two methods solve _substantially different_ task subsets, so ensembling is
  multiplicative. All 2024 leading scores combine both.

The paper also sketches limitations of ARC-AGI-1 (only 100 private tasks, used for years, ~10K scores reported against
it; ~49% of the set was already "solvable by some 2020 brute-force submission") and uses these to motivate ARC-AGI-2.

## Key results

The Kaggle private leaderboard's top open-source entry was the ARChitects (Franzen, Disselhoff, Hartmann) at **53.5%**
using a NeMo-Minitron-8B with extensive augmentation and a stability-based selection criterion. MindsAI hit **55.5%**
(highest of the year) but did not open-source their entry and so could not claim the prize. Other notable Kaggle scores:
Barbadillo 40%, alijs 40%, William Wu 37%.

On the compute-rich ARC-AGI-Pub track (semi-private set, ~$10K API budget per entry, ~1,000× the Kaggle compute), Jeremy
Berman led at **53.6%** semi-private / 58.5% public, with Akyürek et al. at 47.5% / 62.8%, and Greenblatt's GPT-4o
program synthesis at 42% public. Direct-prompting baselines are an order of magnitude below: o1-preview 18% pass@1,
Claude 3.5 Sonnet 14%, GPT-4o 5%, Gemini 1.5 4.5% on the semi-private set.

The longitudinal curve (Figure 2 in the paper) is the load-bearing chart: 2019 ≈ 0%, 2020 = 20.6% (pure brute-force DSL
search), 2022 = 28.5%, 2023 = 31.4%, mid-2024 = 34.3%, end-2024 = **55.5%**. Five years of stagnation, then a step
change in a single year — driven by TTT plus deep-learning-guided synthesis, not by parameter count.

The paper deliberately does _not_ dwell on o3's 87.5% / 91.5% numbers (those came from OpenAI's December 2024
announcement and live on the Pub track at extreme compute). It reports them only by reference. The Garrison
[memory note](../../context/notes/garrison_memory_makes_computation_universal.md) supplies the compute story: o3 reached
82.8% on 400 public tasks at ~$6,677 (~33M tokens), and 91.5% at ~$1.15M (~9.5B tokens) — a 172× compute premium for the
last ~9 points.

## What's reusable for Linus

The right way to read this paper for Linus is _through Garrison_: ARC-AGI is a measurement of how reliably a system can
maintain state and recombine priors at test time, and the 2024 leap was paid for almost entirely in test-time compute
that simulates memory through search-and-selection. The two approaches that worked — TTT and
program-synthesis-with-debugging — are both _external memory mechanisms grafted onto an amnesiac base model_. TTT writes
per-task weights; program synthesis maintains a candidate-program pool with verification against demonstration pairs.
Neither is a clean memory architecture; both buy memory reliability at runtime cost. That diagnosis is exactly
Garrison's frame.

For Linus's local-first memory design, this makes ARC-AGI a _useful frame more than a useful target_. The frame: any
benchmark that measures "reliable computation across many steps on a novel task" is implicitly measuring the two
Garrison requirements (recursive state maintenance, reliable history access). A Linus episodic-memory layer that does
its job well should let a small Worker model approach ARC-AGI-class tasks at a fraction of the o3 compute, because the
memory is _given_ to it rather than reconstructed each time by search.

ARC-AGI as a _benchmark candidate_ for Linus is plausible but heavily caveated. The Kaggle track (single P100, 12 hours,
no internet) is closer to Linus's deployment posture than the Pub track, and the public training and evaluation sets are
freely usable for local smoke tests. A 50–100 task subset on M1 Max with a small Ollama Worker is a tractable Phase 1–2
spike — not to compete on the leaderboard, but to instrument _which task subsets become solvable when episodic memory is
added_ vs. when only the base model is available. That is the experiment Garrison's framework actually motivates: hold
the model fixed, vary the memory, measure the delta.

The TTT pattern has narrower but real reuse value. Per-task LoRA adaptation (the usual TTT recipe) is exactly the kind
of thing a future Phase 6 Linus could do natively on Apple Silicon — 7M to 8B parameter models with a small augmented
training pass per task fits comfortably in 32 GB unified memory. Akyürek et al.'s 8B TTT model hit 47.5% on the
semi-private set; that is a model Linus could plausibly run.

## What's NOT applicable / hype filter

The o3 frontier-compute regime is not Linus territory. $1.15M / 9.5B tokens for one benchmark run is not a number that
scales down — it is the cost of _not_ having robust memory, paid by parallel search. Treat the o3 numbers as a
Garrison-style data point about _what memory unreliability costs_, not as a target Linus could approach by spending
less.

The paper's Pub-track numbers depend on closed-source frontier models and ~$10K/entry API credits. They tell us nothing
portable about local inference, and the rank order on Pub will reshuffle as model providers change.

The Kaggle-track winning entries are 8B-class models with heavy data augmentation pipelines and TTT loops tuned over
months by serious teams. Reproducing them is a research project, not a weekend spike. Plan accordingly.

ARC-AGI-1 itself is acknowledged in the paper to be partially "solvable" by brute-force DSL search (49% by ensembling
2020 submissions). The signal-bearing fraction is smaller than the headline 100 tasks suggests. ARC-AGI-2 (2025) is
intended to clean this up; until it lands, treat ARC-AGI-1 scores as noisy and ARC-AGI-2 as the more interesting future
target.

Finally: ARC-AGI is not a _capability_ benchmark for Linus's actual job (scientific computing, code generation,
literature synthesis). It would be a _diagnostic_ benchmark — useful for measuring whether memory architecture changes
do what they claim — but it is not on the path to Dan's daily-use value.

and program-synthesis-with-debugging both look like _external memory hacks_. This note pairs naturally with that one —
Garrison's note supplies the _why_ and the conceptual claim, this note supplies the _how_ of the 2024 competition that
produced the numbers.

The [JPmHC note (2602.18308v2)](2602.18308v2.md) is the other obvious cross-link: it uses ARC-AGI-1 as its evaluation
benchmark for orthogonal hyper-connections on a 7M-parameter Tiny Recursive Model, hitting 31.4% exact match. That paper
is the closest thing in this folder to a _Linus-scale_ ARC-AGI experiment — small enough to plausibly run on M1 Max —
and the two notes together define the realistic envelope: the technical report sets the leaderboard ceiling and
competition context; JPmHC shows what a tiny model with the right architecture can extract.

The [LLM speed note (2502.16721v1)](2502.16721v1.md) connects on a different axis: both papers argue that single-number
leaderboards mislead. Tok/s misleads about task-completion time; ARC-AGI's headline scores mislead about generalization
(the 2020 49% brute-force lower bound is the parallel of the verbosity confound).

For repos: [autoresearch/](../../repos/autoresearch/) and [autoresearch-mlx/](../../repos/autoresearch-mlx/) are the

[Bonsai-demo/](../../repos/Bonsai-demo/) and [BitNet/](../../repos/BitNet/) are too low-level for ARC-AGI directly, but

Phase mapping: Phase 1 (Recon & Baselines) for any small-scale ARC-AGI smoke test; Phase 3 (Knowledge & Parallel Agents)

## Open questions for Dan

1. **ARC-AGI as an episodic-memory diagnostic.** Garrison's frame says memory is the load-bearing primitive. Would a
   useful Phase 2/3 experiment be: take a small Linus Worker, run it against 50–100 ARC-AGI public-eval tasks twice —
   once with no episodic memory, once with an episodic store that retains intermediate scratchpad and per-task
   observations across attempts — and measure the delta? This is the experiment that turns the memory claim from a
   thesis into a number, and it costs nothing in Maestro tokens.

2. **Memory architecture's lower bound.** o3 at $1.15M for 91.5% is the upper bound on what bad memory architecture
   costs. What is the _lower bound_ — how much of o3's gain over the 55.5% open-source SOTA could be recovered by a
   small model with a clean episodic memory and TTT, without any frontier compute? Is there enough public information
   from the ARChitects' open-source release to attempt a reproduction-plus-memory experiment on M1 Max?

3. **TTT as a Linus primitive.** Per-task LoRA fine-tuning at runtime is a memory mechanism in disguise — it writes
   task-specific state into weights. Does Linus's Phase 6 fine-tuning roadmap want to treat TTT as a first-class
   capability (e.g., a Worker can request "spawn a fine-tuned variant of yourself for this task"), or is that conflating
   training-time and inference-time concerns in a way the architecture should keep separate? The answer affects whether
   the orchestration layer needs a "fine-tune-and-load" primitive.

4. **ARC-AGI-2 timing.** The paper pre-announces ARC-AGI-2 for 2025. If a Linus benchmark suite eventually wants
   ARC-AGI-class tasks for measuring generalization, is it worth waiting for ARC-AGI-2 (cleaner, fewer
   brute-force-solvable items) before investing in any tooling, or is ARC-AGI-1 good enough for the diagnostic use case
   where signal-to-noise is less critical than reproducibility?

5. **Compute-as-memory accounting.** If we accept Garrison's read that o3 paid $1.15M to brute-force memory reliability,
   can Linus's design docs make this _explicit_ — track "memory budget" as a first-class architectural quantity, with
   the o3 number as the cautionary upper bound and the human-with-pen-and-paper as the lower bound? This may belong in
   [ARCHITECTURE.md](../../ARCHITECTURE.md) or as its own ADR.
