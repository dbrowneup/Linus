# BitNet Distillation

## TL;DR
The most directly Linus-actionable paper in the BitNet family. **BitDistill** is a three-stage pipeline that takes any off-the-shelf full-precision LLM (Qwen3, Qwen2.5, Gemma demonstrated) and converts it into a 1.58-bit BitNet *for a specific downstream task* — without retraining from scratch. The result preserves nearly all the FP16 accuracy of the source model on classification and summarization, while giving **10× memory reduction** and **2.65× faster CPU inference**. Unlike all prior BitNet papers, this one doesn't require pre-training on trillions of tokens — it works at fine-tuning scale (10B tokens for warm-up + a downstream dataset).

## The problem (in plain language)
The earlier BitNet papers all showed: *if you train from scratch in 1.58 bits, you can match FP16*. Excellent — but training from scratch costs millions of dollars and trillions of tokens. The natural alternative is *quantization-aware fine-tuning*: take a pre-trained FP16 model, swap its `nn.Linear` for `BitLinear`, and fine-tune it down to ternary. The authors show that this naive approach (called **BitNet-SFT** in the paper) **doesn't work** — and worse, *gets worse as model size increases*. At Qwen3-0.6B the gap to FP16 is ~14 points; at Qwen3-4B it's ~15 points and not closing. So you can't just take a big FP16 model and casually quantize it to ternary by fine-tuning — the optimization is unstable and the bigger you go the worse it gets.

For Linus, this matters because it's the difference between "we can use BitNet someday once Microsoft releases an MLX checkpoint" and "we can convert *any* model on HuggingFace into a Linus-runnable Worker tomorrow."

## What they propose
**Three-stage BitDistill pipeline:**

**Stage 1 — Modeling refinement**: insert **SubLN** (sub-layer LayerNorms) at two specific places in each transformer block: right before the MHSA output projection and right before the FFN output projection. This stabilizes activation variance, which is the proximate cause of the instability. (SubLN is the same trick from the original BitNet paper — but inserted into a *pre-trained* model rather than designed from scratch.)

**Stage 2 — Continued pre-training**: train the SubLN-augmented model on a small slice of pre-training corpus (~10B tokens of FALCON, vs. the ~4T tokens you'd need to train BitNet from scratch). This warm-up step is what closes the scaling gap — without it, larger models distill *worse*; with it, larger models distill at least as well as smaller ones.

**Stage 3 — Distillation-based fine-tuning**, with two parallel losses:
- **Logits distillation**: KL-divergence between the FP16 teacher's softmax and the 1.58-bit student's softmax (with temperature τ=5).
- **Multi-head attention distillation** (MiniLM-style): KL-divergence between the relation matrices `softmax(QKᵀ/√d)` of teacher and student, computed at one carefully-chosen layer (not all layers — single-layer attention distillation outperformed all-layer in their ablations).
- Plus the standard cross-entropy on the downstream task.

Combined loss: `L = L_CE + λ·L_logit + γ·L_attn`. They tune λ, γ per task.

**The biochemistry analogue**: distillation here is doing something like teaching an apoenzyme to mimic a holoenzyme's active site by exposing it to the holoenzyme's behavior on many substrates — the ternary student doesn't need to *understand* what the FP16 teacher knows, it just needs to reproduce its outputs and attention patterns at the relevant layer.

## Key results
- **Qwen3 0.6B/1.7B/4B** distilled to 1.58 bits using BitDistill match FP16 accuracy (within 0.1–0.2 points) on MNLI, QNLI, SST-2, and CNN/DailyMail summarization.
- **Naive BitNet-SFT** (no SubLN, no continued pre-training, no distillation) loses ~13–15 accuracy points and gets worse with scale.
- **2.65× CPU inference speed** (1135 tokens/s vs 427 tokens/s).
- **10× memory reduction** (0.11 GB vs 1.20 GB for the 0.6B model).
- **Backbone-agnostic**: works on Qwen2.5 and Gemma in addition to Qwen3.
- **Cost**: 10B tokens of continued pretraining + standard task fine-tuning. ~0.25% the cost of training BitNet from scratch.

Scale: 0.6B–4B Qwen3 backbones, fine-tuning workloads. Within Linus' M1 Max + LoRA range *if* the BitNet inference path is functional.

## What's reusable in Linus
**Direct (this is the path):** This is plausibly the *single most relevant paper* in the folder for Linus' Phase 6 plan. The combination of:
1. Start from any HuggingFace FP16 model (Qwen3, Qwen2.5, Gemma — or by extension, any model Dan finds useful).
2. Convert to 1.58-bit *for a specific Linus task* (e.g. KnowledgeBase summarization, classification of Dan's papers, code-style refactoring).
3. Run the resulting ternary model on CPU at 2.65× speedup and 10× memory savings.

…is essentially the exact shape of "Linus owns its own fine-tuned domain model" without requiring a training cluster. The 10B-token continued pre-training is feasible on M1 Max if (and this is a real if) the BitNet training path is implemented in MLX. Code is available at `github.com/microsoft/BitNet` — that's [repos/BitNet/](../../repos/BitNet/).

**Direct (architecture pattern)**: SubLN insertion + continued pre-training + multi-head attention distillation is a recipe Linus could implement against any base model. Worth a `docs/specs/bitdistill-on-mlx.md` spec eventually.

**Indirect (model selection)**: The paper validates Qwen3 as a strong distillation source. That informs which Workers Linus should run via Ollama in the near term — Qwen3-family models are not just good FP16 models, they're good ternary-distillation candidates for later.

## What's NOT applicable
- **Training infrastructure**: their experiments ran on 8×AMD MI300X. The 10B-token continued pre-training step is the time-dominant phase; on M1 Max this could take days or weeks depending on whether MLX's BitNet training path is functional. Plausible but not casual.
- **CPU inference numbers** (1135 tok/s, 427 tok/s) are on x86 CPUs. M1 Max is a different beast; numbers will differ but the *direction* (BitNet much faster than FP16 on CPU) holds.
- **Tasks tested are classification and summarization**. Open-ended generation, code synthesis, and tool-use weren't evaluated. The technique probably extends; we'd need to verify.
