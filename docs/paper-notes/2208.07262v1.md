# Retrieval-efficiency trade-off of Unsupervised Keyword Extraction

## TL;DR

This paper introduces **RaKUn 2.0**, a graph-based unsupervised keyphrase extractor optimized for the joint frontier of
retrieval quality and computational speed. The key claim: RaKUn 2.0 achieves retrieval performance statistically
indistinguishable from state-of-the-art methods (MultiPartiteRank, YAKE) while running **up to two orders of magnitude
faster** than YAKE and MultiPartiteRank across 15 benchmark datasets. The scalability demonstration is striking — it
extracted keyphrases from **14 million biomedical documents (MeDAL corpus) in approximately 40 seconds** on a 12-core,
32 GB machine. The algorithm's overall complexity is effectively **O(|T| log |T|)** in practice (near-linear when graphs
are sparse, as they almost always are). Published at DS2022. Code is available at https://github.com/SkBlaz/rakun2.

## The problem (in plain language)

When you have a large document collection — say, Dan's 14M-paper biomedical corpus, or the KnowledgeBase growing over
time — and you need to index, retrieve, or summarize documents, someone has to decide what each document is about. That
"about-ness" is operationalized as a ranked list of keyphrases: multi-word or single-word tokens that best represent the
document's content. Good keyphrases enable document retrieval, topic clustering, knowledge-graph edge labeling, and RAG
context selection.

The problem is that most decent keyphrase extractors are slow — YAKE and MultiPartiteRank take seconds per document. At
14 million documents, that's weeks of compute just to index. Neural methods like KeyBERT are even slower and require a
transformer pass per document. On the other end, simple term-frequency baselines are extremely fast but mediocre — they
don't understand context or phrase structure, so they produce redundant, low-quality keyphrases.

What was missing was a method that sits at the Pareto frontier: fast enough to scale to millions of documents, good
enough to match neural and graph-based competitors on retrieval benchmarks, and simple enough to run without GPU or
fine-tuning infrastructure.

## What they propose

RaKUn 2.0 extends an earlier meta-vertex-based approach (RaKUn) with two engineering-level improvements that jointly
push computation time down by orders of magnitude.

**Step 1 — Tokenization.** The document is tokenized using standard methods. Stopwords are removed with NLTK's default
English stopwords list. This is conventional.

**Step 2 — Token merging (the first main contribution).** The original RaKUn built a token graph first, then merged
nearby nodes into "meta vertices." RaKUn 2.0 inverts this: it merges tokens at the _sequence level_, before the graph is
built. For each adjacent token pair (t_i, t_j), it computes a merge score:

MSscore = (|#t_i - #b_ij| + |#t_j - #b_ij|) / (#t_i + #t_j)

where #t_i and #t_j are individual token counts and #b_ij is the bigram count. If MSscore falls below a user-specified
threshold τ, the two tokens are merged into a single multi-word phrase token, and individual token counts are
downweighted by the MSscore factor. Values of MSscore below one favor multi-word keyphrases; values above one favor
single-token keyphrases. Bigram counts are pre-computed via **bi-gram hashing**, making lookups O(1). Because merging
happens before graph construction, no expensive string-comparison metric is needed — locality is sufficient for quality.

**Step 3 — Graph construction.** A weighted directed token graph G is built from the post-merge token sequence: nodes
are (lowercased) tokens, edges are co-occurrences in a sliding window, edge weights are incremented every time a bigram
repeats. Dense, clique-like graphs only arise if tokens appear in highly diverse contexts — uncommon in practice — so
the resulting graphs are sparse.

**Step 4 — Personalized PageRank.** Rather than plain PageRank, the algorithm uses personalized PageRank where the
personalization vector is constructed from token term counts. This biases the random walk toward high-frequency tokens,
effectively incorporating global frequency information into the graph-structural ranking. The final score for each token
is the element-wise product of the PageRank score and the token length (number of component words). Multiplying by token
length biases the output toward multi-word phrases, which are empirically more informative as keyphrases.

**Step 5 — Deduplication and output.** Case-level duplicates (e.g., 'City' and 'city') are removed. The top-K scoring
tokens are returned.

The complexity analysis: tokenization and merging are O(|T|) (one pass). Graph construction is O(|T|). Personalized
PageRank is O(|T| + |L|) where |L| is the number of links — effectively O(|T|) for sparse graphs. Sorting is O(|T| log
|T|). Total: **O(|T| log |T|)**, near-linear in practice.

## Key results

The paper evaluates across 15 benchmark datasets (wiki20, fao30, fao780, theses100, citeulike180, Nguyen2007,
SemEval2010, SemEval2017, 500N-KPCrowd-v1.1, PubMed, Schutz2008, kdd, Inspec, Schatz2008, Krapivin2009, www) covering
scientific papers, web pages, and news articles, with varying document lengths (74–7728 tokens on average) and corpus
sizes (20–2304 documents).

**Retrieval performance (F1@10, averaged):** MultiPartiteRank leads on F1@10 overall, but RaKUn 2.0 performs comparably
— the critical difference diagram (Friedman-Nemenyi test, p < 0.05) shows RaKUn 2.0, YAKE, and MultiPartiteRank are in
the same non-significantly-different group on F1@15. The difference is statistically insignificant. On Precision@10,
RaKUn 2.0 shows a marginal overall dominance.

**Speed (computation time per document):** RaKUn 2.0 is **1 to 2 orders of magnitude faster** than YAKE and
MultiPartiteRank (the log₁₀(A/B) pairwise matrix shows RaKUn 2.0 vs. YAKE ≈ 1.3–1.7 log-units faster, meaning 20–50×
speedup). Against KeyBERT, the gap is even larger. On the www dataset, RaKUn 2.0 runs in ~0.07 seconds per document vs.
KeyBERT-(1,2) at 5.64 seconds. Only the trivial term-frequency baseline is faster.

**Duplication rate:** RaKUn 2.0's keyphrase output has low duplication (closely grouped with SingleRank,
MultiPartiteRank, and KeyBERT-(1,1) at roughly 2–3% duplication proportion). TextRank and TopicalPageRank are the worst
offenders at ~10–12%. Low duplication means the top-K keyphrases cover distinct concepts, which is what you want for
indexing and retrieval.

**14M biomedical documents (MeDAL corpus):** Processed in approximately 40 seconds on 12 cores with 32 GB RAM. Top
keyphrases extracted at the corpus level (merge threshold = 1): "presence", "molecular weights", "glutamine synthetase",
"growth hormone", "arterial blood" — all plausible biomedical summary terms. Lowering the threshold to 0.5 shifts to
single-word topics: "activity", "concentration", "enzyme."

**Polygon visualization:** The paper introduces a radar-chart view across all three criteria (retrieval, duplication,
time). RaKUn 2.0 dominates MultiPartiteRank on time while being comparable on retrieval, and is better than YAKE on
duplication.

## What's reusable in Linus

RaKUn 2.0 has a direct role in Linus, specifically in the KnowledgeBase ingestion and indexing pipeline.

**Direct — Phase 2 (KnowledgeBase v1 ingestion):** The KnowledgeBase module needs to extract keyphrases from Dan's paper
corpus for at least two purposes: (1) populating knowledge-graph node labels and edge signals, and (2) building a
lightweight full-text index for fast document retrieval. RaKUn 2.0 is the right tool for both. It requires no GPU, no
pretrained model weights, no fine-tuning data — just Python and a tokenizer. The `rakun2` library (`pip install rakun2`)
is CPU-only and would run natively in the `linus` conda environment without any Metal/ANE complications.

**Direct — Phase 3 (Knowledge & Parallel Agents):** At scale, when the KnowledgeBase grows to thousands or tens of
thousands of documents, RaKUn 2.0's near-linear scaling matters. A local Worker agent could re-index the entire corpus
in seconds rather than hours, making incremental KB updates cheap enough to run automatically after each ingestion
event.

**Direct — Phase 2 (RAG context selection):** When a Worker query arrives, the retrieved document chunks need to be
summarized into a context snippet. Keyphrase extraction is a lightweight way to tag chunks with their topics before
passing them to a generative model, reducing the prompt length while preserving the key conceptual signal.

**Indirect — Knowledge graph construction (Phase 3):** The paper's connection to personalized PageRank and graph-based
representations of text is directly relevant to the KnowledgeBase's knowledge-graph layer (see `2003.02320v6.md`). RaKUn
2.0's token graph is a micro-scale co-occurrence graph per document; the KB's knowledge graph is a macro-scale graph
across documents. The same spectral/structural intuitions apply. RaKUn 2.0 keyphrases could serve as node seed
candidates for graph construction.

**Indirect — Biomedical domain validation:** The MeDAL 14M-document experiment used exactly the kind of corpus Dan works
with — biomedical literature. The top keyphrases that came out ("glutamine synthetase", "growth hormone", "molecular
weight") are credible, which is a meaningful signal that the method is domain-appropriate without domain tuning.

**Indirect — Evaluation methodology (Phase 1 Recon):** The paper's multi-criteria evaluation framework — retrieval F1,
duplication rate, and computation time, visualized as a radar chart across algorithms — is a useful template for how
`benchmarks/dan_tasks/` should evaluate any Linus indexing component. Not just "does it retrieve the right things?" but
also "how fast is it?" and "does it produce diverse, non-redundant outputs?"

## What's NOT applicable / hype filter

The main limitation is **domain narrowness in evaluation**. All 15 benchmark datasets are short-to-medium documents in
scientific and web domains with English text. The evaluation doesn't include long documents (books, theses),
multilingual text, or highly structured documents (code files, genomic annotation tables). If Dan's KnowledgeBase
contains non-English papers or highly structured scientific data files, RaKUn 2.0's quality may degrade without
adaptation.

The **merge threshold hyperparameter τ** requires tuning. The paper uses τ = 1 as default, which produces multi-word
phrases. Lowering it to 0.5 yields single-word outputs. There's no automated way to choose τ for a new domain without
some labeled keyphrases to validate against — which is a friction point for unsupervised deployment.

**No semantic understanding.** RaKUn 2.0 is entirely structural (co-occurrence + frequency). It cannot distinguish "gene
expression regulation" (a meaningful biological concept) from "gene expression" + "expression regulation" produced by a
tokenization artifact. Neural methods like KeyBERT — despite being 100× slower — capture semantic similarity in a way
that RaKUn 2.0 cannot. For Linus, this means RaKUn 2.0 is the right choice for fast, large-scale first-pass indexing,
but should not be treated as the final word on semantic keyphrase quality for high-stakes tasks (e.g., building
knowledge-graph edges that will anchor downstream reasoning).

**Multi-document and cross-document keyphrasing is out of scope.** The paper's 14M-document experiment extracts
corpus-level keyphrases by treating all documents as one stream — useful for exploratory topic discovery but not for
per-document indexing at scale. The algorithm is designed for single-document keyphrase ranking.

The **Python library** (`rakun2`) is a pure-Python implementation. At millions of documents, the 40-second runtime from
the paper was achieved with 12 CPU cores. On Dan's M1 Max with 10 CPU cores, expect comparable throughput — but this
relies on Python-level parallelism, not Metal acceleration. The ANE and GPU cores won't help here unless someone
rewrites the PageRank in MLX.

## Connections

RaKUn 2.0 sits at the intersection of graph-based NLP and the KnowledgeBase infrastructure track.

The **knowledge graph** connection is direct: [2003.02320v6.md](2003.02320v6.md) covers knowledge graphs as a
representational framework. RaKUn 2.0's output keyphrases are precisely the raw material from which KG nodes are
bootstrapped — the paper effectively provides a fast, unsupervised node-candidate generator for the kind of
document-level KG that [2003.02320v6.md](2003.02320v6.md) theorizes about.

The **embedding quality** connection runs through [2408.08073v2.md](2408.08073v2.md): that paper establishes how to
build good document embeddings from transformer representations, while this paper establishes how to extract structural
keyphrases without any embedding at all. In a well-designed KnowledgeBase pipeline, these two approaches are
complementary — RaKUn 2.0 provides fast symbolic keyphrase tags; the embedding recipe from
[2408.08073v2.md](2408.08073v2.md) provides the dense vector representation. Both inform Phase 2 KB construction.

The **curse of dimensionality** [2401.00422v3.md](2401.00422v3.md) is indirectly relevant: the token graphs RaKUn 2.0
builds are intentionally sparse (low-dimensional effective structure), which is why PageRank converges cheaply. The
pathology that paper documents — distance concentration in high dimensions — doesn't afflict RaKUn 2.0 because it never
operates in the high-dimensional embedding space.

The **FineWeb** paper [2406.17557v2.md](2406.17557v2.md) and this one share a methodological attitude: both argue that
evaluation methodology is itself an underappreciated contribution, and both run comprehensive multi-dataset ablations
rather than cherry-picking favorable benchmarks.

On the repo side, `modules/KnowledgeBase/` is the primary home for this integration. The `rakun2` Python package would
be an external dependency, not requiring any repo clone.

## Open questions for Dan

1. **Is there a parallelism opportunity in the KB ingestion pipeline?** The paper mentions that further work includes
   "exploration of lower-level implementations" and parallelism. At 40 seconds for 14M documents on 12 cores, the
   throughput is roughly 350K documents/second. Dan's current corpus is orders of magnitude smaller, so this is not yet
   a bottleneck — but the question is whether to structure the KB ingestion loop to process documents in parallel
   batches from the start, so the architecture doesn't need to be refactored later.

2. **Should RaKUn 2.0 keyphrases feed directly into Qdrant as document metadata tags, or into the knowledge graph as
   node labels, or both?** The architecture choice here affects how the KB retrieval layer is structured in Phase 2
   and 3. Keyphrases-as-tags enables fast filter-based retrieval in Qdrant (no embedding comparison needed for tag
   search); keyphrases-as-graph-nodes enables multi-hop reasoning. These are not mutually exclusive, but the
   implementation sequence matters.
