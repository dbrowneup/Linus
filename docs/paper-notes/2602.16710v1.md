# EgoScale: Scaling Dexterous Manipulation with Diverse Egocentric Human Data

## TL;DR

EgoScale (NVIDIA GEAR Lab + UC Berkeley + UMD, Feb 2026) trains a flow-based Vision-Language-Action (VLA) policy on
**20,854 hours of action-labeled egocentric human video** — more than 20x larger than any prior human-to-robot transfer
study — and uncovers a remarkably clean **log-linear scaling law** between human pretraining data scale and validation
loss: `L = 0.024 - 0.003 · ln(D)` with R² = 0.9983, where D is hours of pretraining data. The validation loss
**strongly correlates with downstream real-robot task performance**, establishing offline human data as a predictable
supervision source for embodied control. The two-stage transfer recipe — large-scale unlabeled human pretraining
followed by a small (~50h human + ~4h robot) aligned mid-training stage — yields a 54% absolute average success-rate
improvement on a 22-DoF dexterous robot hand over a no-pretraining baseline, with emergent one-shot generalization
(0.88 success on shirt folding from a single robot demonstration) and cross-embodiment transfer to a lower-DoF
tri-finger hand on the Unitree G1. Sibling paper to [DreamZero](2602.15922v1.md) from the same NVIDIA GEAR drop on
2026-02-19; both are world-action-model lineage descendants of [WHAM](s41586-025-08600-3.md).

## The problem (in plain language)

Robots are expensive to teach by teleoperation. A human running errands, cooking, ironing, sorting cards generates
behavior at a scale that no robot fleet can match — millions of hours of dexterous manipulation are sitting in
egocentric video archives waiting to be used as supervision. The catch: human hands are not robot hands. They have
different kinematics, are observed from a head-mounted camera rather than from the robot's own perception stack, and
the videos are noisy — SLAM drift, hand-pose estimation errors, occlusions, and unconstrained collection environments.

Prior human-to-robot transfer work has been demonstrated, but at small scale (tens to hundreds of hours of human data)
and usually on grippers or low-DoF hands where finger articulation is barely a concern. So the open question was
whether the scaling-law logic that powered language-model progress — pour in more data, watch the loss come down on a
predictable curve, see capability follow — actually applies to embodied human-to-robot transfer for high-degree-of-
freedom dexterous manipulation. If yes, human video is essentially free supervision and the field is very early on a
power curve. If no (saturation, transfer collapse, or noise overwhelming signal beyond some scale), the right strategy
is to keep collecting expensive teleoperated robot data instead.

## What they propose

**EgoScale** is a three-stage pipeline. **Stage I (pretraining)** trains a flow-matching VLA policy — VLM backbone +
DiT action expert, architecture borrowed from NVIDIA's GR00T N1 — on 20,854 hours of egocentric human video. Hand pose
is extracted with off-the-shelf perception (21 keypoints per hand, plus camera pose from SLAM) and converted into a
unified action representation: relative wrist motion in SE(3) for arm trajectories, plus 22-DoF retargeted joint angles
mapped to the Sharpa dexterous hand for finger articulation. Roughly 829 hours come from EgoDex (Apple Vision Pro,
clean kinematics) and the rest from in-the-wild egocentric recordings (9,869 scenes, 6,015 tasks, 43,237 objects).
Training runs on 256 GB200 GPUs, batch size 8,192, 100K steps, all parameters unfrozen.

**Stage II (mid-training)** introduces a small **aligned human-robot play dataset**: 344 tabletop tasks with about 30
human trajectories and 5 robot trajectories each (~50h human + ~4h robot total). Critically, the human data here is
collected with the **same camera rig as the robot** — head-mounted plus two wrist cameras — and the same motion-capture
stack (Vive trackers + Manus gloves) that drives robot teleoperation. This stage freezes the VLM backbone, updates the
vision encoder and DiT action expert, and grounds the abstract pretrained representations in the robot's actual
sensing/control space. **Stage III (post-training)** fine-tunes on per-task robot demonstrations (typically 100, fewer
for deformable tasks).

The key conceptual move is **decoupling scale from alignment**: Stage I provides diversity and semantic grounding from
huge but noisy data; Stage II supplies precise embodiment correspondence from a small but clean dataset. The unified
wrist-level action representation is what allows a single model to ingest both.

## Key results

The headline finding is the scaling law. With models pretrained on 1k, 2k, 4k, 10k, and 20k hours of human data, the
optimal validation loss at convergence falls on a near-perfect log-linear curve: **L = 0.024 - 0.003 · ln(D), R² =
0.9983**. There is no saturation in the explored range — at 20k hours the loss is still descending the line. Average
downstream task completion score climbs monotonically from 0.30 (1k hours) to 0.71 (20k hours), and **validation loss
closely tracks task completion across data scales**, establishing offline loss as a meaningful predictor of online
performance rather than just an offline metric.

On the five-task evaluation suite (Shirt Rolling, Card Sorting, Tongs-for-Fruit-Transfer, Bottle-Cap Unscrewing,
Syringe Liquid Transfer — the latter requiring multi-step long-horizon dexterity), the pretrain+midtrain configuration
beats the no-pretraining baseline by **54% absolute average task success**, and beats human-pretraining-alone or
mid-training-alone consistently. Notably, large-scale human pretraining alone, despite being noisy and unaligned,
already outperforms the mid-training-only baseline on most tasks — diversity is doing real work even before
embodiment alignment is applied.

One-shot generalization is striking: with a **single robot demonstration plus 100 aligned human demos** of an unseen
task, the pretrain+midtrain policy hits 0.88 success on Fold Shirt and 0.55 on Unscrewing Water Bottles. Neither task
is in the mid-training data; the lift comes from shared motion primitives (folding-like, rotation-like) being
transferred from related midtraining behaviors. Cross-embodiment transfer also holds: human-pretrained representations
ported to the Unitree G1 (7-DoF tri-finger hand, much shorter arm reach) yield over 30% absolute success-rate
improvement on Pen-in-Bin and Dish-in-Rack handover compared to a G1-only baseline, suggesting the learned action
representation encodes embodiment-agnostic manipulation primitives (open, close, coordinated finger articulation)
rather than Sharpa-hand-specific features.

A representation-design ablation cleanly justifies the 22-DoF retargeted joint-space action space: wrist-only
supervision performs poorly on contact-sensitive tasks (Cards, Tongs); fingertip-based representations are
inconsistent because small fingertip-pose errors map to implausible joint configurations; full retargeted joint actions
are the most consistent across tasks. This matters because it identifies _which signal in the human video matters_,
not just how much of it there is.

## What's reusable in Linus

EgoScale lives mostly in **Phase 7-8 territory** — embodied AI, robotics, dexterous manipulation. None of those are in
Linus's current scope. But three pieces port forward.

**The scaling-law framing is the single most-portable insight.** A clean log-linear validation-loss-vs-data-scale
relationship, fit with R² = 0.9983, that strongly correlates with downstream task success is a generalizable "**how do
I know my data is enough**" template. For any **Phase 6 fine-tuning** loop where Linus wants a reliable extrapolation
from validation loss to actual task performance — domain-LoRA on Qwen3, on Kimi-K2, or on whatever the Phase 6 base
model turns out to be — the EgoScale recipe is: train at several data scales (1x, 2x, 4x, 10x, 20x), plot loss vs
ln(data), check whether you get a clean log-linear relationship, and only then trust extrapolation. If the curve is
clean, you have a predictable supervision source; if it's noisy, you do not, and adding more data is a gamble. This is
the [Chinchilla](2203.15556v1.md) discipline applied to fine-tuning rather than to pretraining: the relevant axis is
how much loss-per-token-of-data you're getting, and whether that translates to downstream wins. Surface as a
**watch-the-field methodology candidate** for Phase 6.

**The two-stage pretraining → mid-training recipe has structural rhyme with Linus's planned LoRA patterns.** EgoScale
first absorbs huge noisy unconstrained data with full unfreeze (Stage I), then aligns to the deployment surface with a
small clean dataset and partial-freeze (Stage II). Linus's planned **LoRA-on-Qwen3 / LoRA-on-Kimi-K2** workflow is the
same pattern: a large general-purpose pretraining is already done by upstream, and Linus's job is the equivalent of
EgoScale's Stage II — a small high-quality dataset (Dan's papers, threads, code, notes) that anchors the general
representation to a specific deployment surface (Dan's actual workflow). The discipline lesson: keep the alignment
dataset _small and clean_ rather than chasing scale at the alignment stage. Note as **inspiration** for Phase 6
fine-tuning planning.

**Typed structured prediction lens.** The biology-skills convention (S25, in CLAUDE.md) — predictive outputs wrap
machine-queryable structure around free-text rationale — has a parallel in EgoScale's choice to predict structured
22-DoF joint actions rather than raw fingertip trajectories. Structured outputs are easier to verify, debug, and chain
into downstream systems. When Linus eventually builds skills that emit predictions for Dan to act on, the EgoScale
finding (structured beats less-structured for downstream task success) is supporting evidence for the convention.

What does **not** port: the model itself (3D physical control, no language-task analogue in Linus), the GR00T N1
backbone, the GB200 training scale, the Sharpa hand specifics, the entire hardware stack. Watch only — do not study,
do not adapt.

## What's NOT applicable / hype filter

The training scale is preposterously out of Linus's regime: 256 GB200 GPUs at batch size 8,192 for 100K steps is far
beyond a single M1 Max. No piece of the actual training pipeline runs locally. The 22-DoF Sharpa hand and Galaxea R1
Pro humanoid platform are NVIDIA-internal hardware Linus will never touch. The egocentric data pipeline (head-mounted
cameras, Vive trackers, Manus gloves) is a robotics-specific apparatus with no Linus analogue.

The scaling law itself is _domain-specific_: it is a curve fit through human-action-prediction validation loss over
egocentric video data scale, and the coefficients (`0.024 - 0.003 · ln(D)`) say nothing about LLM fine-tuning loss
curves. The transferable insight is the **methodological template** ("test for log-linear loss-vs-data-scale, validate
that loss correlates with task success") not the numbers. Anyone quoting the 0.003 slope in a non-robotics setting is
overclaiming.

The "human as scalable embodiment" framing is rhetorically appealing but technically narrow: it works because hand
kinematics, while different across embodiments, share enough structure (open, close, articulate) that retargeting
provides useful supervision. The same logic does not obviously transfer to, say, "LLM as a scalable embodiment for
scientific reasoning" — the underlying primitives are not as well-shared and the analogy breaks down quickly.

The one-shot generalization is real but narrow: it works on tasks structurally similar to mid-training tasks
(rotation-like, folding-like). It is not zero-shot on truly novel motion structures, and the paper is honest about this
("This generalization is enabled by shared motion structure between mid-training and evaluation tasks"). Read the
0.88-from-one-demo number with that caveat.

## Connections

The most direct sibling is [DreamZero](2602.15922v1.md), authored in parallel from the same NVIDIA GEAR Lab paper drop
on 2026-02-19. Both papers are framed as **world-action-model** descendants — VLA policies that bind perception,
language, and action into a unified flow-matching or autoregressive predictor. Read together, the two papers stake out
NVIDIA GEAR's coherent agenda: scale embodied learning by exploiting non-robot data sources (human video for EgoScale,
synthetic dreams for DreamZero) with structured action representations.

The most important upstream anchor is [WHAM (Microsoft Research, 2025)](s41586-025-08600-3.md), the existing
**world-action-model thread** in `infra-foundations-synthesis.md`. WHAM's tokenization-as-interface argument — train a
single transformer on interleaved heterogeneous modalities (image + action) and let positional embeddings sort it out
— is the design pattern EgoScale and DreamZero both inherit. EgoScale's flow-matching action expert is a continuous
analogue of WHAM's discrete action vocabulary; the architecture-shape lineage is the same.

The scaling-law framing connects directly to [Chinchilla (Hoffmann et al., 2022)](2203.15556v1.md). EgoScale is to
human-to-robot transfer what Chinchilla is to LLM pretraining: the canonical paper that establishes a clean fit between
data scale and loss, validates it with a held-out experiment, and uses it to make predictable claims about downstream
performance. The methodological move — fit the curve, validate the extrapolation, ground operational decisions in
measured scaling — is the most Chinchilla-esque transfer paper to date in robotics.

The infra-foundations rollup ([infra-foundations-synthesis.md](../syntheses/infra-foundations-synthesis.md)) is where
the WHAM thread lives; EgoScale and DreamZero extend that thread into 2026 with the NVIDIA GEAR additions. The
[llm-hardware-design synthesis](../syntheses/llm-hardware-design-synthesis.md) carries an **idea→reality** thread —
how abstract design intent gets bound to physical execution — and human-to-robot transfer is another arm of the same
move: human intent (reaching, grasping, manipulating) bound to robot execution through a learned action prior. EgoScale
adds a robotics datapoint to that cross-thread.

The structured-prediction thread (S25 in CLAUDE.md, BioReason-Pro framing) finds an unexpected ally here: EgoScale's
ablation showing that retargeted joint-space actions beat fingertip trajectories is the same lesson — typed structure
in the prediction wins over flatter representations. The biology-skills convention has a robotics cousin.

## Open questions for Dan

1. **Should Linus prototype the scaling-law methodology on a Phase 6 LoRA loop?** EgoScale's discipline — train at
   1x/2x/4x/10x scales of fine-tuning data, fit a log-linear curve, validate that loss correlates with task success — is
   directly portable to Phase 6 LoRA-on-Qwen3 work. Worth a small experiment when Phase 6 starts: pick a Dan-relevant
   eval task, train LoRAs at five data scales, plot loss vs ln(data), see whether the relationship is clean enough to
   extrapolate from. If yes, Linus has a principled "is my fine-tuning data enough" tool. If no, that is itself useful
   information about the unpredictability of the regime.

2. **Is there a Linus-equivalent of the Stage I / Stage II decoupling?** EgoScale gets value from separating
   "huge noisy data" (Stage I) from "small clean alignment data" (Stage II) with different freeze strategies. Linus's
   Phase 6 plan is closer to Stage II only — small clean Dan-domain data on top of an already-pretrained base model.
   Worth thinking about whether there's a meaningful Stage I analogue Linus could exploit (e.g., a noisy bulk-ingest of
   Dan's full email + chat history before the curated paper-corpus alignment) or whether the upstream pretraining
   already serves that role and Linus only needs Stage II.

3. **Does the world-action-model paradigm deserve its own watch-the-field lane in Linus?** With WHAM (2025) +
   EgoScale + DreamZero (2026), the world-action-model family now has three serious Nature/arXiv datapoints in 18
   months. None are in Linus's current Phase 1-6 scope, but the lineage (image+action tokens, structured prediction,
   capability-grounded evaluation) keeps generating transferable methodology. Worth carrying as a tracked thread in the
   syntheses, separate from but parallel to the LLM thread.

4. **Are there structured-output design lessons Linus should adopt now from the EgoScale ablation?** The
   wrist-only / fingertip / full-retargeted-joint comparison is a textbook demonstration that the choice of structured
   output space dominates downstream task performance. The biology-skills convention (S25) already says "use typed
   structured prediction"; the EgoScale finding sharpens that into "the typed structure should be at the granularity
   that matches the downstream operation, not the easiest-to-emit granularity." Worth reflecting in the doc-type
   conventions when biology-skills authoring spec gets written.

5. **Is the 20x scale advance over prior work reproducible by anyone outside NVIDIA?** EgoScale's 20,854-hour
   pretraining used 256 GB200 GPUs. The smaller-scale fits (1k, 2k hours) are within reach of a small academic lab,
   but the headline 20k-hour number is not. Worth tracking whether the field consolidates around a publicly-released
   pretrained EgoScale-style backbone (analogous to how everyone now uses publicly-released pretrained LLMs rather than
   training their own) — that would change which parts of this paper become Linus-actionable.
