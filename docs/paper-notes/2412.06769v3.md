# Training Large Language Models to Reason in a Continuous Latent Space

## TL;DR

"Training Large Language Models to Reason in a Continuous Latent Space" by Shibo Hao, Sainbayar Sukhbaatar, DiJia Su,
Xian Li, Zhiting Hu, Jason Weston, and Yuandong Tian (FAIR at Meta + UC San Diego) introduces **Coconut** (Chain of
Continuous Thought), a paradigm where the model's last hidden state is fed back as the next input embedding instead of
being decoded into a word token. This frees reasoning from the language bottleneck and lets a single "continuous
thought" superpose multiple alternative next steps — yielding implicit breadth-first search rather than committing
prematurely to one chain-of-thought path. On planning-heavy logical reasoning (ProsQA), Coconut outperforms
language-space CoT while emitting substantially fewer tokens at inference. This is the "slow, deep thinking" paper
Garrison cites as an exemplar of recurrent state maintenance done right.

## The problem (in plain language)

A standard LLM that wants to reason has to do so by emitting words. Chain-of-thought (CoT) prompting works, but it
forces every step of the model's deliberation to be serialized through the discrete token vocabulary and then
re-embedded on the next forward pass. Two costs follow. First, most of the tokens in a typical CoT trace are textual
filler — connectives, hedges, restatements — that exist for fluency rather than for reasoning, but the model spends the
same per-token compute on each of them. Second, the few tokens that actually carry the planning load (where to branch,
which subgoal to pursue) collapse the model's internal distribution over possibilities into a single sampled word,
killing the alternatives the model was implicitly considering one position earlier.

Neuroimaging evidence the authors cite reinforces the architectural intuition: the brain regions responsible for
language comprehension are largely _inactive_ during many reasoning tasks. Human language appears to be optimized for
communication, not for thought. The paper's question is whether an LLM can also be allowed to think outside the language
space and only convert back to words when it needs to communicate an answer.

## What the paper proposes

Coconut keeps the standard transformer architecture and the standard token interface, but inserts a "latent mode"
between the question and the answer. Special tokens `<bot>` and `<eot>` mark the boundaries of latent reasoning. While
inside latent mode, the model takes its own last hidden state at step `t` and uses it directly as the input embedding at
step `t+1`, bypassing the language model head and the embedding lookup entirely. The continuous thought is the reasoning
state; it is fully differentiable end-to-end.

Training uses a multi-stage curriculum inspired by Deng et al. (2024). Stage 0 is normal CoT supervised fine-tuning. At
stage `k`, the first `k` language reasoning steps in each training example are replaced by `k × c` continuous thoughts
(the paper uses `c = 1`). The optimizer state is reset between stages. Crucially, the loss does _not_ try to make the
continuous thought reconstruct the removed language step — the objective only asks that subsequent token prediction stay
accurate. This frees the latent state to encode reasoning in whatever form is most useful, rather than compressing
language.

At inference, the authors found that simply padding the latent segment to a constant length works as well as training a
classifier to decide when to exit latent mode, so they use the simpler constant-pad scheme.

A key emergent property identified in the analysis: a single continuous thought can hold a _superposition_ of candidate
next steps because there is no sampling step forcing a commitment. Across multiple latent steps the model effectively
performs an implicit breadth-first search, with incorrect branches being progressively suppressed and correct ones
reinforced — without ever being trained to do tree search.

## Key results

On math reasoning (GSM8k, Cobbe et al. 2021), continuous thoughts improve accuracy and the benefit grows with the number
of chained continuous thoughts — suggesting that scaling latent reasoning depth is a real lever rather than a
fixed-width trick.

On logical reasoning over directed acyclic graphs of relations, Coconut outperforms language-space CoT on the harder
benchmarks. On the authors' new **ProsQA** (Proof with Search Question-Answering) dataset — which embeds reasoning
targets in DAGs that demand explicit planning — Coconut and several of its variants beat CoT while generating
substantially fewer tokens during inference. Coconut is also compared against ProntoQA (Saparov & He 2022), the
established logical reasoning benchmark.

The interpolation experiments are particularly informative: by manually moving the `<eot>` boundary, the authors sweep
between fully-language reasoning (`k=0`) and fully-latent reasoning (`k=6`) on the same trained model. This sweep is
what lets them argue the latent steps are doing real work rather than being a regularizer or a compression trick.

The base model for the controlled experiments is GPT-2, learning rate 1e-4, effective batch size 128, with `N=6`
training stages of 5 epochs each plus a final 50-epoch stage. Modest scale on purpose — the contribution is the
paradigm, not a frontier sweep.

## What's reusable for Linus

Read through the [memory lens Garrison establishes](../../context/notes/garrison_memory_makes_computation_universal.md),
Coconut is the cleanest published instance so far of a Linus-relevant pattern: use the model's own last hidden state as
durable, recursive reasoning state, and let it be re-read on the next step without ever flattening it through a discrete
vocabulary. This is exactly the _recursive state maintenance_ requirement of Garrison's Theorem 1 — `s_{t+1} = f(s_t)`
with the carry being a real-valued vector rather than a sampled token. CoT also satisfies recursive state maintenance,
but only after a lossy detour through token space; Coconut removes the detour.

The Garrison-framed implication for Linus is direct. The Maestro-Worker protocol assumes a Worker can carry coherent
state across the steps of a delegated task. Today, in practice, that state is whatever survives in the conversation
transcript — i.e. the language-mode carry. Coconut suggests a second axis worth tracking inside Linus: **what continuous
reasoning state, if any, is being preserved between Worker steps, and is it being thrown away when we re-tokenize at
every turn?** Even without adopting Coconut as an inference paradigm, naming this distinction in the architecture
clarifies what kind of memory we are actually preserving.

The latent-as-superposition claim also matters for Linus's _reliable history access_ pillar. If a single continuous
thought can carry a distribution over alternatives, then the memory primitive is not just "remember the chosen path" but
"remember the considered alternatives that were not chosen." For an episodic store that wants to support retry, what-if,
and after-the-fact debugging of a Worker session, retaining the considered-but-not-taken branches is much more valuable
than retaining only the final transcript. Coconut hints at the data structure this would need.

For Phase 1 (Recon & Baselines), the paper's manipulation methodology — moving the `<eot>` token to interpolate between
fully-language and fully-latent reasoning — is a transferable evaluation pattern. Any Linus inference component that
mixes language and latent state (e.g. a future SSM/transformer hybrid Worker) should be evaluated on the same
interpolation sweep, because the average behavior hides where the regime change happens.

For Phase 6 (Fine-Tuning), Coconut's multi-stage curriculum (gradually replacing language reasoning steps with latent
ones, resetting optimizer state between stages) is a concrete recipe that could be applied to a small fine-tuned Linus
model on Dan's task suite. The Meta reference implementation at `https://github.com/facebookresearch/coconut` is a
starting point; checking whether the training loop is MLX-portable is a small Worker-spec.

## What's NOT applicable / hype filter

The headline experiments are on GPT-2 with carefully curated reasoning datasets. ProsQA is constructed to _require_
planning — that's the point of the benchmark, but it also means the gap over CoT is reported under conditions favorable
to Coconut. Whether the same gap holds on heterogeneous real-world Worker tasks (code generation, scientific Q&A, tool
use) is unmeasured.

Training is more expensive than standard CoT fine-tuning. The paper performs `n+1` sequential forward passes per
training step in the latent stage, and the sequential nature does not parallelize cleanly across the latent positions
(KV cache helps but does not eliminate the serial dependency). The authors flag training efficiency as future work. For
Linus's 32 GB unified-memory budget, this matters: a Coconut-style fine-tune would be slower per step than a comparable
LoRA on a CoT trace.

Inference still requires choosing the latent segment length — they default to a constant pad, which is a hyperparameter,
not a learned policy. The "train a classifier to decide when to exit" alternative also worked but was not deployed.

The interpretability cost is real and not waved away. Continuous thoughts cannot be directly read by a human; the only
handle the paper offers is to apply the LM head to the latent state for _probing_ purposes, which is informative but not
a faithful decoding. For Linus's audit-log discipline, a Worker that reasons in latent space leaves an opaque trace by
default, which is in tension with the project's general bias toward inspectable artifacts.

Finally, this is one paradigm, not a new architecture. Coconut does not replace transformers, it modifies the input loop
— so it inherits transformer attention's quadratic cost in the latent segment as well. Garrison's bitter pill (attention
scales badly) is not lifted by Coconut, only sidestepped slightly because latent segments tend to be shorter than the
equivalent language CoT.

## Connections

This paper sits squarely inside the
[Garrison memory thesis](../../context/notes/garrison_memory_makes_computation_universal.md) as one of the constructive
answers to "what does recursive state maintenance look like when you take it seriously?" Garrison cites the structured
state space line (S4, Mamba, Mamba/SSM duality, Hyena) and the "were RNNs all we needed?" work as the architectural side
of the same answer; Coconut is the prompting/paradigm side. Reading Coconut alongside the Garrison note sharpens the
distinction: SSMs change _what kind_ of recurrence the model has, Coconut changes _what gets carried_ across the
recurrence.

It contrasts cleanly with [the Speed paper (2502.16721v1)](2502.16721v1.md) on output verbosity. Coconut's argument that
most CoT tokens are fluency filler is the mechanistic version of the Speed paper's empirical observation that tok/s is a
misleading metric because verbosity dominates wall-clock time. Both papers point at the same waste from different
angles. A Linus-side benchmark that measured tokens-emitted-per-correct-answer would expose this overlap directly.

The training-curriculum reference (Deng et al. 2024 iCoT — "internalizing CoT into latent reasoning by gradually
shortening the CoT") is worth a follow-up note, since iCoT is a closely related lineage and may be more practical for
Linus's compute budget than full Coconut training.

Phase mapping: relevant to Phase 1 (as a methodology to borrow for evaluation interpolation), Phase 6 (as a fine-tuning
paradigm to evaluate against LoRA-on-CoT-traces), and the memory-architecture conversation that the Garrison note argues
should be lifted into Phase 2.

No direct connection to the BitNet quantization papers ([2402.17764v1](2402.17764v1.md) and family) or the keyphrase /
KB infrastructure thread ([2208.07262v1](2208.07262v1.md)) — Coconut is orthogonal to weight precision and to retrieval.
