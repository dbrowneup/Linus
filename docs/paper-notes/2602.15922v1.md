# World Action Models are Zero-shot Policies

## TL;DR

NVIDIA GEAR Lab's DreamZero is a 14B-parameter robot foundation model built by adapting a pretrained image-to-video
diffusion backbone (Wan2.1-I2V-14B-480P) into a **World Action Model (WAM)** that jointly predicts future video frames
and continuous robot actions through a flow-matching objective with shared timesteps across modalities. The thesis is
direct: instead of stacking action heads on a Vision-Language Model (the VLA recipe that dominates the field), inherit
spatiotemporal priors from web-scale video pretraining and let the action head learn an _implicit inverse-dynamics
model_ aligned to predicted visual futures. The empirical payoff is a >2× improvement in zero-shot task progress over
state-of-the-art VLAs (GR00T N1.6, π0.5) on AgiBot G1 and DROID-Franka, and a clean cross-embodiment story: 10–20
minutes of video-only data from a different robot or human demonstrators yields 42%+ relative improvement on unseen
tasks, and 30 minutes of play data adapts the AgiBot-pretrained model to a new YAM robot while retaining zero-shot
generalization. The system contribution is equally load-bearing — a 38× inference speedup stack (CFG parallelism, DiT
caching, NVFP4 quantization, decoupled noise schedules in DreamZero-Flash) drops latency from 5.7s to 150ms on 2×GB200,
enabling real closed-loop control at 7Hz. For Linus this is **out-of-Phase-1-6 territory** — robotics is Phase 7/8
material — but the paradigm is structurally important: a video-model-as-policy is the most credible non-LLM substrate
for action-conditioned reasoning Linus will encounter, and the inference-optimization stack is a cousin of the
weight-streaming and quantization work the corpus already takes seriously. The framing also slots into the
**idea-to-reality** thread that the llm-hardware-design synthesis canonicalizes — robotics is the embodied actor at the
far end of that continuum.

## The problem (in plain language)

State-of-the-art robot foundation models — Vision-Language-Action models (VLAs) like RT-2, π0.5, GR00T N1.6 — extend
pretrained Vision-Language Models with action heads and learn from teleoperation demonstrations. They generalize well on
_semantic_ axes (different objects, different language phrasings of similar tasks) because the underlying VLM has seen
the relevant concepts on the web. They generalize poorly on _physical_ axes — new motions, new environments, new
embodiments — because static image–text pretraining encodes no temporal dynamics. The standard fix is more data:
hundreds of hours of teleoperation, hundreds of environments, hundreds of repeated demonstrations per task. This works
for breadth coverage but is a brute-force answer to a representational deficit, and it does not solve cross-embodiment
transfer at all — every new robot needs its own data collection campaign.

The DreamZero authors propose a different starting point: instead of inheriting from a static image–text model, inherit
from a model that already understands how the world _evolves_ in time. Internet-scale video pretraining produces models
with rich spatiotemporal priors — physics, object permanence, motion plausibility — and the authors argue these priors
are exactly the missing ingredient for physical generalization. The challenge then is turning a video diffusion model
into a real-time policy: video diffusion is iterative (16 denoising steps), expensive (14B parameters), and slow (5.7
seconds per chunk on a single GPU naively). Reactive robotic control needs sub-second latency. The paper's two-pronged
contribution is the WAM training recipe (joint video+action flow matching) and the real-time inference stack (38×
speedup) that makes the video-model-as-policy idea actually tractable on real hardware.

## What they propose

**The architecture.** DreamZero is a 14B autoregressive diffusion transformer initialized from Wan2.1-I2V-14B-480P. It
takes three inputs: visual context (encoded by a pretrained VAE), language instructions (text encoder), and
proprioceptive state (small learned encoder). A single end-to-end DiT backbone processes these jointly and outputs both
predicted future video frames and continuous actions through separate decoders. Multiple camera views are concatenated
spatially into a single frame rather than added as architectural complexity. Only the DiT blocks, state encoder, action
encoder, and action decoder are updated during training; the text encoder, image encoder, and VAE remain frozen. The
authors note that LoRA gave suboptimal results — full DiT fine-tuning was needed.

**The objective.** Flow matching (Lipman, Albergo, Liu) over jointly noisy video latents and normalized actions, with
the model predicting the joint velocity vector. Critically, video and action share the same denoising timestep tₖ during
training (sampled uniformly from [0,1]), enabling tight cross-modal alignment — actions denoise alongside the video
context they are conditioned on. Training uses teacher forcing: each chunk is denoised conditioned on _clean_
ground-truth previous chunks. Chunks contain K=2 latent frames (matching action horizon H=48 steps at 30Hz, so 1.6s per
chunk); the maximum context is 8 latent frames (~6.6s of visual history).

**Autoregressive over bidirectional.** The paper makes a careful argument (Appendix B, Figure 13) that autoregressive
WAMs avoid a structural problem bidirectional WAMs face: when the sampling point falls mid-task, bidirectional models
must subsample video to align with the language caption, distorting native FPS and degrading video–action alignment.
Autoregressive WAMs condition on video context instead, preserving both language–video correspondence and native frame
rate. AR also enables KV-cache reuse during inference and produces noticeably smoother motions in ablation.

**The closed-loop trick.** The structural advantage WAMs hold over open-loop video generation is that during deployment,
real ground-truth observations replace the predicted frames in the KV cache after each action chunk executes. This
eliminates the compounding-error problem inherent to autoregressive video generation — DreamZero's hallucinated frames
are never fed back into itself for more than one chunk before being corrected by reality. The paper presents this as a
load-bearing reason WAMs work as policies: video errors are bounded because the world resets the cache.

**Real-time inference (38× cumulative speedup).** Three layers stack:

- _System level._ CFG parallelism (split classifier-free-guidance forward passes across 2 GPUs, 1.9× on H100). DiT
  caching (cosine-similarity threshold on successive flow-matching velocity vectors; reuse cached velocities when above
  threshold; reduces effective DiT steps from 16 to 4 with minimal loss; 5.5×). Asynchronous execution decouples
  inference from action execution so the motion controller never blocks on the model.
- _Implementation level._ `torch.compile` + CUDA Graphs for kernel fusion and graph capture. NVFP4 quantization on
  Blackwell for weights and activations (with FP8-preserved QKV/Softmax and FP16 LayerNorm/RoPE). cuDNN attention
  backend and GPU-side scheduler operations.
- _Model level — DreamZero-Flash._ Decouples video and action denoising schedules during training: video timesteps are
  drawn from `Beta(7,1)` biased toward high-noise states, while action timesteps stay uniform. This trains the model to
  predict clean actions from noisy visual context, matching the few-step inference regime. Drops inference from 4 steps
  / 350ms to 1 step / 150ms with only ~9% task-progress loss versus the 4-step baseline.

**Cumulative speedup table (Table 1):** baseline → +CFG → +DiT cache → +torch.compile → +kernel/scheduler → +NVFP4 →
+Flash gets to 38× on GB200, achieving 7Hz real-time control.

**Data philosophy.** Unlike VLAs, DreamZero is trained on diverse, non-repetitive teleoperation data: ~500 hours on
AgiBot G1 across 22 environments (homes, restaurants, supermarkets, coffee shops, offices), with episodes averaging 4.4
minutes and ~42 subtasks each. The authors explicitly argue that VLAs need repetition because they are learning direct
observation-to-action mappings; WAMs leverage video pretraining as the heavy-lifting prior and only need to learn
inverse dynamics, which benefits from _diverse_ state-action pairs rather than repeated ones. The DROID-Franka
experiments use the public DROID 1.0.1 dataset converted to LeRobot v2.0 format.

## Key results

- **2× zero-shot generalization over VLAs.** On AgiBot G1 seen-task evaluation in unseen environments, DreamZero scores
  62.2% average task progress vs 27.4% for the best pretrained VLA baseline (π0.5-pretrained), despite VLAs having seen
  thousands of hours of cross-embodiment robot data while DreamZero saw only the 500-hour AgiBot mix. From-scratch VLA
  baselines collapse to near-zero. On DROID-Franka, DreamZero hits 82% task progress vs 62%/69% for VLA baselines.
- **Zero-shot to truly unseen tasks.** On 10 tasks fully absent from training (untie shoelaces, remove hat from
  mannequin, ironing, painting with brush, shake hands, fold map), DreamZero reaches 39.5% average task progress on
  AgiBot G1 — 2.4× the best pretrained VLA (16.3%). Strongest single-task results: "Remove Hat" 85.7%, "Shake Hands"
  59.2%. From-scratch VLAs are at <1%.
- **Post-training retains generalization.** After task-specific post-training on shirt folding, fruit packing, and table
  bussing, DreamZero matches or beats VLA baselines while preserving environment generalization (~10% over the best
  pretrained VLA on average across the three tasks). The pretrained VLAs benefit from cross-embodiment pretraining;
  DreamZero matches without that advantage.
- **Cross-embodiment transfer from video-only data.** 20 min of YAM-robot video (no actions) raises unseen-task
  performance from 38.3% → 55.4%; 12 min of human egocentric video raises it to 54.3%. Both improve substantially over
  baseline using a fraction of the data VLA approaches need.
- **30-minute new-embodiment adaptation.** Post-training the AgiBot-pretrained DreamZero on 30 minutes of YAM play data
  (55 trajectories, 11 tasks) gives a working YAM policy that retains language-following and generalizes to unseen
  objects (pumpkins, teddy bears, pens, cup noodles, paper bags). The paper claims this sets a new benchmark for
  data-efficient embodiment adaptation.
- **Inference: 5.7s → 150ms (38×) on GB200, 5.7s → ~600ms on H100.** Stack contributions documented per-layer in Table
  1. Real-time closed-loop control at 7Hz (~3s on H100 in the README is the practical figure).
- **Ablations.** Diverse data > repetitive data at fixed hours (50% vs 33% PnP-Easy task progress). Model scale matters
  for WAMs — 14B beats 5B (50% vs 21%) — while VLAs at the same sizes both score 0%, suggesting WAM scaling is real and
  VLA scaling is not under this regime. Autoregressive matches bidirectional on accuracy but produces smoother motions
  and runs 3–4× faster via KV caching.
- **Failure mode signature.** Most DreamZero failures stem from _video generation errors_, not action prediction —
  meaning the policy faithfully executes whatever trajectory the predicted video implies. This neatly suggests the
  optimization knob: improve the video backbone, get a better policy.
- **Public release.** RoboArena #1 and MolmoSpaces #1 leaderboard positions as of 2026-02-27. DreamZero-DROID and
  DreamZero-AgiBot checkpoints on HuggingFace (`GEAR-Dreams/DreamZero-DROID`, `GEAR-Dreams/DreamZero-AgiBot`); training
  codebase, preprocessed DROID dataset, post-training scripts, distributed WebSocket inference server all open-sourced
  Apache-2.0 at `dreamzero0/dreamzero`. DreamZero-DROID is trained from scratch on DROID alone — no large-scale robot
  pretraining — and still tops both leaderboards, which the authors flag as evidence for the video-backbone-as-policy
  thesis.

## What's reusable in Linus

**Phase 7/8 — embodied AI is well outside the current phase plane, but mark it as a real possibility.** Linus's roadmap
goes to Phase 8 ("Beyond MacBook"); robotics is one of the genuine destinations that becomes plausible there, and
DreamZero is the most concrete current evidence that video-backbone-as-policy is the architecture for getting there.
This is not a Phase 1–6 work item, but it is a real candidate for the Phase 7+ skill class — particularly if Dan ever
wants Linus to interface with a physical actor (a 3D printer, a fab tool, a microscope stage, a manipulator) in the
**idea-to-reality** loop the llm-hardware-design synthesis canonicalizes.

**Memory architecture, structural rhyme (Phase 2+).** DreamZero's autoregressive KV-cache-with-ground-truth-replacement
mechanism (Algorithm 2: clean observations replace predicted latents at chunk boundaries) is a structural cousin of the
Layer A/B intra-step latent + within-session scratchpad split in Linus's memory pillar (DEC-0028, DEC-0029). Both
architectures pay the cost of stateful representation but bound error accumulation by periodically resetting against
ground truth. The "stateful policy with bounded hallucination via observation grounding" pattern may translate beyond
robotics — e.g., a Linus Worker with persistent reasoning state but periodic re-grounding against the KnowledgeBase or
against tool outputs is the same shape. Worth flagging as an architectural rhyme; not directly reusable code.

**Inference-optimization vocabulary (Phase 6+ research watch).** The 38× speedup stack is GPU-architecture-specific (CFG
parallelism on 2 GPUs, NVFP4 on Blackwell, cuDNN attention) and does not port to Apple Silicon directly. _But_ the
component categories are the same vocabulary Linus's flash-moe and weight-streaming work already operates in:
quantization, KV-cache reuse, CPU-GPU sync elimination, kernel fusion. The DiT-caching trick (cosine-similarity gate on
successive flow-matching velocities) is conceptually portable to any iterative-denoising MLX model — Phase 6+ stretch
work, possibly relevant to MLX-flash diffusion experiments. Mark as inspiration, not direct port.

**The video-model-as-foundation-substrate framing (Phase 6+).** The paper's strongest meta-claim is that the right
choice of foundation-model substrate determines what tasks are tractable — VLMs are wrong for physical generalization,
video models are right. Linus has thus far defaulted to LLM substrates for everything. The WHAM paper-note flagged the
non-LLM-paradigm question; DreamZero pushes it harder by demonstrating that video models, used as policies, leapfrog the
dominant paradigm on its own benchmark. For Linus this is a watch-the-field signal: when Dan eventually considers
non-LLM Worker substrates (scientific simulation, cellular dynamics modeling, materials simulation), video- and
world-model substrates should be on the candidate list.

**Cross-embodiment-style transfer for biology (speculative, Phase 7+).** The 12-minute human-video → robot-policy
transfer result is striking. The structural claim is that the model only needs to learn "video → action" since "video
prediction" is already strong from web pretraining. The biology analogue would be "learn from human-collected
microscopy/imaging data, transfer to instrument-control policies for an automated lab." Speculative; flagged because the
ratio of "minutes of demonstration data needed" to "useful transferred capability" is the right order of magnitude for a
one-person lab. Not a near-term plan, just a possibility.

**Data philosophy: diverse-non-repetitive over task-specific-repetitive.** The ablation showing diverse 500-hour data
beating repetitive 500-hour data (50% vs 33% task progress) is a useful empirical anchor for any Linus skill that ever
considers data scaling: when the underlying foundation model is strong, breadth beats repetition for downstream
generalization. Maps to the KnowledgeBase ingest philosophy and to how Dan would build out personal corpora for any
Phase 6 fine-tunes.

## What's NOT applicable / hype filter

**The whole thing requires NVIDIA datacenter GPUs.** GB200 (Blackwell, ~$60k+ per unit, 8-way nodes for inference) or
H100 (Hopper, similar tier) — the paper measures itself on these and the README explicitly requires Multi-GPU CUDA
12.9+, with most of the 38× speedup contingent on GB200-only optimizations (NVFP4 quantization needs Blackwell;
DreamZero-Flash gets the model to 7Hz only on GB200). The Apple Silicon equivalent does not exist. Even if the model
weights ported to MLX, the inference latency would be hours not seconds without the parallelism stack. Phase 6+
research-direction territory at the absolute earliest, and only if MLX gets diffusion-grade optimization tooling that
matches the NVIDIA stack — which is years out, if it happens at all.

**Robotics is not Linus's product surface.** Dan does not have a robot. Linus does not orchestrate physical
manipulation. The model's primary deliverable — a 14B policy that runs at 7Hz on bimanual robots — has zero use for the
genomics/scientific-computing/personal-AI workflow Linus is built for. The thesis (video models > VLM-action heads) is
interesting; the artifact is for someone else's stack.

**Scale of training is not reproducible.** ~500 hours of teleoperation data across 22 environments with 22 robot
operators (named in the acknowledgements) is industrial-scale data collection. Even the open DROID dataset is 131GB
preprocessed. Pretraining DreamZero is not a thing Dan or Linus reproduces; consuming the released checkpoints at
inference time is the only realistic engagement and even that is GPU-bound.

**14B is too big for Apple Silicon at inference.** Wan2.1-I2V-14B-480P at fp16 is ~28GB of weights — within 32GB unified
memory but with very little headroom for activations during diffusion. The Wan2.2-TI2V-5B alternative the README
mentions is more tractable but still GPU-flavored. There is no obvious "small DreamZero for M1 Max" path; the model is
sized for the cluster.

**LoRA does not work here.** The authors state LoRA gave suboptimal results — full DiT updates were needed. This removes
the standard data-efficient adaptation lever Linus would otherwise reach for in Phase 6.

**RoboArena/MolmoSpaces leaderboards are robotics-specific.** Headline #1 placements are real, but these benchmarks do
not measure anything Linus cares about. The paper's claims about generalization and cross-embodiment are well-supported
on _those_ benchmarks but should not be over-extrapolated to Linus's actual use cases.

**System 1 only — long-horizon reasoning is open.** The paper itself flags this in Section 6: DreamZero is a System 1
model with 6 seconds of visual memory, no long-horizon planner, no explicit task decomposition. Robust long-horizon
execution requires either a System 2 planner on top or an extended-context WAM. This caveat applies to any imagined
Linus integration too — DreamZero is a reactive-controller paradigm, not a deliberation paradigm.

## Open questions for Dan

1. **Phase 7+ robotics question.** DreamZero is explicit evidence that the video-model-as-policy pattern is currently
   the strongest paradigm for embodied AI generalization. Is there value in flagging "embodied actor / lab-instrument
   automation" as an explicit Phase 7+ destination on the roadmap, or is robotics far enough out of scope that even the
   destination marker is over-claiming? The idea-to-reality framing makes a soft case for the destination; the "Linus
   has no robot" reality makes a strong case for not investing now.
2. **MLX video-model substrate as a Phase 6 stretch.** Phase 6 is fine-tuning. The dominant Phase 6 plan is LLM
   LoRA/QLoRA. Does it make sense to scope a parallel Phase 6 _experiment_ — a small video-model port to MLX (say,
   Wan2.2-TI2V-5B or smaller) — purely as a substrate-diversification exercise? Not a roadmap commitment; a bounded
   experiment that would put a working video model on M1 Max and generate transferable MLX know-how. Counterargument: 5B
   at fp16 is ~10GB, leaving room for activations, but the MLX diffusion tooling is immature.
3. **Inference-optimization knowledge transfer.** The 38× speedup stack documents component-by-component how a
   diffusion-based 14B autoregressive model gets to real-time on GPU. Is there value in producing a Linus-internal
   write-up that explicitly maps each component (CFG parallelism, DiT caching, NVFP4, decoupled noise, asynchronous
   execution) to its Apple Silicon equivalent and gap, as a reference for any future MLX-flash diffusion work? This
   would be ~1 day of work and would prepare Phase 6+ research direction without committing to it.
4. **The video-model substrate as non-LLM Worker.** WHAM raised the "non-LLM Worker" question and got "deferred" in the
   open-questions registry. DreamZero is now a second forcing function. Has the answer changed — does Linus have any
   reason to now scope a non-LLM Worker lane explicitly (Phase 6+ or Phase 7), or is the deferral still load-bearing?
5. **Idea-to-reality cross-thread.** This paper-note's connections section flags DreamZero as the robotics arm of the
   idea-to-reality continuum. Should the llm-hardware-design synthesis be lightly extended to acknowledge robotics as a
   third arm (alongside hardware-design via QiMeng and process-engineering via Sketch2Simulation), or is "robotics is
   far enough out of scope that the cross-thread is a watch-link only" the right disposition? The synthesis's
   load-bearing scope is hardware design; robotics is structurally similar but products of a different lab tradition.
6. **G6 / GB200 dependency framing.** DreamZero's leaderboard wins (RoboArena #1, MolmoSpaces #1) are real and worth
   knowing about. Is there value in a recurring "frontier-results-on-incompatible-hardware" tracking discipline — a

   realism of Linus's positioning.
