# WikiAutoGen: Towards Multi-Modal Wikipedia-Style Article Generation

## TL;DR

WikiAutoGen extends the prior **Storm/Co-Storm** line of automated Wikipedia-article generators (which were text-only)
to a **multi-modal** pipeline that retrieves and integrates images alongside text, and adds a **multi-perspective
self-reflection module** in which four named viewpoints — supervisor, writer, reader, editor — critique and revise the
draft against seven explicit criteria (reliability, engagement, informativeness, coherence, readability, consistency,
helpfulness). The system is built on **DSPy** orchestrating GPT-4o (multimodal exploration), GPT-4o-mini (most other
agents), and GPT-o3-mini (the self-reflection critic, chosen for stronger reasoning). They also release **WikiSeek**, a
300-topic benchmark deliberately skewed toward _underexplored_ Wikipedia entries (less than 500, 300, or 100 chars of
body text — "hard," "very hard," "extremely hard"), with topics expressible as text, image, or both. On WikiSeek,
WikiAutoGen beats the strongest text-only baseline (Co-Storm) by roughly 8.8 / 29.1 / 15.0 average points across the
three input modalities, and the gap _widens_ as topics get more obscure. The contribution is mostly _system_
(multi-agent topology + reflection roles + multimodal grounding) and _benchmark_ (deliberately niche topics), not new
model architecture.

## Problem

Automated long-form expository writing — the "explain this topic in a Wikipedia-style article with citations" problem —
has been pushed forward by Storm and Co-Storm with multi-agent perspective-taking and conversational outline generation.
Two limitations remain. First, all of those systems are **text-only**: they don't search for or place images, even
though Wikipedia itself is heavily multi-modal and human evaluators in this paper say that images materially improve
comprehension (99% of MTurk participants agreed). Second, prior systems do well on _common_ topics where retrieval finds
plenty of text, but degrade on _underexplored_ topics where the source material is thin and ambiguous. The Storm-family
pipelines also have minimal explicit quality control: outline → retrieve → write, with little structured criticism at
each step.

For Linus this is a recognizable shape: an orchestration layer dispatches specialized roles, each role calls tools
(retrieval, image search), and a critic loop refines outputs against named criteria. The "deliberate niche topic"
benchmark is also relevant — Dan's KnowledgeBase corpus is dominated by exactly this regime, where a topic
("PRS-mediated stop-codon readthrough in plant chloroplasts," say) has thin Wikipedia coverage and the synthesis must
come from primary literature.

## What they propose

The system has four cooperating modules, all glued together with **DSPy** declarative LM calls:

1. **Outline Proposal.** For a text topic, an LLM drafts a structured outline directly. For an image topic, the system
   runs Google Vision Search on the image, extracts metadata, applies BERT-NER to pull the top-10 most frequent named
   entities, and feeds those plus the original image to the LLM as outline seeds. For image-text topics, both signals
   are combined.

2. **Textual Article Writing.** A _persona generator_ turns the outline into _n_ role-typed agents (e.g., for a
   chemistry topic: a synthesis chemist, a toxicologist, a regulatory expert). A fixed _asker_ agent walks the outline
   asking targeted questions; the persona agents search the web (Serper API, up to 5 pages per query), discuss findings,
   and refine. After enough exploration, a writing agent drafts each section, then iteratively revises each paragraph
   against feedback from the self-reflection module.

3. **Multi-Perspective Self-Reflection.** This is the paper's main novel knob. Four viewpoints, each with its own
   evaluation rubric, fire at different pipeline stages:
   - **Supervisor** — does the content actually answer the asker's questions; is breadth/depth/coherence sufficient; did
     the multi-agent discussion converge?
   - **Writer** — coherence, engagement, factual accuracy, logical consistency; suggests rearranging sentences, adding
     transitions, simplifying jargon.
   - **Reader** — readability, helpfulness, engagement; specifically reviews proposed image _placements_ and image
     descriptions before retrieval.
   - **Editor** — image-text alignment after images are inserted; suggests caption refinements and placement
     adjustments. The seven shared criteria (reliability, engagement, informativeness, coherence, readability,
     consistency, helpfulness) are graded by these viewpoints. Critically, the critic LM (GPT-o3-mini) is _different_
     and stronger than the writer LMs (GPT-4o-mini), echoing the Maestro/Worker pattern.

4. **Multi-modal Article Writing.** After the text article exists, an LLM proposes image positions and captions; images
   are retrieved from general image search, Wikipedia, and the cited reference websites; CLIP shortlists the top-3
   candidates per slot; a multi-modal model picks the best; then the entire article is polished by a multimodal model to
   fix any text-image inconsistencies.

The **WikiSeek** benchmark is curated by sampling WikiWeb2M for articles with less than 500 chars (hard), less than 300
(very hard), or less than 100 (extremely hard), then manually filtering out useless topics like "1997 in Japan" or ".bh"
TLD. Final set: 300 topics, balanced 100/100/100 across difficulty. Topics are presented in three modalities (text-only,
image-only, image-text). Evaluation is GPT-4o-as-judge across nine textual criteria (alignment, consistency, relevance,
repetition; breadth, depth; verifiability; engagement, novelty) plus four image criteria (image-text coherence,
engagement, helpfulness, information supplement).

## Key results

The headline numbers are average textual scores on WikiSeek:

- **Text-only inputs.** WikiAutoGen 78.73 vs. Co-Storm 69.96 (+8.77). The other baselines (oRAG 60.71, Storm 65.26,
  OmniThink 63.98) trail further.
- **Image-only inputs.** WikiAutoGen 77.49 vs. oRAG 48.38 (+29.1). This is by far the largest gap and reflects the fact
  that none of the baselines were designed to handle image-only topics — even when retrofitted with image retrieval,
  they synthesize poorly from visual seeds.
- **Image-text inputs.** WikiAutoGen 78.82 vs. Co-Storm 63.77 (+15.05).

Image-content evaluation (image-text coherence, engagement, helpfulness, info supplement) shows similar dominance, with
WikiAutoGen the best on every cell of every modality.

The **ablation** (Table 3, image-only inputs, additive enabling of each module on top of a stripped baseline at 48.78
average):

- Add multi-agent knowledge exploration → 66.56 (+17.78).
- Add outline proposal → 72.88 (+24.10).
- Add self-reflection → 71.60 (+22.82).
- All three → 77.49 (+28.71). The three modules are roughly comparable in individual contribution and are complementary,
  not redundant. The outline module alone is the single strongest lever, which is consistent with the broader literature
  finding that pre-writing structure dominates downstream quality.

The **difficulty ablation** is the most interesting result for Linus: the gap between WikiAutoGen and Storm grows from
+12.35 (hard) to +15.35 (extremely hard) as topics get more obscure. Most LLM systems degrade _faster_ on hard inputs;
this one degrades _slower_. The authors attribute this to the multi-agent persona exploration finding more diverse
retrieval angles plus self-reflection catching more hallucination on thin-source topics.

**Human evaluation**: 100 text-only topics, 3 MTurk raters per pairwise comparison. 99% agreed images improve
comprehension. Across four pairwise questions (easiest to understand, most engaging, most comprehensive, overall
favorite), participants preferred WikiAutoGen over both Storm and OmniThink on every axis.

## What's reusable in Linus

**Direct, Phase 2 (MVP) and Phase 3 (Knowledge & Parallel Agents).** The four-role self-reflection topology — supervisor
/ writer / reader / editor, each with a distinct rubric, firing at distinct pipeline stages — is a clean, copyable
pattern for Linus' agent registry. It's a more structured version of the generic "self-reflection" loop in
[Fundamentals of Building Autonomous LLM Agents (2510.09244v1)](2510.09244v1.md) and worth lifting into the
orchestration layer's standard agent vocabulary. The trick that the critic uses a _stronger_ model than the writer
(o3-mini critiquing 4o-mini) is exactly the Maestro/Worker pattern at the role level: cheap Worker does bulk text, more
expensive Worker (or even hosted Claude in fully-private-not-required mode) does criticism.

**Direct, Phase 3.** The persona-generator → asker → role-agents → writer pipeline is the closest mature realization of
"fan out specialized Workers, have them search and discuss, then synthesize" that Linus needs for KnowledgeBase-grounded
synthesis tasks. It maps cleanly onto Dan's actual workflow when researching a new biochem subtopic: spawn a "methods"
agent, a "results" agent, a "controversy/replication" agent, let them retrieve from the KB, have them discuss, then
write.

**Direct, Phase 1 / Phase 2.** **DSPy** is a serious practical signal here. The whole pipeline is implemented as DSPy
programs, which means each agent is a typed `Signature` and each chain is a compilable, optimizable module. If Linus'
orchestration layer is going to host a tool registry plus declarative agent specs, DSPy is one of the few open
frameworks that already does the typed-IO compilation. Worth a focused spike before committing to a custom orchestration
DSL — see open question 1.

**Direct, Phase 3.** The CLIP-shortlist → multi-modal-LLM-rank pattern for image selection is exactly the right shape
for _figure retrieval from KnowledgeBase papers_. Dan's papers have figures; a Worker that needs to cite "the cryo-EM
density of complex IV" should do CLIP-prefilter against figure embeddings, then have a vision-capable model pick the
best one. This is a concrete tool to add to the Linus tool registry.

**Indirect, Phase 7.** The "WikiSeek deliberately picks underexplored topics" methodology generalizes to a Linus
benchmark idea: build a `dan_tasks/niche_synthesis` set of 30–50 topics from Dan's actual KB where Wikipedia is thin,
and grade Workers on synthesis quality. This is the "make the benchmark hard in the way that matters to you" lesson,
complementary to the task-completion-time argument in [Speed and Conversational LLMs (2502.16721v1)](2502.16721v1.md)
and to the dataset-quality discipline in [A Practical Guide for Evaluating LLMs (2506.13023v1)](2506.13023v1.md).

**Indirect, Phase 2 memory pillar.** The asker/persona discussion produces a _transcript_ that is itself an episodic
memory artifact: the chain of "what we asked, what we found, where we disagreed, what we kept." Persisting these
transcripts (not just the final article) is a Linus-relevant design choice the paper doesn't discuss but its
architecture invites. The episodic memory layer of Linus' four-tier memory architecture should ingest these directly.

## What's NOT applicable / hype filter

The whole system runs on **closed OpenAI models** (GPT-4o, GPT-4o-mini, GPT-o3-mini). For Linus' privacy-preserving
local-first stance, these need to be replaced with Ollama-served Workers (Qwen2.5, Mistral) and local critic models. The
pattern transfers; the absolute numbers do not. In particular, GPT-o3-mini's reasoning quality as a critic is materially
different from any 7–8B local model, and the self-reflection loop may fail to converge or may converge to wrong answers
when the critic is weaker than the writer. This needs a smoke test before adoption.

The web-retrieval substrate (Serper API, Google Vision Search, Google image search) is a paid, online dependency. Linus
can substitute KnowledgeBase queries plus Kiwix-served Wikipedia plus a local image-embedding index, but the latency and
recall profiles will differ.

The CLIP image selection is fine but assumes a populated, queryable image corpus. The KnowledgeBase paper-figure index
doesn't exist yet; building it is a separate engineering task with its own Phase markers.

The benchmark (WikiSeek, 300 topics) targets _Wikipedia-style_ output, which is a specific genre — encyclopedic,
neutral, multi-source. Dan's actual writing tasks (lab notebook entries, paper drafts, reviews, internal synthesis docs)
have different conventions. The persona / supervisor / editor scaffold is reusable but the _rubrics_ would need to be
re-specced for each genre.

The paper does no inference-cost or latency analysis. With ~4 viewpoints × 7 criteria × multiple iterations × multiple
agents × multiple retrieval calls per article, the token cost per article is plausibly in the high thousands to tens of
thousands. On Apple Silicon with local models this matters; the paper's "make every step a critique loop" attitude does
not fit the budget without aggressive pruning.

The paper also doesn't discuss failure modes of self-reflection — when the critic loop _amplifies_ errors instead of
catching them. This is a known issue in the broader self-correction literature and is unaddressed here.

## Connections

WikiAutoGen sits squarely in the agentic-systems thread of the corpus. It's most directly comparable to
[Kosmos (2511.02824v2)](2511.02824v2.md) — both are multi-agent systems with persistent state and structured roles, but
Kosmos is for autonomous _scientific discovery_ with a world model, while WikiAutoGen is for _expository synthesis_ with
a critic loop. WikiAutoGen is what Kosmos' final-write-up step might look like if treated as a first-class problem.

It pairs naturally with [Fundamentals of Building Autonomous LLM Agents (2510.09244v1)](2510.09244v1.md) as a concrete
instantiation of the survey's planning/memory/execution decomposition, and with
[A Practical Guide for Evaluating LLMs (2506.13023v1)](2506.13023v1.md) as a worked example of multi-criterion
LLM-as-judge evaluation (with all the caveats that guide raises about judge bias).

The pre-writing-structure-dominates finding echoes the broader knowledge-graph and outline literatures and is a useful
corroboration of why an explicit Outline Proposal stage is worth the tokens. The use of CLIP for cross-modal retrieval
and a stronger critic for self-correction are both standard moves that this paper combines well.

Nothing here directly speaks to the BitNet / quantization / streaming-inference threads (2310.11453v1, 2402.17764v1,
2312.11514v3, flash_moe, etc.) — this is purely an orchestration-layer paper.

## Open questions for Dan

1. **DSPy spike.** Should Linus' orchestration layer be built on DSPy, or build its own typed-agent DSL? DSPy gives
   compilation, prompt optimization, and a real ecosystem for free, but adds a Python framework dependency and
   constrains the agent shape. Worth a 2–3-day spike comparing DSPy-as-substrate vs. a thin custom layer over Pydantic +
   Ollama, evaluated on a Linus-shaped task (e.g., write a synthesis of three KB papers).
2. **Critic-stronger-than-writer pattern.** Should Linus formalize a "critic tier" in the Worker registry, where
   critique calls are routed to a stronger (or hosted) model than the bulk-write calls? This is the role-level
   Maestro/Worker pattern. If yes, the orchestration layer needs a per-call cost/quality dispatcher rather than a single
   default Worker.

   _Partially resolved (DEC-0050, see [answered-questions.md](../questions/answered-questions.md)): Role as first-class
   type in the Phase 3 spawner includes critic_eligible: bool; per-call cost/quality dispatch deferred to Phase 3
   orchestration spec._

3. **Niche-synthesis benchmark.** Worth building a `benchmarks/dan_tasks/niche_synthesis/` set of 30–50 topics from
   Dan's KB where Wikipedia coverage is thin? This would let us measure whether Linus' degradation curve flattens (like
   WikiAutoGen's) as topics get more obscure, vs. the typical sharp drop-off.
4. **KB figure index.** Is building a CLIP-indexed figure corpus from KnowledgeBase papers worth a Phase 3 work item? It
   enables a "find-the-relevant-figure" tool for synthesis tasks and would be reused across many downstream agents.
5. **Self-reflection failure modes.** Before adopting the four-viewpoint critic loop, do we want a small ablation on
   Linus' actual Workers measuring whether self-reflection _helps_ or _hurts_ output quality at our model scale? The
   paper doesn't show this and the literature is mixed for sub-13B models.
