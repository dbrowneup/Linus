# Genome modelling and design across all domains of life with Evo 2

## TL;DR

Evo 2 is a genomic foundation model trained on 9.3 trillion DNA base pairs ("OpenGenome2") spanning bacteria, archaea,
eukarya and bacteriophage, with a **1 million-token context window at single-nucleotide resolution**. The architecture
is **StripedHyena 2** — a multi-hybrid of three input-dependent convolution operators plus attention, giving up to 3x
throughput over Transformers at 40B / 1M context. Two main public sizes: **7B** (2.4T tokens) and **40B** (9.3T tokens).
Without any task-specific fine-tuning, Evo 2's per-position likelihoods deliver competitive-to-leading **zero-shot
variant effect prediction** across coding and noncoding human variants — most notably on the **BRCA1
saturation-mutagenesis benchmark**, where Evo 2 outperforms all tested models on noncoding SNVs and (with a
ridge-regression head on its embeddings) achieves AUROC 0.95 / AUPRC 0.88. Sparse-autoencoder probes recover
interpretable features for prophages, exon/intron boundaries, transcription-factor binding motifs, and protein secondary
structure — features that transfer across organisms (including the woolly mammoth genome). Generatively, Evo 2 produces
full mitochondrial genomes (16 kb), 580 kb _M. genitalium_-like prokaryotic genomes, and 330 kb yeast chromosome
segments with realistic synteny and annotatable genes; coupling with Enformer/Borzoi as a beam-search reward model
enables experimentally validated design of multi-kilobase mammalian chromatin-accessibility patterns. **Everything is
open**: weights, training and inference code, OpenGenome2, and the SAEs. Eukaryotic-virus sequences were excluded from
training as a biosafety measure. This is the headline DNA foundation model of the current wave.

## The problem (in plain language)

Genomes are how life stores information, but our ability to _read_ the information has long outrun our ability to
_interpret_ it. Sequencing is cheap; predicting what a particular mutation does to a gene, a regulatory region, or an
organism is still hard, expensive, and largely supervised — every new prediction task wants its own labelled dataset and
model. Existing DNA language models (Nucleotide Transformer, DNABERT, Evo 1, GPN-MSA) helped, but they were either
short-context, single-domain, or both. None could plausibly be called "a foundation model for biology" the way GPT-4 is
for natural language.

The clinical motivation anchoring the paper is variant interpretation. ClinVar holds tens of thousands of human variants
annotated as pathogenic or benign, but most variants in clinical sequencing are "variants of uncertain significance" —
no annotation, no supervision possible. A model that learns the constraints of healthy genomes well enough to score
arbitrary mutations _zero-shot_ changes that directly. BRCA1 is the textbook case: a gene where loss-of-function
mutations confer dramatic cancer risk, where a saturation-mutagenesis screen has measured the effect of essentially
every possible SNV (Findlay et al. 2018), and where misclassification has direct clinical consequences.

The second half of the problem is generation. Synthesizing DNA is cheap; _designing_ DNA that does what you want is not.
Pre-Evo-2 generative methods worked at the scale of hundreds of nucleotides for narrow tasks. A model with a 1M-token
context that can be steered by an external scoring function changes the addressable scale by orders of magnitude.

## What they propose

**Architecture.** StripedHyena 2 — a multi-hybrid layered around three flavors of input-dependent convolution (short
explicit, medium regularized, long implicit) interleaved with rotary attention. Convolutional kernels carry the
long-range modelling; attention provides flexibility. At 40B / 1M-context throughput is roughly 3x a tuned Transformer
baseline. Two open variants: **Evo 2 7B** (2.4T tokens) and **Evo 2 40B** (9.3T tokens), both at 1M context. A 1B
short-context "experimental" version exists but the authors explicitly recommend against it.

**Training data.** OpenGenome2 — a curated, non-redundant nucleotide corpus from GTDB (bacteria, archaea), NCBI
(eukarya), and IMG/VR (phage), more than 8.8 trillion nucleotides. **Eukaryotic-virus sequences are deliberately
excluded** for biosafety; red-teaming confirms direct elicitation of human-virus proteins fails (random sequence
recovery).

**Training procedure.** Two phases: pretraining at 8,192-token context with weighting toward genic regions, then
**midtraining** that progressively extends context up to 1M tokens — mirroring the long-context recipe in modern
natural-language LLMs (Llama 3 and others). Validation perplexity drops with both scale and context length; a
needle-in-a-haystack test confirms recall at 1M.

**Capabilities demonstrated.** Zero-shot mutational effect prediction across all three domains of life; human variant
effect prediction on ClinVar, SpliceVarDB, and BRCA1/BRCA2 saturation-mutagenesis; mechanistic interpretability via
sparse autoencoders on layer-26 activations; genome-scale generation (16 kb mitochondrial, 580 kb _M. genitalium_, 330
kb yeast chromosome III); and inference-time guided design coupling Evo 2 with Enformer + Borzoi as oracles inside a
beam search to design multi-kilobase mammalian DNA with specified chromatin-accessibility profiles, experimentally
validated by ATAC-seq.

**Openness.** Weights for all sizes, distributed training and multi-GPU inference code, the full OpenGenome2 dataset,
SAE checkpoints, and two web tools (designer + mech-interp browser). Among the largest fully open releases across any
modality.

## Key results

On variant effect prediction: Evo 2 40B is the best unsupervised model on ClinVar **noncoding SNVs**; the best of any
model (supervised or not) on **noncoding non-SNVs** (insertions, deletions); competitive on coding SNVs. On **BRCA1
noncoding SNVs** it beats every tested model including supervised splicing predictors (SpliceAI, Pangolin) and GPN-MSA.
With a ridge-regression classifier on Evo 2 40B embeddings trained only on BRCA1 variants, AUROC reaches **0.95** and
AUPRC **0.88** — the kind of separation with direct clinical utility. On SpliceVarDB exonic and intronic variants Evo 2
leads all unsupervised models. It falls behind sequence-to-function specialists (ChromBPNet) on distal regulatory
variants where conservation signal is weak.

The **needle-in-a-haystack** result is technically remarkable: at 1M-token context, Evo 2 40B recalls a 100 bp needle
from 1M bp of random DNA with score >0.8 across most depth positions. A literal long-range retrieval test, and it works.

For **interpretability**, the SAE-recovered features are not toys. Feature f/19746 fires on annotated prophages in _E.
coli_, on phage-derived spacers in CRISPR arrays, and on prophage-like regions geNomad missed but that contain
integrases and invertases. Features for alpha-helices and beta-sheets verify against AlphaFold 3 structures. Exon-intron
boundary features trained on a primates+fly+frog SAE generalize to the woolly mammoth genome. Across human promoters,
Evo 2 SAE features hit 70% of HOCOMOCO transcription-factor binding motifs versus 35% for HOMER, the specialized
motif-discovery tool.

For **generation**, Evo 2 produces complete 16 kb mitochondrial genomes with the right counts of CDSs (13), tRNAs (22),
and rRNAs (2), proper synteny, and AlphaFold-3-confirmed multimeric complex structures. For 580 kb _M. genitalium_
genomes, ~70% of Prodigal-called genes have significant Pfam hits (vs 18% for Evo 1 131k context). On
chromatin-accessibility design, beam search with Enformer+Borzoi as oracle achieves AUROCs of 0.92-0.95 on three
Morse-code patterns (LO, ARC, EVO2) experimentally validated by ATAC-seq in mouse embryonic stem cells, and 33/36 (92%)
of single-cell-line designs reach AUROC > 0.8.

## What's reusable in Linus

This is, by some distance, the most consequential biology paper for Dan in the corpus, and the highest-leverage
candidate skill for Phase 7.

**Phase 7 — the headline genomics skill.** A `linus.skills.evo2` tool wrapping Evo 2 for three operations: (1) score
per-base or per-variant likelihood (zero-shot variant effect prediction), (2) extract layer-26 embeddings for downstream
supervised heads (the BRCA1 ridge-regression pattern), and (3) generate DNA conditioned on a prompt. Even the 7B variant
gives Dan a private, no-API zero-shot variant scorer that beats most supervised baselines on noncoding regions. There is
no other paper in the wave whose output is more directly useful as a Linus tool.

**Phase 6 — operational reality check.** Evo 2 40B is well outside M1 Max territory: 40B parameters at FP16 is ~80 GB
just in weights. Even Evo 2 7B at FP16 is ~14 GB, feasible on 32 GB unified memory but leaving little room for long
context. Realistic local path: **7B at 4-bit or 8-bit quantization with reduced context (32k-128k, not 1M)**.
Fine-tuning 7B locally is plausible only via LoRA/QLoRA at modest context; full fine-tuning is out. Crucially,
StripedHyena 2 is not a vanilla Transformer — quantization tooling (llama.cpp, MLX) does not yet have first-class
support for hyena-style operators, so plumbing work precedes any local inference. This is the central operational
unknown for the skill.

**Phase 4 — OpenGenome2 mirroring.** OpenGenome2 is in scope. At 8.8T nucleotides the raw size is large but tractable
for an external SSD. This is exactly the kind of asset a Data Sovereignty pillar should mirror, especially given the
deliberate biosafety exclusions (Linus inherits the safety property by training on this corpus rather than scraping
fresh). Even if Linus never retrains Evo 2, having OpenGenome2 locally enables RAG over reference genomes and supervised
heads on Evo 2 embeddings.

**Phase 3 — variant-batch agents.** Variant effect prediction parallelizes trivially (each variant independent), so a
`variant-batch` agent fanning a list of variants out to Evo 2 inference is a clean Phase 3 demonstrator and a real piece
of Dan-relevant computational biology executed by Linus rather than a toy task.

**Mech-interp tooling.** The released SAEs and mech-interp browser are immediately usable artifacts. A Linus tool that
exposes "what features fire on this sequence" gives Dan an interactive interpretability layer for hypothesis generation
— unusual for a biology paper to release and worth taking advantage of.

## What's NOT applicable

The 40B model is not a plausible local Worker on a 32 GB M1 Max. If Linus ever calls Evo 2 40B it will be via a remote
endpoint (Arc Institute web tool, NVIDIA hosting, future cloud), not locally. Plan for the 7B model as the local
default.

The 1M-token context window will not be operationally available on M1 Max regardless of model size; activation memory at
that length is the binding constraint.

The chromatin-accessibility design pipeline requires Enformer and Borzoi as oracles, which are themselves nontrivial
models. Designing chromatin patterns is a research workflow, not a Linus-scale skill — call it Phase 8+ or treat as a
thing Dan does interactively rather than something Linus drives end-to-end.

The biosafety exclusions are a feature, not a bug, but they mean Evo 2 cannot be used (and explicitly should not be
repurposed) for human-virus prediction or design. Any Linus tool wrapping Evo 2 should preserve and document this
constraint.

## Connections

**Wave 3 Group B link, flagged.** The Evo-based generative phage genome paper (bioRxiv 2025.09.12.675911v1) sits
directly downstream of this work — it uses Evo 1/2 as the generative backbone for full phage-genome design. Reading that
paper after this one, with a focused Group A → Group B mini-synthesis, would tie the foundation-model results here to a
concrete generative biology application. Strongly recommend pairing them as Wave 3 prep.

**Group A wave constellation.** Evo 2 is the headliner of the DNA foundation-model wave. Companions occupy adjacent
niches: **LucaOne** is the joint-modality counterpart (DNA + RNA + protein, smaller scale, shorter context).
**AlphaGenome** is the regulatory-variant specialist — supervised, narrower-context, but trained directly on functional
genomics data, so it likely beats Evo 2 on the distal regulatory variants where the paper itself acknowledges Evo 2
trails ChromBPNet. **RiNALMo** is RNA-only. **Bacformer** treats whole bacterial genomes at the protein level.
**REMME/REBEAN** is metagenomic-DNA-LM-focused. **ProteinReasoner** and **METL** sit on the protein side. The right
framing: Evo 2 is the generalist genomic backbone; the others are specialists. A Linus that hosts Evo 2 + AlphaGenome
(or Evo 2 + a fine-tuned regulatory head) covers most of the relevant prediction space.

**Long-context vs episodic memory.** The 1M-token claim is technically real (needle-in-a-haystack passes) and connects
to the long-context discussion in the [Llama 3 paper](2407.21783v3.md) and the memory-pillar architecture work. The
lesson is the same as in natural-language LLMs: long context is useful but not a substitute for a real episodic-memory
layer when you're memory-bound.

**StripedHyena 2 is not a Transformer.** Quantization and inference tooling for this architecture is much less mature
than for Transformers — MLX, llama.cpp, and Ollama support all need to be checked before assuming local inference works.
Real cost worth pricing in.

**Mechanistic interpretability tradition.** The SAE methodology comes directly from the Anthropic mech-interp line
(Bricken et al. 2023, Templeton et al. 2024). Evo 2 is one of the first non-LLM applications of dictionary-learning
interpretability that yields features as cleanly biological as these. Worth flagging as a methodology that may transfer
to other Linus models down the line.

## Open questions for Dan

1. **Which Evo 2 variant is the right Phase 7 default?** Operationally only the 7B model is plausible on M1 Max, and
   only at 4-bit/8-bit quantization with reduced context. Is that good enough for the variant-scoring use cases you care
   about, or does the 40B model's clinical-grade BRCA1 performance push us toward a remote-endpoint architecture for the
   genomics skill specifically? (This bends the Linus "fully local" principle and is worth deciding deliberately.)
2. **MLX-loadable checkpoints — has anyone done it yet?** StripedHyena 2 is not a Transformer; standard quantization and
   serving tooling does not assume it. Worth a brief search of the Arc Institute repo, MLX community, and any
   community-released GGUF or MLX conversions for Evo 2 7B before committing engineering time to the skill.
3. **Mirror OpenGenome2 in Phase 4?** 8.8T nucleotides on disk is large but tractable on an external SSD. Mirroring it
   gives Linus the ability to fine-tune supervised heads on Evo 2 embeddings, do RAG over reference genomes, and inherit
   the biosafety-exclusion property. Worth the disk and the bandwidth?
4. **Evo 2 vs AlphaGenome for regulatory variants — host both or pick one?** The paper itself acknowledges Evo 2 trails
   sequence-to-function specialists like ChromBPNet on distal regulatory variants. AlphaGenome is plausibly stronger on
   that subdomain. Does Linus host both as separate skills (with a router that picks based on variant location), or does
   Dan pick one to anchor the genomics pillar?
5. **Wave 3 mini-synthesis.** Confirming: is Evo 2 + the Evo-based phage-genome paper (2025.09.12.675911v1) worth a
   focused 1500-2000 word mini-synthesis as Wave 3 prep, before the broader Group B reading? The pairing is unusually
   clean — foundation model + headline downstream application — and the synthesis would naturally land at "what does a
   private generative-biology workflow look like in Linus."
6. **Variant-batch as the Phase 3 demo.** Would a working `variant-batch` agent that fans 1k ClinVar variants out to Evo
   2 7B inference (locally if quantization works, or via the Arc designer endpoint as a fallback) be a satisfying Phase
   3 milestone — real Dan-relevant computational biology executed by Linus?
