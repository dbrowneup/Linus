# Biotechnology and Today's Warfighter

## TL;DR

A 2022 RAND commentary by Tim Marler (senior research engineer at RAND, professor at Pardee RAND Graduate School) and Daniel Gerstein (senior policy researcher at RAND, former acting undersecretary and deputy undersecretary in the DHS Science and Technology Directorate 2011-2014) framing **biotechnology as inherently dual-use and increasingly relevant to the warfighter** — both as a force-enabling capability (force health protection, performance enhancement, advanced biomaterials, biosensing) and as a threat vector (state and non-state actors weaponizing biological materials). The piece names a confluence of four conditions driving urgency: (1) increasing competition from "near peers" like China (~7% annual increase in biotech R&D 2021-2025, efforts to acquire international health data, WuXi Biologics building U.S. facilities outside CFIUS scrutiny); (2) the need for strong DoD biodefense in light of state biotechnology programs in China, Russia, North Korea, and Iran despite the 1975 Biological and Toxin Weapons Convention; (3) COVID-19 demonstrating U.S. unpreparedness for significant biological incidents (Ebola, Zika, COVID, monkeypox in sequence; the March 2020 Theodore Roosevelt outbreak as a force-readiness case study); (4) **biotechnology proliferation and democratization** — CRISPR available in high schools, individual DNA mappable for disease/endurance/strength assessment. The piece's structural argument: biotechnology has broad implications across the bioeconomy ($959.2B / >5% of U.S. GDP in 2016 per a 2020 National Academies study) but suffers from unclear definitions, insufficient public awareness, dispersed governance across DHS / FBI / DoD / OSTP / national labs plus Commerce / Energy / NIH / HHS / CDC, and overlapping/underlapping biotechnology areas across organizations. Recommendations are framing-level: greater visibility, hierarchy and deconfliction across federal biotechnology programs, synergy with the private sector (analogous to government support of the Human Genome Project and Operation Warp Speed). For Linus, this piece is **the 2022 baseline articulation of the dual-use biotechnology threat framework** that DEC-0047's biosecurity-tier-control framework operationalizes. It predates the 2025-2026 AI-bio nexus framing (Holko/Wilbanks/Howell, Genesis EO, NSCB 2025) but supplies the underlying threat-model logic: biology is dual-use, the warfighter is increasingly impacted both as beneficiary and target, and democratization plus near-peer competition raise the stakes.

## The problem (in plain language)

The piece opens with the framing problem: biotechnology has a "broad and often misunderstood scope" — modular and ambiguous in ways that semiconductors or aviation are not. The National Academies' 2020 study characterized the bioeconomy as >5% of U.S. GDP ($959.2B in 2016), but the definition is still emerging. Marler and Gerstein restrict biotechnology to "manipulation of living organisms or their components to produce useful products" (a working definition) and the bioeconomy to "products, services, and processes derived from biological resources." The vocabulary problem is real: when policymakers say "biotechnology," they may mean industrial fermentation, pharmaceutical bioprocessing, gene editing, biosensor technology, or weaponized pathogens — and the policy implications differ sharply across these.

The substantive argument frames biotechnology along **two axes**: the **beneficiary** axis (improving warfighter performance, force health protection, mission readiness) and the **threat** axis (weaponization of biological materials by state or non-state actors). On the beneficiary axis, the piece names concrete examples: human-microbiome balance for digestive and mental health, enhanced environmental sensing, advanced neural interfaces for cognition and decision-making, next-generation explosives reagents, rare-earth-material harvesting via biology, enhanced armor protection (including for the individual soldier), bio-based airfield construction, specialized bio-resins and polymers. On the threat axis: state-level programs (China, Russia, North Korea, Iran), non-state actors developing offensive biological warfare capabilities, weaponized pathogens harming fragile ecosystems, inadvertent release of modified species, weaponization of biological data itself.

The confluence-of-conditions argument is the operationally important framing:

- **Near-peer competition.** China's ~7% annual biotech R&D growth 2021-2025; efforts to acquire international health data ("not to mention potential efforts to weaponize various aspects of biological data"); strategic U.S. investments by WuXi Biologics building U.S. facilities outside CFIUS scrutiny (because international companies that build in the U.S. from the ground up are not subject to CFIUS review). The framing of "biological data" as itself a target — not just the underlying biological materials — is the bridge to the AI-bio nexus thread that the 2025-2026 policy pieces develop.

- **Sustained DoD biodefense need.** Despite the 1975 Biological and Toxin Weapons Convention, the U.S. still has questions about the intentions of China, Russia, North Korea, and Iran, all of which have active biotechnology programs. Engaging with the international community — both partners/allies and nations with questionable programs — could help protect against threats. The BWC has not solved the verification problem.

- **COVID-19 as the operational lesson.** The pandemic demonstrated that the U.S. was not prepared for a significant biological incident. Ebola (2014), Zika (2016), COVID-19 (2019-present), and monkeypox have shown that preparedness and response capability need re-evaluation. The Theodore Roosevelt aircraft-carrier outbreak in March 2020 is the warfighter-specific case study: force health protection and mission readiness are vulnerable in ways that traditional force planning did not anticipate.

- **Biotechnology proliferation and democratization.** Individual DNA can be mapped, stored, and used to assess disease susceptibility, physical endurance, strength. CRISPR is available in some high schools — democratized and deskilled. The cost barrier to biological manipulation is falling rapidly, which broadens the threat surface to include non-state actors and small groups.

## What they propose

The piece is commentary rather than a policy recommendations document; the substantive proposal is **better federal coordination across the biotechnology-aware organizations**. The argument is structural: today there are overlapping and underlapping biotechnology areas across many federal organizations — White House OSTP, DHS, DoD, FBI, national labs, plus Commerce, DOE, NIH, HHS, CDC. Each organization faces unique challenges and may require unique management, but **increasing higher-level coordination would net real benefits to the agencies themselves and to the warfighter**.

Three operational suggestions emerge from the piece:

1. **Greater visibility into biotechnology applications across government.** A broad and common understanding of the scope of biotechnology as it relates to the warfighter could facilitate more-coordinated discussions. The recent executive order and accompanying implementation strategy (the piece is referring to the Biden 2022 EO on Advancing Biotechnology and Biomanufacturing Innovation) provide a common baseline.

2. **Hierarchy and deconfliction across federal biotechnology programs.** Balance centralized coordination with decentralized needs. Each organization works with biotechnology faces unique challenges and may require unique management. Increasing higher-level coordination would net real benefits.

3. **Synergy with the private sector.** Industry and academia drive biotechnology R&D. Promoting and supporting these leadership efforts gives DoD the "inside track" on emerging technologies. Historical precedents: government R&D support was vital to the Human Genome Project; government and DoD played a pivotal role in Operation Warp Speed for COVID-19 vaccine development, employing the Defense Production Act.

The closing framing is calibrated: "Biotechnology may be reaching a critical junction. As it continues to mature, proactive policy becomes necessary for the federal government to leverage emerging capabilities effectively and remain competitive. Consistent communication, coordination, and collaboration may help retain this competitiveness and support today's warfighter most effectively."

## Key takeaways for Linus

**Calibration 1: dual-use is the foundational frame.** "Biotechnology is inherently dual use, meaning it could be used both for legitimate and nefarious purposes." This is **the** operative framing for DEC-0047's biosecurity-tier-control framework. The 2022 piece supplies the underlying threat-model logic for the 2026 DEC-0047 implementation. Linus's biosecurity tiering is the operational realization of the dual-use framework Marler and Gerstein articulate.

**Calibration 2: democratization is real and accelerating.** CRISPR in high schools, individual DNA mappable, falling cost barriers. The threat surface includes small groups and non-state actors. For Linus's Phase 7 biology skills, this means the safety/biosecurity architecture must assume **low-skill-threshold adversaries**, not just nation-state-scale capabilities. DEC-0047's tier framework's gating on Tier 3+ skills is calibrated for this.

**Calibration 3: biological data itself is a target.** The piece names "potential efforts to weaponize various aspects of biological data" as a near-peer threat. This is the bridge to the AI-bio nexus framework (Holko/Wilbanks/Howell 2026): biological data is not just an input to biology; it's a strategic asset that can be exfiltrated, manipulated, or weaponized. Linus's local-first architecture, auditable provenance, and DEC-0047 biosecurity tiering together address this threat at the orchestration-layer level.

**Calibration 4: government-academic-industrial synergy is the historical pattern for success.** Human Genome Project and Operation Warp Speed are the recurrent reference cases. For Linus's eventual commercial positioning, this argues that **government partnerships are a viable path** — particularly with DoD-adjacent agencies (BARDA, DARPA, JPEO-CBRND, the DOD's Office of Strategy Capital named in the MIT biomanufacturing brief) that have a track record of supporting dual-use technology development with appropriate security frameworks.

## What's NOT applicable / hype filter

**The piece is 2022, before the 2025-2026 AI-bio nexus framing.** The Holko/Wilbanks/Howell 2026 commentary explicitly frames biodata as the AI-bio binding constraint; the MIT 2024 brief frames biomanufacturing as the U.S. production-layer bottleneck. Marler/Gerstein 2022 is the earlier, broader articulation of the dual-use threat that pre-dates the specific AI-bio framing. Linus's documentation should cite Marler/Gerstein for the foundational dual-use logic and the 2026 sources for the AI-bio-specific updates.

**The piece is commentary, not policy specification.** Like the other policy pieces in this batch, the recommendations are framing-level: better coordination, greater visibility, private-sector synergy. There are no specific technology stacks or implementation guidance.

**The CFIUS gap is not Linus's problem.** The piece names a specific structural gap — international companies building U.S. facilities from the ground up are not subject to CFIUS review. This is a federal policy problem, not a Linus orchestration-layer problem. Linus's documentation should not over-claim that local-first AI assists in solving the CFIUS gap.

**The piece's threat framing is heavy on state and near-peer adversaries.** Marler/Gerstein 2022 focuses on China, Russia, North Korea, Iran. The 2025-2026 biosecurity threat landscape has broader concerns (small-group adversaries with democratized tools, AI-uplift of biological-design capabilities). DEC-0047's tier framework should be calibrated for both threat classes, not just the state-level.

**No specific AI/LLM concerns in the 2022 piece.** Generative AI for biological design was not yet a salient threat in 2022. The piece's "biotechnology" framing is dual-use generic; the specific AI-uplift biosecurity concerns (e.g., the Anthropic / Apollo / NIST AI-bio uplift evaluations that have appeared 2024-2025) require additional substrate.

**The Defense Production Act / Operation Warp Speed framing is positive but selective.** The piece cites OWS as a success case. The full picture is more mixed (Emergent BioSolutions' COVID-vaccine destruction case, cited in the MIT biomanufacturing brief; vaccine equity controversies; questions about cost overruns). Linus's documentation should cite OWS as a partnership-model precedent without over-claiming its success.
