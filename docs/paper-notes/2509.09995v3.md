# QuantAgent: Price-Driven Multi-Agent LLMs for High-Frequency Trading

## TL;DR

QuantAgent is a four-agent LangGraph framework (IndicatorAgent, PatternAgent, TrendAgent, RiskAgent — feeding a fifth
DecisionAgent) that does short-horizon directional prediction on OHLC candlestick data alone, with no news, sentiment,
or fundamentals input. The novelty for the trading literature is the move away from text-driven LLM finance systems
(TradingAgents, FINMEM, FINCON) toward structured-signal-only reasoning at 1h and 4h bar resolutions. Across nine
instruments (Bitcoin, NQ, ES, SPX, QQQ, DJI, VIX, CL, plus a held-out evaluation set), QuantAgent beats random, linear
regression, and XGBoost baselines on directional accuracy and on three rate-of-return metrics (Rcc, Rmax, Rmin), with a
case-study peak of 80% directional accuracy on a 10-window SPX rolling test. The authors are explicit about two limits:
predictive precision degrades on 1–15 minute candles where noise dominates, and the per-cycle latency (one LLM call plus
indicator/pattern tool calls) is too high for true sub-minute trading. **For Linus, the value is not the trading
content; it is the cleanest worked example yet of a four-agent decomposition where each agent has a specialized tool
belt and the integration agent reasons over a structured aggregate — a near-direct template for the orchestration
layer's dispatch and aggregation logic.**

## The problem (in plain language)

Most LLM-based finance systems treat the model as a reader of news and earnings reports — they pull in text, summarize
sentiment, and recommend trades over horizons of days to months. That works because text contains information the market
hasn't fully priced in yet. But at high frequencies (sub-day to minute resolution), the price action itself is the most
information-dense signal, and text is _lagging_ relative to it: by the time a news article publishes, the price already
moved. Worse, text is noisy, hard to validate, and slow to ingest. So the existing wave of "LLM trader" papers, while
interesting as orchestration demos, do not address what professional HFT desks actually do — read indicators and chart
patterns off OHLC bars and act on them in seconds.

The authors frame their question as: can a multi-agent LLM system reason directly over structured price data —
indicators, geometric patterns, trend slopes — and produce traceable, language-native trade decisions that are
competitive with traditional ML baselines (linear regression, XGBoost on technical features)? The "LLM-native" part is
what they want to defend: not just predictive performance, but interpretable rationales that a human trader can audit
alongside execution.

## What they propose

QuantAgent decomposes trading into four upstream agents and one downstream decision agent, all communicating through
structured prompts via LangGraph:

- **IndicatorAgent** maps raw (O, H, L, C) tuples to a compact set of five technical indicators — RSI, MACD, RoC, STOCH
  (slow stochastic), and WILLR (Williams %R) — and writes a contextual natural-language summary rather than firing fixed
  thresholds. The job is feature extraction with light reasoning.
- **PatternAgent** is the multimodal one. It uses an LLM-bound charting tool to render simplified candlestick images,
  then matches detected geometric features (peaks, troughs, symmetry) against an in-prompt library of named patterns
  (double bottom, descending triangle, etc.) with concise descriptions of each pattern's typical implication. The
  "image + library lookup" structure is essentially retrieval-augmented chart reading.
- **TrendAgent** runs Algorithm 1: ordinary-least-squares fits over highs and lows in a sliding N-bar window, average
  slope κt = (mr + ms)/2, classify as Uptrend / Downtrend / Sideways against a threshold τ, then reason over the
  geometry of the channel (parallel slopes = strong trend; converging = wedge / indecision).
- **RiskAgent** does not generate signals; it integrates upstream outputs into a risk-reward envelope. Stop-loss is
  fixed at ρ = 0.0005 (5 bps); take-profit is R = r·ρ where r ∈ [1.2, 1.8] is LLM-predicted given current signal
  coherence.
- **DecisionAgent** is the layer that actually emits LONG or SHORT (no HOLD allowed). It takes the structured aggregate
  (indicator state + pattern label + trend classification + risk envelope), checks signal consistency (it acts only when
  the majority of upstream agents agree, ideally reinforced by a confirming event like an indicator crossover or
  completed breakout), and outputs (direction, justification, R/r ratio).

The whole system is built on LangGraph, which gives them the agent-graph topology and the structured-prompt routing for
free. Importantly: zero fine-tuning, zero in-context demonstrations. It runs zero-shot on a general LLM backbone (the
paper doesn't make a song and dance about which one drives the headline numbers; the point is the orchestration pattern,
not the base model).

## Key results

On the 4-hour benchmark (Table 1, 100 evaluation segments × 100 candles per asset across BTC, CL, DJI, ES, VIX, NQ, QQQ,
SPX), QuantAgent wins directional accuracy on all 8 instruments. The biggest gain over the random baseline is +34.6% on
SPX (47.3% → 63.7%); the biggest gain over the strongest baseline is on NQ where QuantAgent at 55.3% beats LR (48.7%)
and XGBoost (47.3%) by a clear margin. On the risk-constrained return Rcc (the metric that matters for actual P&L),
QuantAgent wins 7 of 8 markets. On Rmax (best-case return) it wins or near-ties in all 8. On Rmin (worst-case loss), it
has the least-negative value in most assets — meaning it doesn't just chase upside, it also limits downside.

The 1-hour results (Figure 5) are presented as a radar/line plot rather than a clean table, but the authors claim
QuantAgent dominates baselines across most metrics and markets, with the strongest separation on SPX, QQQ, and BTC. They
do flag that Rmin (downside containment) is less consistently best at the 1-hour horizon — i.e., faster decisions are
noisier on the loss-control side.

The case study on a single 100-bar SPX segment with 10 overlapping 5-bar-offset windows hits 8/10 = 80% directional
accuracy. The two errors are attributed to "overreliance on incomplete patterns and premature bullish calls" — pattern
recognition firing before the formation completes. This is honest reporting of a failure mode, not just trumpeted
accuracy.

The two stated limitations matter and are worth quoting in spirit: (1) "predictive precision drops on ultra-short
candles (≈ 1–15 min)" — at very high frequencies the noise overwhelms the signal and the zero-shot ensemble can't
separate transient spikes from real entries; (2) "the architecture is not truly real-time" — one LLM call plus several
bound tool calls per cycle takes longer than the 1-minute opportunity window. These are the same two problems anyone
building a tool-using agent on a latency budget will hit.

## What's reusable in Linus

**Direct, Phase 2 (Linus MVP, orchestration backend) — the most important takeaway.** The four-specialist +
one-integrator topology is a clean, working template for the kind of multi-agent dispatch the orchestration layer will
need. Each upstream agent has (a) a narrow domain, (b) a specific tool or set of tools, (c) a structured output schema.
The integrator (DecisionAgent here) only acts when "the majority align and are reinforced by confirmations." That
majority-with-confirmation rule is exactly the kind of cheap, auditable consistency check an orchestration layer should
impose on Worker outputs before committing to an action — it filters noise without requiring the integrator to re-do
every Worker's reasoning. This pattern generalizes immediately: replace IndicatorAgent / PatternAgent / TrendAgent with,
say, LiteratureSearchAgent / CodeSearchAgent / ExperimentDesignAgent, and the topology transfers wholesale. See the
directly comparable [TradingAgents (2412.20138v7)](2412.20138v7.md) for the role-specialization and structured-debate
version of the same idea — QuantAgent is the lighter-weight, no-debate, all-tool-grounded variant.

**Direct, Phase 2.** LangGraph as the agent-graph framework. The paper's success on a non-trivial multi-agent topology
with tool binding, structured prompts, and conditional routing is a reasonable datapoint that LangGraph is mature enough
to be considered for the Linus orchestration layer's first cut, especially if Dan wants to avoid writing the agent-graph
machinery from scratch. Worth a one-page evaluation note before committing.

**Direct, Phase 2.** The IndicatorAgent's "LLM contextualizes raw signals rather than firing fixed rules" pattern is the
right shape for many Linus tools. A KnowledgeBase-backed retrieval tool, for instance, should not just dump top-k chunks
into the next agent — it should have a thin agent layer that says "the retrieval returned X, Y, Z; in context, X is the
relevant one because…" This is cheap (small generation, small input) and dramatically improves downstream-agent
reasoning quality.

**Indirect, Phase 1 (Recon & Baselines).** The latency limitation the authors honestly report — one LLM call plus
several tool calls per cycle exceeds the actionable window — is a quantitative warning for Linus. Any "Linus reads inbox
/ monitors a process / responds to events" use case will hit the same wall. The Linus benchmarks should include
end-to-end agent-cycle latency from event to first action, not just per-token speed (see
[Speed and Conversational LLMs (2502.16721v1)](2502.16721v1.md) for the related argument that token throughput is the
wrong axis).

**Indirect, Phase 7 (Skills & Autonomy Graduation).** The RiskAgent's structure — fixed conservative defaults (ρ =
0.0005), bounded LLM-tunable parameters (r ∈ [1.2, 1.8]) — is a nice pattern for the autonomy-tier work. Give the model
a constrained envelope, let it tune within it, hard-cap the worst case. This maps cleanly to giving Linus tools where
"the model picks parameters, but the parameters are bounded by hand-tuned safety limits."

**Indirect.** The PatternAgent's "render → multimodal LLM → match against in-prompt library of named patterns" workflow
is a small but useful template for any Linus task that has visual structure with a known vocabulary — figure parsing in
scientific papers, diagram reading, log-graph interpretation. The "library lookup in the prompt" approach is essentially
few-shot retrieval against a curated catalog, which fits Linus' KnowledgeBase model better than fine-tuning a vision
specialist.

## What's NOT applicable / hype filter

Dan does not trade. The trading content — the indicator selection, the OHLC benchmark construction, the rate-of-return
math, the 5 bps stop-loss — is not directly relevant to any Linus deliverable. This note treats the trading domain as
scaffolding around the orchestration insight; a future Linus tool for any other domain (literature triage, code review,
experiment design) would lift the topology and discard the trading specifics.

The paper does not name or quantify the underlying LLM driving the headline numbers, which makes the results harder to
credit for Linus' purposes — the gap between QuantAgent and XGBoost might be 80% the LLM and 20% the agent
decomposition, or vice versa. There is no ablation isolating the contribution of agent decomposition from the
contribution of just calling a frontier LLM with all five indicators concatenated into a single prompt. Without that,
"multi-agent helps" is plausible but not proven by this paper.

LangGraph is Python-native and tightly coupled to LangChain. If Linus' orchestration layer ends up Rust-based
(consistent with Dan's Rust-learning trajectory and the pmetal direction), a direct LangGraph adoption is unlikely to
survive long-term — it would be a Phase 2 prototyping shortcut, not the production substrate.

The benchmark uses 100 evaluation segments per asset and a fixed sample size of 5000 historical bars. Standard
deviations and confidence intervals are not reported in the main table. The 80% case-study figure is from a single
100-bar segment with 10 overlapping windows — that's 10 datapoints. Treat all the headline accuracy numbers as
suggestive, not definitive.

The paper does not engage with the problem of LLM hallucination on the pattern-matching step — when PatternAgent claims
"this is a descending triangle," there's no calibration evidence that the rate of false-positive pattern detection is
low enough for the downstream decision. This matters for Linus too: any tool agent that names a pattern or claims a
finding needs an associated confidence and an audit trail.

## Connections

The closest sibling in the corpus is [TradingAgents (2412.20138v7)](2412.20138v7.md), and it's worth reading the two
together. TradingAgents builds an "agent firm" with debate-style consensus, sentiment analysis, and a hierarchy that
mirrors a real trading desk; QuantAgent strips that down to four tool-grounded specialists and one integrator. The two
papers represent two design points on the same axis — heavyweight role-play with debate vs. lightweight tool-grounded
specialists — and the right Linus orchestration pattern is probably closer to QuantAgent than TradingAgents because of
latency and budget concerns.

It also pairs with [Boiko/MacKnight/Gomes (2304.05332v1)](2304.05332v1.md) on multi-agent scientific autonomy: same
planner-executor-tool decomposition pattern, different domain. And it sits next to
[Kosmos (2511.02824v2)](2511.02824v2.md) as another worked instance of structured aggregation across specialist agents —
Kosmos at the high end (world model + parallel rollouts + episodic memory), QuantAgent at the lean end (zero
fine-tuning, zero memory, just structured prompts).

The latency-budget honesty connects to [Speed and Conversational LLMs (2502.16721v1)](2502.16721v1.md): both papers are
reminders that wall-clock end-to-end completion time is the actual constraint, not throughput numbers in isolation.

The "render image then reason multimodally over a curated pattern library" is structurally a retrieval-augmented
generation problem and connects to the KnowledgeBase line: [embeddings (2408.08073v2)](2408.08073v2.md) and the
[knowledge-graph survey (2003.02320v6)](2003.02320v6.md) — replace "pattern library" with "concept library from
KnowledgeBase" and the same scaffold transfers.

Nothing in this paper bears on the BitNet / quantization thread, the memory pillar (Coconut, TTT, ARC), or the
bio-foundation-models work. It is purely orchestration-pattern content.
