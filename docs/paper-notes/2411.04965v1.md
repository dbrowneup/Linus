# BitNet a4.8: 4-bit Activations for 1-bit LLMs

## TL;DR
The first attempt to push BitNet activations down to 4 bits. The trick: a **hybrid scheme** — use 4-bit quantization where activation distributions are well-behaved (Gaussian-like inputs to attention QKV and FFN up/gate), and use **sparsification + 8-bit quantization** where they aren't (the outlier-heavy inputs to attention output projection `Wo` and FFN down projection `Wdown`). Matches BitNet b1.58 accuracy at the same training cost, while activating only ~55% of parameters and supporting 3-bit KV cache. **Superseded by [BitNet v2](2504.18415v2.md)**, which solves the same problem more elegantly using Hadamard transforms instead of sparsification.

## The problem (in plain language)
BitNet b1.58 has ternary weights but 8-bit activations. To run on emerging 4-bit hardware (and to halve memory bandwidth on activations), we need 4-bit activations. The catch: **outlier dimensions**. A small number of channels in the inputs to `Wo` and `Wdown` have activations dozens to hundreds of times larger than the rest. Quantizing them naively to 4 bits crushes everything else into a few bins. Standard outlier-mitigation tricks (Hadamard rotation, learnable rotations à la SpinQuant) work for 4-bit-weight FP-activation models, but for 1-bit weights you can't easily fold the rotation matrix *into* the weights — the weights are ternary and have no precision left to absorb a transformation.

The biochemistry analogue: this is the same problem as detecting both bulk metabolites and trace ones in the same mass-spec run — if your dynamic range is fixed and a few peaks dominate, the rest of the signal disappears. The fix here is to gate on signal type rather than try to compress everything into the same scale.

## What they propose
**Hybrid quantization + sparsification**, applied per-layer based on activation distribution:

- **Inputs to attention (QKV) and FFN up/gate**: ~Gaussian. Quantize to **INT4** with absmean.
- **Inputs to attention output (`Wo`)**: outlier-heavy. **Top-K sparsify** (K=50%, keep biggest, zero out rest), then quantize to INT8.
- **Inputs to FFN down (`Wdown`)**: extremely sparse already because of squared-ReLU GLU activation. **Just quantize to INT8** — the natural sparsity (>80%) already does the outlier-mitigation work.

**Architectural tricks** that enable the sparsity:
- Replace SwiGLU with **squared-ReLU GLU**: `ReLU²(x·W_gate) ⊙ (x·W_up)`. Squared ReLU produces ~67% sparse gates, which combines with the 12% sparse up-input to give ~71% sparse computation in the up projection.
- TopK sparsification at attention output, K=50%.

**Training**: continue-train from BitNet b1.58 — 95B tokens at A8, then 5B tokens at A4. Cheap.

**Also tested**: an FP4 (E2M1 floating-point 4-bit) variant in place of INT4. Roughly equivalent accuracy.

## Key results
- **Matches BitNet b1.58** on perplexity and zero-shot accuracy at 700M / 1.3B / 3B / 7B (all on RedPajama 100B tokens).
- **44.5% overall sparsity** on a 7B model — 3.4B "active" parameters out of 7B.
- **80%+ sparsity** at FFN down projection inputs (essentially free from squared-ReLU).
- **3-bit KV cache** with negligible accuracy loss at 3B and 7B. Even 4-bit Q + 3-bit KV holds.
- Superseded by **BitNet v2**: same goal (W1.58A4) achieved more cleanly via Hadamard transforms with no sparsity-management overhead and slightly better numerics.

Scale: 700M to 7B params, RedPajama. Production-relevant for the hybrid scheme but should generally be replaced by BitNet v2.

## What's reusable in Linus
**Indirect (architecture pattern):** The squared-ReLU GLU + Q-Sparse pattern is a general-purpose tool for inducing activation sparsity. Even on FP16 models, this can give Linus inference-time speedups via skipping zero-mass computations — and Apple Silicon's compute-vs-bandwidth ratio means sparse activations are particularly attractive on M1 Max.

**Indirect (the "hybrid" mindset):** The bigger lesson here is *match the quantization scheme to the local activation distribution rather than imposing one uniform scheme*. That's a model-engineering principle that may generalize beyond BitNet (e.g. mixed-precision LoRA, layer-specific KV cache compression).

**Reference value (historical):** BitNet a4.8 is the *predecessor* of BitNet v2. If we ever debug why BitNet v2 works, this paper is the comparison point. Otherwise, prefer v2.

## What's NOT applicable
- **Don't implement this as-is**: BitNet v2 ([2504.18415v2](2504.18415v2.md)) does the same job better. The Hadamard transform approach is more elegant, has fewer moving parts, and is denser — better for batched inference, which is what Linus would care about for parallel-Worker queries.
- The TopK sparsification adds complexity to inference kernels. On Apple Silicon, where sparse-matmul kernels are not as mature as dense ones, this overhead may eat the savings.
