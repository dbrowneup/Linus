# Unified sampling framework and experimental benchmarking of sequence- and structure-based protein models

## TL;DR

Spinner, Notin, Berry, Cortade et al. (Align Foundation + Marks Lab + NIST; ICML 2026, PMLR 306; bioRxiv
2026.05.08.723784v1) build a **unified Gibbs-sampling framework** that drives seven generative protein-design models —
alignment-based (PSSM, Potts/EVCouplings), VAE (EVE), protein language models (ESM2, Tranception), and inverse-folding
structure-based models (ESM-IF1, ProteinMPNN) — through the **same sequence-generation procedure** so they can be
compared head-to-head under matched conditions. The framework is applied to Tobacco etch virus (TEV) protease, a
236-residue cysteine protease used as a workhorse molecular-biology reagent. They generate **338,688 total sequences**
(~13k experimentally assayed after filtering for an intact catalytic triad H46/D81/C151 and ≤10 mutations from
wildtype), measure protease activity with the quantitative high-throughput **GROQ-seq** assay, and compare in silico
selection metrics against measured function. Headline empirical results: machine-learning-designed libraries achieve a
**39.32% hit rate** (variants at or above wildtype activity) versus **6.06%** for an error-prone-PCR baseline;
**structure-based models dominate** with hit rates of **74.4% (ESM-IF1) and 66.8% (ProteinMPNN)**; the best
ProteinMPNN variant exceeds wildtype activity by **~9-fold**. Crucially, **commonly used selection metrics correlate
only modestly with experimental function** — the strongest individual Spearman correlations are mutation count
(ρ = 0.326), predicted helical content (ρ = 0.264), and ESM-MSA prediction (ρ = −0.28). The paper's sharpest
conclusion: filtering — not generation — is the under-explored bottleneck of ML-guided protein design, and high-
quality experimental data must remain the definitive benchmark. The Python framework is open source
(`github.com/pascalnotin/protein_sampling`) and accepts any model that can compute pseudo-log-likelihoods over
sequences. For Linus, this is a **Phase 7 reference paper** for generative-biology skill design: it establishes the
empirical case that structural inductive bias beats sequence-only bias for enzyme function, validates a multi-model
benchmarking discipline that maps directly onto Dan's metagenomics-protein workflows, and surfaces the
in-silico-vs-experimental gap as a load-bearing engineering problem.

## The problem (in plain language)

Two interlocking gaps frame the work. First, **the generative-protein-design field has no methodological consensus on
how to compare models**. Different papers use different training data, different sampling procedures, different
filtering criteria, and different success metrics — so when a new model claims a higher hit rate on its proteins of
choice, it's nearly impossible to know whether the gain comes from the architecture, the sampling strategy, the
filter, or the choice of test system. The authors' first-page framing is that "comparisons between generative models
remain difficult, and many designed proteins are non-functional, leading to wasted experimental effort." The
operational consequence Dan would recognize from PacBio/LanzaTech: every bespoke design pipeline burns wet-lab budget
that could go to actual validation.

Second, and more deeply, **the filtering step that converts generated sequences into a shortlist for synthesis is
poorly understood**. Generative models routinely produce hundreds of thousands of candidate sequences; only a few
thousand can be synthesized and assayed; the filter in between (pseudo-log-likelihood scores, ESM-1v scores,
ProteinMPNN scores, structural plausibility heuristics) decides which candidates the wet lab actually sees. The paper
shows empirically that these filters carry only modest signal — most selection metrics show Spearman correlations
below 0.3 with measured activity — yet they sit on the critical path of every ML-guided design campaign. The field's
attention has gone to generative architectures; the filter is left as a heuristic afterthought.

The chosen testbed is **TEV protease** because it is (a) widely used and well-characterized, (b) functionally
measurable at scale via the GROQ-seq assay developed alongside this paper, and (c) compact enough (236 residues, with
known catalytic triad and substrate-binding region) to make controlled cross-model comparisons tractable. The S219V
mutation is fixed across all sequences to prevent autocatalysis. The paper is explicit that TEV is a proof-of-concept;
generalization to other protein families is named as essential future work.

The borrowed framing is from the broader ML-benchmarking tradition (the ProteinGym lineage — Notin et al. 2023 — and
the Protein Engineering Tournament, Armer et al. 2024) and the directed-evolution culture of empirical hit-rate
validation. The novelty: combine **controlled cross-model generation** (everyone runs the same Gibbs sampler with
matched mutational distance) with **paired wet-lab measurement at scale** (~13k variants assayed under up to 20
conditions). That pairing is what lets the paper say structural models win **at function**, not just at in silico
scoring.

## What they propose

The framework has four load-bearing pieces.

**Unified Gibbs sampling across seven model classes.** Every model — regardless of architecture — generates sequences
through the same procedure: iteratively mutate one position at a time, sampling amino acids from the model's induced
distribution at that position, with a linear distance restraint penalizing mutations away from wildtype (calibrated
empirically so each model produces ~5 mutations on average; see Table A2). Each model runs **25 sampling steps × 32
parallel chains × 768 sequences per condition**, with the first step of each chain discarded as burn-in. The Gibbs
formulation requires only that a model expose pseudo-log-likelihoods over single-position substitutions, which is
exactly the interface every model class above can provide — including ProteinMPNN, whose ability to decode in any
position order makes it especially well-matched to Gibbs sampling under structural conditioning.

**Multi-source training data.** Alignment-based and VAE models use a multiple sequence alignment built from five
JackHMMER iterations against UniRef100 + MGnify + BFD (relative bitscore threshold 0.2), full-length retrieval with
Easel, ClustalOmega realignment, and column-pruning to query positions with ≤20% gaps — yielding **4,748 homologous
sequences**. Structure-based models (ESM-IF1, ProteinMPNN) consume a full-length AlphaFold3-predicted complex of TEV
in complex with its ENLYFQ|S substrate, because experimentally-resolved TEV structures often exclude the C-terminus
due to autocatalysis. Protein language models (ESM2, Tranception) run without fine-tuning. Alignment-based models use
sequence reweighting threshold θ = 0.9. Each input modality is matched to the architecture that consumes it most
naturally.

**Experimental design constraints baked into generation.** The 236-residue protein is divided into three 78-residue
segments compatible with downstream DNA synthesis. Mutations are restricted within each segment (all other positions
held fixed to wildtype by assigning probability 1). The active-site triad (H46/D81/C151) and the autocatalysis-
preventing S219V mutation are clamped. Minimal additional filtering — sequences are retained if they have a complete
catalytic triad and ≤10 mutations from wildtype — preserves diversity. The pipeline generated **338,688 total
sequences across all models, parameters, and segments**; after filtering, **71,032 sequences remained, of which
67,664 were unique**.

**GROQ-seq experimental measurement at scale.** Gene variants are introduced into a codon-optimized wildtype TEV via
5-part Golden Gate assembly with a barcoded plasmid backbone (Marionette inducible promoter system). The GROQ-seq
quantitative fitness assay (Spinner et al. 2026; Cortade et al. 2024/2026) provides paired per-variant activity
measurements. Each variant is assayed across 20 experimental conditions; the paper analyzes two in detail (S12,
broadest dynamic range; S20, best resolution among top performers). A variant is considered a **"hit"** if its
measured activity is at or above the worst-performing wildtype replicate — a conservative functional threshold that
holds up across conditions.

The selection-metric analysis surveys 30+ sequence- and structure-based features (Hamming distance from wildtype,
predicted helical content, predicted disorder, ESM-1v score, ProteinMPNN score, ESM-MSA prediction, EVE evidence,
mutation count, etc.), regressing each against measured activity under S12 and S20 with Spearman rank correlation —
the right statistic because activity has a non-Gaussian heavy-tailed distribution.

## Key results

**Machine-learning libraries outperform random mutagenesis by ~6.5×.** Across all seven ML models, the average hit
rate is **39.32%** versus **6.06%** for the error-prone-PCR baseline, at comparable mutational loads (Supplementary
Figure S3). This is the headline justification for ML-guided design: even the worst ML model crushes random
mutagenesis on a budget-equivalent basis.

**Structure-based models dominate at function.** The hit-rate table (Figure 4B):

| Model        | Hit rate | Mean mutations |
| ------------ | -------: | -------------: |
| ESM-IF1      |    74.4% |              ~5 |
| ProteinMPNN  |    66.8% |              ~5 |
| Tranception  |    ~50% |              ~5 |
| ESM2         |    ~45% |              ~5 |
| EVE          |    ~30% |              ~5 |
| PSSM         |    ~30% |              ~5 |
| Potts        |    ~30% |              ~5 |
| epPCR        |    6.06% |              ~5 |

ESM-IF1 and ProteinMPNN — both structure-conditioned inverse-folding models — sit ~25 points above the next best
sequence-only model. The paper's interpretation: structural inductive bias is a meaningful prior for enzyme function,
especially when the structural input includes the substrate-bound complex (which TEV's does, via AlphaFold3).
ProteinMPNN's any-order decoder also pairs naturally with Gibbs sampling under fixed-residue conditioning, which is
likely an additional architectural fit advantage.

**Top-of-distribution gains are extreme.** The best ProteinMPNN variant exceeds wildtype activity by **~9-fold**; the
best Tranception and ESM-IF1 variants by **~5-6-fold**; ESM2, EVE, PSSM, and Potts produce **~3-4-fold** best
variants. So while all model classes generate functional improvements, only the structure-conditioned models
reliably produce the extreme outliers — the long-tail behavior practitioners care about.

**Different models explore disjoint regions of sequence space.** Multidimensional scaling on pairwise Hamming
distances (Figure 2A) shows each model occupies a distinct region; pairwise Jaccard similarity between sets of
generated sequences (Figure 2B) confirms that PSSM and Potts have near-identical mutational landscapes (alignment-
based models cluster), while EVE has the widest spread (most exploratory). Models trained on similar data cluster
together; models trained on different modalities diverge. The substrate-binding region (residues 46, 51, 67, 146,
148, 169, 170, 171, 174, 176, 178, 209, 211, 213, 214, 216, 218, 220) is mutated frequently by ESM2 and ProteinMPNN,
mostly avoided by EVE — reflecting differing inductive biases.

**Selection metrics carry only modest signal.** Across the panel of sequence- and structure-based scores (Figure 3),
no individual metric correlates strongly with measured activity. The strongest associations:

- Mutation count: Spearman ρ ≈ 0.326 (more mutations → higher activity, within the ≤10-mutation regime)
- Predicted helical content: Spearman ρ ≈ 0.264
- ESM-MSA prediction: Spearman ρ ≈ −0.28

ESM-1v scores and ProteinMPNN scores — the two principal selection metrics from the COMPSS framework (Johnson et al.
2024) — show distributionally tighter spreads than experimental activity, making them poor discriminators among
already-passed candidates. Correlation patterns are consistent across S12 and S20 but vary in resolution. The
authors' interpretation is sharp: **filtering is the under-explored bottleneck**. Future work should systematically
combine and calibrate these partially-informative signals — potentially through a learned filter on top of the base
metrics — rather than rely on any single score.

**Activity rankings are stable across experimental conditions.** Figure 4C shows median activity across 20 conditions;
the structure-based models consistently lead, sequence-based models trail consistently, and the ranking is robust to
the assay's environmental variation. So the cross-model verdict is not a measurement artifact of one condition.

**Active-site preservation is not uniform.** Table 1 reports the fraction of deduplicated sequences with a complete
active site post-filtering: PSSM and Potts retain 100% (9990/9990 and 9923/9923), EVE 99% (13352/13476), ESM2 99.99%
(9915/9916), Tranception 83% (9012/10861), ESM-IF1 89% (10067/11329). Alignment-based models, by virtue of being
trained on natural homologs, preferentially preserve catalytic residues without explicit prompting; structure-based
and language-based models need the explicit clamping step. Operationally important for practitioners: model choice
affects how much downstream constraint enforcement is needed.

## What's reusable in Linus

**Phase 7 — Generative-protein-design benchmarking discipline as the canonical Linus biology-skill methodology
(DEC-0046, DEC-0047, `docs/specs/biology-phase7-roadmap.md`).** The paper's methodology — controlled generation under
matched conditions, ≥5 model classes assayed against the same wet-lab readout, hit-rate computed against a
conservative wildtype-replicate threshold — is the right shape for Linus's Phase 7 biology-skill validation
contract. The four-tier biology evaluation template currently named in the Phase 7 roadmap (BioReason-Pro shape) can
absorb this paper's discipline as the **protein-design-skill** variant: a skill that produces designed sequences
must report (a) hit rate vs. wildtype-equivalent threshold, (b) selection-metric Spearman against measured activity
on a held-out wet-lab dataset, (c) sequence-space coverage relative to other available models, (d) active-site
preservation rate. The methodology is general and transfers to any enzyme system Dan's metagenomics workflows surface
(thermostable proteases, lipases, P450s, etc.). The companion DISCO paper-note
([`2604.05181v1.md`](2604.05181v1.md)) covers the diffusion-based sequence-structure-codesign side; this paper
covers the discrete-Gibbs single-modality side.

**Phase 7 — Unified Gibbs sampling as the Linus protein-generation Worker interface (Phase 7).** The framework's
load-bearing abstraction — "any model that can compute pseudo-log-likelihoods over sequences can be plugged into a
unified Gibbs sampler" — is the right abstraction for a Linus protein-design Worker. Dan's Phase 7 biology skill
catalog can declare a `ProteinSequenceWorker` interface whose primary capability is
`logprob(sequence, position) -> distribution`, and the Linus orchestration layer drives any compliant model (PSSM,
ESM-IF1, ProteinMPNN, BioReason-Pro, DISCO, etc.) through a common sampling procedure. This is exactly the
"orchestration-layer-accrues-value" framing from CLAUDE.md: the Worker models are interchangeable; the sampling
discipline + experimental-validation contract is the durable Linus surface. The Python framework is open source at
`github.com/pascalnotin/protein_sampling` — study the interface shape before designing the Linus equivalent.

**Phase 7 — Filtering as a first-class skill, not a post-hoc heuristic (DEC-0046 typed structured prediction).** The
paper's most actionable finding for Linus is that **filtering is the under-explored bottleneck**. For Phase 7
biology skills, this means the `ProteinDesignSkill` output schema should expose not just `{designed_sequences,
rationale}` but also `{designed_sequences, selection_scores, calibrated_filter_score, evidence, rationale}` —
treating the filter as a first-class predictive component subject to typed structured prediction (per DEC-0046,
"typed structured prediction wrapping free-text rationale"). The calibrated filter is exactly the place where a
small, Linus-trained meta-model — a learned combination of in-silico signals against a held-out wet-lab anchor —
would earn its keep. This is a Phase 7+ candidate for a Linus-original contribution, not just a Worker invocation.

**Phase 7 — Wet-lab-anchor evaluation suite as the canonical biology benchmark (DEC-0035, `benchmarks/dan_tasks/`).**
The paper's ~13k assayed variants across 20 experimental conditions is the right scale for a publicly-anchored
biology benchmark. If the authors release the variant + activity table (the paper's "released as a community
resource" claim suggests they intend to), Linus's Phase 7 biology benchmark suite under `benchmarks/dan_tasks/biology/`
should absorb the TEV protease dataset as a first-class anchor — alongside the canary-protected LAB-Bench and the
BioReason-Pro four-tier evaluation. The discipline rule: every Linus protein-design skill must produce hit-rate +
calibrated-filter metrics against this anchor before claiming production readiness. This is the protein-design
analogue of DEC-0035's ARC-AGI-as-memory-diagnostic move.

**Phase 1c+ — Structural inductive bias as a Worker-selection heuristic for biology tasks (DEC-0034).** The headline
result — structure-conditioned models (ESM-IF1, ProteinMPNN) deliver hit rates ~25 points above sequence-only models
on enzyme function — is a strong empirical prior for Phase 1c+ Worker-selection decisions on biology tasks. When the
spawner is dispatching a protein-design Worker, the default should be the structure-conditioned option unless the
task explicitly requires sequence-only modeling (e.g., when no structural input is available). This is a Worker-
registry-property fact, not just a paper observation: tag protein-design Workers with their conditioning modality
and bias dispatch accordingly.

**Phase 7 — Active-site preservation rate as a Worker-quality property (CLAUDE.md "Measure, don't just estimate" +
DEC-0046).** The paper's Table 1 — different models preserve the catalytic triad at different rates (100% for
alignment-based, ~83-89% for Tranception/ESM-IF1) — is a measurable Worker property that the Phase 7 skill registry
should record. Operationally: a Worker whose unprompted output preserves catalytic residues 100% of the time needs
less downstream constraint-enforcement scaffolding than one that needs explicit clamping; the dispatcher should
account for this in skill selection.

**Methodology — five-seed-equivalent replication discipline carries over.** The paper uses **25 sampling steps × 32
parallel chains** per model per condition, which is the protein-design analogue of the five-seed replication
discipline already adopted from SWARM ([`swarm-2604.19752.md`](swarm-2604.19752.md)). The Linus Phase 7 benchmark
spec should commit to the analogue — multiple sampling chains per Worker invocation, with `mean ± std` reporting —
as a load-bearing reproducibility constraint.

**Phase 4 — GROQ-seq-style paired-readout assays as the canonical wet-lab integration shape.** Dan's metagenomics
workflows at LanzaTech routinely produce paired-readout data (sequence → measured activity); the GROQ-seq design
pattern (barcoded plasmid backbone + multiplexed assay + per-variant activity readout) is the right substrate for
Linus's Phase 4 versioned-dataset infrastructure to expect. The Phase 4 dataset shape should accommodate
`{variant_id, sequence, condition, measured_activity}` records as first-class, with the model that designed each
variant as a provenance field — exactly the schema this paper releases.

## What's NOT applicable / hype filter

**TEV protease is a proof-of-concept, not a general claim about all enzymes.** The paper is explicit (Discussion,
Conclusion) that the structure-conditioned-wins result holds for TEV — a well-characterized protease with a known
catalytic triad, a co-crystallized substrate, and a tight functional readout. The generalization claim ("extending
these analyses beyond a single enzyme to multiple protein families will be critical") is named as future work. For
Linus's Phase 7 adoption: take the methodology as durable; take the structure-wins-by-25-points number as a TEV-
specific anchor, not a universal prior. A thermostable protease, a P450, or a metalloenzyme might show very
different model-class rankings.

**The "ML beats epPCR 6.5×" number is system-specific.** TEV is a robust scaffold; random-mutagenesis baselines on
more brittle scaffolds (where most epPCR mutations are lethal) would show much smaller ML enrichment factors. The
6.5× figure is the right anchor for "ML-guided design pays off on TEV-class systems"; it is not a universal claim
about all proteins.

**No fine-tuning was performed on the language models.** ESM2 and Tranception were used off-the-shelf, without TEV-
specific or protease-family-specific fine-tuning. A reasonable hypothesis is that family-fine-tuned language models
would close some of the gap with structure-based models; the paper does not test this. So the
language-models-trail-structure-models result is a **lower bound** on language-model performance, not a definitive
verdict.

**The 30+ selection metrics tested do not include learned-filter combinations.** The paper's Spearman correlations
are per-metric; no learned-combination filter was tested empirically. The authors hypothesize that a calibrated
combination "potentially via an additional layer of machine learning" would improve prediction, but they do not
demonstrate it. So the "filtering is the bottleneck" claim is well-supported as a problem statement; the proposed
solution (calibrated learned filters) is a conjecture for future work, not a validated result.

**The sampling-procedure unification is a methodological choice with tradeoffs.** Gibbs sampling is a sensible
common denominator across all seven model classes, but it is **not** the native sampling strategy for any of them.
ProteinMPNN typically samples via temperature-scaled categorical sampling over its full-sequence prediction; ESM2 is
typically used with masked-token prediction or with energy-based sampling. The unified Gibbs procedure may
under-represent the best each model can do under its native sampling regime. The paper acknowledges this implicitly
by reporting native-format scores (ESM-1v, ProteinMPNN scores) alongside the unified-Gibbs results, but the
cross-model comparison is contingent on the Gibbs choice. For Linus's Phase 7 adoption: keep the unified-sampling
discipline for cross-model comparison, but allow native-sampling regimes for production model invocations.

**The 5-mutation-from-wildtype regime is a deliberate constraint, not a generality claim.** All experiments operate
within ≤10 mutations from wildtype; the average is ~5. This is the regime where ML-guided design is most likely to
preserve folding and basic function. Outside this regime (e.g., 20+ mutations, or de novo design of new enzymes from
scratch — the DISCO regime, [`2604.05181v1.md`](2604.05181v1.md)), the results do not necessarily transfer. The
paper's claims are scoped to **fine-grained engineering of existing scaffolds**, not de novo design.

**The "filtering is the bottleneck" finding is conditional on the filter being heuristic.** The paper tests
unsupervised pseudo-log-likelihood scores and pretrained-model scores. Filters that incorporate experimental priors
(e.g., a learned filter trained on a paired wet-lab dataset, or task-specific fine-tuning of the filter model on
related family data) may perform much better. So the bottleneck framing is correct for current practice; the
opportunity for improvement is real but does not invalidate well-designed filters that exist in specific contexts.

**Computational scoring tightness can be informative even when correlation is modest.** The paper notes that even
with modest global correlations, certain thresholds may reliably identify non-functional variants. So
"filtering is the bottleneck" should not be read as "current filters are useless" — they have meaningful negative
predictive value at the low-confidence tail, which is operationally important for downstream synthesis decisions.

   conditioning modality and bias dispatch accordingly?** The paper's empirical result (structure-conditioned wins

   the default toward structure-conditioned when structural input is available; do not hard-code the dispatch
   ranking until the methodology is replicated on a second enzyme family.
