# Biophysics-based protein language models for protein engineering

## TL;DR

METL (Mutational Effect Transfer Learning) is a transformer-based protein language model that **pretrains on Rosetta
biophysical simulation data instead of natural sequence evolution**, then fine-tunes on small experimental
sequence–function datasets. Across 11 deep-mutational-scanning benchmarks (GFP, GB1, PTEN, TEM-1, DLG4, GRB2, Pab1,
Ube4b), METL-Local matches or beats evolutionary models (ESM-2, EVE, ProteinNPT, Linear-EVE) particularly in the
small-data and position-extrapolation regimes that matter most for real protein engineering. The headline demo:
**METL-Local fine-tuned on only 64 GFP variants designs functional fluorescent variants**, hitting 100 % success (5/5
and 5/5) on "use observed amino acids" designs at 5 and 10 mutations from wild-type, and 80 % / 40 % on the harder "use
unobserved amino acids" setting where the model must invent substitutions never seen during fine-tuning. Code, weights
and a Hugging Face Spaces demo are public. The whole system is small (METL-Local ~2.5 M params, METL-Global ~20–50 M
params), trained on a few A100s.

## The problem (in plain language)

Most protein language models — UniRep, ESM-2, ProtT5, ESM-3 — learn from natural protein sequences scraped from UniProt
and friends. They treat a protein as a "sentence" and let masked-language-modeling absorb whatever signal is implicit in
evolutionary co-occurrence. This works because evolution does compute a fitness landscape for you, just slowly and in
confounded ways: phylogenetic biases, drift, unequal sampling, unknown selective pressures.

But protein engineering is not the same problem as predicting natural variation. The engineer wants to know what happens
when you make a mutation evolution never sampled — a non-natural amino acid, a position no homolog mutates, a
combination of substitutions wild populations never combined. Evolutionary PLMs are weak in exactly this regime because
the training data is silent on it. Meanwhile, biochemists have spent a century building biophysical models — Rosetta,
Amber, force fields, solvation, hydrogen-bonding — that _do_ score arbitrary sequences. They are slow and approximate,
but unbiased toward natural-vs-not, and they encode mechanism instead of correlation.

METL's premise is that a PLM can be pretrained on **synthetic data from biophysical simulation** instead of natural
sequences, giving it a complementary inductive bias that generalizes better to the off-distribution mutations engineers
actually want to design.

## What they propose

METL has three steps:

**1. Synthetic data generation.** Sample millions of sequence variants of a protein of interest (up to 5 amino-acid
substitutions) and run each through Rosetta to model the structure and extract 55 biophysical attributes (total energy,
van der Waals, hydrogen bonding, solvation energies, surface areas, etc.). For METL-Local this is 20 M variants of one
protein; for METL-Global this is ~30 M variants spanning 148 diverse base proteins.

**2. Pretraining.** Train a transformer encoder to predict the 55 biophysical attributes from the sequence. The
architecture innovation is a **structure-based 3D relative position embedding** that encodes residue–residue distances
in the wild-type structure rather than sequence positions. This bakes the fold into the inductive bias and is shown
(Extended Data Fig. 1) to make the post-pretraining attention map closely resemble the contact map, before the model has
seen any experimental data.

**3. Fine-tuning.** Take the pretrained encoder and fine-tune on the (typically tiny) experimental sequence–function
dataset for the engineering task at hand. The output head predicts the experimental property — fluorescence, binding
affinity, thermostability, catalytic activity, expression — from sequence.

Two pretraining flavors: **METL-Local** ties pretraining to one specific protein scaffold (most accurate, but
per-protein cost). **METL-Global** pretrains across 148 folds for general use (more reusable, but overfits to those 148
— out-of-distribution Spearman drops from 0.85 to 0.16 on the pretraining task, though downstream fine-tuning still
works).

A crucial extension is **METL-Bind**: pretrain not only on standalone-protein Rosetta scores but on Rosetta scores of
the _protein–ligand complex_ (here, GB1–IgG). This injects function-specific biophysics into pretraining and outperforms
generic METL-Local at low data, especially at the binding interface residue (E27 for GB1–IgG).

## Key results

**Small-data benchmarks.** Across 11 datasets, learning curves from 8 to 16,384 training examples show METL-Local
consistently beating ESM-2 and matching or beating Linear-EVE in the small-data regime (training size <500). On GFP and
GB1 specifically, METL-Local dominates. Once experimental data passes ~thousands of examples, dataset-specific signal
overtakes the biophysics prior and the methods converge.

**Extrapolation tasks.** The paper splits four difficult extrapolation tests:

- _Mutation extrapolation_ (predict unseen amino acids at observed positions): METL-Local, ProteinNPT, ESM-2 and
  Linear-EVE all reach Spearman ~0.70–0.78. Roughly tied.
- _Position extrapolation_ (predict mutations at positions never seen in training): ProteinNPT 0.65 and METL-Local 0.59
  lead, with everything else falling off. METL-Local's local-pretraining data covers all positions, which the paper
  credits for the advantage.
- _Regime extrapolation_ (train on singles, predict multi-mutants): supervised methods all reach >0.75 average Spearman.
  The landscapes are dominated by additive effects, so even linear regression does well.
- _Score extrapolation_ (train on below-WT, predict above-WT): hard for everything (<0.3 Spearman on most datasets),
  with the GB1 dataset as a clean exception where METL-Local and METL-Global both hit >0.7. The takeaway: predicting
  _gain of function_ is fundamentally different from predicting _loss of function_, and Rosetta total score / EVE
  largely separate active from inactive variants rather than ranking activity.

**Information value of simulated vs experimental data.** A clean trade-off plot for GB1: ~29 simulated data points have
the same downstream effect as 1 experimental data point. Diminishing returns kick in around 16,000–128,000 simulated
examples — far below the 20 M used in the main experiments — meaning the practical pretraining cost can be much lower
than the paper's headline numbers.

**The GFP design experiment.** The most concrete and engineering-relevant result. Fine-tune METL-Local on **64**
randomly sampled GFP variants (avg 3.9 mutations from wild-type), then use simulated annealing over the predicted
fitness landscape to design new sequences with 5 or 10 mutations from wild-type, under two regimes: "Observed AA"
(mutations seen at any position during fine-tuning) and "Unobserved AA" (mutations the model has never seen). Synthesize
20 designs plus 20 random baselines, fuse to mKate2, measure relative brightness in a fluorescence assay.

Result: 16 of 20 designs are functional. Observed AA: 10/10 fluorescent. Unobserved AA at 5 mutations: 4/5. Unobserved
AA at 10 mutations: 2/5. Random baselines are uniformly dark, with one accidental hit that METL also predicted as
functional. Designs do not exceed wild-type brightness, but several show higher absolute mKate2 fusion fluorescence
(suggesting better folding/stability).

**Architecture ablations.** 3D structure-based position embeddings beat 1D sequence embeddings, particularly for tasks
where structural context matters. Fine-tuning beats feature extraction. The pretrained attention map cleanly
recapitulates the contact map even before fine-tuning. METL-Bind (function-specific pretraining) outperforms generic
METL-Local at low data. All of these confirm that the biophysics signal is doing real work, not just providing extra
parameters.

## What's reusable in Linus

**Phase 6 (Fine-Tuning) — the pattern is exactly Dan's regime.** METL is a working, published, code-released example of
"small experimental dataset + biophysics-pretrained foundation model = real designs." This is the operating regime any
Linus protein-design Worker would live in: Dan does not have ImageNet-scale wet-lab data, and never will. The METL
fine-tuning workflow (load pretrained encoder, swap head, train on hundreds of examples) is structurally identical to
the LoRA/QLoRA fine-tuning Linus is planning for code/text Workers. The compute is also small enough to matter —
fine-tuning the 20 M-param METL-Global is 20–42 min on an A100; on M1 Max via MLX this is plausible CPU-time territory
rather than aspirational. **METL is the most directly tractable fine-tuning case study Linus has seen so far.**

**Phase 7 (Skills) — candidate "protein engineering" skill, drop-in.** Code, pretrained weights, Hugging Face Spaces
demo, Colab notebooks, Rosetta-data-generation pipeline are all public at `https://github.com/gitter-lab/metl/` and
`https://huggingface.co/gitter-lab/METL/`. The model is tiny (2.5–50 M params, three encoder layers, 256 embedding) —
runs comfortably in 32 GB unified memory. A `linus.skills.protein.metl_predict(sequence)` and
`linus.skills.protein.metl_design(target_protein, n_mutations, training_csv)` pair is a low-risk Phase 7 skill
prototype. The bottleneck is not Linus side; it is the Rosetta pretraining pipeline, which assumes either a precomputed
checkpoint exists for the target protein or Open Science Pool access for new ones.

**Architectural pattern: pretrain on simulation, fine-tune on real data.** This is the most transferable lesson. It
generalizes well beyond proteins to any Linus domain Worker where (a) the engineering target has scarce real data, and
(b) a slower-but-correct simulator exists. Candidate domains in Dan's neighborhood: enzyme kinetics (MD/QM-MM
simulations → real assays), RNA structure (folding simulators → SHAPE data), small-molecule docking (Rosetta/AutoDock →
binding assays), spectroscopy interpretation (DFT/molecular simulation → measured spectra). This is worth promoting to a
named pattern in `docs/synthesis/` — "biophysics-pretrained, experimentally-fine-tuned" or more generally
"simulation-pretrained, observation-fine-tuned" — alongside the data-curation and verbosity-calibration patterns already
noted.

**Multi-task extension is straightforward.** The paper notes METL can predict multiple properties (e.g., GB1
thermostability _and_ GB1–IgG binding) simultaneously. For a Linus skill that exposes "predict everything we know about
this variant" rather than separate one-property endpoints, the architecture is already set up.

**The 29-simulated ≈ 1-experimental ratio is a usable budget heuristic.** When sketching feasibility for a new
protein-design task, this gives a back-of-envelope answer to "how much Rosetta compute should we run before each wet-lab
cycle?" — order-of-magnitude useful even if protein-specific.

## What's NOT applicable

METL was trained on 4× A100s; the 20 M-variant Rosetta dataset took substantial cluster compute. The pretraining stage
is not reproducible on M1 Max for new proteins from scratch. Linus would have to either (a) consume the public
METL-Global checkpoint for fine-tuning only, (b) use METL-Local checkpoints for the proteins the authors already
pretrained, or (c) outsource Rosetta data generation to Open Science Pool / a cloud burst. The "design any protein"
version requires non-Linus compute upstream.

The 3D structure-based relative position embedding requires a wild-type structure as input. For proteins with no
experimental structure and no AlphaFold prediction, METL-Local does not directly apply — though AlphaFold-predicted
structures are now standard fallbacks and the paper does not flag this as a constraint in practice.

The benchmarks are all deep-mutational-scanning datasets — single proteins varied by point mutations. METL is not a _de
novo_ design tool (RFdiffusion / Chroma occupy that niche) and is not aimed at large insertions/deletions or chimeric
proteins. For Linus, "design a new fold" is out of scope; "engineer this enzyme" is in scope.

The score-extrapolation result (going from below-WT to above-WT activity is hard) is a real limitation that any
practical Linus protein-engineering skill must surface to Dan as a caveat: METL is much better at recognizing "is this
mutation harmful?" than at proposing "is this mutation an improvement over wild-type?" The honest framing is
mutational-effect _triage_, not de novo improvement.

## Connections

The closest neighbor in the Group A wave is [ProteinReasoner](2025.07.21.665832v2.md), which is also a structure-aware
PLM but takes a different route: pretrain on natural sequences with structural objectives layered in, then add
reasoning-style fine-tuning. METL is the _biophysical-simulation_ corner of the same design space ProteinReasoner
occupies via _natural-sequence + structure_. Together they mark out two complementary axes Linus' protein-foundation
thinking needs to cover: simulation-grounded vs evolution-grounded, both with structural priors. A Linus protein Worker
that wraps both, dispatching based on whether the target is engineerable-by-mutation (METL) or characterize-and-reason
(ProteinReasoner), is a natural Phase 7 design.

LucaOne and Evo 2 (also in this wave) sit at the _largest scale, sequence-only, joint nucleic-acid + protein_ end of the
spectrum. They are powerful generalists but inherit the evolutionary-bias limitation METL is explicitly designed to
escape. The contrast is sharp: METL chose small-and-focused with mechanistic priors; the giant FMs chose
massive-and-general with statistical priors. For Dan's actual workflow — engineering specific proteins with limited
wet-lab data — METL's small-and-focused bet is the better match.

Within Linus more broadly, the simulation-pretrain + experiment-fine-tune pattern echoes the data-curation arguments
from [FineWeb (2406.17557v2)](2406.17557v2.md) and
[Karpathy's autoresearch loop](https://github.com/karpathy/autoresearch): the actionable insight in all three is that
_data quality and data shape matter more than data quantity_, and that the right preprocessing (curation, simulation,
agent-loop synthesis) can shrink the experimental corpus needed by orders of magnitude. This pattern deserves to be
lifted out of any single paper-note and into `docs/synthesis/` as a first-class architectural concept.

The structure-based 3D relative position embedding is also a useful datapoint for the broader question of "how do we
inject structural priors into transformers cheaply?" — relevant to the long-term Linus interest in fine-tuning small
models well.

1. **Is "biophysics-pretrained, experimentally-fine-tuned" worth naming as a Linus architectural pattern?** This paper
   plus the wider trend toward simulation-augmented foundation models (AlphaFold, Boltz, OpenMM-trained models) suggests
   yes. Proposed location: `docs/synthesis/simulation-pretrained-fine-tuned.md`. Should this be on the Phase-1 synthesis
   backlog, or held until a second concrete instance lands in the paper queue?

2. **METL as a Phase 7 skill — pull the trigger now?** The checkpoints are public, the model is M1-Max-sized, and a
   wrapper around the Hugging Face inference + Colab fine-tuning notebooks would give Linus a usable
   `protein_engineering` skill within a day or two of focused work. The blocker is not technical; it is whether protein
   engineering is on Dan's actual near-term task list. Worth a yes/no decision so we don't accidentally build a skill
   nobody uses.

3. **M1 Max viability of the fine-tuning step.** The paper reports 20–42 min on an A100 to fine-tune the 20 M-param
   METL-Global. Translating that to MLX on M1 Max is a small experiment — run a Rosetta-pretrained checkpoint, fine-tune
   on the public GB1 split, measure wall-clock. Worth a Phase-1 spike (`experiments/metl-m1-fine-tune.md`)? This would
   also stress-test our LoRA/MLX toolchain on a real foundation model, well before we attempt it on a Linus-internal
   model.

4. **Does the GB1-style "function-specific simulation" extension matter for any of Dan's actual target proteins?**
   METL-Bind is the most striking architectural extension — pretrain on the simulated complex, not just the simulated
   monomer — and it materially beats generic METL-Local at low data. If Dan has any specific protein-engineering project
   where the relevant function is binding/catalysis with a known partner, that would be the ideal candidate to test the
   pattern in-house. If not, METL is most useful as an _exemplar_ rather than a tool.

5. **Should the Linus paper-note tagging system gain a `lora-candidate` or `fine-tuning-tractable-on-m1` tag?** This
   paper is the strongest exemplar so far of a fine-tuning workflow Linus could realistically reproduce. A queryable tag
   would make that class of papers easy to surface when Phase 6 work begins in earnest.

6. **Open Science Pool / Rosetta-data pipeline.** The METL repo links a Jupyter notebook for generating Rosetta
   pretraining data on the Open Science Pool. Dan is research-eligible. If we ever want METL pretrained on a
   non-published target, OSP is the off-Linus compute path — worth a one-pager in `docs/repo-notes/` before the need is
   urgent?
