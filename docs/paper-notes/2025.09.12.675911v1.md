# Generative design of novel bacteriophages with genome language models

## TL;DR

This is the **first experimental demonstration that a genome language model can design viable, complete genomes from
scratch**. The authors take Evo 1 (7B/131K) and Evo 2 (7B/8K), fine-tune both on ~15,000 Microviridae sequences, prompt
with the conserved 4–9 nt start of ΦX174's consensus, sample at temperature 0.7–0.9, and apply a tiered constraint stack
(sequence quality, host tropism via spike-protein identity, and evolutionary diversification via AAI and synteny). They
synthesize and assemble 285 of 302 candidates; **16 produce viable phages** lysing E. coli C. Some encode novel
architectures: Evo-Φ36 carries an evolutionarily distant J packaging protein from phage G4 — a swap _not viable_ in
wild-type ΦX174 — and cryo-EM at 2.9 Å reveals a structurally compatible reorientation. Several generated phages
outcompete ΦX174 in fitness assays (Evo-Φ69 with 16–65× cumulative fold change), one (Evo-Φ2483) lyses faster, and a
cocktail of all 16 plus ΦX174 overcomes resistance in three E. coli strains within five passages while ΦX174 alone never
does. Recovered resistance-breakers turn out to be recombinants of generated phages with novel SNVs on the outer
capsid/spike surfaces. Whole-organism generative design now has experimental validation, and the recipe — pretrain → SFT
→ prompt → inference-time guidance → score/filter — is publicly documented. It is also the most dual-use-relevant paper
in the Group B wave.

## The problem (in plain language)

Generative biology has lived at sub-genome scale: protein design (RFdiffusion, ProteinMPNN, ESM3), CRISPR-Cas
composition with Evo 1, transposable elements, antibody affinity maturation. These are component-level designs — small
numbers of interacting parts, embedded in a host organism whose genome is fixed and known.

Whole-genome design is qualitatively harder. Genomes are emergently complex: gene order, overlapping ORFs, regulatory
elements, terminators, origins of replication, recognition sequences, and structural motifs all interact. ΦX174 contains
overlapping reading frames so dense that none of six standard gene predictors annotate all 11 of its genes correctly. A
single point mutation can render an entire genome non-viable. Decades of rational engineering and directed evolution on
phages have struggled to produce viable variants with substantially different lysis kinetics, novel architectures, or
length changes. The viable-genome manifold is thin and irregularly shaped within a vast search space.

The question this paper answers is whether modern genome LMs — Evo 1 and Evo 2, trained on ~2 million phage genomes
among other DNA — have learned that manifold well enough to **sample functional whole genomes from it**, conditioned on
a desired host. Until this paper, the question was open: Evo 2 had been used for component design and zero-shot variant
scoring, but no one had written a functional genome end-to-end. The authors close that gap by combining the language
model with a deliberately layered design pipeline.

## What they propose

The pipeline is six stages and worth naming explicitly because it generalizes:

1. **Choose a target host** of biological or therapeutic interest (here, E. coli C).
2. **Choose a design template** — a known phage that infects the host (ΦX174) — and collect evolutionarily related
   sequence data (the Microviridae family).
3. **Fine-tune a genome language model** on that family-scoped dataset. They SFT both Evo 1 7B/131K and Evo 2 7B/8K on
   ~15K Microviridae sequences. SFT dramatically improves ΦX174-like sequence recall over the base models, which fail to
   recall ΦX174 even with long consensus prompts.
4. **Prompt with template-specific conserved context.** They identify a conserved 4–9 nucleotide start sequence in ΦX174
   variants and use it as the autoregressive prompt; longer prompts (≥9 nt) collapse to memorized recall, shorter
   prompts (1–2 nt) under-condition. Prompt length and sampling temperature (0.7–0.9) are tuned together to control the
   diversity-vs-fidelity trade-off.
5. **Inference-time guidance via constraint scoring.** A three-tier filter cascade narrows ~thousands of candidates to a
   hand-curated set of 302. Tier 1 (quality): nucleotide composition, length 4–6 kb, GC 30–65%, homopolymer cap. Tier 2
   (tropism): a custom-built CDS predictor (because no off-the-shelf tool annotates all 11 ΦX174 genes), requiring ≥7
   protein hits to natural ΦX174 proteins and a spike-protein identity ≥60% to ΦX174 (host receptor specificity is
   dominated by spike-LPS binding). Tier 3 (diversification): <95% AAI to natural proteins; "genetic architecture"
   constraint preferring 10–12-gene synteny variants. CheckV classifies >87% of the final candidates as High Quality or
   Complete.
6. **Wet-lab validation.** dsDNA fragments synthesized by Twist Bioscience, in-vitro assembled, transformed into E. coli
   C using a phage-rebooting protocol, screened by growth inhibition, then verified by long-read sequencing, propagated,
   and titered. Tropism is confirmed by negative results across seven other E. coli strains.

The overall recipe — **pretrained genome FM → family-scoped SFT → template-prompt with consensus context →
inference-time guidance via custom scoring tools → experimental ranking** — is the architectural pattern worth naming.
It is the genome-design analogue of the "agentic loop with tool-use over a base LLM" pattern that has come to dominate
code generation, with one critical addition: the inner loop is closed by wet-lab biology, not by a unit test.

## Key results

- **16 viable phages out of 302 designed and 285 assembled.** A 5.6% hit rate from generation, 5.3% from successful
  synthesis. Nine of the 16 had no acquired mutations relative to the designed sequence; the other seven picked up SNVs
  or small deletions during assembly/propagation.
- **Substantial novelty.** Generated genomes carry between 67 and 392 novel mutations relative to their nearest natural
  neighbor; nucleotide identities range 93.0%–98.8%. Evo-Φ2147 at 93.0% ANI to its nearest hit (NC51) crosses the
  conventional <95% ANI threshold for a new phage species. 13 of 16 had mutations that could not be reconstructed from
  any known natural sequence, including extended novel noncoding regions in Evo-Φ63, Evo-Φ2147, and Evo-Φ2483.
- **Novel architectures.** Evo-Φ63 has a 268 bp genome insertion (5% longer than ΦX174). Evo-Φ114 lost gene K. Evo-Φ75
  and Evo-Φ100 carry truncated gene C. Evo-Φ4 and Evo-Φ46 elongated gene E.
- **A protein swap that should not have worked.** Evo-Φ36 replaces ΦX174's J packaging protein (38 aa) with phage G4's J
  (25 aa, lacking domains 0 and I that contribute to ΦX174 capsid binding). Prior work showed this swap is non-viable in
  the wild-type ΦX174 background. Cryo-EM at 2.9 Å reveals that Evo-Φ36's J adopts a structurally distinct binding mode
  preserving compatibility with the rest of the capsid — a genuine novel co-evolutionary solution discovered by
  sampling.
- **Higher fitness than the wild type.** In three head-to-head competition assays at equal MOI in E. coli C, Evo-Φ69
  dominated all comers (16×–65× cumulative fold change vs ΦX174's 1.3×–4.0×). Evo-Φ2483 had the strongest lytic
  dynamics: minimum OD600 of 0.07 vs ΦX174's 0.22, reaching minimum density in 135 min vs 180 min.
- **Cocktail overcomes resistance.** The authors evolved three independent ΦX174-resistant E. coli C strains (CR1, CR2,
  CR3 — all carrying mutations in waaT or waaW LPS-synthesis genes). A cocktail of all 16 generated phages plus ΦX174
  inhibited CR1 in one passage, CR2 in two, CR3 in five. ΦX174 alone never overcame any of them. Sequencing recovered
  Evo-ΦR1 and Evo-ΦR2 — recombinants composed of segments from Evo-Φ111, Evo-Φ114, and Evo-Φ2147, with novel SNVs
  concentrated on capsid and spike outer surfaces (per AlphaFold 3 modeling).

## What's reusable in Linus

**Direct, conceptual — the architectural pattern.** The pipeline (pretrained FM → family-scoped SFT → template prompt →
inference-time scoring → ranked candidates) is the canonical Linus "generative-biology archetype." It maps cleanly onto
tool-registry orchestration: the FM is one tool, the SFT loop another, the prompt builder another, the constraint
scorers (CheckV, geNomad, custom CDS predictor, BLAST, ESMFold/AlphaFold) are tools, and the wet-lab validator is the
human-in-the-loop terminator. A `docs/syntheses/generative-biology-archetype.md` write-up pairing this with Evo 2
(paper-notes/s41586-026-10176-5.md) is a natural Wave 3 deliverable; the Group A synthesis flagged "what would Evo 2
enable downstream?" — this paper _is_ the answer.

**Direct, deployable artifacts.** Fine-tuned Microviridae checkpoints on Hugging Face
(`evo-design/evo-1-7b-131k-microviridae`, `evo-design/evo-2-7b-8k-microviridae`); generation and analysis code at
`evo-design/evo` and `arcinstitute/evo2`; training data on Zenodo (10.5281/zenodo.17101843). The computational half is
reproducible _if_ an Evo 2 deployment exists on Mac hardware — currently a pending question for Phase 2/3.

**Direct, KnowledgeBase content.** Designed genomes + parent prompts + experimental fitness data + cryo-EM maps are
ideal KG content with clean provenance: every node has a prompt parent, a model version, a score vector, an experimental
outcome. Ingesting the Microviridae corpus, 302 candidates, and 16 viable genomes gives the KB a multi-layer,
multi-modal, cleanly-provenanced subgraph to stress-test schema and retrieval against.

**Indirect, the inference-time-guidance thread.** Constraint scoring during generation (not just post-hoc filtering) is
the genome-design analogue of test-time compute applied to reasoning. It connects to the broader thread running through
the memory pillar, ARC Prize 2024, and the hypercube-geometry work: more inference-time compute through the right
scoring functions often beats more pretraining. For Linus this generalizes — the right answer to "make Linus better at
X" is often "compose a scoring function and search at inference time," not "fine-tune harder."

**Indirect, Phase 7.** The protocol for orchestrating a generate→score→rank→propose loop with named tools and versioned
intermediates is exactly the multi-step generative-with-scoring workflow Linus's tool registry should support,
regardless of whether Evo 2 itself ever runs locally.

## What's NOT applicable / hype filter

**Hardware mismatch is total.** Evo 1 7B/131K and Evo 2 7B/8K were trained on Arc Institute / Stanford compute clusters
and run on H100-class GPUs. There is no MLX port. The 131K context length on Evo 1 is well beyond what 32 GB unified
memory comfortably supports for inference, and Evo 2's 8K context is more tractable but still nontrivial. "Run this on
the MacBook" is not the framing — the framing is "this is what an external Worker tier looks like, and here is what it
costs."

**Wet-lab is not a Linus capability.** The 16 viable phages exist because Twist synthesized DNA, the Hie lab assembled
and transformed it, and grad students screened plates. Linus has no wet-lab tier and will not have one. This paper's
loop closes outside the software stack.

**Whole-genome design is not Dan's day-to-day workflow.** Dan is a genomics/computational biology PhD; phage design is
squarely in his domain in the sense that he can read this paper deeply and reason about it, but it is unclear whether
_designing_ phages is a workflow he wants Linus to support. The answer to that question (see Open Questions) determines
whether this paper is "an architectural reference" or "a feature target."

**Biosafety constraints are real and serious.** The authors performed all experiments under a biosafety regime
appropriate for non-pathogenic phages on non-pathogenic hosts, and they note explicitly that Evo 2's training data was
curated to _exclude_ eukaryotic viruses including pathogenic human viruses. That curation is a model-level safeguard.
Linus, even hypothetically, should never become a tool that lowers the barrier for someone to apply this recipe to a
pathogen.

## Connections

**Direct substrate: Evo 2 (paper-notes/s41586-026-10176-5.md).** This is the experimental validation Evo 2 was _designed
to enable_. The architecture argument and the demonstration argument complete each other. A paired mini-synthesis is the
highest-leverage Wave 3 doc the Group B synthesis can produce.

**Group A genome FMs.** LucaOne, AlphaGenome, Bacformer, and RiNALMo are candidate substrates for the same recipe
applied elsewhere — bacterial whole-genome (Bacformer), regulatory-region (AlphaGenome), RNA (RiNALMo). The recipe
generalizes; the substrate swaps.

**Group B siblings.** mCSM-metal (single residue), GenNA (text-conditioned mid-scale nucleotide), Trias (codon), DISCO
(per-protein), DeepSeMS (metabolite) — all live below the whole-organism scale this paper occupies. King et al. is the
_most ambitious_ Group B paper by an order of magnitude. GenNA is the closest methodological sibling (text-conditional
nucleotide generation with scoring).

**Dual-use biotech (paper-notes/2306.03809v1.md).** The connection that demands attention. 2306.03809 warned about
exactly this scenario: generative AI lowering the barrier to designing functional pathogens. King et al. designed phages
— not human pathogens — and Hie's lab applied appropriate safeguards including Evo 2 training-data exclusions for
eukaryotic viruses. But the _recipe is general_: any sufficiently large genome FM, family-fine-tuned with a known
template, prompted appropriately, with off-the-shelf scoring tools, can in principle produce viable designs in that
family. SAFETY.md should reference this paper directly when discussing autonomy tiers for generative-biology tooling.

**Inference-time-compute thread.** Constraint scoring during generation is the genome-design face of test-time compute —
connects to ARC Prize 2024 and the memory architecture's "more compute at retrieval/synthesis time beats more
parameters."

   generative-biology product question.
