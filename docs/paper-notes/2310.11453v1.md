# BitNet: Scaling 1-bit Transformers for Large Language Models

## TL;DR

The original BitNet paper. It introduces **BitLinear**, a drop-in replacement for `nn.Linear` that uses binary
`{-1, +1}` weights and 8-bit activations, trained from scratch with quantization-aware training. Result: a 1-bit
Transformer that follows the same Chinchilla-style power-law scaling as FP16 baselines, beats every
post-training-quantization method tested, and uses dramatically less memory and energy. This is the seed paper for the
entire BitNet line — every subsequent BitNet paper inherits from BitLinear.

## The problem (in plain language)

Inference on FP16 LLMs is bottlenecked by two things: the energy cost of floating-point multiplications, and the
bandwidth cost of moving 16-bit weights from DRAM to the chip. Standard quantization (GPTQ, SmoothQuant, QuIP, AWQ)
takes an already-trained FP16 model and squashes it down to 4-bit or 2-bit _after_ training. This works tolerably at
8-bit and tolerably-ish at 4-bit, but accuracy collapses below that — the model wasn't trained to live there.

Quantization-aware training (QAT) is the alternative: bake the low precision into training itself, so the model learns
weights that are robust to being binarized. Prior QAT work focused on small CNNs or BERT-scale encoders; nobody had
shown it scales to autoregressive LLMs at the sizes that matter (1B+).

## What they propose

**BitLinear**, a layer that:

1. Binarizes weights to `{-1, +1}` via the **sign function** with a centralization step (subtract the mean to use the
   full sign range).
2. Quantizes activations to 8-bit using **absmax quantization** (per-tensor at training, per-token at inference).
3. Inserts a **SubLN** (sub-layer LayerNorm) before activation quantization to preserve output variance — without it,
   gradients destabilize.
4. Rescales the matrix-multiply output by `β` (mean weight magnitude) and `γ` (max activation magnitude) to dequantize
   back to a usable range.

Every other Transformer component (residual connections, embeddings, attention QKV at full precision) is left alone —
those don't dominate cost at scale.

**Training tricks** that turned out to be load-bearing:

- **Straight-through estimator (STE)** for the non-differentiable sign and clip functions, so gradients flow as if the
  binarization weren't there.
- **Latent FP16 weights** maintained alongside the binarized weights; updates accumulate in the latent copy,
  binarization happens on-the-fly during the forward pass. (Inference uses only binarized weights.)
- **Large learning rate** — small updates to FP16 latents often don't flip the sign of the binarized weight, biasing
  gradients. BitNet _needs_ a large LR to escape this; FP16 Transformers diverge at the same LR.
- **Group Quantization & Group Norm** so model parallelism doesn't require an all-reduce per layer (each device computes
  its own scaling factors locally).

## Key results

- **Power-law scaling** holds: BitNet's loss-vs-params curve has the same shape as FP16 Transformers; it just sits a
  small constant above. Authors fit the law on 125M–6.7B and accurately predict 13B and 30B losses.
- **"Inference-optimal" scaling law** introduced: loss vs. _inference energy_, not FLOPs. BitNet wins decisively on this
  axis — same loss at ~10× lower inference energy.
- **Energy savings on matmul** (their Table 2): at 7nm, FP16 multiply costs 0.34 pJ; INT8 multiply 0.07 pJ; the BitNet
  matmul (only the rescaling needs multiplies) is bounded by the additions at ~0.16 pJ FP16 add → ~0.007 pJ INT8 add.
- **Beats SoTA post-training quantization** at 6.7B on Winogrande/Winograd/Storycloze/Hellaswag — BitNet at W1A8 beats
  GPTQ at W2A16 and SmoothQuant at W4A4.
- **More stable training** than FP16 at high learning rates.

Scale: 125M to 30B params, English corpora (Pile + CC + RealNews + CC-Stories), Sentencepiece 16K vocab.
Production-relevant.

## What's reusable in Linus

**Direct reference implementation:** [repos/BitNet/](../../repos/BitNet/) is Microsoft's reference impl of this paper.
If we ever go deeper than "use a pre-trained BitNet checkpoint" — say, for fine-tuning a domain-specific Linus model —
BitLinear and the STE+latent-weight training pattern are what you'd implement against.

**Indirect (ANE/pmetal alignment):** The paper's whole optimization story is _integer addition is cheap, floating-point
multiplication is expensive, and the goal is to remove the multiplication_. That logic lines up with Apple's Neural
Engine, which is built around fixed-point multiply-accumulate arrays optimized for low-bit integer math. The BitNet →
ANE bridge is one of the most promising single-machine-LLM stories, and [pmetal/](../../repos/pmetal/) and
[ANE/](../../repos/ANE/) are exactly where that bridge gets built.

**Aspirational (training):** The paper's claims about scaling assume training from scratch on big clusters. Not
happening on Linus. We're consumers, not trainers.

## What's NOT applicable / hype filter

- **W1A8 vs. W1.58A8**: this paper's BitNet is `{-1, +1}` only; 1.58-bit `{-1, 0, +1}` came later in
  [2402.17764v1](2402.17764v1.md). Most of the practical gains people quote ("matches FP16 at 3B") came from adding the
  zero state, not from this paper. Don't conflate the two.
- **Energy numbers are 7nm/45nm silicon estimates from Horowitz's classic numbers**, not Apple Silicon (5nm/3nm)
  measurements. Order of magnitude is right; the exact ratios aren't.
- **Group Quantization** is a model-parallelism optimization. Linus runs single-machine. Not relevant unless we someday
  split a Worker model across multiple Macs.
