# Biomanufacturing in the U.S.: A MIT Policy Brief

## TL;DR

A 2024 MIT policy brief by Chris Love (MIT ChemE; founder of Sunflower Therapeutics, a small-footprint continuous-biomanufacturing startup), Liz Reynolds (MIT DUSP; former White House NEC and special assistant for the manufacturing supply-chain executive order), and David Goldston/Hannah Frye (MIT Washington Office) arguing that **U.S. biomanufacturing capacity is the binding constraint on the U.S. bioeconomy** — not biological innovation, which the U.S. leads, and not capital, which is abundant. The structural argument: the global bioeconomy may generate >$4 trillion in revenue over the next decade with ~$1 trillion in the U.S.; biological sources could supply up to 60% of materials used in all products; but **~80% of U.S. biopharmaceutical companies rely on Chinese biomanufacturers** for early development and commercialization. The cost-and-time gap is concrete: current state-of-the-art biomanufacturing facilities take 2-5 years to build at up to $2B capital investment; emerging "leapfrog" technologies — small-footprint continuous-manufacturing systems with all-in-one equipment and modular smart cleanrooms — promise the same throughput at 10-100x lower cost ($10-100M) and 1-3 year setup time, plus 90% reductions in energy/water/waste. These leapfrog technologies have been funded modestly (DARPA / JPEO-CBRND, ~$100-500M over 20 years from NSF/DoD) but have languished at TRL 4-7 in academia and SMEs without timely access to the financial resources needed for commercial transition. Three policy recommendations: (1) invest in the science of biomanufacturing — 2-3 transformative initiatives over 10 years, focused on ultra-low-cost proteins/cells ($1/kg at 100-kg scale), platform processes for product classes, AI/ML-based design tools; expand NIIMBL and BioMADE charters; commission cross-sector studies; (2) build regional bioeconomy innovation hubs modeled on CHIPS-and-Science regional tech hubs, with the craft-brewing-industry model as a regional-specialization comparator; (3) financial incentives — investment tax credits, low-interest long-term loans via BARDA Ventures / In-Q-Tel / EX-IM, leveraging DOD's Office of Strategy Capital. For Linus, this brief is the **manufacturing-policy companion** to the AI-Ready Biodata commentary; together they establish the strategic case that the U.S. AI-bio nexus is bottlenecked at both the data layer (biodata) and the production layer (biomanufacturing), and that Linus's local-first auditable architecture is aligned with the policy direction in both layers.

## The problem (in plain language)

The U.S. produces world-class biological innovation — synthetic biology, gene therapy, mRNA vaccines, novel proteins — but the manufacturing pathway to bring these innovations to market is structurally broken. Three interlocking failures define the broken state.

**Capacity shortfall.** New ideas for biologically derived products are generated by academic centers, national labs, and private startups in the U.S. far more quickly than manufacturing capacity increases. New facilities take 2-5 years to build and commission at up to $2B capital expense. Companies cannot afford the cost or the delay; they go overseas. The 80%-rely-on-Chinese-biomanufacturing statistic is the headline; the structural reason is that **Chinese government strategic decisions over the last 20 years have grown integrated contract-manufacturing capacity** (medical and non-medical) that U.S. innovators rationally take advantage of.

**Bespoke-process tax.** A biomanufacturing facility optimized for one type of product (monoclonal antibodies, say) is usually incompatible with other product types without significant additional capital and time. There are very few examples globally of biomanufacturing processes capable of producing a wide range of products within a class. The "platform" pattern — established for monoclonal antibodies, more recently for mRNA vaccines — works for those classes because of pre-competitive academic-industrial collaboration plus government support, but most classes (protein enzymes, plasmid DNA, small proteins, cell and gene therapies) lack platforms.

**Adoption inertia in the existing industry.** Companies are slow to invest in or adopt new biomanufacturing technologies due to concerns about readiness, capital-investment impact, perceived regulatory risk. The most disruptive technologies advance in academia or SMEs but languish at TRL 4-7 without timely access to financial resources or purchasing commitments.

The brief's framing is precise about what "leapfrog" technologies enable: combining all-in-one equipment with modular smart cleanrooms makes it possible to build small manufacturing facilities **virtually anywhere** (warehouses, labs) at 10-100x lower cost; continuous manufacturing reduces the need to scale up by enabling scale-out (extending production time rather than enlarging batches); single-use technologies plus automated process controls reduce energy/water/waste 90% versus traditional batch biopharmaceutical manufacturing. These technologies have been demonstrated but are in adolescence with respect to adoption.

## What they propose

Three recommendations form the policy substrate.

**Recommendation 1: Invest in the science of biomanufacturing.** The TRL 1-3 (basic research) and TRL 4-8 (scale-up) stages are both underinvested in the U.S. relative to the opportunity. The brief proposes 2-3 transformative large-scale initiatives over 10 years, targeting specific outcomes: (1.1) ultra-low-cost proteins/cells at $1/kg, 100-kg scale, to enable regional commercial development across the bioeconomy; (1.2) platform/standardized production processes for product classes (proteins, cells, viruses); (1.3) data-sharing platforms and AI/ML-based design tools to accelerate biomanufacturing process design and TRL transitions. NIIMBL and BioMADE's charters should expand to cover TRL 4-7 disruptive technology solutions, with revised IP terms (shared revenue pools) and cost-matching (tax credits) to reduce SME participation barriers. Federal agencies should fund shared tools for data sharing and modeling in biomanufacturing, including federated-learning approaches that balance competitive advantage with national resilience. Commission studies on alternative business models and cross-sector technology adoption.

**Recommendation 2: Invigorate a national innovation pipeline via regional bioeconomy innovation hubs.** Future government-sponsored capacity focuses on regionally distributed pilot hubs and early commercialization facilities. These hubs do not operate as service-provider CMOs (which the brief explicitly observes have not met evolving needs of the biomanufacturing sector — citing the Emergent BioSolutions COVID-vaccine destruction case as a high-profile failure). Instead they transfer capabilities and expertise to regional industries, modeled on the Commerce Department's Regional Technology and Innovation Hubs created under CHIPS and Science. The **craft-brewing industry** is offered as a model for regional specialization and SME growth — $300.4B economic impact in 2021, 600K jobs, 8 additional indirect jobs per direct job, with revitalization of rural economies. Identify bio-related goods requiring domestic production for national security and develop strategies using Advanced Market Commitments. Partner with states, local governments, technical and community colleges, and universities for K-12 through post-graduate education.

**Recommendation 3: Financial incentives.** Investment tax credits (ITCs) for startups and SMEs to develop their own biomanufacturing facilities, encouraging iteration and innovation. Low-interest long-term loans via BARDA Ventures, In-Q-Tel, the EX-IM bank's Make More in America program. DOD's new Office of Strategy Capital is also positioned to ensure U.S. superiority in key areas.

The brief closes with the framing argument: "The current national strategy for assuring timely, sustainable capabilities in innovative biomanufacturing is unclear and too reliant on assuming that having more facilities that use current technology will solve the problem. The convergence of biological engineering with artificial intelligence offers a unique opportunity for the U.S. to accelerate its bioeconomy."

## Key takeaways for Linus

**Calibration 1: the AI-bio nexus has a production-layer bottleneck, not just a data-layer bottleneck.** The Holko/Wilbanks/Howell commentary (also in this batch) makes the strategic case for biodata as the binding constraint on U.S. AI-bio leadership. This MIT brief makes the parallel case for **biomanufacturing capacity** as the binding constraint on translating AI-bio innovation into product. Together they define a two-tier strategic argument: AI-bio leadership requires winning on both layers, and the U.S. is currently weak on both. Linus's relevance lives at the **interface between the two layers** — local-first AI Workers that operate on private biological data (data layer) and that can generate designs for products manufacturable in regional biomanufacturing hubs (production layer).

**Calibration 2: AI/ML design tools are an explicit Recommendation 1 component.** Sub-recommendation 1.3 — data-sharing platforms and AI/ML-based design tools to accelerate biomanufacturing process design — is the most directly Linus-aligned policy recommendation in the brief. Phase 7 biology skills built around typed-structured-prediction (S25) plus paired physics-based validators (per the Gallup & Steel CVAE paper-note) are aligned with this recommendation. The Phase 7 biology roadmap should reference this brief as a strategic motivation for the AI/ML-design-tool angle.

**Calibration 3: federated learning is named as a policy direction.** The brief's "policies to promote open data sharing in manufacturing and implementation of certain data modeling approaches (e.g., federated learning models) could balance competitive advantage with essential resources for national security and resilience" is the same federated-learning architectural primitive Recommendation 2 in the AI-Ready Biodata commentary names. Two independent 2024-2026 policy sources converge on **federated learning + compute-to-data as the architectural pattern**. Linus's local-first Worker architecture is aligned.

**Calibration 4: regional hubs imply distributed-but-coordinated deployment.** The CHIPS-style regional bioeconomy innovation hub model is a distributed-coordination pattern. For Linus's eventual commercial positioning, this argues that **Linus deployments will be regional** (each hub has its own deployment, with shared standards and federated coordination) rather than centralized. The DEC-0028 memory pillar with Layer C (cross-session episodic) and Layer D (multi-agent investigation) supports this — multiple Linus instances at different regional hubs can share investigation context selectively without requiring centralized data.

## What's NOT applicable / hype filter

**This is policy commentary aimed at U.S. policymakers, not implementation guidance.** Linus's adoption should treat it as **strategic-context calibration**, not as a source of technical specifications. The policy recommendations (CHIPS-style regional hubs, ITCs, low-interest loans) are not technology-stack decisions; they're industrial-policy decisions for which the federal government is the actor.

**The 80%-rely-on-Chinese-biomanufacturing statistic is repeatedly cited but not directly verified.** The brief cites a BIO survey (bio.org gooddaybio archive) for this number. The figure is widely repeated in the bioeconomy-policy community but is a single-source data point. Linus's documentation should cite it as "widely-cited industry survey number" rather than as established fact.

**The craft-brewing-industry analogy is rhetorically strong but architecturally loose.** Regional specialization in craft brewing emerged organically from prohibition-repeal dynamics, urban-rural reverse migration, and consumer preference shifts — not from federal industrial policy. The model is suggestive but the policy mechanisms that produced it don't translate directly to biomanufacturing.

**The "leapfrog technology" framing is optimistic about technology readiness.** The brief is honest that leapfrog technologies are at "adolescence with respect to adoption" (TRL 4-7), but the framing implies that the technology is largely solved and the binding constraint is investment/commercialization. The reality is that small-footprint continuous biomanufacturing for novel product classes is still active research, with many open technical problems (e.g., bioreactor scaling for emerging cell-therapy applications, automated process controls for >3-protein circuits, regulatory pathways for non-traditional cell substrates).

**No specific quantitative benchmarks for the AI/ML design tools.** Sub-recommendation 1.3 calls for AI/ML-based design tools but does not specify what kinds of design problems, what benchmarks, what success criteria. Linus's Phase 7 biology skills can fit under this umbrella but the brief doesn't constrain the specific tool choices.

**Time-scoped quickly.** The brief was published in 2024 and refers to "FY2026" requests. By 2026, the policy landscape has evolved (Genesis EO, new NDAA provisions, etc.) — see the Holko/Wilbanks/Howell commentary for the 2026 update. The two pieces should be read together; the MIT brief provides the structural argument, and the War-on-the-Rocks commentary updates the policy specifics.
