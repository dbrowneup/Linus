# General Multimodal Protein Design Enables DNA-Encoding of Chemistry (DISCO)

## TL;DR
DISCO (DIffusion for Sequence-structure CO-design) is a multimodal generative model from the Frances Arnold lab and Mila that **jointly designs a protein's amino-acid sequence and its 3D backbone in a single denoising trajectory**, conditioned on arbitrary biomolecular context (small molecules, metallocofactors, reactive intermediates, DNA, RNA). Sequence is generated by **masked discrete diffusion**, structure by **continuous diffusion over atomic coordinates**, coupled by cross-modal recycling and trained as a single network on the unfiltered PDB. Conditioned only on the geometry of an iron–carbenoid reactive intermediate — with **no theozyme, no pre-specified active-site residues, no inverse-folding step** — DISCO produced 90 candidate enzymes that, on whole-cell *E. coli* screening, catalyzed four new-to-nature carbene-transfer reactions: alkene cyclopropanation (4,050 TTN, 99:1 dr), B–H insertion (5,170 TTN), C(sp3)–H alkylation (2,360 TTN), and a sterically demanding spirocyclopropanation. **Top hits exceed previously evolved P450/cytochrome-c variants.** Error-prone PCR on dCT-H11 produced ~35 improved variants, confirming the designs occupy fitness landscapes accessible to evolution. By a comfortable margin, this is the most architecturally and experimentally ambitious protein-design paper in the corpus.

## The problem (in plain language)
Enzymes catalyse chemistry under mild conditions and are genetically encoded — design one and you can put a chemical reaction into a cell, a bioreactor, or a therapeutic. The trouble is that nature has sampled a tiny slice of the chemistry DNA-encoded catalysts could in principle perform. For "new-to-nature" reactions — carbene transfer, B–H insertion, C(sp3)–H functionalisation — there is no natural enzyme to start from, and the Arnold-lab paradigm of directed evolution requires *some* starting protein with measurable activity.

Computational enzyme design was supposed to bridge this gap. The state of the art (RFdiffusion2, BindCraft, BoltzGen, theozyme-based pipelines) has two structural limits. First, those pipelines almost universally require a **theozyme** — a hand-built model of the active-site residues arranged around the transition state — to seed generation. For reactions whose mechanism is poorly understood (and new-to-nature reactions are exactly those), no theozyme can be reliably built. Second, those pipelines are **sequential**: generate a backbone first, then use inverse-folding (ProteinMPNN, LigandMPNN) to find a sequence that folds into it. Because sequence and structure jointly determine function, decoupling them leaves each stage blind to the other's constraints. DISCO drops both: condition on the reactive intermediate alone (no theozyme), and generate sequence and structure together (no inverse folding).

## What they propose
The architecture is a **single neural network** that, at each denoising step, jointly produces sequence logits and denoised 3D coordinates for the protein backbone and conditioning biomolecules. Three components do the heavy lifting.

**Two diffusion processes, coupled.** Sequence uses **masked discrete diffusion** (mask tokens by a noise schedule, predict the clean sequence, cross-entropy loss). Structure uses **continuous Gaussian diffusion** over atomic coordinates with denoising-score-matching loss. A theoretical result the authors lean on (Rojas et al., "Diffuse Everything", ICML 2025) shows that if noise is sampled independently per modality at training, you can provably learn the *joint* reverse process from the two unimodal losses — no special joint-loss term is needed. This is the mathematical hinge that makes the whole thing tractable.

**Cross-modal recycling.** At every step the network sees four things: current noised sequence and structure, *and* its previous best estimates of the clean sequence and structure. A frozen protein language model encodes sequence; a LigandMPNN-derived structure encoder encodes structure; both feed a Pairformer stack (the AlphaFold 3 / Chai-1 backbone) and then a joint diffusion module. Sequence and structure predictions inform each other continuously.

**Inference-time tricks that turned out to matter a lot.** Vanilla masked discrete diffusion (unmask once, never revise) yielded sequences that didn't fold. The fix combines (a) **self-correction / remasking**, (b) a **sequence temperature mechanism** smoothing overconfident tokens early in the trajectory, and (c) **noisy guidance** combining a "bad" model with the main one (Karras et al. style). Each is necessary; together they push co-designability to ~90% on unconditional monomers.

Non-protein molecules are represented identically to proteins — discrete tokens for chemical identity plus 3D coordinates — which is what lets the same network condition on small molecules, metal clusters, reactive intermediates, and nucleic acids without architectural changes.

For **inference-time steering**, the authors apply the **Feynman-Kac corrector** framework (Skreta et al., ICML 2025) in two new ways: **FKC-Multimodal** tilts toward joint sequence+structure rewards (disulfide-bridge count, cation-π interactions); **FKC-Specificity-Guidance** tilts toward binding an on-target ligand while penalising likelihood under an off-target decoy. FKC is a more principled alternative to best-of-N filtering — it modifies the sampling distribution rather than rejecting after the fact.

Training data is **the unfiltered PDB** — deliberate, to avoid the "designability bias" introduced by filtering on what current folding methods can predict. SE(3) symmetries are injected softly through data augmentation, not as architectural constraints.

The wet-lab campaign for new-to-nature carbene transferases works as follows. Carbene transfer proceeds via an iron–carbenoid intermediate formed from a heme group and a diazo precursor; formation is rate-determining (>20 kcal/mol). Rather than model the full transition state, DISCO is conditioned on the **DFT-derived geometry and bonding pattern of the heme–carbene precursor itself** and asked to design a protein around it. ~10⁴ candidates were generated, filtered by AlphaFold 3 / Chai-1 confidence (chain pAE, ipTM), active-site contacts, solvent exposure, charge, and exposed hydrophobicity — yielding 90 designs synthesised by Twist Bioscience and screened in *E. coli*.

## Key results
**In silico.** Unconditional monomer design hits ~90% co-designability with the highest novelty and diversity among baselines. On STUDIO-179 (a new benchmark of 179 natural and non-natural ligands the authors release), DISCO produces the highest fraction of diverse, co-designable complexes for **178 of 179 ligands**. The model handles small molecules, metal cofactors, nucleic acids (specific DNA and RNA binders demonstrated), and multi-ligand contexts. FKC-MM enriches rare features: top 2% of 100-aa designs contain six disulfide bonds, a density matched by only the top 0.2% of comparable PDB proteins.

**Wet-lab carbene transferases (the headline).** Of 90 designs screened across four reactions:
- **Alkene cyclopropanation** of *p*-methoxystyrene with EDA: 72% yield, **4,050 TTN**, 99:1 dr. References: hemin 96 TTN; evolved P450H2-5-F10 364 TTN; theozyme-based de novo design PNC2 (Hou et al. 2025) 630 TTN. **DISCO is an order of magnitude better than the prior best de novo design.**
- **B–H insertion** with EDP: 98% yield, **5,170 TTN**, vs. evolved *Rma* cyt c at 2,490 TTN — DISCO beats the *evolved* enzyme without any wet-lab evolution.
- **C(sp3)–H alkylation** of 1-phenylpyrrolidine: 42% yield, 2,360 TTN, vs. evolved P411-CHF at 2,030 TTN. The previous campaign required **14 rounds of directed evolution** to reach this level.
- **Spirocyclopropanation**: fewer active variants, modest activity, enantiomeric excesses up to 35% with both enantiomers represented.

**Novelty of the active sites.** >80% of designs have >3 Å RMSD over the five nearest active-site residues vs. the closest motif in AlphaFoldDB — the catalytic geometries themselves are new. The 90 designs form 75 distinct structural clusters (TM-score < 0.5); no pair shares more than 50% sequence identity. The closest PDB match to top design dCT-H11 is a TetR-family transcription factor from a halophilic archaeon — DISCO **repurposed a non-catalytic fold** for heme binding and carbene transfer. dCT-F9 and dCT-G9 have *no* AlphaFoldDB match for their five-residue active sites at all.

**Evolvability.** One round of error-prone PCR on dCT-H11 (~700 mutants): ~35 variants showed improved spirocyclopropanation; some inverted enantioselectivity (+35% to −35% ee). Top variants had substitutions scattered across the protein — long-range epistasis characteristic of natural enzyme evolution. DISCO designs sit in fitness regions with accessible uphill paths for directed evolution.

## What's reusable in Linus
**Architectural pattern, not the model itself.** DISCO is a heavy compute object — Pairformer + AlphaFold-3-class infrastructure trained on the full PDB. There is essentially **no path to running DISCO on a 32 GB M1 Max** at meaningful scale; even inference in the paper used Mila/AMD HPC. The reusable piece is the **workflow shape** and the **architectural archetype**, not the weights.

**Phase 7 — generative protein design as an external-API skill.** The DISCO codebase + checkpoints are released. For Linus, this is the right shape for a Phase 7 skill: a tool-registry entry wrapping a remote DISCO inference endpoint (GPU box or future Mac Studio) with a structured input contract (target biomolecule + reactive intermediate geometry + objectives) and structured output (ranked sequence-structure pairs with confidence scores). **Local Linus orchestrates, remote heavy model executes** — one of the cleanest cases for the Maestro/Worker/external-tool boundary.

**Maestro/Worker fit is unusually clean.** A design campaign decomposes naturally: Maestro (Dan + Claude) frames the chemistry, picks the reactive intermediate, defines screening reactions, chooses the wet-lab subset. Workers run DISCO inference, score with AlphaFold 3 / Chai-1, apply the computational filter, produce a ranked candidate list. This is exactly the kind of multi-step generative pipeline `src/linus/orchestration/` should express.

**KnowledgeBase content pattern.** Designed enzymes are first-class KG entities: sequence, structure, target biomolecule, catalysed reaction, measured TTN/yield/selectivity, parent organism (or "de novo"), evolution history, mutational variants. The DISCO supplementary catalogue (90 designs × 4 reactions, plus dCT-H11 mutational landscape) is the kind of dataset KnowledgeBase should ingest. "Designed enzyme catalogue" is a worth-naming KG pattern alongside "paper" and "natural protein."

**Math reuse — Flow Matching textbook (paper-notes/2506.02070v3.md).** DISCO is exactly the paper Dan needs the textbook for. Continuous diffusion over coordinates, masked discrete diffusion over sequences, the "Diffuse Everything" decomposition theorem, Karras noisy guidance, the Feynman-Kac corrector framework — all live inside that textbook's perimeter. If Dan wants to engage architecturally with DISCO, the textbook is the on-ramp.

**Architectural archetype to name.** "Joint discrete-sequence + continuous-structure diffusion" is a distinctive multimodal generative pattern that merits a named entry in `docs/syntheses/`. It applies beyond proteins: small molecules (SMILES + 3D pose), nucleic acids (sequence + secondary structure), even materials (composition + lattice). **FKC steering** is also reusable as an inference-time pattern wherever per-trajectory rewards exist (code synthesis with test-pass rewards, SQL with execution rewards) — better than best-of-N when applicable.

## What's NOT applicable
**Local inference is out of reach.** Even ignoring training, DISCO inference at the scales reported (~10⁴ candidates per campaign) requires substantial GPU. The Pairformer/AlphaFold-3 backbone alone is many gigabytes. M1 Max Metal/MLX has no path to hosting this at useful throughput. Phase 8 is the earliest realistic horizon for *local* DISCO-class work.

**Wet-lab validation isn't a Linus problem.** The TTN numbers only mean what they mean because the Arnold lab ran plate-based screening with proper controls. Linus has no role in wet-lab execution; any "Linus designs an enzyme" framing is incomplete without an external wet-lab partner. Linus's contribution caps at *in silico* design and ranking.

**The training-data choice doesn't carry over.** "Train on the unfiltered PDB" is a legitimate scientific argument for DISCO but has no analogue for Linus's local fine-tuning work.

**Specific reactions are not Dan's chemistry.** Carbene transfer is canonical Arnold-lab chemistry; Dan's biochemistry/genomics background overlaps the methodology cleanly but not necessarily the substrate space (see open question 5).

**No fair de novo head-to-head.** The wet-lab comparison is to historically evolved enzymes, not to a sequential pipeline (RFdiffusion → ProteinMPNN) on the same task. Don't over-claim "DISCO beats inverse folding for this task" — that comparison wasn't run.

## Connections
**To METL (paper-notes/s41592-025-02776-2.md).** METL and DISCO are the corpus's two protein-engineering papers, on opposite ends of the design-vs-engineering axis. METL is small-data fine-tuning of a biophysics-pretrained foundation model, predicting mutation effects on an *existing* protein. DISCO is large-data generation from scratch of a *new* protein. They are complementary — DISCO produces a scaffold, METL-style models guide the directed-evolution refinement that follows. The dCT-H11 error-prone PCR experiment is precisely where METL could replace random mutagenesis with informed proposals. Naming this DISCO→METL pipeline is a worthwhile synthesis target.

**To Group A protein FMs (ProteinReasoner, LucaOne, ESM).** Group A models are largely **inference-only** representation learners. DISCO is fundamentally **generative** with a different architectural commitment. DISCO uses an ESM-style pLM as a frozen input component during cross-modal recycling — so Group A FMs are upstream tooling for Group B generative pipelines, not competitors.

**To Group B siblings.** This wave maps onto a "scale of biological object" axis: **mCSM-metal** (single residue) → **DISCO** (whole protein) → **GenNA** (nucleotide-level text-conditional) → **DeepSeMS** (small molecule) → **generative phages** (whole genome). DISCO occupies the **protein-scale design niche** and is the only one with experimentally validated *de novo function* at its scale. It also has architectural reach the others lack: the same framework conditions on small molecules, metals, DNA, and RNA.

**To the Flow Matching textbook (paper-notes/2506.02070v3.md).** DISCO is among the strongest motivating examples in the corpus for engaging with the textbook. A focused Wave 3 mini-synthesis pulling DISCO + generative phages + GenNA + the textbook into one document — themed "diffusion-class generative models for biological sequences and structures" — would consolidate the wave.

**To Maestro/Worker protocol.** DISCO is one of the cleanest examples of where Maestro + remote-Worker decomposition wins — worth using as the worked example when `docs/maestro-worker-protocol.md` is next revised.
