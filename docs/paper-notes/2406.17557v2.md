# The FineWeb Datasets: Decanting the Web for the Finest Text Data at Scale

## TL;DR

Hugging Face's effort to **open-source the data side** of LLM training. **FineWeb** is a 15-trillion-token
Common-Crawl-derived dataset; **FineWeb-Edu** is a 1.3T-token educational subset filtered with a custom classifier. Both
outperform every other public pretraining corpus (RefinedWeb, C4, RedPajama, SlimPajama, Dolma) when used to train
identical 1.71B ablation models. Critically for Linus: **FineWeb-Edu is one of the corpora used to pretrain BitNet b1.58
2B4T** ([2504.12285v2](2504.12285v2.md)). The paper's main contribution beyond the datasets themselves is a _systematic
methodology_ for ablating filter and deduplication choices, plus the open-source
[`datatrove`](https://github.com/huggingface/datatrove) library that does it.

## The problem (in plain language)

Modern LLMs are made of three things: architecture (mostly converged on Transformer + variants), compute (mostly NVIDIA
GPUs), and **data**. Of those three, data is the most opaque — closed labs publish architectures and compute scales
freely but treat their training corpora as trade secrets. The "open-weight" models from Meta, Mistral, Qwen, etc.
publish weights but not the data they were trained on. This creates a knowledge asymmetry: you can fine-tune Llama-3,
but you can't reproduce the conditions under which it learned what it knows.

The biological analogue: imagine if molecular biology only had access to _purified proteins_ but never to the _cell
types they came from_. You could study the protein in isolation but never understand the regulatory environment that
produced it. That's where open LLM science was before FineWeb.

The specific data-curation question is also more subtle than it sounds. "Just take Common Crawl and filter the junk"
sounds simple but breaks immediately on questions like: _what's junk?_, _fuzzy or exact deduplication?_, _deduplicate
within snapshots or across them?_, _which heuristic filters from C4 / MassiveText / Gopher actually improve quality?_.
FineWeb's value is treating these as empirical questions and ablating them rigorously.

## What they propose

The paper has two artifacts and one methodology:

**Artifact 1: FineWeb (15T tokens)** — Common Crawl 96 snapshots, processed via:

1. **Trafilatura** for text extraction from raw WARC HTML (not the default WET text — WET retains too much boilerplate).
2. **Base filter**: URL blocklist for adult content + fastText English-language filter (score ≥ 0.65) + MassiveText
   quality and repetition filters.
3. **Per-snapshot MinHash deduplication** (5-grams, 112 hash functions, 14 buckets × 8 hashes, 75% similarity
   threshold). Crucial counterintuitive finding: **per-snapshot deduplication outperforms global cross-snapshot
   deduplication** because global dedup over-removes high-quality content that happens to appear in multiple crawls.
4. **C4-style heuristic filters** (drop pages with curly braces, "lorem ipsum", "javascript", short documents) — but
   _not_ C4's terminal-punctuation rule, which removes ~30% of tokens.
5. **Custom heuristic filters** developed by comparing 50+ statistics between known-high-quality and known-low-quality
   slices and choosing thresholds where the distributions diverge most. The _fraction of lines ending in punctuation_
   turns out to be a particularly strong quality signal.

**Artifact 2: FineWeb-Edu (1.3T tokens)** — FineWeb filtered through a custom classifier trained to recognize
_educational_ content (recipes, tutorials, explanations) vs. boilerplate or low-information text. Models trained on
FineWeb-Edu are dramatically better on knowledge-and-reasoning benchmarks (MMLU, ARC) than models trained on the same
compute on raw FineWeb or any other public corpus.

**Methodology**: 70+ ablation models trained from scratch (1.71B params each, ~28B–350B tokens of training each, ~80,000
H100 hours total) to validate every design choice. Each filter, every dedup parameter, every text-extraction tool —
measured against a held-out evaluation suite (HellaSwag, OpenBookQA, PIQA, ARC, MMLU, etc.) chosen for low variance and
monotonic learning curves.

## Key results

- **FineWeb beats every other public pretraining corpus** on a 1.71B-parameter ablation when matched for token count.
- **FineWeb-Edu** offers **dramatically** better MMLU and ARC at fixed training budget — used by recent Microsoft,
  Mistral, and HuggingFace models.
- **Per-snapshot dedup > global dedup**: a clean empirical finding that overturns conventional wisdom.
- **Trafilatura > WET text**: bigger uplift than most filtering rules; just choose a better extractor.
- **C4 terminal-punctuation filter is too aggressive** (removes 30% of tokens for marginal gain). Use the other C4
  filters but skip this one.

Scale: 15 trillion tokens released. The largest open pretraining dataset at the time of publication, by an order of
magnitude over RefinedWeb (~5T) and SlimPajama (~600B).

## What's reusable in Linus

**Direct (data sovereignty, Phase 4):** ROADMAP Phase 4 is "Data Sovereignty: Kiwix, ProtoMaps/OSM, versioned datasets."
FineWeb-Edu is the cleanest example we have of _what a versioned, curated text dataset looks like_. If Linus ever needs
to maintain its own versioned text corpus (e.g. a curated subset of Dan's papers + reference materials + verified web
text), `datatrove` is the tool to do it with, and FineWeb's filter ablations are a free design template.

**Direct (Phase 6 fine-tuning corpus):** If we ever do continued pretraining on Linus (per
[BitNet Distillation](2510.13998v1.md)'s 10B-token warmup step), FineWeb-Edu is the natural source corpus. It's
released, well-characterized, and known to produce models that perform well on the benchmarks Linus might care about.

**Indirect (KnowledgeBase quality filtering):** The FineWeb filter-design methodology — collect statistics on known-good
vs. known-bad slices, find statistics where distributions diverge, choose thresholds where the bad distribution
dominates — is directly applicable to filtering papers in [modules/KnowledgeBase/](../../modules/KnowledgeBase/).
Specifically: when ingesting a new paper into KB, you might want to filter out heavily-formatted ads, legal disclaimers,
or machine-translation artifacts. Same statistical approach.

**Indirect (Maestro budget discipline):** This paper is also implicitly an argument for _systematic ablation over
intuition_. CLAUDE.md flags "evidence beats intuition" and "set a metric, set a goal, devise tests." FineWeb's data-side
ablations are a textbook execution of that philosophy at a different layer. Worth reading not just for the data but for
the methodology template.

## What's NOT applicable

- **80,000 H100 hours of ablation training** is not on Dan's roadmap. The dataset and methodology are what we use; we
  don't reproduce the ablations.
- The whole paper assumes you're training a model from scratch on trillions of tokens. Linus is downstream of that — we
  use the _data_ and the _trained models_, we don't do trillion-token pre-training.
- **English-only**: fastText filter at score ≥ 0.65 keeps English. If Dan's papers are in English (mostly true), this is
  fine; if multilingual support becomes a Linus goal, FineWeb is not the corpus.
