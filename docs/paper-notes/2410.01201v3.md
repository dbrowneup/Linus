# Were RNNs All We Needed?

## TL;DR

Strip LSTM and GRU of every gate dependency on the previous hidden state and every `tanh` range restriction, and the
resulting **minLSTM** and **minGRU** become fully parallelizable via prefix-sum (parallel scan), use roughly 13–38% of
the parameters of their classical counterparts, and **match Mamba and Transformers on selective copy, RL benchmarks, and
Shakespeare-scale language modelling** — with **175–1361× faster training per step** than vanilla LSTM/GRU at sequence
lengths 512–4096. The argument is not that minGRU is the new state of the art, but that the architectural ceiling on
recurrent sequence models was lower than the field assumed: a pre-Transformer formalism, lightly modernized, sits inside
the same performance band as Mamba/xLSTM/S6.

## The problem (in plain language)

The standard story of the 2017–2024 era is that Transformers won because they were parallelizable during training, and
RNNs lost because backpropagation through time forces sequential computation. The cost of Transformer victory is well
known: attention is quadratic in sequence length, which becomes a hard wall somewhere between 100k and 1M tokens —
exactly the range modern context windows are bumping up against. That wall has driven a recent wave of alternatives —
S4, Mamba, xLSTM, RWKV, HGRN — all of which are recurrent in inference but engineered around the parallel scan algorithm
so they can train at Transformer speed.

What the paper asks is genuinely embarrassing in its simplicity: did the original LSTM (1997) and GRU (2014) actually
need the architectural complexity that prevented their parallelization, or did the field abandon them too quickly? If a
few targeted deletions make them parallel-trainable, do they remain competitive? The answer turns out to be yes on both
counts, which sharpens a question that the Garrison memory note is also pushing: maybe the substrate for sequence
modelling under tight memory budgets has been hiding in plain sight for two decades.

## What they propose

The paper performs a careful surgical reduction on GRU and LSTM. The pivotal observation is that the parallel scan
algorithm (Blelloch 1990) computes recurrences of the form `v_t = a_t ⊙ v_{t-1} + b_t` in parallel across the sequence,
_provided_ `a_t` and `b_t` depend only on inputs `x_{1:t}`, not on previous hidden states `h_{t-1}`. GRU and LSTM
violate this because their gates condition on `h_{t-1}`. So:

**minGRU** drops the previous-hidden-state argument from both the update gate `z_t` and the candidate hidden state
`h̃_t`, and removes the reset gate entirely (it becomes vacuous once `h̃_t` no longer sees `h_{t-1}`). The `tanh` range
restriction on `h̃_t` is also dropped, because once gate inputs are no longer hidden-state-derived, the original
justification for compressing the candidate's range disappears. The resulting recurrence is
`h_t = (1 − z_t) ⊙ h_{t-1} + z_t ⊙ h̃_t` with `z_t = σ(Linear(x_t))` and `h̃_t = Linear(x_t)`.

**minLSTM** applies the same hidden-state-stripping to the forget, input, and candidate-cell gates, removes both `tanh`
range restrictions, drops the output gate entirely, and folds away the cell state (now redundant with the hidden state).
To keep state scale time-independent — important for long sequences where unbounded forget+input can blow up — the
forget and input gates are L1-normalized: `f'_t, i'_t ← f_t/(f_t+i_t), i_t/(f_t+i_t)`, ensuring `f' + i' = 1`.

Both can be trained in **log-space using a numerically stable log-domain parallel scan** (Heinsen 2023), giving the
BPTT-free training loop the paper exploits. Implementations are short: a few-line PyTorch forward function, with both
sequential (inference) and parallel (training) modes.

## Key results

The training-runtime numbers are the core of the empirical case. On a T4 GPU at batch 64:

- **Sequence length 512**: minGRU/minLSTM/Mamba complete a step in ~2.97/2.72/2.71 ms respectively. Vanilla GRU/LSTM are
  **175× and 235× slower** per step.
- **Sequence length 4096**: minGRU/minLSTM/Mamba in ~3.41/3.25/3.15 ms (essentially flat with sequence length, because
  the scan is logarithmic-depth). Classical RNNs are **1324× and 1361× slower**. The paper's framing: "in a setting
  where minGRU would take a day to finish training for a fixed number of epochs, its traditional counterpart GRU could
  take over 3 years."

On capability benchmarks:

- **Selective Copying** (Mamba's signature task): minGRU 99.5%, minLSTM 96.0%, Mamba S6 99.8% — minimal RNNs match or
  beat Mamba. S4, H3, and Hyena only partially solve it (18–57%).
- **D4RL MuJoCo offline RL** (Decision-Transformer-style): averaged across HalfCheetah/Hopper/Walker ×
  Medium/Medium-Replay/Medium-Expert (9 datasets), minLSTM 78.1, minGRU 78.2, Decision Mamba 78.8, Decision S4 68.6,
  Decision Transformer 76.4. The minimal RNNs are competitive with everything in the table.
- **Shakespeare-level language modelling** (nanoGPT framework, character-level): test cross-entropy loss minGRU 1.548,
  minLSTM 1.555, Mamba 1.575, Transformer 1.547. The Transformer needed **~2.5× more training steps** to reach
  comparable loss and then began to overfit.
- **Long Range Arena + Chomsky Hierarchy** (extended results, Table 4–5): minLSTM averages 0.73 across the seven LRA +
  CH tasks reported, vs. xLSTM 0.71, Mamba 0.64, RWKV-4 0.51, Llama 0.48 — outperforming Llama and RWKV, edging xLSTM,
  beating Mamba.
- **Parameter count**: minGRU uses ~33%/22%/17%/13% of the parameters of a GRU at expansion factor α = 1/2/3/4. minLSTM
  uses ~38%/25%/19%/15%.
- **Memory cost of parallelism**: minGRU/minLSTM use ~88% more memory than classical RNNs during training (the
  parallel-scan computational graph is wider). Mamba uses 56% more than minGRU. This is the cost paid for parallel
  training; inference memory is unaffected.

The honesty is worth noting: the authors explicitly say their goal is not state-of-the-art on any specific task, but to
demonstrate that simplifying old architectures can yield modern-competitive results. The Limitations section concedes
that experiments were run on older P100/T4/Quadro 5000 GPUs (16 GB) rather than the A100s used by Mamba and xLSTM,
requiring gradient accumulation that reduced effective batch sizes and slowed training.

## What's reusable for Linus — the memory lens

The Garrison framework demands two things from any computational substrate Linus might host: recursive state
maintenance, and reliable history access. minGRU/minLSTM are pure recursive-state-maintenance machines — they read
state, apply an update, and durably keep the new state — by construction. They are not the whole memory story (reliable
history access still needs an external episodic store, as the Garrison note insists), but they are exactly the kind of
"recurrent approach with rolling hidden state" that the path-forward section of Garrison's blog and paper explicitly
point to.

Where this matters operationally:

**Direct, Phase 1 (Recon & Baselines):** A minGRU or minLSTM Worker is a tractable Apple-Silicon-native experiment. The
reference implementations are short PyTorch and translate cleanly to MLX. A small character-level minGRU trained on the
Shakespeare benchmark (or, more usefully, on a slice of Dan's notes corpus) is a realistic Phase 1 reproduction — it
would establish a local training baseline that does not exist today and would calibrate expectations for Phase 6
fine-tuning. The paper's nanoGPT-comparable result on a single GPU at 5k training steps suggests this is a
days-of-compute experiment, not a months-of-compute one.

**Direct, Phase 2 (MVP):** Linus's session memory layer needs a representation that can be updated cheaply and queried
at every Worker turn without paying quadratic context cost. minGRU's recurrence is a candidate substrate for a learned
session-state encoder — small, cheap to update incrementally, and naturally constant-time per token at inference. This
is a memory-pillar component, not a primary-LLM component.

**Aspirational, Phase 6 (Fine-Tuning):** This paper is the cleanest published argument that an Apple-Silicon-native,
attention-free sequence model trained from scratch by a single person on consumer hardware can be empirically
competitive in its weight class. If the eventual ambition is a Linus-trained domain model — biochem/genomics-flavored,
local, 32 GB-budget — then minGRU/minLSTM/xLSTM/Mamba define the design space worth surveying, and minGRU is the
simplest entry point. The 175–1361× training speedup over classical RNNs maps directly onto the M1 Max budget: training
that would have been infeasible on consumer hardware in the BPTT era is now plausible.

**Indirect, architecture:** The paper validates the deeper bet that the orchestration layer should not assume
Transformers are the only inference substrate. A future Linus router that dispatches to a Transformer for short-context
coding tasks and to a minGRU/Mamba/xLSTM for long-context document reasoning is a more interesting architecture than
"one model for everything," and this paper widens the menu of credible alternatives.

## What's NOT applicable / hype filter

The selective-copy and Shakespeare results are encouraging but small. The largest minGRU/minLSTM language model trained
in the paper is character-level on 1M tokens of Shakespeare. There is no 1B-parameter minGRU trained on 1T tokens. The
Limitations section is honest about this: hardware constraints prevented large-scale experiments, and the conclusions
are extrapolated by structural similarity to Mamba/xLSTM rather than demonstrated at scale. Until someone publishes a
1B+ minGRU on a real corpus, "matches Transformers" should be read as "matches at small scale on tasks where small-scale
experiments converge."

The training-time speedups (175–1361×) are versus the _unoptimized PyTorch reference RNN_ — not versus PyTorch's
built-in cuDNN-fused RNNs, which the paper acknowledges are heavily optimized. The fair comparison is parallel-scan
minGRU vs. fused-CUDA LSTM, and the paper does not run that. The qualitative point (parallel scan removes the asymptotic
bottleneck) survives, but the multiplier is overstated relative to a serious baseline.

The paper runs on T4/P100 NVIDIA hardware. Apple Silicon performance is not measured. Parallel scan is
bandwidth-friendly and should map well to Metal, but the absolute speedup vs. classical LSTM on the M1 Max needs to be
measured, not assumed. MLX has scan primitives but the ergonomics of the log-space scan from Heinsen 2023 in MLX is
unverified.

The paper says nothing about long-context behavior past sequence length 4096. The whole motivation of post-Transformer
recurrent models is breaking the 100k–1M context wall; the paper does not actually demonstrate minGRU/minLSTM in that
regime. The Long Range Arena results go up to length 4000, well below the interesting regime.

Finally, the paper is a "look how cheap this can be" intervention, not a final architecture. minGRU has no
input-dependent transitions in the Mamba sense, no associative memory in the xLSTM sense, no convolutional pre-mixer in
the Mamba sense (Appendix C uses Conv4 → minRNN for some tasks). It is the floor of what works, not the ceiling.

## Connections

The paper sits squarely in the recurrence-resurgence thread that Garrison's
[memory makes computation universal](../../context/notes/garrison_memory_makes_computation_universal.md) note frames as
the architecturally right direction for memory-pillar components. Garrison's path-forward section explicitly cites this
work ("Were RNNs All We Needed?") as evidence that "minimal-state RNNs reorganized for parallel training" can match
Transformer-class sequence models — that citation is the reason the paper is in the corpus at all. The minGRU/minLSTM
recurrences are the simplest concrete instance of the recursive-state-maintenance requirement Garrison's Theorem 1
demands.

The BitNet thread ([2310.11453v1.md](2310.11453v1.md), [2402.17764v1.md](2402.17764v1.md),
[2411.04965v1.md](2411.04965v1.md), [2504.18415v2.md](2504.18415v2.md), [2510.13998v1.md](2510.13998v1.md)) is
structurally analogous: both "Were RNNs All We Needed?" and the BitNet program argue that aggressive simplification of
an architectural primitive (gates → input-only gates here, FP16 weights → ternary weights there) can preserve capability
while collapsing cost. They are complementary axes — the same minGRU could in principle be implemented with BitNet
ternary linear layers, giving a recurrent + 1-bit substrate optimized for both Apple Silicon's adder-friendly ANE and
the 32 GB unified memory budget. The Related Work explicitly mentions Zhu et al. 2024b ("scalable matmul-free language
modeling") which combines HGRN-style recurrence with ternary quantization — the cross-product is already being explored
upstream.

The mlx-flash and flash-moe threads (see [flash_moe.md](flash_moe.md) and the [mlx-flash repo](../../repos/mlx-flash/))
are about streaming larger-than-RAM weights through the M1 Max memory hierarchy. minGRU's parameter count (13–38% of
GRU) and constant-time-per-token inference change the math on what "fits" — a recurrent model of competitive quality
might not need streaming at all in the parameter ranges Linus would train. Flash-streaming becomes a tool for when scale
demands it, not a default.

The [LLM in a Flash (2312.11514v3)](2312.11514v3.md) and [Speed and Conversational LLMs (2502.16721v1)](2502.16721v1.md)
papers are both about wall-clock cost of inference; this paper widens the design space those papers are measuring
within. If the Linus benchmark suite from the Phase 1 work includes recurrent baselines alongside attention-based ones,
the task-completion-time methodology from 2502.16721 applies cleanly.

Less directly, the paper connects to [pmetal](../../repos/pmetal/) and the ANE work: Apple's ANE is a parallel adder
array optimized for elementwise and small-matmul operations, and minGRU's recurrence is overwhelmingly elementwise
multiplications and a single linear projection per gate. The kernel mapping is plausibly easier than for attention.

## Open questions for Dan

1. **Memory-pillar substrate question.** Is minGRU (or a minGRU-flavored recurrence) the right candidate substrate for
   Linus's session-memory encoder — the thing that compresses a long Worker turn history into a fixed-size rolling state
   without paying quadratic cost? If yes, it elevates from "interesting paper" to "Phase 2 architectural component" and
   deserves a small prototype: take a 384-dim minGRU, train it as an autoencoder/next-token predictor on Linus session
   transcripts, and measure whether the recovered state is useful as a Worker-prompt prefix. The Garrison framework says
   memory is an early-architecture concern; this is a concrete way to act on that.

2. **MLX port and Apple-Silicon benchmark.** The paper's training-time numbers are T4-NVIDIA. Is it worth a Phase 1
   spike to port the few-line minGRU/minLSTM PyTorch reference to MLX, run the Shakespeare experiment on the M1 Max, and
   publish the result as a small benchmark contribution? It is a low-cost, high-information experiment — if the speedup
   transfers to Metal, the architecture moves up the Phase-6 candidate list; if it does not, that is also useful
   information about Metal scan kernels.

3. **Substrate for a future Linus-trained model.** Phase 6 currently leans on LoRA-on-pretrained-LLM as the realistic
   fine-tuning path on M1 Max. This paper opens a second door: train a small (say, 100M–500M parameter)
   minGRU/xLSTM/Mamba-style model from scratch on Dan's domain corpus (genomics papers, lab notes, biochem text). Is
   that a real ambition for Phase 6, or does it stay parked behind LoRA-on-Qwen as the safer bet? The decision shapes
   how much architectural exploration is justified now.

4. **minGRU + BitNet cross-product.** Zhu et al. 2024b already combine HGRN with ternary weights; the same recipe should
   be possible for minGRU. Is a "minGRU with BitLinear gates" worth a Phase 6 (or earlier) experiment as the most
   extreme hardware-friendly substrate — recurrent (no quadratic attention), 1-bit (no FP16 multiplies),
   Apple-Silicon-friendly (parallel scan + adder arrays)? The architectural bet would be that this is the direction the
   local-first inference stack should crystallize around.

5. **What is the minimum useful sequence length for Linus's recurrent components?** The paper's experiments stop at 4096
   tokens, which is well below where attention's quadratic cost starts to bite. If Linus's session-memory and
   document-RAG components routinely operate on 32k–128k token contexts, the case for recurrence over attention is
   strongest there — but that is exactly the regime the paper does not directly evaluate. Is there a small benchmark Dan
   would want run on the M1 Max at 16k–64k context lengths to validate the extrapolation before committing to recurrent
   architecture for these layers?
