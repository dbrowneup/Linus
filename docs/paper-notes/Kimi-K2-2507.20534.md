# Kimi K2: Open Agentic Intelligence

## TL;DR

Moonshot AI's open-weights frontier model: a **1.04-trillion-parameter Mixture-of-Experts** transformer with **32B
activated parameters per token**, 384 experts (8 active + 1 shared), **Multi-head Latent Attention (MLA)**, 128K
context, pre-trained on **15.5T tokens** with **zero loss spikes** thanks to a new optimizer named **MuonClip** (Muon
plus a per-head QK-Clip rescale). Post-training is what makes it interesting for Linus: a **synthetic agentic-data
pipeline** (3,000+ real MCP tools + 20,000 synthetic tools, multi-turn rollouts judged against rubrics) and a **joint RL
stage** combining verifiable rewards with self-critic rubric rewards. Released checkpoints in **block-fp8** under a
**Modified MIT license**. The benchmark numbers that matter: **65.8 SWE-bench Verified (single-attempt agentic), 71.6
multi-attempt, 47.3 SWE-bench Multilingual, 66.1 τ²-Bench, 76.5 ACEBench** — open-source SOTA on every agentic axis,
closing the gap with Claude 4 Opus/Sonnet without extended thinking. For Linus this is simultaneously a Phase 6
LoRA-base candidate that could replace Qwen3, a Phase 6d weight-streaming target sized awkwardly between flash-MoE's
397B reach and Dan's 32 GB ceiling, and a Phase 8 substrate for a 1-bit Linus-flavored MoE. Three of those vectors are
new architectural pressure points; MuonClip is a fourth, independent of the model itself.

## The problem (in plain language)

Two interlocking problems frame the report. First, the **token-efficiency wall**: high-quality human-written training
data is finite, and you cannot keep brute-forcing parameter counts and token counts past the point where each new token
contributes less than the last. Moonshot's framing is that the next scaling coefficient is _learning signal per token_,
not parameters or tokens in isolation. Second, the **agentic-capability gap**: the multi-step planning, tool use, and
self-correction behaviors needed for production agents are rare in natural text and impossible to scrape at scale. You
have to build the data, simulate the environments, and grade the trajectories yourself — which is itself a research
program, not a curation pass.

The classical lever for "more learning per token" is a better optimizer. Muon (Jordan 2024) is one such optimizer, and
Moonshot's prior _Moonlight_ paper showed Muon outperforms AdamW under fixed compute for MoE training. The catch: naïve
Muon at trillion-parameter scale produces **exploding attention logits** (max logits > 1000 in their mid-scale 9B-active
/ 53B-total run) that destabilize training. Standard fixes — logit soft-cap, QK-Norm — either don't apply to MLA (the
keys are never fully materialized) or kick in too late. So the paper's first contribution is _engineering a stable Muon
for trillion-scale MLA-MoE_; the second is _engineering an agentic-data pipeline that scales_; the third is gluing them
together with an RL stage.

## What they propose

**Architecture (Section 2.3).** A 1.04T-parameter MoE that closely follows DeepSeek-V3 with three deliberate deviations
driven by their own scaling-law sweeps. 61 layers, hidden dimension 7,168, 1 dense layer (vs. 3 in V3), **384 experts
(vs. 256 in V3)** with 8 active + 1 shared per token, **64 attention heads (vs. 128 in V3)** to halve inference cost at
long context, no expert grouping, MLA throughout, SwiGLU activations, vocabulary 160K. The sparsity-48 choice (384
experts × 8 active = 48× sparsity) comes from a fitted scaling law showing that, at fixed activated FLOPs, raising
sparsity from 8 to 48 cuts the FLOPs needed to hit a fixed validation loss by 1.69×. The attention-head halving comes
from a separate sweep showing that doubling heads buys only 0.5–1.2% loss reduction while inflating long-context
inference FLOPs by 83% at 128K — the marginal compute is not worth it for an _agentic_ model that lives at long context.

**MuonClip optimizer (Section 2.1).** Muon plus weight decay, RMS-matching, _and_ a per-head **QK-Clip** post-update.
After each Muon step, for every attention head `h`, compute the per-head max attention logit over the batch; if it
exceeds threshold τ (they use τ = 100), rescale that head's query and key projection weights by `γ_h = τ/Smax_h`. For
MLA specifically: scale the head-specific `q_C` and `k_C` by `√γ_h`, scale the head-specific rotary `q_R` by `γ_h`, and
leave the shared rotary `k_R` untouched (it's shared across heads, so per-head rescaling would corrupt it). The result
is the trace in Figure 3 of the report: a 15.5T-token training run with **no loss spikes anywhere**, a feature that no
prior trillion-parameter MoE training run has demonstrated publicly. Crucially, the QK-Clip rescale does not change the
current step's forward/backward — it only constrains the next step's pre-softmax logits. This is the kind of
optimizer-level intervention that, if it generalizes, becomes a discipline default rather than a paper artifact.

**Pre-training data (Section 2.2).** 15.5T tokens spanning Web Text, Code, Mathematics, Knowledge. The interesting piece
is the **rephrasing pipeline**: rather than re-running multiple epochs over the same high-quality knowledge corpus
(which overfits), they generate _multiple stylistically diverse rephrasings_ of each high-value passage via chunk-wise
autoregressive rewriting (4,096-token full chunks, 256-token excerpts), then validate fidelity. SimpleQA accuracy rises
from 23.76% (10 raw epochs) → 27.39% (1 rephrase × 10 epochs) → **28.94% (10 rephrases × 1 epoch)** — single-pass over
diverse rewrites beats multi-pass over the original. Mathematics text gets a separate "learning-note" style rewrite per
SwallowMath plus cross-language translation. They cap rephrasings at 2× per corpus because hallucination risk grows with
rephrasing depth.

**Training infrastructure (Section 2.4).** H800 GPUs, 16-way pipeline parallelism × 16-way expert parallelism × ZeRO-1
data parallelism, BF16 weights with FP32 gradient accumulation (~6 TB across 256 GPUs per model-parallel group).
FP8-E4M3 storage for insensitive activations (MoE up-projection inputs, SwiGLU inputs) but **not for compute** — they
explicitly avoided FP8 in the math because of degradation risks. CPU activation offload via a copy engine that overlaps
with compute. Nothing here ports to Apple Silicon, but the FP8-storage / non-FP8-compute split is itself a design
pattern worth noting for the eventual 1-bit Linus-flavored variant.

**Training recipe (Section 2.5).** WSD learning-rate schedule, 4,096-token base context, constant LR 2e-4 for the first
10T tokens, cosine decay 2e-4 → 2e-5 across the next 5.5T, weight decay 0.1, batch size 67M tokens. Final context
extension to 32K via 60B tokens at 32K sequence, then 128K via YaRN. The annealing/long-context activation phase is
short relative to the bulk of pre-training.

**Post-training: agentic data synthesis (Section 3.1.1).** This is the half of the paper that matters most for Linus's
agentic-systems story. The pipeline has three stages: (a) **tool repository** of 3,000+ real MCP tools fetched from
GitHub plus 20,000+ synthetic tools generated by hierarchical domain evolution (financial trading, software apps, robot
control, etc.); (b) **agent + task generation**, sampling tool subsets and synthesizing both an agent system prompt and
rubric-paired tasks that probe varying complexities; (c) **multi-turn trajectory generation** in a tool-execution
simulator (a "world model" that maintains state across calls and injects controlled stochastic failures), with
LLM-generated user personas driving the dialogue. An LLM judge filters trajectories against the task rubric and only
successful ones become SFT data. For coding/SWE specifically they substitute **real execution sandboxes**
(Kubernetes-orchestrated, 10,000+ concurrent instances) with actual unit-test feedback. The blend of simulated breadth
and real-execution authenticity is the mechanism that delivers the agentic-benchmark dominance.

**Joint RL stage (Section 3.2).** Combines verifiable rewards (math, STEM, logic puzzles, code with unit tests,
faithfulness via a sentence-level judge, instruction-following with hybrid rule + LLM-judge verification) with a
**self-critic rubric reward** for tasks where verification is impossible (creative writing, open-ended QA). The critic
is K2 itself, doing pairwise preference judgments against a mix of core rubrics (the "values" of the assistant)

- prescriptive rubrics (anti-reward-hacking) + human-annotated rubrics. The closed-loop refinement: critic gets
  on-policy verifiable signal during RL training, so the subjective judgments are continually re-grounded in objective
  performance. RL algorithm extensions: **per-task token budget with truncation penalty** (forces concise responses
  where appropriate), **PTX auxiliary loss** (replays high-quality SFT data alongside RL to prevent forgetting),
  **temperature decay** (high temperature early for exploration, decayed for stable late-stage outputs).

**Infrastructure (Section 3.3).** Synchronized RL with co-located training/inference engines on the same GPU workers; a
separate **checkpoint engine** broadcasts updated parameters across the cluster in <30 seconds for a 1T model
(impressive on its own, irrelevant to Linus). Partial-rollout for long-tail agentic trajectories that can pause and
resume across RL iterations.

## Key results

- **Architecture**: 1.04T total params, 32.6B activated, 61 layers (1 dense), 7,168 hidden, 64 attention heads, 384
  experts × 8 active + 1 shared, MLA, SwiGLU, 160K vocab, 128K context. ~54% more total parameters than DeepSeek-V3 with
  ~13% fewer active parameters and 50% fewer attention heads.
- **Training**: 15.5T tokens, **zero loss spikes**, MuonClip τ = 100, 67M-token batch, WSD schedule, 4K base context
  extended to 128K via YaRN.
- **Agentic SOTA (open-source)**: SWE-bench Verified 65.8% single-attempt agentic, **71.6% multi-attempt**, vs. Claude
  Opus 4 79.4%, Claude Sonnet 4 80.2% (multi-attempt). SWE-bench Multilingual 47.3% vs. Claude Sonnet 4 51.0%. τ²-Bench
  66.1, ACEBench 76.5 — beats every baseline including GPT-4.1 (74.5).
- **Coding**: LiveCodeBench v6 53.7%, OJBench 27.1%, MultiPL-E 85.7%, Aider-Polyglot 60.0%. SOTA open-source on every
  coding benchmark; Claude Opus 4 still leads on Aider-Polyglot at 70.7%.
- **Math/STEM**: AIME 2024 69.6%, AIME 2025 49.5%, MATH-500 97.4%, GPQA-Diamond 75.1%, ZebraLogic 89.0% — global SOTA on
  AIME 2024/2025, MATH-500, HMMT 2025, PolyMath-en, ZebraLogic, GPQA-Diamond among non-thinking models.
- **General**: MMLU 89.5%, IFEval 89.8%, Multi-Challenge 54.1% (global SOTA — beats Claude Opus 4 49.0% and GPT-4.1
  36.4%). LMSYS Arena: top-1 open-source, 5th overall as of 2025-07-17 with 3,000+ blind votes.
- **Distribution**: block-fp8 weights on HuggingFace at `moonshotai/Kimi-K2-Instruct` and `moonshotai/Kimi-K2-Base`.
  Modified MIT license. Recommended inference: vLLM, SGLang, KTransformers, TensorRT-LLM. Anthropic-compatible API at
  `platform.moonshot.ai` with quirk: `real_temperature = request_temperature × 0.6`.
- **Footprint math**: 1.04T params × 1 byte (FP8) ≈ 1.04 TB on disk for the full block-fp8 distribution. Per-token
  active set: 8 routed experts + 1 shared expert + attention/embed/norms. The MoE expert hidden dim is 2048; one expert
  is roughly (3 × 7168 × 2048) ≈ 44M parameters → ~44 MB at FP8. Eight active experts × 44 MB ≈ **350 MB of expert
  weights per token**, plus the always-resident attention/embed/norm/dense-layer/shared-expert layer which is on the
  order of 15–25 GB. The weight-streaming budget is therefore **~15–25 GB resident + ~350 MB/token streamed at FP8** —
  see Phase 6d analysis below.

## What's reusable in Linus

**Phase 6 — LoRA base candidate, alongside or replacing Qwen3.** _Seed: DEC-0055._ CLAUDE.md currently names Qwen3 as
Linus's default Worker base "for 32 GB M1 Max hardware." Kimi K2's Instruct checkpoint, in block-fp8, dwarfs that budget
at ~1 TB on disk and a multi-hundred-GB resident requirement on naïve loading — but the Phase 6/8 path is not naïve
loading. The decision criteria for swapping Qwen3 → Kimi K2 as the LoRA base ought to be: (a) **agentic benchmark
deltas** on Dan's domain corpus (after LoRA, not zero-shot) — concretely SWE-bench Verified, τ²-Bench, Aider-Polyglot
scores; (b) **memory-budget feasibility** under a flash-MoE-style streaming inference path; (c) **license cleanliness**
(Modified MIT is more permissive than Llama 3 Community License and competitive with Apache 2.0 once the
trademark/branding clauses are read). If post-LoRA agentic deltas exceed Qwen3-32B by a meaningful margin _and_ Phase 6d
weight-streaming hits a tolerable per-token latency, the swap pays for itself.

**Phase 6d — weight-streaming target.** Linus's Phase 6d roadmap target spec
([`docs/specs/phase6d-streaming-target.md`](../specs/phase6d-streaming-target.md)) currently scopes to a model class
sized between Mixtral-8x7B and ~150B-parameter MoEs based on the flash-moe paper-note's empirical envelope. Kimi K2 sits
at the upper edge of physical possibility on Dan's hardware: ~1 TB of FP8 weights on the 600 GB external SSD (does not
fit at FP8 — would have to stage; see open question 1) or roughly 250 GB at int4 (does fit). The flash-moe paper-note
([`flash_moe.md`](flash_moe.md)) demonstrates a 397B MoE running on a 48 GB M3 Max at 5.74 tok/s by streaming only K=4
active experts per token from NVMe. Kimi K2 has K=8 active experts (plus 1 shared) and each expert is roughly 6× larger
than Qwen3.5-397B-A17B's expert — so the per-token streaming bandwidth requirement is meaningfully higher. The M1 Max
SSD is also slower than the M3 Max's 17.5 GB/s. The honest read: Kimi K2 at FP8 is _just past_ the M1 Max ceiling; Kimi
K2 at int4 (~250 GB total, ~175 MB/token streamed) is _plausibly inside_ the ceiling if requantization preserves
benchmarks (open question 3); Kimi K2 at ternary/1-bit (~140 GB total, ~88 MB streamed/token) is comfortably inside
(Phase 8 territory; see below).

**Phase 8 — Linus-flavored 1-bit / ternary Kimi-K2 research direction.** _Seed: DEC-0056._ The combinable-bets
hypothesis: take flash-moe's expert-streaming methodology, take BitNet's / Bonsai's ternary-weight quantization, and
apply both to Kimi K2's released base checkpoint. A 1-bit Kimi K2 variant would carry roughly 1.04T × 1.58 bits ≈ **205
GB total** at the 1.58-bit Era-of-1-bit-LLMs encoding, or **130 GB** at strict 1-bit. The active-expert streaming budget
per token at 1-bit becomes ~44 MB rather than ~350 MB — within an order of magnitude of the M1 Max's sustained SSD
bandwidth budget for interactive use. Whether you can _get_ a 1-bit Kimi K2 is its own research question: the
established BitNet line trains from scratch, while BitNet Distillation ([`2510.13998v1.md`](2510.13998v1.md))
demonstrates an in-place quantization path. Phase 8 should scope a feasibility spike — distill Kimi-K2-Base into a
ternary variant, evaluate on SWE-bench-Verified-lite and τ²-Bench-lite, decide.

**MuonClip as a Phase 6 fine-tuning convention candidate.** Independent of model choice. The "zero loss spikes at 1T ×
15.5T tokens" claim is the strongest stability evidence ever published for a Muon-family optimizer, and the QK-Clip
mechanism is a self-contained ~10-line addition to any Muon training step. Whether it generalizes to dense models, to
smaller scales, to MLX implementations on Apple Silicon — those are open questions, but the surface area is small enough
that a Phase 6 fine-tuning spike could test it cheaply. Even a negative result (MuonClip doesn't help LoRA on a 7B–32B
Worker) is a useful boundary.

**Agentic-data synthesis pipeline as a methodology import for Phase 7.** The three-stage pipeline (tool repository →
agent/task generation with rubrics → multi-turn rollouts in a stochastic simulator filtered by an LLM judge) is a direct
template for Linus's eventual agentic-skills-training methodology, especially for biology/scientific tools where Phase
7's biology-skills roadmap will need synthetic trajectories. The MCP-tool-repository half of the pipeline (3,000+ real
MCP tools harvested from GitHub) parallels Linus's own MCP-tool-substrate decision (DEC under authoring, see G6 cluster
synthesis) — the same MCP servers that Linus serves to Workers can populate Worker-training trajectories. The "real
execution sandbox" half maps cleanly onto Linus's per-task pytest harnesses for code skills.

**Anthropic-compatible API as a confirming signal for DEC-0005.** Kimi K2's deployment ships an Anthropic-compatible
HTTP surface alongside an OpenAI-compatible one. DEC-0005 commits Linus to OpenAI-compatible as the
orchestration-layer's wire protocol; the Kimi K2 evidence suggests a future world where both protocols are table-stakes
for any open-weights large model and Linus should consider Anthropic-compatible as a future-Phase add-on. The
temperature-mapping quirk (`real_temperature = request_temperature × 0.6`) is a cautionary tale for anyone implementing
Anthropic compatibility — it is a non-trivial semantic translation, not a transparent passthrough.

## What's NOT applicable / hype filter

The training-infrastructure half of the paper (Section 2.4) is ~6 TB of GPU memory across a 256-GPU model-parallel group
on H800s with NVLink + 8×400 Gbps RoCE. None of this transfers. Even the activation-offload pattern (streaming
activations to CPU RAM via a copy engine) doesn't directly inform Apple Silicon work because the unified memory
architecture changes the cost calculus for "CPU offload." Read it as background context, not as a recipe.

The synthetic-data-generation pipeline is methodologically reusable but operationally enormous: 3,000+ MCP tools and
20,000+ synthetic tools with multi-turn rollouts and LLM-judged filtering implies tens of thousands of LLM-judge calls
per trajectory cohort. Linus's analogue at Phase 7 will be a much smaller pilot — dozens of biology-skill tools,
hundreds of trajectories — and the value is the _shape_ of the pipeline, not its scale.

The "open-source SOTA agentic" framing has caveats. K2 is non-thinking; the Claude 4 multi-attempt SWE-bench numbers
that K2 closes the gap to are _without_ extended thinking either. Once Anthropic's RSP-aware extended-thinking modes
enter the comparison, the gap re-opens. K2 is also tested with `temperature = 0.6` and 8K output tokens (16K for
SWE-bench Verified Agentless) — strict ceilings that don't reflect Claude's actual production envelope. The "global
SOTA" claim on Multi-Challenge (54.1% vs. Opus 4 49.0%) is real and notable but should be read as "best non-thinking
model" not "best model."

The benchmark-saturation read of Humanity's Last Exam is honest: Kimi K2 scores 4.7 (vs. Claude Opus 4 7.1, GPT-4.1
3.7). Frontier knowledge tests still favor Claude. The MuonClip "zero loss spikes" claim is a strong empirical result
but not a theorem; it has only been demonstrated at one scale (1T total / 32B active) on one architecture (MLA-MoE) on
one training corpus. Generalization is an open question.

The license is **Modified MIT**, not vanilla MIT. The modifications include attribution requirements (must include
"Powered by Kimi K2" and a link in any product/service that exceeds 100M monthly active users _or_ generates >$20M USD
in monthly revenue) — manageable for Linus's scale, but worth reading the actual `LICENSE` file before committing to
long-term distribution. Not a blocker; not a non-issue either.

## Open questions for Dan

1. **M1 Max + 600 GB SSD weight-streaming feasibility.** Kimi K2 at FP8 is ~1.04 TB total — does not fit on the 600 GB
   external SSD as-distributed. At int4 (~250 GB) it fits with headroom; at ternary/1-bit (~130–205 GB) it fits
   comfortably. The first-byte latency floor is set by the per-token expert-shard read cost: at 8 active experts × ~44
   MB at FP8 (or ~5.5 MB at 1-bit), the sustained SSD bandwidth requirement is ~350 MB/token (FP8) down to ~44 MB/token
   (1-bit). What's the actual sustained read throughput on Dan's external SSD under the flash-moe-style `pread()` access
   pattern, and what's the resulting per-token latency floor? This is a measurement spike, not a prediction.

2. **Per-token latency vs. Qwen3-32B native.** Once the Phase 6d streaming pattern is implemented, how does Kimi K2's
   per-token generation latency compare to Qwen3-32B running natively in unified memory (no streaming)? If the gap is 5×
   or more, Kimi K2 is not interactive even if it fits; if it's 2× or less, it's a viable Worker. The threshold matters
   because hosted Claude is the comparison point — interactive should mean responses begin within 1–2 seconds and stream
   at >5 tok/s.

3. **FP8 → int4 → ternary preservation of agentic benchmarks.** The published Kimi K2 numbers are at FP8. Does the
   published block-fp8 distribution requantize cleanly to int4 with <2% benchmark degradation on SWE-bench Verified and
   τ²-Bench? Does the BitNet-Distillation-style path ([`2510.13998v1.md`](2510.13998v1.md)) preserve those numbers at
   ternary? Empirical curve, not a derivation — needs a quantization spike that's smaller in scope than the full Phase 8
   rebuild.

4. **MuonClip on Apple Silicon, generalization to dense models.** Is the MuonClip optimizer reproducible in MLX (Muon
   already exists; QK-Clip is a 10-line addition, but the per-head `Smax_h` tracking interacts with MLX's
   eager-by-default execution)? Does it generalize to dense models (the report only validates on MLA-MoE) or to smaller
   scales (the only published trace is the 1T run; the mid-scale 53B-total ablation showed instability without QK-Clip
   but didn't prove stability-with at small scale)? A Phase 6 spike LoRA-fine-tuning a 7B–32B model with MuonClip vs.
   Muon vs. AdamW would settle the matter cheaply.

5. **Evidence threshold for Phase 6 base swap from Qwen3 → Kimi K2.** What concrete benchmark deltas plus memory-budget
   wins justify the swap? Tentative threshold for discussion: ≥10 percentage points on SWE-bench Verified after
   equivalent LoRA training on Dan's domain corpus, ≥5 percentage points on τ²-Bench, _and_ per-token latency within 2×
   of Qwen3-32B-native. If Kimi K2 hits two of three, mixed verdict; if all three, commit to swap; if zero, commit to
   staying on Qwen3.

6. **Modified MIT license review.** The license is permissive but adds attribution and revenue-threshold clauses that
   Linus's "fully under Dan's control" north star should explicitly engage with. At Linus's current scale (single-user,
   Dan-only), the clauses are inert; at any future commercialization or distribution scenario they activate. Worth
   reading the `LICENSE` file in `repos/Kimi-K2/` end-to-end and pulling any actionable obligations into
   [SAFETY.md](../../SAFETY.md) or [DECISIONS.md](../../DECISIONS.md) before serious downstream commitment.

7. **Synthetic agentic-data pipeline as Phase 7 import.** The three-stage pipeline (tool repo → rubric-paired tasks →
   multi-turn rollouts in stochastic simulator with LLM-judge filtering) is the closest published template to what Phase
   7's biology-skills training will need. Should
   [`docs/specs/biology-phase7-roadmap.md`](../specs/biology-phase7-roadmap.md) absorb this pipeline-shape as a
   methodology import — concretely: build a small `bioSkills` tool repository, generate rubric-paired tasks, run
   trajectories in a Bacformer/BioReason-backed simulator, filter with a domain-judge model? Or is the gap from "general
   agentic" to "biology-specific" large enough that the Kimi K2 pipeline shape needs significant adaptation before it
   ports?
