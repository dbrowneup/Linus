# World and Human Action Models towards Gameplay Ideation

## TL;DR

Microsoft Research and Ninja Theory release **WHAM**, a 1.6B-parameter decoder-only transformer that models a real 3D
multiplayer video game (Bleeding Edge) as a sequence of interleaved image tokens (VQGAN-encoded) and discretized
controller actions. Trained on roughly 7 years of anonymized human gameplay (~500k sessions) it can generate consistent,
diverse, and modifiable 1-2 minute gameplay sequences from a 1-second seed. The paper is unusual for Nature in that its
core contribution is **methodological**: it grounds model design and evaluation in a 27-creative user study to derive
three operational capabilities — _consistency_, _diversity_, _persistency_ — and proposes evaluating world models
against those criteria rather than against pixel reconstruction alone. Weights, evaluation dataset, and a "WHAM
Demonstrator" UI are open-sourced on Hugging Face. The paper sits outside Linus' biology corpus, but its tokenization
scheme, user-grounded capability framework, and creativity-support framing are directly transferable to how Linus
orchestrates and evaluates Worker models for Dan's iterative ideation work.

## The problem (in plain language)

Generative AI for creative work tends to be benchmarked on whether outputs look right in isolation — does an image look
photorealistic, does a video flow smoothly. But the actual day-to-day practice of creatives, as established by decades
of HCI literature, is _iterative_: you make something, you tweak it, you fork it, you try variations, you preserve the
bits you liked. State-of-the-art generative models support a "prompt and pray" workflow well but support that iterative
tweak-fork-preserve loop poorly. Game development makes this especially visible because a game is a temporally extended
interactive artifact — it is not enough to generate one nice frame; the frames must cohere over time, vary meaningfully
across runs, and respect manual edits a designer makes mid-stream.

The authors interviewed 27 game development creatives across multiple studios, did thematic analysis on the transcripts,
and distilled two themes: (1) divergent thinking needs to be contextualized within the consistency of a professional
craft, and (2) creative agency depends on being able to control an iterative process where edits actually stick. From
these two themes they derive three measurable model capabilities — consistency, diversity, persistency — and argue that
these, not raw video quality, are what creative practice actually requires.

## What they propose

**The capability framework.** Three criteria, defined precisely enough to evaluate against:

- _Consistency_: generated sequences must be coherent frame-to-frame and respect the underlying game mechanics (no
  clipping through walls, no objects vanishing without cause).
- _Diversity_: from the same seed, the model should produce meaningfully different continuations — different paths,
  different teammate behavior, different visual variations.
- _Persistency_: when a user edits a frame mid-generation (paints in a character, moves an object), that edit should be
  carried forward into subsequent generated frames rather than being washed out.

**The model.** WHAM is a decoder-only causal transformer trained as a next-token predictor over interleaved sequences of
image tokens and controller-action tokens. Images at 300x180 resolution are encoded by a VQGAN into 540 discrete tokens
per frame from a vocabulary of 16,384. Xbox controller actions become 16 tokens per timestep — buttons natively
discrete, joystick x/y discretized into 11 buckets each — drawn from a separate vocabulary of 68 symbols. No explicit
delimiter separates images from actions; the model learns from positional embeddings which type of token comes next.
Context length is 1 second of gameplay (5,560 tokens). The largest variant is 1.6B parameters; smaller 15M-894M variants
were used for scaling studies.

**The data.** ~500,000 anonymized Bleeding Edge sessions across all 7 maps (~7 years of continuous play) constitute the
"7 Maps" dataset; a filtered single-map subset (~1 year) is the "Skygarden" dataset used for cheaper scaling
experiments. Critically, this is _real human play data_, not synthetic rollouts.

**The evaluation.** For consistency, the authors adapt Frechet Video Distance (FVD): condition WHAM on 1 second of human
gameplay, generate 10 seconds while feeding the human's actual controller inputs, and measure FVD between generated and
ground-truth video. For diversity, they generate multiple continuations from the same seed and quantify behavioral and
visual variation. For persistency, they manually edit frames (insert characters, jump pads, power cells) and
human-annotate whether the edit "persisted", "persisted until out of frame", "did not persist", or rendered "unusable"
generations.

**The artifact.** Beyond the model, they release the "WHAM Demonstrator" — a concept UI that exposes the three
capabilities interactively and is meant to seed further research into creativity-support workflows.

## Key results

- **Scaling works as expected.** FVD improves smoothly with FLOPs across the 15M -> 894M -> 1.6B series, validating that
  the architecture is well-posed for scaling.
- **Long-horizon consistency.** The 1.6B WHAM generates coherent gameplay for up to 2 minutes from a 1-second seed — far
  beyond the 1-second training context — without the usual world-model collapse.
- **Diversity is real.** From identical seeds, WHAM produces sequences with meaningfully different camera movements,
  navigation paths, and visual variation in player gear (e.g., distinct hoverboards).
- **Persistency is partial.** Edits often persist, but success depends on the prominence of the inserted element and the
  starting location. Small or visually subtle edits (a small power cell in an unusual location) frequently fail to
  persist or morph into other objects. The paper is honest about these failure modes — Extended Data Fig. 6-7 lay out
  the cases explicitly.
- **Higher resolution helps when paired with capacity.** The 1.6B model uses 300x180 images (540 tokens each); smaller
  models use 128x128 (256 tokens). Higher resolution lifts the FVD ceiling because reconstruction quality is no longer
  the bottleneck.
- **Open-sourcing.** Weights, an evaluation dataset, and the Demonstrator are all available at
  huggingface.co/microsoft/wham — non-trivial for a flagship Microsoft Research Nature paper.

## What's reusable in Linus

**Methodological framing, Phases 1-2.** The paper's strongest contribution for Linus is not the model but the
_capability-first evaluation framework derived from a user study_. Linus is a creativity-support tool for Dan — for
scientific writing, code exploration, paper synthesis, hypothesis generation. The standard LLM benchmarks (MMLU,
HumanEval, etc.) measure something different than what Dan actually needs. Borrowing this paper's playbook, Linus'
`benchmarks/dan_tasks/` should be derived from a structured introspection of Dan's actual workflow — what does iteration
look like for him, what does diversity-of-options mean in his ideation, what does it mean for a Worker to "persist" his
edits across a multi-turn refinement. The three-capability schema (consistency, diversity, persistency) is a concrete
starting template; the consistency/diversity axis maps cleanly onto Linus session metrics already implied by the
Maestro/Worker protocol.

**Tokenization as interface, Phases 5-6.** The decision to interleave heterogeneous modalities (image tokens +
controller tokens) into one stream and let positional embeddings sort it out is a clean design pattern. If Linus
eventually wires together code, tool calls, and structured outputs through a single Worker model, the WHAM tokenization
recipe — discrete vocabularies per modality, no delimiter, learned position semantics — is a known-good reference. The
Bleeding Edge action vocabulary (68 symbols for an Xbox controller) is also a useful sanity check on how cheap
discretizing a complex action space can be.

**VQGAN as a token bottleneck, Phase 6+.** If Linus ever wants to handle figures from papers (Dan's corpus is
image-rich) as first-class tokens for downstream processing, VQGAN-style discretization at moderate resolution is the de
facto choice and this paper is a fresh datapoint on what works at scale. 540 tokens per 300x180 frame is a useful budget
reference.

**Open weights, MLX porting candidate.** The 1.6B WHAM is small enough that porting to MLX on M1 Max is plausible if Dan
ever wanted a local world-modeling experiment for fun or pedagogy. Not a priority, but flagged as feasible — it would
fit comfortably in 32GB at fp16.

**Engineering discipline.** The paper's smallest variant (15M params) was used to do scaling experiments before
committing to the 1.6B run — a textbook smoke-test discipline that maps directly to Linus' "no full-dataset run without
a 10-50 item sample" rule.

## What's NOT applicable

This is a video-game world model. Linus is a private AI orchestration backend for scientific work. The vast majority of
the paper — Bleeding Edge specifically, controller-action discretization, FVD on gameplay video, the Demonstrator UI —
has zero direct port into Linus' product surface. The paper sits outside the biology FM corpus (Group A), the
generative-biology corpus (Group B), and the function/discovery corpus (Group C).

The training scale (~500k sessions, ~7 years of gameplay) presupposes a corpus Linus does not have an analogue for; the
model was trained on Microsoft compute, not Apple Silicon. The fp16 1.6B training is at the edge of what Dan's M1 Max
could reproduce even with LoRA, and the data pipeline (rendering, action capture from Xbox events) has no Linus
equivalent.

The user-study methodology (27 creatives across studios) is itself not directly applicable — Linus has a user population
of one. The _spirit_ of the methodology (derive capabilities from real workflow needs, not from leaderboard metrics) is
the transferable part; the literal interview-and-thematic-analysis pipeline is overkill for a single-user product.

Persistency as defined here (preserving manual frame edits in a generated video stream) does not map cleanly onto Linus'
workflow. The closest analogue might be "Worker preserves Dan's manual edits across turns" — a real concern, but the
failure modes will look different from "the inserted Powercell turned into a wall."

## Connections

The paper's central methodological argument — that the right metric for a model depends on what users actually do with
it, not on what is easy to measure — is structurally identical to the argument in [2502.16721v1](2502.16721v1.md)
(tokens-per-second is the wrong proxy for task-completion time in conversational LLMs). Both papers say: _the community
is benchmarking the wrong thing because the right thing is harder to measure but more honest_. Linus should treat both
as motivation for building benchmarks grounded in Dan's real tasks rather than borrowed leaderboards.

The transformer-as-sequence-model-over-arbitrary-discrete-vocabularies framing connects to most of the language-model
corpus in `docs/paper-notes/`, but the closest design parallel is the family of bio foundation models in Group A (Evo 2,
LucaOne, AlphaGenome) — they too treat heterogeneous biological signals as discrete token streams over a learned
vocabulary. WHAM is a useful out-of-domain confirmation that this recipe generalizes well beyond text and DNA.

The "consistency, diversity, persistency" framework rhymes with what a memory architecture should provide a personal AI:
episodic continuity (consistency), exploration of alternatives (diversity), and durable preservation of user-asserted
facts (persistency). This is worth flagging when the memory pillar of Linus is being designed — the WHAM capability
vocabulary may be a useful imported lexicon.

Nothing in the paper connects to the BitNet/quantization thread, the KnowledgeBase embedding work, or the hypercube
geometry papers.

## Open questions for Dan

1. **Capability framework for Linus.** Is the consistency/diversity/persistency triple worth lifting wholesale as a
   Phase 1-2 evaluation framework for Linus, or is the more honest path to derive a Dan-specific triple from your own
   workflow introspection? The WHAM playbook says: do the introspection, distill capabilities, then evaluate. Want me to
   draft a short Dan-workflow-introspection spec?
2. **Memory pillar vocabulary.** The recently-added Memory Architecture spec talks about episodic/semantic/procedural
   distinctions. Would re-framing some of those as consistency/diversity/persistency (or layering both vocabularies)
   clarify what the memory pillar actually has to _do_ for ideation workflows? This is a cheap renaming experiment if it
   sharpens design discussion.
3. **MLX port as pedagogy.** Is there value in an MLX port of WHAM-1.6B as a side-experiment — purely for understanding
   how Apple Silicon handles a non-trivial multimodal autoregressive transformer outside the LLM monoculture? Not a
   roadmap item, but a possible weekend-experiment that would generate transferable MLX know-how.
4. **Image tokens for the KnowledgeBase.** Dan's papers contain figures. Is there a Phase 3-4 case for VQGAN-tokenizing
   figure crops as searchable units in the KnowledgeBase, or is that out of scope until much later?
5. **User study, n=1.** Beyond CLAUDE.md, is there value in a more formal "what Dan actually does in a creative session"
   written artifact — analogous to the Microsoft 27-creative interview output — that future Linus design decisions can
   be grounded against? It would be a one-time investment that the WHAM paper makes a strong case for.
