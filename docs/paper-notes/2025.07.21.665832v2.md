# ProteinReasoner: A Multi-Modal Protein Language Model with Chain-of-Thought Reasoning for Efficient Protein Design

## TL;DR

ProteinReasoner is a multi-modal generative PLM that inserts the **evolutionary profile** (an L×21 position-wise amino acid frequency matrix derived from MSAs) as an explicit intermediate "reasoning" modality between sequence and structure, trained autoregressively in both folding and inverse-folding directions. The framing is unapologetically borrowed from chain-of-thought prompting in LLMs: rather than fusing modalities at the input embedding (ESM3) or coupling them via diffusion (DPLM-2), each modality is a separately processed token stream with its own decoder head, and the model is forced to predict the profile before the next-modality target. At 150M and 650M parameters, the model **beats ESM3-Open-1.4B and DPLM-2-3B** on zero-shot structure prediction (TM-score / RMSD on CAMEO, CASP14, CASP15, PDB date split), inverse folding (AAR and scTM), and ProteinGym fitness prediction. The second half of the paper extends the same CoT idea to protein optimization: a *directed evolutionary profile* (fitness-weighted amino acid frequencies from a prior round of wet-lab assays) is appended to the input chain as an in-context reasoning step, and the resulting ICL pipeline outperforms an active-learning baseline (one fine-tuned model per protein, 63 of them) on the Megascale stability benchmark using a single un-tuned model. The paper does not announce checkpoint releases.

## The problem (in plain language)

Multi-modal PLMs (ESM3, ProSST, SaProt, DPLM-2) bring sequence, structure, and sometimes MSA-derived information into one model, and they outperform sequence-only PLMs on essentially every benchmark that probes structural or evolutionary constraints. But the dominant integration strategy is to fuse modalities at the input embedding layer — concatenate or sum them, then let the transformer figure it out. The authors argue this misses something specific about how protein scientists actually reason: AlphaFold2 isn't a black-box sequence→structure map; it explicitly threads MSA-derived co-evolutionary signal as the substrate from which residue-residue contacts are inferred. Directed evolution isn't a black-box library→winner map; it's a stepwise update of a fitness-weighted distribution over mutations. In both cases, the **evolutionary signal is the reasoning bridge**, not just an auxiliary feature.

The mismatch the paper targets is structural: sequence-only PLMs lack structure; existing multi-modal PLMs include structure but flatten the evolutionary intermediate into either an input feature or an implicit hidden state. The proposal is to make that evolutionary intermediate an explicit, predicted modality, then pretrain the model to walk through it in a fixed order — sequence → profile → structure, or the reverse — exactly the way CoT prompting forces an LLM to verbalize intermediate reasoning before the answer.

## What they propose

**Three modalities, three token streams, three decoder heads.** The amino acid sequence is residue-tokenized (26 AA tokens + 11 special). The 3D structure is discretized via the DPLM-2 structure tokenizer (vocab 8192). The evolutionary profile is an L×21 numerical matrix (20 AAs + gap), embedded with a linear projection rather than an embedding lookup since it's continuous. The three streams are concatenated into a 3L×D sequence, passed through standard transformer layers with rotary positional encoding (position indices reset within each modality, no inter-modality attention masking), and read out by three modality-specific heads — cross-entropy loss on structure and sequence, KL divergence on profile, weighted 1.0/1.0/2.0.

**The CoT-as-architecture move.** During pretraining, the input is concatenated in either (sequence → profile → structure) or (structure → profile → sequence) order. The autoregressive objective forces the model to predict the profile *before* it predicts the target modality. The profile thus functions as a forced intermediate reasoning step — analogous to "Let's think step by step" producing tokens that are then attended to when generating the final answer. The bidirectional pretraining gives both folding and inverse-folding flow, and an ablation shows unidirectional (forward-only or inverse-only) actually beats bidirectional on the corresponding single-direction task, while bidirectional wins on tasks (ProteinGym fitness) that benefit from cross-modal generalization.

**Two-stage pretraining.** Stage 1 is a pretrained sequence-only backbone from the BioMap "Compute-Optimal PLM" paper (Cheng et al. 2024, ref 23), trained on 1T amino acid tokens. Stage 2 initializes from this and runs multi-modal pretraining on 9.45M AlphaFold-DB structures (pLDDT >= 70, FoldSeek non-singleton clusters) plus 311k PDB-REDO chains. MSAs are generated with HHblits against UniRef30. Two model sizes (150M, 650M) trained for 600k steps with batch 1024 on 32 A800 GPUs in BF16; the 650M takes ~6 days. Total budget ~1.89×10^11 tokens.

**ICL for protein optimization.** This is the second contribution. To extend the CoT chain to optimization, they construct a *directed evolutionary profile* — same L×21 shape as the natural profile, but where each row is a fitness-weighted (softmax-normalized ΔΔG) amino acid frequency over a small set of experimentally scored variants (with the WT included). The five-modality input chain becomes (structure → natural profile → WT sequence → directed profile → mutant sequence). Because the pretrained model never saw five modalities, they fine-tune (SFT then DPO) the 150M model on this format using the Megascale stability dataset, then evaluate ICL inference: 50 prior mutants form the score matrix, and the model generates predictions for the rest. The advertised payoff is that the same single fine-tuned model handles arbitrary new proteins via prompt-conditioning — no per-protein retraining as an active-learning pipeline would require.

## Key results

**Zero-shot structure prediction (Table 1).** ProteinReasoner-650M in *externally guided* mode (MSA-derived profile provided) beats every baseline on every dataset. RMSD/TM-score: CAMEO 2.50/0.774 vs ESM3-Open-1.4B at 2.70/0.747 and DPLM-2-3B at 3.02/0.711; CASP14 3.63/0.624 vs ESM3 3.90/0.566 and DPLM-2-3B 4.30/0.527; PDB date split 2.01/0.850 vs ESM3 2.37/0.794 and DPLM-2-3B 2.34/0.811. The *internally inferred* mode (model generates its own profile from sequence alone) is roughly competitive with baselines but trails the guided mode by 9.5–33.8% TM — the authors explicitly flag profile-generation quality as the main remaining weakness. Scaling 150M → 650M yields 5.7–18.2% TM gains.

**Zero-shot inverse folding (Table 2).** ProteinReasoner-650M (inferred-only, since a profile from MSA would leak the answer) scores AAR 47.77 / scTM 0.786 on CAMEO+CASP and AAR 59.22 / scTM 0.918 on PDB date split — best scTM across all models, with DPLM-2-3B winning AAR (62.39 on PDB split) but losing scTM (0.901). The framing: ProteinReasoner generates structurally compatible but more sequence-diverse solutions, which is arguably what you want for design.

**Zero-shot fitness prediction (Table 3, ProteinGym).** ProteinReasoner-150M hits Spearman 0.489 / AUC 0.767, ProteinReasoner-650M 0.482 / 0.765, beating ESM3-Open-1.4B at 0.466 / 0.755. Scaling does *not* help here — the authors hypothesize fitness prediction is less scale-sensitive than generative tasks.

**Ablations (Tables 4–5).** The profile-as-reasoning-step matters: removing the profile drops bi-directional structure prediction TM by 23.8–35.8% in guided mode and ProteinGym Spearman from 0.469 to 0.423 (-10.9%). Two-stage init beats from-scratch substantially, especially in inferred mode. One inverse-folding wrinkle: the no-profile variant unexpectedly beats the with-profile model when the model generates the profile internally, which the authors attribute to systematic noise in self-generated profiles propagating downstream.

**ICL vs active learning on Megascale (Figure 4).** Single ICL model with no per-protein hyperparameter tuning: Spearman 0.778 (all) / 0.738 (overlap), AUROC 0.854. Sixty-three per-protein AL fine-tuned models with best-epoch selection: Spearman 0.752 / 0.711, AUROC 0.838. ICL wins on double-mutant prediction too (AUROC 0.841 vs AL 0.805 vs vanilla 0.740). Performance scales with support set size from 25 to 200 priors for both methods, but ICL stays ahead.

## What's reusable in Linus

This paper sits cleanly at the intersection of two Linus threads — Dan's domain (proteins, genomics, design) and the memory/CoT pillar — so the reuse story is unusually concrete.

**Direct, Phase 6 (Fine-Tuning) and Phase 7 (Skills) — protein-design Worker.** The 150M model fits comfortably within M1 Max RAM at any precision; the 650M model fits in BF16 with room to spare and trivially in 4-bit. Both are exactly the size class where MLX inference is fast and where LoRA fine-tuning on the M1 Max is tractable. *If* BioMap releases checkpoints (the paper does not announce them — see open questions), ProteinReasoner is a near-ideal candidate for the first non-coding domain Worker: small enough to keep loaded, smart enough to outperform 1.4B–3B baselines on the tasks Dan actually cares about (structure, design, fitness). A Phase 7 "protein design" skill could bundle a ProteinReasoner Worker with PyMOL, HHblits/MMseqs2 for MSA generation, and the ProteinGym/Megascale benchmark harnesses.

**Architectural lesson, all phases — typed-artifact CoT for scientific Workers.** The deeper takeaway is methodological. ProteinReasoner shows that for a domain with well-known intermediate scientific artifacts (in this case the MSA-derived profile), making those artifacts into *explicit predicted modalities with their own decoder heads* outperforms either implicit fusion or unstructured natural-language CoT. The Linus orchestration layer should hold space for a "scientific CoT Worker" archetype where intermediate reasoning steps are typed scientific objects — sequence alignments, predicted secondary structures, gene-set enrichments, GO term tuples, contact maps — rather than free-text "let me think." This is conceptually the bio analog of Coconut's continuous-latent reasoning (paper-notes/2412.06769v3.md): the reasoning state isn't text tokens, it's a structured representation. The lesson for Linus: when designing tools/Workers for Dan's scientific domains, the right intermediate isn't always natural language — it might be an L×21 frequency matrix, a graph, a sparse vector.

**Methodological, Phase 3 (Parallel Agents) — ICL beats active-learning loops.** The Megascale result is sharp: a single un-tuned model, prompted with prior examples in context, beats 63 separately fine-tuned per-protein models with best-epoch selection. For Linus' multi-agent fan-out story, this is permission to default to ICL with rich context rather than spawning fine-tuning loops. Workers that retrieve relevant prior examples from the KnowledgeBase and stuff them into the prompt may consistently beat Workers that try to adapt model weights per task — at least until proven otherwise on a specific benchmark. This generalizes the autoresearch pattern.

**KnowledgeBase integration.** MSA-derived profiles are a kind of compressed retrieval result over a sequence database — structurally the same pattern as a Linus retrieval-augmented Worker pulling top-k passages from KnowledgeBase, weighting by relevance, and aggregating. ProteinReasoner is a worked example of how to consume that aggregate as a first-class reasoning input, not just a list of citations.

## What's NOT applicable

**Training scale is out of reach.** 32 A800 GPUs for 6 days for the 650M, on 1.89×10^11 tokens — Dan does not have this. Reproducing pretraining is not on the table; this is a "use the released model if it appears, or fine-tune via LoRA from a hypothetical released checkpoint" play, not a "train from scratch" play.

**Inference fits.** Both 150M and 650M are within M1 Max comfort. Even at FP16, 650M is ~1.3 GB of weights, with KV cache and activations on top — comfortably within the 32 GB unified memory envelope alongside other Linus components. At 4-bit it's negligible.

**Checkpoint availability is the blocker.** I read the full paper; it does not state that ProteinReasoner checkpoints will be released. Methods describe the BioMap-internal training infrastructure (custom Megatron-DeepSpeed). The DPLM-2 and ESM3 baselines are explicitly downloaded from Hugging Face (`airkingbd/dplm2_650m`, `airkingbd/dplm2_3b`, `esm3-sm-open-v1`, `airkingbd/struct_tokenizer`), but ProteinReasoner's own weights are not given a Hugging Face path. Without a release, this paper is reusable as architecture inspiration but not as a Linus Worker.

**Format conversion.** Even if checkpoints appear, they would arrive in PyTorch / Megatron format. Conversion to MLX or GGUF is non-trivial because the model has three separate decoder heads (cross-entropy on tokens for two modalities, KL on a continuous matrix for the third) plus modality-specific input projections — not a stock transformer. A naive llama.cpp conversion will not work; either MLX with custom heads or a from-scratch reimplementation would be required.

**Single-round ICL only.** The optimization framework was validated only in a two-round setting (one round of priors → predict round-2 sequences) because no public multi-round directed-evolution dataset exists. The authors flag multi-round as future work.

The conceptual adjacency to Coconut is striking: both papers replace text-token reasoning with continuous-valued intermediate states. Coconut uses learned latent vectors; ProteinReasoner uses an L×21 amino acid frequency matrix that has explicit biological semantics. The lesson — that the reasoning substrate doesn't have to be natural language — generalizes across both.

The ESM3 comparison anchor is worth noting for any future ESM3 paper-note: ProteinReasoner uses ESM3-Open-1.4B (the publicly released version, not the larger frontier variants) as its baseline, and consistently beats it at 650M. The DPLM-2 comparison is more apples-to-apples since DPLM-2 also tokenizes structure with the same tokenizer ProteinReasoner adopts.
