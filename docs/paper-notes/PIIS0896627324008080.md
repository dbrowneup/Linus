# The Unbearable Slowness of Being: Why Do We Live at 10 bits/s?

## TL;DR

Human behavior — across typing, reading, perception, memory sports, and competitive gaming — operates at a strikingly consistent **~10 bits/s** of information throughput, while our peripheral sensory hardware ingests data at **~10⁹ bits/s**. The brain therefore filters incoming signals by a factor of **Si = 10⁸ (the "Sifting Number")**, the largest unexplained constant in brain science. This Perspective from Zheng and Meister at Caltech surveys candidate explanations — noisy neurons, photographic memory illusions, unconscious processing, redundancy — dismisses most of them, and proposes that the brain operates in two anatomically and computationally distinct modes (outer and inner brain) whose interface is poorly understood. It closes by calling for new naturalistic, high-dimensional experiments to illuminate the inner brain's function and, with it, the fundamental speed limit on human cognition.

## The problem (in plain language)

A concert pianist, a competitive Tetris player, a memory athlete committing 52 shuffled playing cards to memory, a typist copying a manuscript, and a subject in a classic choice-reaction experiment all produce approximately the same information throughput: about 10 bits every second. This number is absurdly small. A modern WiFi link runs at hundreds of megabits per second; even a single optic nerve carries ~100 megabits/s. Yet the brain, receiving that torrent from the senses, never translates more than ~10 bits/s of it into behavior. Why?

Before this paper, there was no satisfying answer. The usual suspects — noisy neurons requiring averaging, photographic memory short-circuiting the limit, unconscious processing operating at higher rates, brain redundancy soaking up extra capacity — are each carefully examined and found to fall short. The ten-bits-per-second ceiling is not a measurement artifact; it is a real and remarkable feature of cognition, and it stands unexplained.

## What they propose

This is a Perspective (a synthetic review with original framing), not a paper reporting new experimental data. The methodological contribution is systematic application of Shannon information theory to a century of behavioral measurements, unified under a single framework of mutual information between context and action (Box 1, Equations 3–5). The authors define a task-specific "action" as any distinguishable output that matters for performance, compute the entropy of that action distribution, and subtract the conditional entropy given the context, yielding bits per second that are comparable across radically different tasks.

From this analysis two structural proposals emerge. First, the brain is organized into an **outer brain** — peripheral sensory regions and motor output areas operating at high dimensionality and high information rates — and an **inner brain** — prefrontal, parietal, and associative cortex operating on a drastically compressed, low-dimensional representation at ~10 bits/s. The interface between them, partly implemented through the superior colliculus acting as a sifting bottleneck, compresses information by ~10⁸-fold at each moment. Second, the paper proposes that the single-strand serial architecture of the inner brain may reflect the evolutionary origin of nervous systems: early metazoan brains evolved to keep a body in one place moving toward food or away from predators, a problem that is strictly local in space and time and requires only one decision thread. Human cognition inherited that architecture and has never escaped it.

The authors explicitly reject the idea that the bottleneck is peripheral (neurons are too noisy, hence redundancy is needed) by showing that single neurons can transmit several bits per spike reliably, that the retina and primary visual cortex contain no meaningful redundancy, and that the brain's upper-bound storage capacity (~50 TB from synapse counts) vastly exceeds what the perceptual channel can fill in a lifetime (~5 GB at 10 bits/s for 100 years awake).

## Key results

The empirical foundation is a table spanning 12 behaviors across more than a century of studies. Highlights: binary digit memorization 4.9 bits/s, blindfolded speedcubing 11.8 bits/s, listening comprehension ~13 bits/s, object recognition 30–50 bits/s (sub-second snapshots), reading 28–45 bits/s, StarCraft (e-athlete) ~10 bits/s, Tetris ~7 bits/s, typing 10 bits/s. The range is a factor of ~6–7 across all tasks, regardless of modality, time scale, or training level. The authors treat estimates as reliable to within a factor of 2, meaning the 10-bit-per-second figure is robust across an order of magnitude of behavioral diversity.

The Sifting Number Si = 10⁸ is calculated as the ratio of peripheral sensory bandwidth (~1 Gbit/s for the optic nerve alone) to behavioral throughput (10 bits/s). This is presented as the largest unexplained ratio in neuroscience, larger than the factor of 2 speed differences that fill journals. The brain memory capacity upper bound from synapse count (~5 × 10¹⁴ synapses × 5 bits dynamic range = 50 TB) exceeds the lifetime perceptual input by four orders of magnitude, ruling out storage as the bottleneck. The fly visual system analysis (0.62 bits/s torque response vs. hundreds of bits/s in single neurons) suggests the paradox extends to insects, indicating evolutionary depth.

For brain-computer interfaces, the authors calculate that a speech BCI decoding 62 words/minute reaches 1.5 bits/s — far below even the ~10 bits/s of the behavioral channel — meaning current BCIs are bottlenecked by decoding algorithms, not the underlying neural signal or the human cognitive throughput limit.

## What's NOT applicable

The paper offers no computational algorithm, no dataset, and no model that Linus can import. It is a Perspective — argument by synthesis, not empirical result. The 10 bits/s number is a behavioral aggregate and does not translate into a per-task engineering spec: object recognition at 30–50 bits/s for sub-second exposures is not achievable by a human reading a chat window. The outer/inner brain framing is a conceptual metaphor, not a validated architectural model with defined circuit components; the authors acknowledge it openly and call for experiments to test it. The paper's critique of brain-computer interfaces and Neuralink is pointed but does not propose solutions. For Linus engineering purposes, this paper informs design philosophy and prioritization of human-facing output compression, not any specific algorithm or training procedure.
