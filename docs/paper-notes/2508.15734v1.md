# Measuring the environmental impact of delivering AI at Google Scale

## TL;DR
Google publishes the first first-party, full-stack measurement of the environmental cost of serving a production AI assistant. The headline numbers for the median Gemini Apps text prompt in May 2025: **0.24 Wh of energy, 0.03 gCO2e of emissions, and 0.26 mL of water consumed** — equivalent to nine seconds of TV and roughly five drops of water. The methodological contribution is a "Comprehensive Approach" that draws the measurement boundary around active accelerators, host CPU/DRAM, idle reserved capacity, and data-center overhead (PUE), rather than just the active accelerator silicon as prior empirical work has done. Applying that boundary to Gemini gives a number 2.4x larger than the narrower "Existing Approach" (0.10 Wh) but still one to two orders of magnitude *smaller* than most prior published estimates. Over twelve months they report a **33x reduction in per-prompt energy and a 44x reduction in per-prompt total emissions**, attributed to model architecture (MoE, distillation), software stack (XLA, Pallas, Pathways, AQT quantization, speculative decoding), custom TPUs (Ironwood is 30x more energy-efficient than the first public TPU), and clean-energy procurement.

## The problem (in plain language)
Estimates of the energy cost of a single chat prompt vary by an order of magnitude across the literature — from De Vries' 3 Wh (2023, model-based) and EcoLogits' 1.83-6.95 Wh, to Epoch.AI's 0.3 Wh (2025, updated assumptions), to Sam Altman's undocumented 0.34 Wh, to Mistral's 1.14 gCO2e LCA, to Samsi et al.'s direct measurement of 0.3 Wh on LLaMA-65B. The reason isn't that the underlying physics is mysterious; it's that nobody agrees on what to count. Some studies measure only the active GPU. Some add CPU/DRAM. Some apply a global PUE multiplier; others ignore overhead. None account for the idle capacity that production fleets must reserve to handle traffic spikes and failover. None have access to first-party utilization data from a hyperscaler running real user prompts at real scale.

The paper's diagnosis is that this is a *measurement-boundary* problem more than a measurement-precision problem. Until the field agrees on what energy-consuming activities belong inside the box, "Wh per prompt" is unfalsifiable advocacy. And without comparable numbers, a model provider can't be properly incentivized to reduce environmental cost, because there's no shared scoreboard. Google, having both the telemetry and the operational control to define the box rigorously, proposes a standard and reports its own number against it.

## What they propose
A four-part methodology. **(1) A measurement boundary** rooted in the functional unit of one "serving AI computer" — one or more accelerator trays plus a host tray — with four energy components: active AI accelerator (the silicon doing prefill and decode); active host CPU and DRAM; idle machines (provisioned for availability and traffic spikes); and data-center overhead, captured by PUE. Explicit exclusions: external networking, end-user devices, model training, and data storage. **(2) An energy measurement procedure** built on Google's internal PSU telemetry: tag jobs and machines by model, measure tray power (host + accelerators) for total and idle states over hours, multiply by PUE for overhead, and divide by total prompt count to get per-prompt figures. They also compute an "Existing Approach" number — only active accelerators, sampled from the top-10% most efficient data centers — to mimic what an idealized external benchmark would see, enabling apples-to-apples comparison with the literature. **(3) Emissions and water methods** that apply Google's market-based grid emission factors (94 gCO2e/kWh in 2024 net of clean-energy procurement, down from 135 in 2023), include Scope 1+3 (operational + embodied) following Schneider et al. 2025, and convert energy-minus-overhead to water consumption via a fleet-average WUE of 1.15 L/kWh. **(4) An aggregate metric** — the daily *median* per-prompt energy across models, weighted by prompt volume — chosen because the distribution is right-skewed by a small number of high-token-count prompts that distort the mean.

The list of efficiency techniques the paper credits is itself worth reading: Mixture-of-Experts (10-100x reduction by activating sparse subsets), Accurate Quantized Training (AQT), distillation into Flash and Flash-Lite serving variants, speculative decoding with embedded draft models, KV caching, disaggregated serving (prefill and decode on separate accelerators), batch inference at production scale, the XLA compiler, Pallas kernels, the Pathways runtime, and co-designed TPUs. Many of these are recognizable as the same efficiency-frontier techniques the rest of the inference-research literature is exploring; Google's contribution is showing what their compounding effect looks like in production telemetry.

## Key results
The Comprehensive Approach gives 0.24 Wh per median Gemini Apps text prompt in May 2025: 0.14 Wh from active AI accelerators (58%), 0.06 Wh from host CPU and DRAM (25%), 0.02 Wh from idle reserved capacity (10%), and 0.02 Wh from data-center overhead (8%). The Existing Approach — narrower boundary, top-10% efficient DCs only — gives 0.10 Wh, a 2.4x understatement. Translated, the Comprehensive number means a per-prompt scaling factor of 1.72x on top of active-accelerator energy alone, smaller than the 2x rule-of-thumb that MIT Tech Review applied to ML.ENERGY data.

Emissions: 0.03 gCO2e per prompt total (0.023 Scope 2 market-based, 0.010 Scope 1+3 embodied). Water: 0.26 mL per prompt — five drops, two orders of magnitude smaller than Mistral's 45 mL or Li et al.'s 50 mL estimates. The year-over-year trajectory from May 2024 to May 2025 shows a 33x energy reduction (23x from model improvements, 1.4x from utilization), a 1.4x reduction in grid-emissions intensity from clean-energy procurement, and a 36x reduction in Scope 1+3 emissions from fewer machine-hours per prompt — combining to a 44x reduction in total per-prompt emissions over twelve months. Google's fleet PUE is reported as 1.09 (only 9% overhead), and TPU efficiency has improved 30x from the first public TPU to Ironwood.

## What's reusable in Linus
**Direct, Phase 1 (Recon & Baselines):** The paper gives Linus a methodological template for per-prompt energy measurement on Apple Silicon. The decomposition into active-accelerator + active-host + idle + overhead translates almost cleanly to a single M1 Max: active-GPU and active-ANE energy (queryable via Powermetrics), active-CPU and unified-memory energy (also Powermetrics), and an "idle baseline" that captures what the laptop draws when no inference is running but Ollama is loaded. The data-center overhead term collapses to almost nothing on a laptop — there is no PUE multiplier, no cooling tower, no reserved failover capacity. That collapse is itself the interesting result. A well-designed Phase 1 deliverable: pair the existing throughput / task-completion benchmark suggested in [2502.16721v1.md](2502.16721v1.md) with a Powermetrics-based Wh/prompt column, broken down by ANE / GPU / CPU / DRAM, for each Worker model Linus runs (Qwen2.5-Coder, Mistral-7B, future Linus). This is a small script over `sudo powermetrics --samplers cpu_power,gpu_power,ane_power -i 1000` plus prompt-volume bookkeeping.

**Direct, Phase 5 (Interface):** Energy as a user-visible signal. The Linus chat surface could plausibly show per-prompt energy and accumulated session energy in the same UI corner as token count and latency. For Dan — environmental science background, philosophical commitment to local/sovereign compute — this isn't decoration; it's making the cost legible. The Google number (0.24 Wh per Gemini text prompt) gives a public reference point a Linus interface could compare against, similar to how a Tesla shows you Wh/mile against an EPA baseline.

**Direct, Phase 8 (Beyond MacBook):** When Linus eventually expands to a Mac Studio, possibly mobile, possibly a Vision Pro, the per-device energy footprint changes nontrivially. The methodology in this paper — measure the right boundary, normalize by useful work, track over time — is what lets that expansion be a sustainability decision rather than a hardware-acquisition reflex. The "Comprehensive Approach" framing is also useful in reverse: a Linus running on a single Mac Studio in Dan's house *should* report what its idle-when-not-in-use energy is, not just its active-inference energy, because that's the honest accounting if the machine sits powered for availability.

**Indirect, Phase 1:** The paper makes a quietly important point about *median-of-models-by-prompt-volume* as the right aggregate when distributions are skewed by occasional huge prompts. Any Linus benchmark suite that mixes short tool-use prompts with long literature-synthesis prompts will see the same skew. Reporting medians (and percentiles), not means, is a measurement-discipline lesson worth porting.

**Cross-cutting:** The reframing question this paper enables — *is local actually greener than hosted, when the boundary is drawn comprehensively?* — has a non-obvious answer. A Gemini prompt at 0.24 Wh is genuinely tiny; Apple Silicon is energy-efficient but not magic, and a laptop running Ollama also has its own idle and overhead components. The honest answer is "measure both," and this paper gives the recipe for one side of the comparison.

## What's NOT applicable
The Wh/prompt numbers themselves are not directly portable. Google is measuring in-fleet utilization on TPUs at production batch sizes with co-designed XLA/Pallas/Pathways software, against Gemini Flash/Flash-Lite distilled MoE models. None of those preconditions hold for a Qwen-7B running on Ollama on Dan's laptop at batch size 1. Google's number is *small* in part because batching at scale spreads fixed accelerator-on costs across thousands of concurrent prompts; a single-user laptop has no such amortization. So the comparison "Google = 0.24 Wh, my M1 = X Wh" tells us about the per-user efficiency penalty of sovereignty more than about whose engineering is better.

The PUE discussion (Google's 1.09 is industry-leading), the clean-energy procurement story, the Scope 2 market-based vs. location-based debate, the data-center water-stewardship framework, and the embodied-emissions amortization across machine-hours — none of these have direct Linus operational decisions attached. They're context, not actions. The fleet-wide idle-capacity discussion is similarly inapplicable: Linus does not provision for failover.

The 33x and 44x year-over-year reductions are Google's, not anyone else's, and reflect a year in which Gemini went through a major model-architecture and serving-stack overhaul. They are not a forecast for what any other system will achieve.

Conde et al. say tok/s misleads; Elsworth et al. say Wh-per-active-accelerator misleads. A Linus benchmark suite that takes both papers seriously would report wall-clock task-completion time *and* full-stack energy per task, not tok/s or active-GPU-Wh.

Phase 4's data sovereignty story (Kiwix, ProtoMaps, versioned datasets) has a parallel here: *energy sovereignty* — knowing what your compute actually costs — is itself a sovereignty axis Linus could foreground.

## Open questions for Dan
1. **Should `benchmarks/dan_tasks/` add a Wh/prompt column?** Powermetrics-based per-prompt energy measurement on M1 Max is a small Worker-spec away. Adding it now (alongside latency, quality, task-completion-time from 2502.16721v1) costs nothing and accumulates a year-long efficiency trajectory the way Google's Figure 4 does. Would you like a Phase 1 spec for this?

2. **Linus carbon ledger.** Is there value in a per-project or per-session accumulated-energy log — `~/.linus/energy.jsonl` — that records Wh per prompt and lets you see, e.g., "this paper-synthesis run cost 12 Wh on the Mac plus 3 Wh of Maestro Claude API calls"? This would be the carbon-aware analog of an audit log. Phase 2 or Phase 5?

3. **Local-vs-hosted honesty.** Is it worth one focused experiment that measures the same task (say, a 50-prompt scientific Q&A workload) under (a) Linus on M1 with Ollama, (b) Linus on M1 with mlx-lm, (c) hosted Claude API, full-stack energy where measurable, and reports them side by side? This would give a defensible answer to the "is local actually greener" question rather than asserting it.

4. **Median vs. mean for skewed prompt distributions.** Google reports daily medians weighted by prompt volume because a small number of huge prompts dominates means. Linus' real workloads (mostly short tool calls, occasional long synthesis) will have the same shape. Is the right primary aggregate in `benchmarks/results/` the median per-task-class, with mean and p99 reported alongside?

5. **Energy as a router signal.** If the orchestration layer eventually knows the per-prompt energy cost of dispatching to each Worker (small fast model vs. large slow model), should energy enter the routing decision the way latency and quality already do? On a laptop with no PUE overhead this might be a marginal difference; on a future Mac Studio drawing real wall-power 24/7, it matters more.
