# An Introduction to Flow Matching and Diffusion Models

## TL;DR

This is an ~85-page graduate textbook (a self-contained companion to an MIT course, with lecture videos and labs at
diffusion.csail.mit.edu) covering the modern stack behind essentially every state-of-the-art generative image, video,
and scientific-structure model: flow models, diffusion models, flow matching, score matching, SDE sampling,
classifier-free guidance, latent diffusion, U-Nets and DiTs, the Stable Diffusion 3 / Movie Gen case studies, and
discrete diffusion via continuous-time Markov chains. **For Linus, this is a reference text, not an implementation
target.** Linus will almost certainly never train a diffusion model — but Dan's Wave-3 generative-biology papers (DISCO,
and behind it RFdiffusion / AlphaFold3 / Boltz / Chroma) are diffusion or flow-matching models, and to read them
critically Dan needs a place to look up vector fields, score functions, conditional vs marginal probability paths, and
CFG without re-deriving them from blog posts. This note is a chapter map plus an honest statement of when reaching for
the book is worth the cost.

## The problem (in plain language)

Modern generative models — for images (Stable Diffusion, FLUX, Nano Banana), video (VEO-3, Sora, Movie Gen), and
increasingly molecular structure (AlphaFold3, RFdiffusion) — share a common mathematical core: they generate by
iteratively converting noise into data along a trajectory governed by an ODE or SDE, with a neural network predicting
the velocity (vector field) or the score (gradient of the log density) at each step. Two
equivalent-but-differently-presented frameworks dominate the literature: classical denoising diffusion
(Ho/Sohl-Dickstein/Song lineage, often discrete-time, often presented via "forward processes" and time-reversal) and
flow matching (Lipman et al., continuous-time, presented via probability paths and conditional vector fields). Reading
any of these papers cold means navigating a thicket of overlapping notation: probability paths, forward processes, score
functions, vector fields, ELBOs, marginal vs conditional formulations, classifier-free guidance, EDM-style
preconditioning. The textbooks of the previous generation (Bishop, Murphy) predate all of this.

For Dan: when a Wave-3 protein-design paper says "we train a flow-matching model on the SE(3) manifold of protein
backbones with classifier-free guidance on the sequence prompt," he needs a single coherent source where each of those
terms is defined the same way. This book is that source.

## What's in the book (chapter map)

The structure tracks the MIT course directly, and is the main reason to keep this PDF locally.

- **§1 Introduction / Generative Modeling as Sampling.** Frames generation as sampling from `p_data`, with images,
  videos, and molecules (`R^{3xN}` atom coordinates) as the running data types. Sets up guided/conditional generation as
  sampling from `p_data(·|y)`. Short, plain-language; safe to skim.
- **§2 Flow and Diffusion Models.** ODE basics (vector fields, flows, Euler / Heun simulation), SDE basics (Brownian
  motion, the Ito update form, diffusion coefficient `σ_t`). Defines a "flow model" as a neural-network vector field
  `u^θ_t` with a random initial condition `X_0 ~ p_init`. This is the chapter to read first if differential-equations
  notation is rusty.
- **§3 Flow Matching.** The core algorithm: conditional vs marginal probability paths, conditional vs marginal vector
  fields, the marginal-vector-field training objective. This is the chapter Dan would actually open when reading a
  flow-matching protein paper.
- **§4 Score Functions and Score Matching.** The diffusion-model dual to §3. Conditional/marginal score functions, SDE
  sampling (probability flow ODE vs Langevin/reverse-time SDE), denoising score matching. Includes the Fokker-Planck
  connection (proven in Appendix B).
- **§5 Guidance.** Vanilla guidance and classifier-free guidance (CFG). Short chapter, but CFG is _the_ mechanism by
  which "prompt this with a string" works in every text-to-image and text-to-protein system. Worth knowing exists.
- **§6 Building Large-Scale Image/Video Generators.** The applied chapter. Neural-network architectures (U-Nets, DiTs,
  Fourier features, FiLM conditioning), latent-space modeling via VAEs, then case studies of Stable Diffusion 3 and Meta
  Movie Gen. Reasonably self-contained; useful as a "what does the production stack actually look like" reference.
- **§7 Discrete Diffusion / Language Models with Diffusion.** Continuous-time Markov chains, masked-token diffusion
  (LLaDA-style). Optional in the course; relevant if Dan tracks the diffusion-LM thread (LLaDA 2.0 is in the
  references).
- **Appendices.** A: probability theory refresher. B: full Fokker-Planck proof. C: existence/uniqueness for CTMCs. D:
  extra VAE perspectives, ELBO, the rate-distortion / reconstruction-vs-generation tradeoff. **E: A Guide to the
  Diffusion Model Literature** — a Rosetta-stone explaining why different papers use different but equivalent
  formulations (discrete-time vs continuous-time, forward-process vs probability-path, time-reversal vs Fokker-Planck,
  flow matching vs stochastic interpolants). For Dan, **§E is the highest-leverage section in the entire book** for
  reading other people's papers.

## Key results

This is a textbook, so "results" means what it teaches you to do, not novel claims:

- A unified continuous-time framework where flow matching and diffusion models are two views of the same underlying
  construction (probability paths + vector fields, with score functions a derived quantity).
- A clean derivation of the marginal flow-matching objective from conditional probability paths via the Fokker-Planck /
  continuity equation, avoiding the historical detour through forward processes and time-reversal.
- A working understanding of classifier-free guidance as the dominant prompt-conditioning mechanism, including why
  guidance scale > 1 trades diversity for fidelity.
- The latent-diffusion recipe (encode with a VAE, run the diffusion in latent space, decode) as the engineering pattern
  that made Stable Diffusion practical, with the rate-distortion tradeoff (Appendix D) as the principled framing of "how
  much should the autoencoder do vs the latent generator."
- A bridge to discrete-state generation (CTMCs) showing that the same training principles extend to language and other
  discrete-state-space data — the Generator Matching framework as the most general statement.

The book also explicitly disclaims being a research contribution: it's a teaching synthesis, with the author's own
research (Generator Matching, GLASS Flows) cited as further reading rather than presented in detail.

## What's reusable in Linus

**Reference, Wave-3 (Generative Biology) reading.** When Wave-3 lands the DISCO paper (2604.05181) and any AlphaFold3 /
RFdiffusion / Boltz / Chroma follow-ups, this book is the lookup table. §3 (flow matching), §4 (score matching), and §5
(CFG) cover the math those papers will assume. **Read §E first** — it tells Dan how to translate the paper's notation
into the book's notation, which is most of the cost of reading a diffusion paper from a different lineage.

**Reference, Phase 5/6 (Interface / Fine-Tuning), if Linus ever generates anything.** If Linus eventually adds an
image-generation tool (e.g., FLUX or SD3 served locally on MLX) for figure drafting, slides, or scientific
visualization, §6 is the orientation: what the components are, what U-Net vs DiT means architecturally, what CFG scale
does to outputs. Linus would _use_ a pretrained model from MLX-community or HuggingFace; it would not train one. The
reference value is in being able to debug knobs (sampler steps, CFG scale, scheduler) when the output looks wrong.

**Reference, Phase 3 (Knowledge & Parallel Agents).** If Linus' KnowledgeBase eventually ingests biophysics / structural
biology corpora, the embedding and tagging pipeline benefits from understanding which papers are diffusion-based vs
autoregressive vs equivariant-network-based. §1 and §6 give the vocabulary to tag papers correctly, even without deep
reading.

**Indirect, methodology.** The "everything is a Markov process with a generator" framing in §7 / Generator Matching is
conceptually nice and worth a single read for taste reasons — it's an example of the kind of unification that good
textbooks do, and the same instinct (find the smaller unifying primitive) is a Linus design principle.

## What's NOT applicable

**Linus will not train a diffusion model.** 32 GB unified memory and Apple Silicon are sufficient for inference of
mid-sized diffusion models (SD3 medium, FLUX-dev quantized) but training even a small image diffusion model from scratch
requires order-of-magnitude more compute than the M1 Max provides. The labs at diffusion.csail.mit.edu are educational,
not Worker recipes. If Dan ever wants to actually train one, that's a Phase 8+ "beyond MacBook" question, not something
this book unlocks today.

**No retrieval, no agents, no orchestration content.** This is a generative-modeling textbook. It says nothing about
RAG, knowledge graphs, agentic loops, tool use, MoE, KV caching, quantization, or any of the inference-side concerns
that dominate Linus' Phase 1-2 work. It is a _parallel_ technical universe to the Transformer/attention thread, not a
continuation of it.

**Inference engineering stops at "use a sampler."** The book covers Euler / Heun / DDIM-style samplers conceptually but
not the engineering of fast SDXL/FLUX inference on Metal. For that, MLX example repos and Diffusers documentation are
the right sources.

**No protein-specific math.** The molecular-structure example in §1 is one paragraph. SE(3)-equivariance, frame-averaged
flows, Riemannian flow matching on manifolds (Chen & Lipman 2023, cited in the references) are _pointed at_ but not
developed. For protein-design papers Dan will still need supplementary reading on equivariant networks. This book is a
prerequisite, not a complete preparation.

**Honesty about depth.** This is graduate-textbook material. The note in front of you is a map; it cannot replace ~30-50
hours of working through the chapters when Dan actually needs to derive or modify something. The right way to use the
book is to skim §1-§2 once for orientation, read §E once for notation translation, then open §3 / §4 / §5 in a targeted
way the first time a Wave-3 paper forces the issue.

## Connections

**Companion to the Transformer paper
([NIPS-2017-attention-is-all-you-need](NIPS-2017-attention-is-all-you-need-Paper.md)).** Together these are the two
foundational-reference anchors of this wave. Transformer = the architecture at the core of every modern model (including
the DiT backbone of SD3 and the score networks in many diffusion models). This book = the _training and sampling
framework_ that turns a transformer into a generator of continuous data. Knowing both is the minimum to read modern
generative ML.

**Wave-3 Group B (Generative Biology) prerequisite.** This is the math-prereq companion for the DISCO paper (2604.05181)
and any RFdiffusion / AlphaFold3 / Boltz papers Dan adds. The reading order is: this book's §1-§3 + §E first, then the
protein paper, with §4-§5 consulted as needed.

**Reference-text peer to the Hogan KG survey ([2003.02320v6](2003.02320v6.md)).** Same usage pattern: long,
comprehensive, kept locally because the chapter index is what's valuable. Both are "open this when a downstream paper
assumes the framework."

**Tangential to KnowledgeBase work ([2408.08073v2](2408.08073v2.md)).** Diffusion-based document or paper tagging is not
currently a Linus need — embedding-based retrieval covers that. But if scientific image embedding ever becomes a KB
requirement (e.g., for figure search), the latent-diffusion encoder (§6, Appendix D) is the natural feature extractor.

**Orthogonal to the Linus inference / orchestration thread.** Nothing in this book speaks to Ollama, MLX-LM, BitNet,
mlx-flash, the speed-vs-tokens-per-second papers ([2502.16721v1](2502.16721v1.md)), or autoresearch / agentic loops.
This is a clean separation: Linus' core stack is autoregressive-LLM and orchestration; this book covers an adjacent
generative-modeling stack that Linus consumes but does not produce.
