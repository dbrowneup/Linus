# DeepSeMS: a large language model reveals hidden biosynthetic potential of the global ocean microbiome

## TL;DR

DeepSeMS is a roughly 100M-parameter Transformer that reads a biosynthetic gene cluster (BGC) and writes the SMILES
string of the secondary metabolite the cluster is likely to produce. The architectural moves are two: (i) represent the
BGC not as raw amino acids and not as whole-enzyme tokens but as an ordered sequence of **functional-domain Pfam
identifiers** detected by HMMER, which gives a tractable 250-token input with strong signal; (ii) augment the tiny MIBiG
training set (3,029 paired BGC-SM examples) by **scaffold-aligned SMILES enumeration** — generate many syntactically
different SMILES strings of the same molecule that all keep the Murcko scaffold's atom indices fixed, blowing the
training set up to 55,903 examples without losing the structural prior. On a held-out set of 326 known BGCs, DeepSeMS
hits Tanimoto 0.60 vs. ground-truth metabolites versus 0.45 for PRISM 4 and 0.14 for antiSMASH 7, and recovers 41% of
structures exactly versus 8% for PRISM. On 940 cryptic BGCs (no known product) it produces a chemically valid candidate
for 96.6% of them versus < 50% for either rule-based tool. The authors then crank the model on 27,139 ocean MAGs from
the Ocean Microbiomics Database, predict structures for 46,786 BGCs, deduplicate to **65,868 unique novel metabolites**,
and screen 8,783 of them as antibiotic candidates. Code and weights at github.com/lab-of-biochemai/deepsems; web server
at biochemai.cstspace.cn/deepsems. Trained on up to eight RTX 4090s in PyTorch.

## The problem (in plain language)

Microbes make small molecules that have given us most of our antibiotics, statins, and many anticancer drugs. The genes
that build those molecules cluster together on the chromosome in BGCs — biosynthetic gene clusters — and the dream of
"genome mining" since at least the 2010s has been: sequence a genome, find the BGCs, predict what each one makes, and
then go test the predicted molecules. The dominant tools, antiSMASH and PRISM, do this with hand-encoded biochemistry
rules: "this assembly-line module looks like a polyketide synthase loading domain that takes acetyl-CoA, that one
extends with malonyl-CoA, this one tailors with a hydroxyl, so the product looks like X." The rules work for canonical
NRPS and PKS pathways where the chemistry is well-understood, but they collapse on **cryptic BGCs** — clusters from
uncultured organisms in metagenome-assembled genomes (MAGs) where the enzymes do not match canonical templates closely
enough for the rules to fire.

That collapse matters because uncultured organisms are most of the microbial diversity on Earth, and the ocean is the
largest biome of all. Phylum-level biosynthetic novelty is sitting in metagenomic assemblies that the rule-based tools
can predict little or nothing useful for. The same problem appears for any context-dependent enzyme family — cytochrome
P450s being the canonical example: a single P450 can hydroxylate carbon, oxygenate heteroatoms, dealkylate, epoxidize,
reduce, dehalogenate, depending entirely on substrate context. Substrate-specific libraries cannot enumerate what
context-dependent enzymes do in unseen combinations.

So the question is whether you can learn the BGC → metabolite mapping from data instead of writing more rules — and
whether the few thousand experimentally verified pairs in MIBiG are enough training signal to do it.

## What they propose

Treat the problem as sequence-to-sequence translation, the way neural machine translation treats English-to-Chinese. The
source language is BGC structure; the target language is SMILES. Transformer encoder-decoder, six encoder layers, six
decoder layers, eight attention heads, embedding dimension 512, ~100M parameters. Standard PyTorch, AdamW,
cross-entropy, dropout 0.1, learning rate 1e-4, batch 64, early stopping on validation.

The two non-obvious choices are the input representation and the data augmentation — both of which are dictated by the
small dataset.

For input representation, the authors compared three granularities: amino acid sequences (too long, often >50,000
tokens, exceeds model capacity), enzyme-level tokens (too compressed, ~50 tokens, too lossy to converge), and
**functional-domain Pfam IDs from HMMER searches against Pfam 36.0** (~250 tokens, vocabulary of 1,020 distinct Pfam IDs
across the dataset). Functional domains are the unit at which biosynthetic logic actually lives — substrate specificity,
chain extension, tailoring — so the representation matches the biology. This is exactly the level Hannigan et al. 2019
and Skinnider et al. 2020 also identified as informative. The vocabulary on the output side is small: just 35 distinct
SMILES tokens after canonicalization with RDKit (stereochemistry stripped to keep generation tractable).

For data augmentation, the trick is **scaffold-aligned SMILES enumeration**. A molecule has many valid SMILES strings
depending on which atom you start from and which traversal you pick. Naively randomizing the traversal (Arús-Pous 2019)
helps the model learn SMILES grammar but scrambles structural features. Instead the authors decompose each training
molecule into its Murcko scaffold and its substituents, randomize only the substituent atom indices while keeping the
scaffold's atom indices fixed, then re-emit a new SMILES string. The result is up to 100 syntactically different SMILES
per molecule, all of which expose the scaffold (the part that matters for biological activity) in aligned token
positions. This boosts the dataset from 3,029 to 55,903 paired examples and, per Supplementary Table 1, materially
improves both validity of generated SMILES and structural similarity to ground truth versus naive randomization.

Inference uses standard greedy/length-penalty decoding (length penalty 0.6) with a top-10 ensemble across the ten
cross-validation folds, so each input BGC produces up to ten candidate metabolites.

## Key results

**Accuracy on 326 known BGCs (Table 1).** Success rate (at least one chemically valid prediction): DeepSeMS 97.55%,
PRISM 4 88.96%, antiSMASH 7 63.50%. Mean Tanimoto similarity to ground truth: 0.60 / 0.45 / 0.14. Exact structure
recovery: 41.10% / 8.11% / 0.00%. Exact scaffold recovery: 53.68% / 16.87% / 1.93%. The Tanimoto 0.60 number is the
headline.

**Generalization on 940 cryptic deep-ocean BGCs (Fig. 2).** DeepSeMS predicts a chemically valid SM for 908 (96.60%) of
the 940 BGCs, generating 5,104 valid structures with 78.66% uniqueness. PRISM 4 predicts 455 structures for 203 BGCs
(46.45% success, 62.42% unique). antiSMASH 7 predicts 189 structures for 159 BGCs (16.91% success, 24.87% unique).
Predicted molecular weights cluster in the natural-product-typical 300–500 Da range; QED and synthetic-accessibility
distributions look biologically plausible. DeepSeMS produces valid structures for 38 of 39 BGC type categories (NRPS,
PKS, terpenes, RiPPs, hybrids, and clusters not fitting any documented category).

**Ocean microbiome mining (Fig. 3, Section 3).** 27,139 MAGs from the Ocean Microbiomics Database → 46,786 BGCs → 65,868
unique predicted metabolites. 65,735 (97%) are structurally novel relative to MIBiG; 69% have novel scaffolds; 58% have
novel Murcko shapes. The Arctic Ocean contributes the most metabolites (22,426); the Southern Ocean has the highest
within-province diversity. Abyssopelagic, low-oxygen, and 5–15 °C environments produce the highest molecular novelty.

**Antibiotic screening (Fig. 4).** Substructure-based virtual screening for eight antibiotic functional-group classes
(β-lactams, aminoglycosides, tetracyclines, oxazolidinones, chloramphenicols, macrolides, ansamycins, quinolones) on the
65,868 predictions yields **8,783 unique antibiotic candidates** with novel side chains. The ectoine-pathway sub-mining
yields 2,078 novel cell-protectant candidates. A specific MAG, UBA7446 sp002478685 from a North Atlantic sample, is
highlighted because four of the top ten globally most-novel structures all come from one of its BGCs.

**Limitations the authors flag.** Pfam coverage is incomplete, so domains can be missed and the resulting SMILES can be
fragmentary. BGC border definition in prokaryotic genomes is unreliable, so for unidentified BGC types the predicted
scaffold may not be the true scaffold. All predictions are computational hypotheses requiring wet-lab validation.

## What's reusable in Linus

**Phase 7 skill candidate, directly.** "Predict secondary-metabolite structure from a BGC sequence" is a clean,
well-bounded tool: input is a GenBank-format BGC or an antiSMASH job ID, output is a ranked list of SMILES with
prediction scores and substructure-based annotations. The web server already exposes this via HTTP, so a Linus tool
wrapper is one Python file. For Dan's natural-products and antibiotic-discovery interests this is a high-leverage
capability that no general LLM has — it is exactly the kind of domain-specific power tool the Phase 7 skill catalog is
meant to host.

**KnowledgeBase content, directly.** The 65,868-metabolite ocean catalog is structured KG-friendly content: each
predicted metabolite carries provenance (source MAG, ocean province, depth, temperature, oxygen), structural annotations
(scaffold, shape, weight, QED, synthetic accessibility, novelty score), and biosynthetic context (Pfam domain string,
BGC type). Mirroring this into KnowledgeBase as a triple store gives Linus a queryable substrate for natural-product
questions: "show me ocean-microbiome ectoine analogs from low-oxygen abyssopelagic samples with QED > 0.6." That query
pattern does not exist anywhere in Dan's current corpus.

**Architectural reference for sequence-to-sequence biology→chemistry tasks.** The Pfam-domain-tokens /
scaffold-aligned-SMILES-augmentation recipe is reusable beyond DeepSeMS. Any task where a small protein-context
vocabulary maps to a small chemical vocabulary (e.g. enzyme → reaction product, BGC → structural class, modification
site → product family) can use the same pattern. Worth reading once, transcribing the recipe into a docs/specs note,
then forgetting the specific paper.

**Phase 4 (Data Sovereignty) candidate.** The 65k ocean-metabolite catalog is downloadable from the web server. Pairing
a Linus mirror of this catalog with mirrors of MIBiG 3.x and an offline antiSMASH database would give Linus a
self-contained natural-product reference layer — useful both as KB content and as evaluation substrate for any future
domain models.

**Cross-paper architectural lever.** Functional-domain tokenization is a much smaller, much more targeted representation
than the giant raw-sequence representations used by [LucaOne](2024.05.10.593587v1.md) or ESM-style models. There is an
obvious experiment hiding here: replace the Pfam-ID one-hot input with **LucaOne or ESM embeddings of the Pfam-domain
protein regions**, see whether that buys accuracy on the cryptic BGCs without inflating model size catastrophically.
This is a Phase 6 / Phase 7 fine-tuning experiment Dan could spec for a future Worker.

## What's NOT applicable

The 100M-parameter Transformer trains on up to eight NVIDIA RTX 4090s in PyTorch — CUDA-native, not
Apple-Silicon-native. Training reproduction on the M1 Max is not realistic without a port to MLX, and even then 100M
params with 55k augmented training pairs is an overnight job at best. Inference on a 100M Transformer is fine on the M1
(well within unified memory budget, well within MLX or even pure PyTorch CPU), but reproducing the training to swap out
the input representation requires either a CUDA box or an MLX rewrite. This puts the model in the same Group B "use the
artifact, do not retrain" bucket as several recent additions to the corpus.

The web server is the path of least resistance for using DeepSeMS today: HTTP API, no login, accepts antiSMASH job IDs.
But it is hosted on Chinese infrastructure (cstspace.cn) and is therefore not local, not private, and not under Dan's
control — exactly the things Linus exists to fix. For sensitive use cases the local inference path (load weights from
GitHub, run via PyTorch on the M1) is the right answer.

The paper says nothing about uncertainty calibration of the predicted structures beyond the length-penalty prediction
score, and no wet-lab validation is reported — every "antibiotic candidate" is a computational hypothesis. For Dan's
downstream use the candidates are discovery starting points, never ground truth.

The model strips stereochemistry during canonicalization, which for natural-product chemistry is a nontrivial loss —
stereochemistry often determines bioactivity. Output SMILES need to be treated as constitutional formulas only.

## Connections

**Closest spirit-neighbor: [REMME / REBEAN (gkaf836.md)](gkaf836.md).** Both are LLMs applied to metagenomic function
inference. REBEAN annotates EC classes from raw reads; DeepSeMS predicts metabolite structures from assembled BGCs.
Wired together they sketch a metagenomic discovery pipeline: REBEAN tells you which functional categories are enriched
in a sample, DeepSeMS tells you what specific molecules the BGCs in that sample are likely producing. Both papers also
share the shape of the contribution — domain-specialized small Transformer that beats hand-encoded baselines on the
hardest cases.

**Group B siblings.** [GenNA (2026.04.22.720063v1.md)](2026.04.22.720063v1.md) is the obvious mirror image — GenNA
generates nucleotide sequences from natural-language prompts; DeepSeMS generates molecule structures from biosynthetic
genomic input. Different generation modalities (one writes DNA, one writes SMILES), same architectural pattern
(encoder-decoder Transformer with a small target vocabulary, trained on a curated paired dataset, augmented heavily).
[mCSM-metal (1-s2.0-S0022283626000513-main.md)](1-s2.0-S0022283626000513-main.md) shares the "small task-specific
Transformer / GNN trained on a curated MIBiG-scale dataset" template. The Trias codon and generative-phages papers in
this same wave round out a coherent Group B genre: small, domain-specific, supervised generators built on top of curated
biology databases.

**Group A foundation models.** DeepSeMS does not use any. The input is one-hot Pfam IDs, not learned protein embeddings.
The natural successor experiment is to substitute embeddings from
[LucaOne (2024.05.10.593587v1.md)](2024.05.10.593587v1.md), [Evo 2](2025.02.18.638918v1.md), or a protein FM (ESM,
ProteinReasoner) for the per-domain protein regions. This is exactly the architecture-modernization play the Group A /
Group B distinction is supposed to surface — and DeepSeMS is one of the cleaner candidates because the Pfam-domain
tokenization makes the substitution surgical.

**Engineering pattern.** The substructure-based virtual screening step (RDKit filters SMILES for β-lactam,
aminoglycoside, etc.) is a clean orchestration shape: the worker model proposes, a deterministic chemistry library
disposes. Worth keeping in mind when designing the Phase 7 skill harness.
