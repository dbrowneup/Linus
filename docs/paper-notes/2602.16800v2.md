# Large-scale online deanonymization with LLMs

## TL;DR

Pseudonymous online accounts have historically been protected not by cryptography but by **practical obscurity**:
deanonymization was theoretically possible but too expensive to do at scale. This paper shows that frontier LLMs
collapse that cost by orders of magnitude. A four-stage pipeline — Extract identity-relevant features from unstructured
text, Search candidate profiles via embedding similarity, Reason over the top candidates with a strong model, Calibrate
confidence — is evaluated on three ground-truth datasets: 338 Hacker News users with linked LinkedIn profiles, 9,781
Reddit users split across movie subreddits, and 10,000 Reddit users split temporally. End-to-end agentic deanonymization
on the Anthropic Interviewer transcripts recovers 9 of 33 scientists at 82% precision from a single interview and public
web search alone. Headlines: **67% recall at 90% precision linking HN to LinkedIn**; **45.1% recall at 99% precision**
with reasoning, where the classical Netflix-Prize-style baseline gets **0.1%**; attacks degrade only log-linearly with
candidate pool size and remain effective even when the prior probability of any match is 1-in-10,000. The conclusion:
"the practical obscurity that has long protected pseudonymous users no longer holds," and the capability is available to
any moderately resourced adversary using public APIs.

## The problem (in plain language)

Privacy on the open internet has rested on a quiet asymmetry. The signals that identify a person — writing style,
recurring interests, demographic tells, incidental disclosures, the particular topics they get angry about — are smeared
across thousands of posts in unstructured text. A skilled human investigator can extract those signals and find a match,
but it takes hours per target, which keeps the practice rare and reserved for high-value cases. Classical algorithmic
deanonymization (Narayanan–Shmatikov's Netflix Prize attack, group-membership attacks) requires _structured_ features
and breaks down on raw prose.

LLMs erase that asymmetry on both sides. They are excellent at extracting structured attributes from unstructured posts,
at semantic search over millions of candidate profiles via embeddings, and — with enough reasoning effort — at looking
at a query profile and a candidate shortlist and answering "is this the same person?" with calibrated confidence. Cost
per query is single-digit dollars on hosted APIs. The previously expensive part of deanonymization — being the human
investigator — is now a function call. The paper's contribution is to make this concrete and quantify how much cheaper
deanonymization has become. The implication is not a new attack vector but the industrialization of a capability that
used to be artisanal.

## What they propose

The framework, **ESRC** (Extract / Search / Reason / Calibrate), augments the Narayanan–Shmatikov pipeline:

- **Extract.** An LLM converts raw posts, comments, and bios into a semi-structured profile of identity-relevant
  features: demographics, career, interests, writing-style markers, incidental disclosures. For movie matching they
  extract `(title, inferred rating, timestamp)` tuples; for HN, biographical sketches.
- **Search.** Each profile is embedded (Gemini embeddings or `text-embedding-3-large`) and indexed in FAISS; the top-100
  candidates by cosine similarity are pulled forward. This narrows from millions to dozens cheaply.
- **Reason.** Two-stage: a fast model (Grok 4.1 Fast or Gemini 3 Pro) picks the most plausible candidate or abstains; a
  stronger model (GPT-5.2 with high reasoning effort) verifies the pair against full profile text and produces a
  structured confidence score. This step closes the gap between "narrow shortlist" and "high-precision named match."
- **Calibrate.** Threshold the verifier's confidence, or run a Swiss-system Bradley–Terry tournament where the LLM
  judges which of two pairs is the more plausible match; the resulting ratings give a precision-recall curve usable when
  individual confidences are unreliable.

A separate **agentic** variant hands a frontier LLM agent a sanitized profile and a web-search tool and asks it to find
the real person — no embedding index, just autonomous web search. This is what they run on the Anthropic Interviewer
transcripts.

For ground truth (normally impossible because anonymous accounts are by definition unlabeled), two tricks: (a) collect
users who voluntarily link their pseudonymous account to a real identity, sanitize the explicit links, and ask the
attack to recover them; (b) split a single user's history by community or by time to create two pseudonymous profiles
with a known shared author.

## Key results

- **HN → LinkedIn matching, 987 queries against ~89,000 candidates.** The Netflix-Prize-style baseline (binary feature
  vectors over 332 attributes like programming languages, employers, schools) achieves **0.1% recall at 90% precision**.
  Embedding-only Search achieves 26.3%. Adding the Reason step with GPT-5.2 high reasoning lifts this to **54.2% recall
  at 90% precision and 45.1% at 99% precision**.
- **Agentic web search on the same domain.** Given an anonymized HN profile, a frontier agent identifies the real
  LinkedIn-linked person for **226 of 338 (67%) at 90% precision**. Smaller Reddit subsets give 25–52% recall at 72–90%
  precision.
- **Anthropic Interviewer dataset.** Agentic deanonymization correctly identifies **9 of 33 scientists at 82%
  precision** from a single AI-conducted interview transcript, beating prior work (Li, 6 of 24) by ~50% without using
  task-decomposition jailbreaks.
- **Reddit cross-community matching, 9,781 users.** Movie discussion split between r/movies and niche subreddits
  (r/horror, r/Letterboxd, r/TrueFilm, etc.). The classical baseline gets effectively 0%. The LLM pipeline with high
  reasoning achieves **8.5% recall at 90% precision and 2.8% at 99%** — modest in absolute terms, but identifying ~280
  strangers correctly out of 10k profiles based only on their movie sentiment is qualitatively new. Recall climbs to
  **48.1% for users sharing 10+ movie titles** — more disclosure, more identifiability.
- **Reddit temporal split, 10,000 profiles separated by a one-year gap.** With Search + Reason + Calibrate (tournament),
  recall stays non-trivial at all precision thresholds and substantially exceeds Search-only.
- **Scaling.** Both Search and Reason degrade only log-linearly with candidate pool size. Reason loses just 13
  percentage points of recall going from 1k to 89k candidates at 90% precision. Log-linear extrapolation predicts
  roughly **45% recall at 90% precision against 1M candidates and ~27% against 100M**.
- **Sparse-match regime.** Even when only 1 in 10,000 query users has any true match in the candidate pool, the LLM
  attack achieves ~9% recall at 90% precision. Translation: an attacker scanning a population where almost no one is
  identifiable still finds and confidently outs a meaningful fraction of those who are.
- **Reasoning effort matters.** High vs. low reasoning effort doubles recall at 99% precision in the movie setting (2.8%
  vs. 1.4%) and produces a 9-point gain in HN (45.1% vs. 36.0%). Test-time compute is a privacy attack lever.
- **The signals are semantic, not just stylometric.** The attack exploits _what_ people say (interests, incidental
  disclosures, topical fingerprints) more than _how_ they say it. This is a different threat model from classical
  authorship attribution and is harder to defend against because the content is the value.

The authors are pessimistic about defenses: simple PII redaction leaves residual semantic signal; LLM provider
safeguards struggle because each pipeline stage looks like benign summarization, embedding, or comparison; rate-limiting
bulk data access is the most plausible mitigation but only friction, not prevention.

## What's reusable in Linus

**The most important reusable thing is the threat model itself.** This paper is the strongest empirical case yet for
Linus's local-first ethos. Hosted LLMs do not just _store_ the data you send them; they actively _reason_ over it in
ways that can re-identify the human behind a stream of content. A local Worker on Dan's M1 Max literally cannot do this
— there is no third party to leak to. The privacy benefit of local inference, historically argued in terms of compliance
or sovereignty, gets a sharper reformulation: **local inference is the only architecture in which content cannot become
identity signal for someone else's pipeline.** This reframing belongs in [VISION.md](../../VISION.md) and
[SAFETY.md](../../SAFETY.md).

**Direct, SAFETY.md, near-term:** Add a "stylometric and identity leakage to hosted models" section. SAFETY.md covers
sandbox policy and forbidden ops; it does not currently cover what content is allowed to leave the machine. Any content
sent to hosted Claude (Maestro) accumulates as identity signal at Anthropic, and a third-party adversary with the same
tooling could in principle do the same correlation against any other corpus that includes Dan's writing. The right
policy is probably not "never use hosted Maestro" — that defeats the project — but a tiering: scientific code excerpts
and abstract architecture discussion are low-risk, while pasting personal notes, KB content with Dan's voice, or
anything overlapping with public pseudonymous output is higher-risk and should be conscious.

**Direct, orchestration layer, Phase 2:** The audit log should record content that crosses the local/remote boundary
when Linus proxies to hosted models. Provenance fields per request: `destination` (local-worker | hosted-model),
`content-classes` (code | kb-snippet | personal-note | tool-arg), `bytes-sent`. The paper's pipeline shows that the
_aggregate_ of small disclosures is what kills you; an audit log that lets Dan grep "what did I send to hosted models
last month" is a precondition for ever knowing.

**Direct, KnowledgeBase boundary:** A future Linus-KB integration that retrieves documents and dumps them into a
hosted-Claude prompt would be the worst-case version of this threat model — Dan's curated corpus, including his own
annotations and notes, becoming context for an external model. Default policy: **KB content is local-Worker-only**
unless explicitly overridden per-request. The router needs a `privacy_tier: local-only | hosted-allowed | hosted-warn`
flag per tool/skill, and KB retrieval should default to `local-only` for personal notes or unpublished writing.

**Direct, Phase 5 (Interface refinement):** When Linus is exposed via VS Code, openclaw, or a future native UI, the
front-end needs to surface _where_ a request is going to be served. A simple cue ("this will go to hosted Claude" vs.
"this will run on a local Worker") before send/paste — similar to browsers warning before submitting a form over HTTP.
Cheap UX, expensive to retrofit.

**Direct, Phase 7 (Skills):** Each skill that wraps an external API leaks metadata. A `privacy_tier` annotation per
skill, with a router policy that refuses to chain a hosted-allowed skill onto local-only KB content without explicit
consent, makes the threat model enforceable rather than aspirational.

**Indirect:** The Extract / Search / Reason / Calibrate decomposition is a clean general-purpose pattern for any
retrieval-then-verification problem. KB retrieval, citation matching, paper deduplication, agentic-search — all can
borrow the four-stage shape: cheap embedding shortlist, expensive reasoning verification, calibrated confidence.

## What's NOT applicable

The paper attacks public-internet pseudonymous accounts, not local data. Linus is on the defensive side here, not the
offensive side; the pipeline itself is something Linus should _not_ build. The agentic web-search variant requires open
web access plus identity-database access, neither of which is in Linus's intended capability set.

The specific numbers (67% / 45.1%) are bound to current hosted frontier models (GPT-5.2 high reasoning, Gemini 3 Pro).
Local 7–14B Workers on Dan's M1 Max would almost certainly be much weaker at the Reason step today, but the gap will
close; the threat model should be specified against capability, not against any current model.

The Calibrate stage's Swiss-system tournament is well-suited to large-scale attacks but probably overkill for any benign
Linus retrieval task.

The paper provides limited defensive recommendations — it documents that PII redaction leaves residual signal and
provider-side detection is hard, but proposes no working mitigation. Linus's defensive design has to be derived from the
threat model, not lifted from the paper.

## Connections

This is the **privacy** axis of Group F (safety / alignment / privacy), orthogonal to the other Group F papers:

- The **dual-use biotech paper** ([2306.03809v1.md](2306.03809v1.md)) is about _misuse_ — LLMs lowering the barrier to
  harm. Defense is capability and access control on the model.
- **Steering and monitoring** (Beaglehole) and **Values in the Wild** (Anthropic) are about _control and alignment_ —
  making the model itself do the intended thing. Defense lives inside the model.
- This paper is about _privacy_ — third parties using benign LLM capabilities to extract identity from content the user
  already published. Defense is architectural: keep the content local, or reduce what gets sent.

Three orthogonal failure modes, three mitigation strategies, all under one SAFETY.md. The structure of that doc should
reflect this trichotomy.

The paper extends [security-synthesis.md](../syntheses/security-synthesis.md), which covers supply-chain attacks and
prompt injection. This adds a third leg: **stylometric and semantic identity leakage** — no compromise or injection
required, just capable LLMs operating on content the user voluntarily produced. Worth folding in as a third top-level
section.

It also resonates with [memory-synthesis.md](../syntheses/memory-synthesis.md): episodic and semantic-knowledge memory
accumulate exactly the personal signal this paper exploits. Linus's memory pillars are private by virtue of being local;
if any sync, mirror, or leak through hosted-model context, this threat model applies in full.

On technique, the Extract/Search/Reason/Calibrate decomposition shares structure with retrieval-then-verification
patterns in [agentic-systems-synthesis.md](../syntheses/agentic-systems-synthesis.md) — reusable in benign settings like
KB-citation matching.
