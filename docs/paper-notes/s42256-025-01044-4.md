# Generalized biological foundation model with unified nucleic acid and protein language (LucaOne)

## TL;DR

LucaOne is a 1.8-billion-parameter transformer encoder pre-trained jointly on DNA, RNA, and protein sequences spanning
**169,861 species**, using two self-supervised mask objectives plus eight semi-supervised annotation tasks (genome
region type, taxonomy, protein keyword, domain, site, structure, etc.). A 39-token vocabulary unifies nucleotides and
amino acids, with a token-type embedding flagging which is which. The headline scientific claim is that **the central
dogma emerges from joint training** without any explicit DNA-protein pairing in the loss: in a few-shot DNA-protein
matching task, the unified LucaOne substantially outperforms the concatenation of independent specialist embeddings
(DNABert2 + ESM2-3B) and also outperforms its own ablations trained on nucleic acids or proteins alone (LucaOne-Gene,
LucaOne-Prot). On seven downstream benchmarks spanning single-sequence (genus taxonomy, ncRNA family, protein
subcellular localization, protein thermostability) and paired-sequence tasks (influenza HA assay, protein-protein
interaction, ncRNA-protein interaction), LucaOne meets or beats the modality-specific state of the art, almost always
with substantially less downstream training data. For Linus, this is the **generalist biological foundation model** of
the Group A wave, and the central question it forces is operational: one big LucaOne Worker, or a constellation of
specialist Workers (RiNALMo, ProteinReasoner, AlphaGenome)?

## The problem (in plain language)

Biology's three information-bearing polymers — DNA, RNA, protein — are conventionally modeled by separate specialist
foundation models: DNABert / Nucleotide Transformer / Evo for DNA, RiNALMo for RNA, ESM / ProtTrans / Ankh for protein.
Each model has its own vocabulary, its own pretraining corpus, its own benchmarks. When a downstream task crosses
modalities — predict a protein from a CDS, classify a non-coding RNA-protein interaction, link a regulatory variant to
its expressed product — practitioners stitch independent embeddings together and hope the concatenation captures the
cross-modal relationship. The DNABert2 + ESM2-3B baseline in this paper is the canonical example of that
stitched-together approach.

The deeper problem this paper goes after is that **the central dogma is the most basic regularity in molecular biology,
and a stitched-together pipeline cannot learn it**. DNA encodes RNA encodes protein, with codon-level rules and
species-level codon-usage variation. Two specialist embeddings, each trained only on its own modality, have no shared
geometry in which "this DNA codes for this protein" is a learnable relation. You can train a downstream classifier on
top of the concatenation, but the shared structure has to be re-learned from scratch with paired labels — which is
exactly the regime where labels are scarce.

LucaOne reframes the question: train one model on both modalities at once, share the parameter space, and let the
central dogma fall out as an emergent property of the joint distribution. If this works, it changes the unit of
biological foundation modeling from "one model per polymer" to "one model per kingdom of biology."

## What they propose

LucaOne is a 20-layer transformer encoder, 40 attention heads per layer, embedding dimension 2,560, totaling 1.8B
parameters. Three departures from a vanilla BERT-style design matter for Linus:

- **Unified 39-token vocabulary** covering 4 nucleotides (with U folded to T), 20 amino acids, IUPAC ambiguity codes,
  the rare amino acids pyrrolysine and selenocysteine, and the standard special tokens. To break the alphabet collision
  between nucleotides and amino acids that share single-letter codes (A, T, C, G also appear as amino acids), the
  nucleotide letters are remapped to digits 1-5 internally. A **token-type embedding** (0 for nucleic acid, 1 for
  protein) lets the model know which alphabet a given sequence is drawn from, so the same surface token can mean
  different things in different contexts.
- **Pre-layer normalization** instead of post-layer norm, for trainability of deep stacks; **rotary position
  embeddings** (RoPE) instead of absolute positions, so the model can extrapolate beyond the 1,280-token max training
  length to longer downstream sequences.
- **Semi-supervised pretraining** with ten objectives, not two. Beyond the standard masked-token objectives on nucleic
  acid and protein sequences, eight annotation-prediction tasks are included: nucleotide-level genome region type (CDS,
  intron, tRNA, ncRNA, rRNA, miscRNA, tmRNA, regulatory), order-level nucleic-acid taxonomy, protein homologous
  superfamily, protein domain, protein site, order-level protein taxonomy, protein keyword, and protein 3D coordinates.
  Different tasks receive different loss weights (1.0 for the four most informative, 0.2 for the rest) to balance task
  complexity.

Pretraining data sources: RefSeq (DNA / RNA sequences and annotations) for nucleic acids; UniRef50, UniProt, and
ColabFoldDB for protein sequences; InterPro for protein domain / site / superfamily annotations; RCSB-PDB and
AlphaFold2-Swiss-Prot for protein 3D structure. Reverse-strand DNA is included explicitly. Sequences longer than 1,280
tokens are segmented.

Compute: **8x NVIDIA A100, 120 days** of training, batch size 8 with gradient accumulation step 32. The 5.6M-step
checkpoint (36.95B tokens, ~20x parameters) is the comparison point against ESM2-3B; a longer-trained 17.6M-step
checkpoint (116.62B tokens) is also released and shows only marginal additional improvements.

Downstream usage is parameter-frozen: LucaOne is used as an embedding backbone, with a small task-specific head
(pooling + dense layer, sometimes attention pooling) trained on top. This is the deliberate "general-purpose embedding
service" deployment pattern, not the "fine-tune the whole model" pattern.

## Key results

**Central dogma emergence (the headline result).** On a few-shot DNA-protein matching task drawn from RefSeq across 13
species (8,533 positive and 17,067 negative pairs, with a deliberately inverted 4:3:25 train:val:test split to stress
the few-shot regime), the unified LucaOne reaches accuracy 0.8453 / F1 0.8048, substantially above DNABert2 + ESM2-3B
(0.7309 / 0.6680) and above LucaOne-Gene + LucaOne-Prot trained on the same corpus separately (0.7892 / 0.7491). The
unified model does materially better than the sum of its modality-restricted ablations — which is the cleanest evidence
that joint training learns something neither half can learn alone. Performance degrades on species with unusual codon
usage (Ciona intestinalis, GC-poor and tunicate-divergent), and improves when training data is augmented with related
urochordates, suggesting the central-dogma capability is a learned codon-usage table, not an axiomatic genetic-code
lookup.

**Embedding geometry.** On three t-SNE benchmarks (12-marine-species nucleic-acid contigs from CAMI2; 12 Pfam-clan
protein dataset; 12 most-common-GO-term protein dataset), LucaOne embeddings cluster more tightly by species / clan / GO
term than MultiHot, DNABert2, or ESM2-3B-3B baselines. Same-gene DNA and protein sequences also converge in LucaOne
embedding space without any paired training signal — a soft confirmation of the central-dogma claim at the geometry
level.

**Seven downstream benchmarks.** Using frozen LucaOne embeddings plus a simple downstream head, the model competes with
or beats modality-specific state of the art across the board: genus taxonomy (better than BERTax), ncRNA family
classification (better than RNAGCN), protein subcellular localization (better than DeepLocPro), protein thermostability
(Spearman correlation, better than TAPE), influenza haemagglutination assay prediction from paired HA / NA sequences
(better than PREDAC), protein-protein interaction (better than DeepPPI), ncRNA-protein interaction (better than
ncRPI-LGAT). For most tasks, LucaOne matches or surpasses these specialists with **substantially smaller downstream
training sets** — the value of the embedding is realized in the few-shot regime.

**Scale realism.** Authors are upfront that the model is not at the frontier of biological scale: the 1.8B parameter
count is comfortable rather than ambitious, training was bounded by available compute, and they explicitly note in the
discussion that "small specialized models occasionally outperform large pre-trained models" on certain downstream tasks.
The contribution is the joint-modality formulation, not raw capability.

## What's reusable in Linus

**Direct, Phase 3 (Knowledge & Parallel Agents) and Phase 7 (Skills & Autonomy):** LucaOne is a strong candidate for the
**default biological-sequence Worker** in Linus's tool registry. Embedding inference for a 1.8B encoder is comfortable
on M1 Max (similar parameter scale to ESM2-3B, which the MLX community has run). One Worker that handles DNA, RNA, and
protein queries is operationally simpler than dispatching to three specialists, and the few-shot benchmark profile means
downstream task setup costs are low. Code, inference scripts, and checkpoints are all open: model code at
`github.com/LucaOne/LucaOne`, embedding inference at `github.com/LucaOne/LucaOneApp`, downstream tasks at
`github.com/LucaOne/LucaOneTasks`, weights and data at Zenodo (DOI 10.5281/zenodo.15171943). License is CC-BY-4.0 —
fully usable in Linus.

**Direct, Phase 3 (KnowledgeBase integration):** LucaOne embeddings are a natural anchor for a **multi-modality
biological knowledge graph**. Same-gene DNA and protein sequences cluster together in the embedding space without paired
training; this property is exactly what a KG needs to link "the EGFR gene" and "the EGFR protein" as the same entity
expressed in two modalities. Adding a LucaOne embedding column to KnowledgeBase entries (genes, transcripts, proteins)
gives the KG a cross-modal similarity metric out of the box.

**Direct, Phase 6 (Fine-Tuning):** 1.8B parameters is well within LoRA-on-M1-Max range. Frozen-encoder + task-head
training (the paper's downstream setup) is even cheaper. For Dan's specific task distribution (genomics, computational
biology, virology given the influenza HA experiments in the paper), a LucaOne + Dan-corpus fine-tune is a tractable and
well-scoped Phase 6 milestone. The frozen-embedding deployment pattern also means that swapping in fine-tuned downstream
heads can happen without retraining the backbone.

**Indirect, architectural pattern:** The token-type embedding mechanism (0 for nucleic acid, 1 for protein) generalizes.
If Linus ever trains or fine-tunes a model that handles multiple structured input types (e.g., code + natural language +
chemical SMILES), token-type embeddings are a clean way to disambiguate alphabets that share characters. Worth keeping
in the design library.

**Indirect, benchmark methodology:** The few-shot evaluation regime — 4:3:25 train:val:test, deliberately inverted to
stress generalization — is a useful pattern for `benchmarks/dan_tasks/`. Most ML benchmarks use ~80:10:10 splits and
miss the regime where Dan actually operates (small labeled corpora, large unlabeled background). LucaOne's split is
closer to scientific reality.

## What's NOT applicable

The training compute (8 A100s for 120 days) is not reproducible on Apple Silicon and is not the contribution Linus needs
to absorb. Linus uses LucaOne as a frozen or LoRA-tuned backbone, not a from-scratch retrain target.

The 1,280-token max training length is short relative to whole-genome work. RoPE allows extrapolation, but the paper
doesn't claim quality at extreme lengths. For long-context genomic tasks (full chromosomes, large viral genomes), Evo /
Evo 2 or HyenaDNA-derived models are likely a better fit; LucaOne is the **generalist for sequence-fragment tasks**, not
the long-context model.

The semi-supervised annotation tasks are deeply tied to RefSeq / UniProt / InterPro / PDB annotation schemas.
Reproducing or extending pretraining on a different corpus would require re-curating those annotation streams. For Linus
this is a "consume the released checkpoint" paper, not a "rerun pretraining" paper.

The downstream tasks are evaluated as classification or regression on top of frozen embeddings. LucaOne is **not a
generative model** — it's an encoder. It will not produce sequences, it will not do sequence completion, it will not
chat. For generative DNA / protein tasks (de novo design, mutagenesis prediction in a generative framing), Evo 2 or
RFdiffusion-style models are the right tools, not LucaOne.

The paper does not report MLX or Apple-Silicon performance. Whether the official PyTorch checkpoint converts cleanly to
MLX, and at what quantization level inference fits comfortably in 32 GB unified memory, are open empirical questions for
Linus's recon phase.

## Connections

**Group A wave.** LucaOne is the **generalist** in a wave of biological foundation models that mostly went specialist.
Evo 2 (genome FM, DNA-only, long-context) is the long-context counterpoint; RiNALMo is the RNA specialist;
ProteinReasoner is the protein-with-CoT specialist; AlphaGenome focuses narrowly on regulatory variant effects; REMME /
REBEAN target metagenomic DNA; Bacformer is the bacterial protein specialist. LucaOne's contribution is the unified
embedding space across DNA / RNA / protein that none of those models have. The architectural question for Linus's Phase
7 tool registry is whether the generalist is "good enough" across the board, or whether the specialists each win on
their home task by a wide enough margin to justify the operational cost of running three or four Workers instead of one.

**Methodology connection to the LLM literature.** The semi-supervised pretraining with ten weighted objectives is
reminiscent of T5-style multi-task training, and the few-shot transfer with frozen embeddings is the same paradigm ESM2
popularized for proteins. The novelty is the _cross-modality_ part, not the methodology.

**KnowledgeBase connection.** Same-gene DNA and protein convergence in embedding space (the geometry result) is the most
directly KB-relevant finding. If KnowledgeBase entries can be tagged with LucaOne embeddings, cross-modal similarity
search and gene/protein deduplication become near-free downstream operations.

## Open questions for Dan

2. **MLX checkpoint compatibility.** The released weights are PyTorch. Does LucaOne convert cleanly to MLX, and at what
   quantization (fp16, int8, int4) does it fit comfortably alongside the rest of Linus's resident models in 32 GB
   unified memory? This is a Phase 1 / Phase 2 recon spike — a Worker can run it from a 1-page spec.

3. **Is the central-dogma-emergence claim load-bearing or marketing?** The headline number (LucaOne 0.8453 vs
   DNABert2+ESM2-3B 0.7309 on DNA-protein matching) is real and the LucaOne-Gene + LucaOne-Prot ablation is the right
   control. But the ablation is at the same checkpoint and same architecture; could the gain be partly attributed to the
   semi-supervised annotation tasks (which the ablations also have) rather than to joint nucleic-acid + protein training
   specifically? Worth re-reading the ablation section carefully before relying on the central-dogma framing in any
   Linus-facing claim.

4. **KnowledgeBase integration scope.** Does it make sense, in Phase 3, to add a LucaOne-embedding column to
   KnowledgeBase entities that have a sequence representation (genes, transcripts, proteins)? The cross-modal similarity
   it would unlock is appealing, but the storage and compute cost is non-trivial — every KB entity would carry a
   2,560-dim float embedding. Worth scoping before building.

5. **Downstream head library.** The paper trains seven downstream heads (genus taxonomy, ncRNA family, ProtLoc,
   ProtStab, InfA, PPI, ncRPI). Should Linus expose these as out-of-the-box tools in the registry
   (`linus.bio.predict_protein_localization(seq)`, etc.), or wait until a real Dan task pulls one in? The tools are
   cheap to wrap given the released downstream-task code, but adding seven tools speculatively violates "delete every
   requirement" until they're needed.

6. **17.6M vs 5.6M checkpoint.** The paper releases both. The 17.6M checkpoint is trained on ~3x more tokens for
   marginal downstream gain (94.93% vs 94.78% on ncRPI). For Linus, is the smaller checkpoint the right default given
   storage and quantization constraints, with the larger one available for tasks where the marginal gain matters?
   Probably yes; worth confirming when we benchmark.
